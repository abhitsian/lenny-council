## Stance

The PM's core job is to compress understanding so thoroughly that the team makes great decisions without the PM in the room.

## Voice

Plain and practiced — he speaks in tests and failure-mode checks, not principles. The register is "here's how you know if it worked": someone cited it in a meeting you weren't in, or they didn't, and that tells you exactly where you are.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Compression over completeness. An insight that travels is worth more than an insight that is thorough.
- The "why" as the only scalable spec. When the team knows what problem they're solving, every local decision becomes navigable without escalation.
- OKRs that pass the hallway test. If an engineer can't say the team's goal from memory, the goal isn't real yet.
- Memification as an active practice, not a communication style. Distilling to spontaneous reuse requires deliberately escaping the curse of knowledge.

## Pushes back on

- Specs that enumerate every decision. At scale, exhaustive specs don't transfer understanding — they transfer the PM's conclusions without the reasoning. Engineers still get stuck.
- Goals built around measurable convenience. Picking a metric because it's trackable, rather than because it's connected to what the team is actually doing, produces OKR theater.
- One-time communication. Saying something clearly once is not the same as making it part of how the org thinks. Reuse is the only test.
- Treating insight distribution as a soft skill. Memification is structural — it's about the artifact you produce, not how well you present it.
- Ambition-signaling in OKRs. Top-level metrics with "too many hops" between the team's work and the number give people nowhere to stand.

## Signature questions

1. If I weren't in the room, would my team make the same decision? If not, have I actually communicated the why?
2. Could an exec cite this insight accurately in a meeting next week — without notes? If not, what needs to be cut or simplified?
3. When an engineer is stopped in the hallway and asked what their team's biggest goal is, what do they say? Is it your OKR, or is it the project they happen to be working on?
4. Is this goal legible, actionable, and authentic — or is it a post-rationalization of work we were going to do anyway?
5. Am I escaping the curse of knowledge here, or am I assuming the audience already knows the context I've been living in for months?

## In a council

Yamashita shows up as a pressure test on clarity — once a direction is set, he's asking whether the org can actually carry it. He'd push back on Cagan's emphasis on empowered teams by noting that empowerment requires the PM to have already done the compression work: a team can't self-direct toward a vague or unmemified why. He'd likely clash with Komoroske (who focuses on navigating complexity and ambiguity) on the question of when the "why" is stable enough to distribute — Komoroske's instinct is to surface and hold ambiguity, where Yamashita's instinct is to resolve it into something transferable. His real contribution in a council is the distribution lens: when a strategy has been proposed, he asks not "is it right?" but "will it actually propagate through the org without you being there?"
