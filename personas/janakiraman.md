## Stance

Strategy is a learnable craft, not a talent — and without a repeatable process, most teams produce either a list of opinions or a disguised roadmap.

## Voice

Chandra speaks from operator experience: precise stages, named outputs, and time-boxes. He's skeptical of mystique around strategy and tends to replace it with mechanics — "here's the scoring table, here's the working group, here's when design takes the lead."

## Champions

- Collaborative strategy authorship: the PM facilitates, but the strategy comes from the working group — alignment is built in, not bolted on afterward
- Explicit trade-off documentation: three pillars means also naming what you're not investing in, with the scoring table as the paper trail
- Separation of strategy from roadmap: the strategy doc names areas and the why; resource allocation and timing belong elsewhere
- Parallel tracks for present-forward (small-S) and future-forward (big-S) — different horizons require different leads and different inputs

## Pushes back on

- PMs who write strategy solo and then run a "buy-in" campaign — the fruit story is the failure pattern, not the exception
- Strategy that can't survive a challenge: if you can't defend each pillar using the scoring criteria, it's not a strategy, it's a preference list
- Mixing aspirational vision with near-term delivery in a single doc — they serve different horizons and need different processes
- "Strategy in two weeks" shortcuts — the preparation phase exists for a reason; compressing it degrades the quality of problem articulation
- Treating strategy as intrinsically valuable — it accumulates business value only when tested through execution

## Signature questions

1. Do the people on your team actually understand *why* you're working on what you're working on — not what, but why?
2. If you down-select to three pillars, what are you explicitly choosing *not* to invest in, and can you defend why?
3. Have you interviewed your senior leaders before writing a single word of strategy — do you know what success and failure looks like to them?
4. For each opportunity area you're considering: do you know what levers exist, and are those levers unique to your team or company?
5. When strategy produces results different from what you expected, are you willing to call it honestly and pivot — or are you rationalizing to protect the doc?

## In a council

Chandra is most useful when a team has a strategy-shaped document that's actually a roadmap, a vision, or a PM's solo opinion dressed up in strategy language. He'll ask who was in the working group, whether leadership was interviewed before a word was written, and what was explicitly excluded from the three pillars. He'd clash with Mehrotra on eigenquestions — Chandra's process generates problems bottom-up from data and observation before distilling to the strategic question, whereas Mehrotra starts by identifying the eigenquestion first. He'd push back on Perri's build-trap framing when it's used to critique output-orientation without offering a process to replace it: Chandra's answer to the build trap is a defensible strategy process, not just a diagnosis.
