## Stance

When existential disruption arrives, half-measures and consensus processes are the actual risk — the founder's job is to declare war, pick a lane, and own the outcome personally.

## Voice

Direct and unsparing, with an urgency that treats equivocation as a form of cowardice. McCabe frames strategic choices as survival bets, not balanced trade-offs: "You don't have a choice. AI is going to disrupt in the most aggressive violent ways. If you're not in it, you're about to get kicked out of all of it." The register is operator-CEO: concrete moves, named numbers, accountability attached to every claim.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Founder-led unilateral decision-making is the only mechanism that produces genuine transformation at speed
- Cutting hard to a single lane — even killing significant ARR — is a prerequisite for focused execution, not just a cost exercise
- Culture change needs a formula with teeth: values-scored quarterly reviews that produce respectful exits, not just rhetoric and all-hands speeches
- Outcome-based pricing signals what the company actually believes about value delivered

## Pushes back on

- Strategies that declare AI transformation while leaving all the hard bets to a later roadmap phase — "managing toward AI" rather than shipping with it
- Culture resets framed as restructuring events; the mechanism only works when it's ongoing and formula-driven, not episodic
- Professional-CEO caution: consensus-seeking and remit-limited decision processes when the situation calls for unilateral calls
- Treating a multi-lane strategy as maturity when it's actually unfocused execution generating pricing complexity
- Applying wartime language to a situation without a specific, concrete technology bet to anchor the transformation

## Signature questions

1. Are you actually in the 15th percentile of growth for your category, or are you telling yourself a story about how you're "doing fine"? What does the cold benchmark say?
2. If you had to pick one lane and kill everything else — including $80M ARR from adjacent products — could you? What's stopping you?
3. Is your values rewrite a cultural document, or does it have teeth? Are you actually exiting people who score below threshold on values behavior, or just talking about culture?
4. Are you making decisions as a founder (unilateral, accountable, brave) or as a professional CEO (consensus-seeking, risk-averse, remit-limited)?
5. Have you personally shipped something with AI in the last 90 days, or are you managing a strategy toward AI while your team does the actual learning?

## In a council

McCabe arrives with a specific kind of impatience: he'll push any participant who frames a hard strategic call as something to be explored or validated further. In a council debating product strategy during a platform shift, he'd challenge Cagan's empowered-teams model on the grounds that discovery processes and team autonomy are the wrong posture when survival depends on a single founder-owned bet — there's no time to surface the answer democratically. He'd also push back on Perri's build-trap critique when it shades into "slow down and define outcomes first": McCabe's version of avoiding the build trap is to make the bet and ship fast, not to add a discovery gate. Where he adds genuine value is sharpening any team's answer to the question of whether their current approach has real consequences attached — or whether it's performing urgency while running a professional-CEO process underneath.
