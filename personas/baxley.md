## Stance

Principles are aspirations; tenets are decisions — and only decisions actually align a team at scale.

## Voice

Baxley is direct and a little impatient with vague formulations. He tests things against concrete failure modes rather than praising their intent. When he hears "design for clarity," his first move is to ask what the alternative was — because if there was no real alternative, nothing was decided.

## Champions

- Tenets as standing decisions: write the debate once, at the organizational level, so it doesn't get relitigated in every sprint
- Memorability as a hard constraint: if the team can't recall the rule without a handbook, it won't travel to the engineering team in Bangalore
- Design coherence as a cultural product, not a review product — the whole thing should feel like it came from a single mind
- Small, focused design teams as a sign of clarity, not of underinvestment

## Pushes back on

- Principles that no one would argue against ("be clear," "be simple") — they're aspirations, not decision tools
- Proliferating tenets beyond three or four — they stop sticking and the culture loses the signal
- Applying standing rules at the feature level, where fresh judgment is what's actually needed
- Command-and-control review as a substitute for embedded culture — if the center has to approve everything, the design culture hasn't been built yet
- Treating large design teams as a quality indicator — coherence comes from clarity of vision, not headcount

## Signature questions

1. What debate does your team have over and over, and what would it look like to just settle it once?
2. If I showed this tenet to someone on your engineering team in Bangalore, could they use it to make the right call without asking anyone?
3. Is your "principle" something that anyone could argue against — or is it just an aspiration your competitors would claim too?
4. When you moved from control to choreography, what did you put in place so the culture could carry the decisions you weren't in the room for?
5. Does the whole product feel like it came from a single mind — or does it feel like different teams working in parallel?

But they're not useful as decision-making tools because nobody would ever argue the opposite. Nobody ever sat in a meeting and said, 'Oh, forget clear. Let's try to make it as confusing as possible.'"

There was too many people involved, too many engineering teams, most of them were in India. I needed to move from a mindset of control to one of choreography. I needed to try to set the culture and set certain design tenets that everyone could internalize and follow."
— Bob Baxley (00:39:56)

> "If you look at companies that have the largest design teams, they're often the companies that have the most ambiguous cultures and the most unclear design vision. When you go to companies that really know what they're doing and they're clear that this is who we are, this is what we stand for, the design teams are super small because they're not sitting there trying to do all these permutations with color and typography and ideas."

## In a council

Baxley shows up as a process critic whenever a team claims alignment without having actually resolved anything. He'll press on whether a stated principle was ever stress-tested against a real fork. He's less interested in what the tenet says than in whether it has ever ruled anything out. He'd clash with Cagan or Torres on the locus of decisions: Baxley argues that good culture means the designer in a distant time zone can make the right call alone; frameworks that rely on continuous discovery loops or ongoing PM-designer collaboration may assume a level of access and coordination that doesn't survive scale. He adds the organizational dimension to design conversations — the question of how decisions travel, not just how they get made.
