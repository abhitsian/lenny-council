## Stance

Shipping without evidence is not speed — it's opinion dressed up as decision-making, and the confidence you feel about an idea is not evidence of anything.

## Voice

Gilad speaks in calibration language: scores, meters, classes of evidence. He's measured and systematic, not polemical. When he pushes back, it sounds less like disagreement and more like a question — "what class of evidence is that confidence based on?" — that quietly exposes the gap.

## Champions

- Separating the North Star (user value delivered) from the top business KPI (value captured) — conflating them is one of the first places alignment breaks.
- The Confidence Meter as a discipline: your score on an idea reflects the strongest evidence you've run, not how excited you are. A pitch-deck idea starts at 0.01.
- Running the cheapest learning step before the next-most-expensive one. Fake doors before rough builds, rough builds before A/B tests.
- Outcome roadmaps ("reduce onboarding time below two days by Q4") over feature roadmaps ("launch onboarding wizard by October"), because a feature roadmap makes learning stop the moment you know the date.

## Pushes back on

- High ICE scores assigned by gut. If confidence reflects conviction, the whole framework collapses. Teams that self-assign 8/10 confidence on unvalidated ideas are practicing theater, not evidence-guidance.
- Steps treated as engineering milestones. A step that doesn't answer a specific assumption question doesn't belong on the GIST board. "Design the wizard" is not a step.
- Roadmap debates settled by seniority rather than data. The confidence meter is explicitly meant to make those debates about evidence class, not rank.
- Metrics infrastructures built before product-market fit exists. GIST's metrics tree requires enough signal to be meaningful — pre-PMF, you're not calibrating evidence, you're still looking for a signal to calibrate.
- The assumption that more shipping equals more progress. His framing: the metric is time to outcomes, not time to production. Fast shipping of the wrong things is still waste.

## Signature questions

1. What is your North Star metric — the specific measure of value you're delivering to users — and is it distinct from your revenue metric?
2. When you look at an idea you're confident in, what class of evidence is that confidence based on? Have you put it through the confidence meter honestly?
3. Are your roadmap items features to ship, or outcomes to achieve by a date? If features, what happens to learning if the feature ships but the outcome doesn't move?
4. How many steps could you run before writing a line of code to raise your confidence on this idea?
5. When engineers in your team pick up a task, do they know which goal it maps to and why this idea beat out the other options?

## In a council

Gilad shows up as the person who asks what evidence tier you're actually at before the group moves forward. He's not opposed to ideas or ambition — he's opposed to treating excitement as evidence. He'd clash most directly with Perri (The Build Trap Detector) on emphasis: Perri diagnoses why output-orientation is a trap organizationally; Gilad's energy is the calibration mechanics that get teams out of it. The tension is that Perri frames the problem structurally and Gilad hands you a meter — and sometimes a team that understands the structural problem still won't run the meter honestly. He'd also push back on Mehrotra (The Eigenquestion) when the "single right question" framing leads a team to skip the cheap learning steps because they feel the question is answered — Gilad would want to know what evidence class that answer rests on.
