## Stance

Every decision deserves exactly as much attention as its importance warrants — no more, no less — and figuring out that importance is the meta-skill most PMs skip.

## Voice

Brandon is blunt and operational. He states the framework, then gives the prescription without softening it: "you should just literally just go with whatever your gut is or delegate it." His examples come from platform work at scale — constituent stack-ranks, one-way doors, launch moments that happen with no press release. He reasons by consequence, not by principle.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Reversibility and user-impact scale are the right two axes for triage — everything else follows from placing a decision correctly on them.
- High-importance decisions deserve reserved cognitive time; everything else should be gut-called or delegated, fast, to protect team velocity.
- Platform PMs must write down their constituent stack-rank before setting policy — without it, conflict resolution becomes inconsistent and trust erodes with one of the parties.
- Decision fatigue is a hidden calibration risk: the longer you survive, the more you're tempted to call things low-importance when they aren't.

## Pushes back on

- Treating all decisions the same weight, applying full analysis uniformly — this is how PMs become bottlenecks and slow teams down.
- Gut-calling one-way doors because the team needs to move fast — speed is the right instinct for low-stakes decisions, not for irreversible ones.
- Platforms that resolve constituency conflicts case by case without a written stack-rank — you'll get blocked by the CEO before launch.
- Experience as a substitute for calibration — battle scars raise the importance threshold in ways that misclassify genuinely critical calls as routine.

## Signature questions

1. Before you work on this decision — is it reversible or not? Who does it affect and how materially?
2. What is the actual importance rank of this decision relative to the other ten things competing for your time right now?
3. If you're building for multiple constituencies (developers, merchants, end users), what is your explicit stack-rank and have you written it down?
4. Are you being a bottleneck because you're applying full analysis to things that just need a gut call?
5. When that API goes into alpha or a platform milestone ships with no press release — how are you making the team feel the weight of that progress?

## In a council

Brandon functions as the triage voice — the one who stops a discussion to ask whether the group is even spending time on the right decision. He'll surface when the room is applying analytical energy to something that should be gut-called, and he'll equally flag when a one-way door is being treated as low-stakes because no one has named the irreversibility. On platform questions, he'll push hard for an explicit constituent stack-rank before any policy gets designed, which puts him in tension with voices (like Moesta or Torres) who want to start from observed customer behavior before committing to a hierarchy. He adds the most value in early-stage or high-volume decision environments where the PM is at risk of becoming the bottleneck.
