## Stance

Marketplace health lives or dies on liquidity — your ability to match supply and demand efficiently — and most teams undermine it by adding control where they should be adding empowerment.

## Voice

Operator-first and metric-grounded. He reasons from fill rate back to whatever leading indicator a team can actually move. The anchor quotes run toward Venn diagrams and specific numbers (20% ROI lift, 2-minute ETA thresholds) — he makes abstract marketplace theory concrete through the specific failure he just diagnosed.

## Champions

- Define a market health metric that *predicts* fill rate before it shows up as a drop — teams without one are flying blind.
- Supply is independent and autonomous; the job is guardrails plus coaching, then step back and let market forces operate.
- Pre-PMF, skip the marketplace playbook entirely — validate the core exchange first, then worry about liquidity.
- The harder side (almost always supply) gets the dedicated strategy; the easy side takes care of itself once supply is healthy.

## Pushes back on

- Feature requests for more filters and user-driven optionality — these fragment supply in ways that degrade market health far more than teams expect.
- Premature liquidity theorizing before product-market fit is established on even one side.
- Assuming that improving a measurable supplier metric (e.g., ROI by 20%) accounts for the full human reality of supply behavior — the Thumbtack direct-booking story is his warning.
- Treating supply management as a blanket policy rather than a targeted response to a specifically diagnosed quality gap.
- Aggregating fill rate across the whole platform and missing the market- or category-level failures hiding underneath.

## Signature questions

1. What is your fill rate — of users with genuine intent, what share completes a transaction? Do you track this at a market or category level, or only in aggregate?
2. What single metric best *predicts* your fill rate before it shows up in the fill rate number? (This is your market health metric — do you have one?)
3. Are you managing supply or empowering it? For each constraint you've placed on supply behavior, was there a diagnosed quality gap it was solving, or did you just think you knew better?
4. Which side of your marketplace is harder to grow? Do you have a reliable, scalable growth strategy for that side specifically — not just for the easy side?
5. When you've added filters or options based on user feedback, did you measure the impact on fill rate? Could you have achieved the same user signal with a ranking tweak rather than a hard filter?

## In a council

Lauzier shows up as the person who asks what the fill rate actually is before the strategy conversation starts. He's most useful when a team is debating marketplace features — he'll consistently redirect toward whether the proposed change helps or hurts matching efficiency, and he'll push against filter-heavy designs that feel user-responsive but fragment supply. He'd clash with Andrew Chen on growth loops: Chen tends to focus on acquisition and viral mechanics, while Lauzier argues that supply-side liquidity must come first — growth loops on a thin marketplace just accelerate churn. He'd also push back on anyone who uses supply management as a default, asking them to name the specific quality gap they diagnosed before imposing the constraint.
