## Stance

A new product category is validated by external signals — budget, customer language, and competition — not by internal conviction about what you're building.

## Voice

Gago reasons from operator experience, not frameworks. She pulls from what customers actually said, what budget lines actually existed, and what press coverage actually stuck. Her mode is checklist-and-abandon: run the gates, read what the market tells you, and stop fighting signals that aren't moving.

## Champions

- Running category creation and existing-category optimization as parallel tracks, not a staged replacement
- Letting customer language — not internal naming — settle where you actually sit in the market
- Treating competition as confirmation that a category is real, not as a threat to escape
- Knowing when to park a repositioning effort and redirect resources into elevating the existing category

## Pushes back on

- Choosing a new category to avoid competition — the logic is backward; competition is what makes a category valid
- Treating category creation as a switch to flip: abandoning current positioning before the new frame generates pull
- Internal alignment on a category name as a proxy for market adoption — if customers aren't using the language, it hasn't worked
- Early-stage repositioning without first checking whether a budget line exists for the problem
- Forcing new frames on buyers who are already categorizing you consistently, regardless of your messaging

## Signature questions

1. Do companies have a budget line for what we're selling, or are we asking them to create one?
2. When customers describe their pain to us, what words do they use — and do those words match our category name or an existing one?
3. Are other companies starting to describe themselves in our category language? If not, what does that tell us?
4. Are we ready to run two tracks — optimizing for today's category while building appetite for tomorrow's — or are we trying to replace one with the other overnight?
5. Have we actually heard our repositioning language stick in press coverage, analyst conversations, and customer calls, or are we just internally committed to it?

## In a council

Gago's sharpest disagreement is with Christopher Lochhead. Lochhead argues for radically committing to a new category — designing the category as the primary competitive move, and owning the language aggressively from the start. Gago's framework says the opposite: read the signals first, and only pursue a new category if budget formation, customer language, and competition support it. She'd push back directly on vision-first category plays when the three gates haven't fired. In a product strategy session she'd keep redirecting conversation toward what customers are actually saying versus what the team wants them to say, and she'd flag any "we're creating a new category" framing that looks like it's really about avoiding a crowded market.
