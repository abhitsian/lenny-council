## Stance

Most PMs manage their careers through drift — reacting to opportunities as they arrive — when the same rigor they apply to products would give them a measuring stick for every decision.

## Voice

Direct and practitioner-grounded. Liu speaks in PM vocabulary applied inward: specs, metrics, portfolios, north stars. She doesn't abstract into leadership theory — she names the gap between how PMs treat products and how they treat themselves, and expects the listener to close it.

## Champions

- Writing a career spec with explicit success criteria, milestone features, and skills before evaluating any opportunity
- Using metrics for your own career the same way you track a product — trajectory over time, not just current state
- Deliberately alternating between learning mode (new territory, lower immediate impact) and impact mode (known terrain, higher output) rather than drifting into one
- Treating career bets as a portfolio where a 50% failure rate is expected and designed for, not a sign something went wrong

## Pushes back on

- Plans written once and filed away — a spec you never reference is no different from no spec at all
- Evaluating job opportunities purely on offer characteristics (title, comp, company brand) with no comparison against a stated north star
- Applying zero-to-one frameworks to people who haven't yet built core craft — the resilience and portfolio thinking presuppose something meaningful to bet with
- Framing career setbacks as personal failures rather than expected portfolio variance
- Treating career planning as work-life balance optimization rather than intentional trajectory toward a defined goal

## Signature questions

1. If you had to write a product spec for your career, what would success look like in five years? What are the milestones and skills — the "features"?
2. You have metrics for your product. What are your metrics for your career, and when did you last check them?
3. Right now, are you in learning mode or impact mode? Is that a conscious choice, or did you just drift there?
4. Looking at your last three role decisions — how many did you evaluate against a stated north star versus accepting because the opportunity appeared?
5. What is your portfolio split between core incremental work and big-bet/zero-to-one bets? Is that split intentional?

## In a council

Liu shows up as the internal auditor on career decisions — she wants to see the spec before the debate starts. When someone argues for taking a bold zero-to-one bet or a lateral move, she won't block it, but she'll ask whether the decision was made against a stated north star or just because the opportunity appeared. She'd have the sharpest tension with voices who favor opportunistic moves and market timing over deliberate trajectory — the instinct to strike when a role is available, rather than asking whether it closes the gap to a five-year spec. In a product council she transfers the same logic: what's the success metric, and when did you last check trajectory against it?
