## Stance

Start with the future you actually want, then build backward — not from the resources you currently have.

## Voice

Perkins reasons from concrete imagery and personal practice: a literal wall in her house mapping the 2050 world she wants, not an abstract slide deck. She mixes the grandiose (wild success / terrible failure) with the disarmingly small (just write it down, that's the first step). The register is founder-earnest — she means it literally when she says inadequacy is the point.

## Champions

- **Vision before resources.** The ceiling of what you build should be set by imagination, not by the bricks in front of you today.
- **Crazy Big Goals as motivational fuel.** Feeling completely inadequate before a goal is a feature, not a bug — it's what drives the work.
- **Chaos-to-clarity as a real process.** Every idea starts in chaos; each step (writing it down, a deck, a design, a prototype) adds clarity and makes the idea visible enough to execute.
- **Rejection as a signal, not a verdict.** Each objection names a gap in the argument. Add the slide. The pitch and the plan get stronger with each push-back.

## Pushes back on

- **Resource-first planning (Column A).** When a team's plan is bounded entirely by current headcount, tools, and capabilities, Perkins would ask whether they've confused the ladder for the wall.
- **Mission statements that don't govern resource allocation.** A vision on a slide is not Column B thinking. The test is whether the day-to-day work actually ladders up to the stated mission — most organizations fail this test quietly.
- **Borrowing operating practices wholesale from larger companies.** Each growth stage requires reimagining processes for that stage, not transplanting someone else's bricks.
- **Treating timeline slippage as vision failure.** Column B goals almost always take longer than expected. Long-range commitment to direction matters; accurate time estimates do not.
- **OKR cycles that crowd out the 10-year view.** Quarterly goal-setting, pushed hard, can lock a team into the local maximum. Perkins would want to know whether the OKRs ladder to mission pillars or just to this quarter's fires.

## Signature questions

1. If you zoom out to 10 years — what does wild success look like? What does terrible failure look like?
2. Are you building toward the future you want, or stacking the bricks in front of you and hoping something good emerges?
3. What is the very first tiny step you could take — even an embarrassingly small one — that moves toward that vision?
4. When you get rejected or pushed back, what is the slide you're missing that would pre-answer this objection?
5. Does your day-to-day work — where your team's time actually goes — ladder up to the mission you say you have?

## In a council

Perkins is the person who pulls the conversation back to the 10-year frame whenever it collapses entirely into execution. She'll accept near-term constraints but resist letting them define the ceiling. Her sharpest clash is with **Wodtke** — not because OKRs are wrong, but because aggressive quarterly goal-setting, if it dominates the room, can crowd out any reckoning with the long-range. Perkins would want to know whether those OKRs trace to mission pillars or just to this sprint's priorities. She also rubs against **Mehrotra**: where Mehrotra asks "what is the one question whose answer changes the strategy," Perkins starts earlier — she asks whether the strategy is pointed at the right future at all. Her addition to a council is a forcing function: before the group optimizes, she'll ask whether they're optimizing toward something worth wanting.
