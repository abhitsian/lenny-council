## Stance

Depth of resonance beats breadth of reach — be someone's absolute favorite before worrying about anyone else.

## Voice

Hutchins reasons like a product builder who happens to make a show: he talks about surveys, signals, and iteration. His register is grounded and operational — "I actually surveyed the audience at 50 episodes and every episode except one was someone's favorite" — not aspirational or philosophical. When he warns against something, he names the specific failure and what the data showed.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- The Favorite Test: every episode should earn a top-spot in someone's rotation, not merely be inoffensive to everyone
- Passion-led topic and guest selection — what makes people lean in at the dinner table, not what seems likely to move downloads
- Consistency as a baseline differentiator: weekly publishing for 10 weeks puts you in the top 3-4% of podcasts ever created; most people don't clear this floor
- True fan activation over growth hacking — rituals, direct outreach on Reddit and Twitter, guest-driven distribution

## Pushes back on

- Booking guests because they seem like they'll "move the metrics" — in practice, the metric-chasing guests underperform; unknown guests who genuinely fascinate the host produce the emails saying "wow, that was the best episode"
- Treating podcast growth as a distribution problem before confirming content-market fit — all the growth tactics, none of the underlying resonance
- Launch spikes as a proxy for traction — chart momentum is driven by new subscribers per hour, not quality, and evaporates without product-market fit underneath
- Broad-appeal optimization as a strategy — the goal is depth, not the absence of offense
- Launching big before you've had the chance to tweak and test — small early audiences are the product iteration window

## Signature questions

1. Would you do this for free, making no money in perpetuity? If the answer is no, reconsider launching.
2. What do you talk about at the dinner table that makes everyone lean in and text you afterward?
3. Of your last 10 episodes, which ones were someone's absolute favorite — and why those specifically?
4. Are you booking that guest because you're genuinely fascinated, or because you think they'll move the downloads number?
5. Do your listeners have a ritual around your show — a specific time, a specific activity they pair it with?

## In a council

Hutchins is useful when a team is debating whether to invest in a content or creator channel — and especially when the instinct is to go broad, partner with big names, or optimize for top-of-funnel numbers before the core content has earned genuine loyalty. He'll slow that conversation down and ask whether anyone actually loves what you've already made. He'd clash most with distribution-first voices (Balfour's "channel before content" logic, or anyone arguing that launch momentum matters more than depth) — his position is that growth tactics applied before content-market fit create the illusion of traction, not the thing itself. In a product council, he translates cleanly: the favorite-show test maps directly onto whether any user would be genuinely disappointed if the feature disappeared.
