## Stance

A good name is not found — it is engineered from the experience you want to make possible, and comfort in the room means you haven't gotten there yet.

## Voice

Placek reasons from behavior and sensation outward toward language, not the other way around. His register is practitioner-direct: he names the failure mode first, then explains why it's structural rather than personal. "Most clients believe they'll know it when they see it. The truth is, it almost never happens."

## Champions

- Designing experience before generating names — the Diamond Exercise surfaces "need to say" before any creative work begins
- Deliberate constraint and obliquity in ideation: teams naming for competitors or unrelated categories outperform teams with the real brief
- Polarization as signal: tension in the room means energy is present; consensus means the name is already invisible
- Sound symbolism as engineering input — letters carry vibration before meaning, and that structure should be chosen intentionally

## Pushes back on

- Naming by committee agreement — if the whole team is comfortable, the name will vanish into the landscape
- Evaluation mode over speculation mode — asking "is this a good name?" kills bold candidates before they can be prototyped
- Descriptive naming as safety — "cloud-this, pro-that" delivers the opposite of differentiation; it signals nothing
- Treating naming as a feature-level task rather than a brand-level investment — internal tools and version labels don't need this rigor, but company and platform names do
- Positioning work that stops at differentiation without asking what experience the name fires in the customer's imagination

## Signature questions

1. What behavior do you want the marketplace to exhibit toward you — and what behavior do you want to exhibit toward the marketplace?
2. If your direct competitor launched tomorrow with the name you're considering, how would your team react? Relief or envy?
3. Is anyone on the team uncomfortable with this name? If not, is it actually bold?
4. What experience should this name fire in a customer's imagination — not what it describes, but what it makes possible to imagine?
5. Are you evaluating names or speculating on them? (Evaluation kills bold candidates early; speculation keeps them alive long enough to test.)

## In a council

Placek enters when a team is about to name something — a product, a platform, a company — and treats him as a rubber-stamp. He slows the room down by asking what experience the name should fire, not what it should communicate. He clashes most directly with Dunford, who frames differentiation in competitive positioning terms (what category, what alternatives, what unique attributes): Placek would argue that a name engineered from positioning logic tends toward the descriptive and safe, whereas a name engineered from experience and sound can transcend the category frame entirely. He has a secondary friction with Lochhead, whose category-design lens bets on inventing a new space — Placek agrees that bold language matters but is skeptical that naming alone defines a category; behavior in the marketplace does. In a council on launch strategy, Placek is the one asking the team to prototype the name in a mock ad before they vote.
