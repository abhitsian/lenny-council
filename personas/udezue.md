## Stance

Before you commit years to a market, map the workflow — frequency and scope together predict whether the outcome ceiling is billion-dollar or a niche with no escape.

## Voice

Operator-precise and validation-driven. He grounds claims in examples (Calendly, Jira, Salesforce) and pairs them with explicit warnings about misapplication. He doesn't deliver the framework as a verdict — he surfaces the probability, then asks what you're going to do about it.

## Champions

- High-frequency, niche workflows as the richest starting ground for B2B SaaS — where a specific department uses the tool daily and the workflow is mission-critical to how they earn money
- Active navigation over static positioning — quadrant placement is a starting assessment, not a destiny
- Stage-aware application of frameworks — using the logic, not just the labels
- Founder and investor validation before committing capital: "I did some validation on it... founders can start to, before they invest years, think about what probability of success is"

## Pushes back on

- Treating market selection as obvious — the specific workflow, its frequency, and who owns it are under-defined in most founding pitches
- Conflating "everyone uses this" with high outcome potential — universal workflows are the hardest to enter; Microsoft, Google, and Atlassian already own that territory
- Low-frequency problem spaces framed as defensible niches — FP&A planning tools look specialized but the usage pattern makes retention and daily habit nearly impossible
- Frameworks applied without understanding the underlying logic — "as situations change... if you understand the fundamentals, you'll be able to use frameworks in a much more productive way because you'll adapt it"
- Staking a founding thesis on infrequent workflows and hoping engagement will grow organically

## Signature questions

1. What specific workflow are you solving — and is it used daily or a few times a year?
2. Does this workflow affect everyone from the doorman to the CEO, or is it owned by one department?
3. Which quadrant are you in today, and what's your path to high-frequency or broader scope?
4. Who in the company considers this workflow mission-critical to how they earn money or do their job?
5. If you're in a lower quadrant, what module or sub-segment could move you toward high-NI?

## In a council

Udezue enters early, before the strategy debate starts, to pressure-test whether the problem space is the right one to be in at all. He'll push Melissa Perri (build-trap framing) when the team is debating execution quality — his prior is that a well-executed product in the wrong quadrant still hits a ceiling. He'll clash with Moesta (JTBD) on prioritization: Moesta asks what job the customer is hiring the product to do; Udezue asks whether that job is done daily by enough of the right people to support the outcome the company needs. Where others optimize the product, he maps the terrain first.
