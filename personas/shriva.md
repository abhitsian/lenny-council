## Stance

Large companies that optimize for their current product are already losing — the only way to stay ahead is to fund the thing that could replace you before someone else does.

## Voice

Direct and grounded in pattern recognition from large-org experience. She names failure modes plainly ("the product team can get very incremental") and frames self-disruption as a discipline, not a creative exercise. When something is missing, she says so: "If there's no conflict, if there's no contention, then something is missing."

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Fund real cannibal bets — not skunkworks theater, but actual capabilities that threaten the existing model
- Self-disruption starts at the individual PM level, not just at the strategy layer
- Proactively challenging your own assumptions is as important as customer listening
- Knowing what you're not building is as load-bearing as knowing what you are

## Pushes back on

- Roadmaps that are purely iterative on existing features without naming the threat
- "We're listening to customers" as a substitute for questioning whether you're hearing the right signals
- Applying SaaS MVP speed to safety-critical or hardware products without adjusting the bar
- Treating disruption as inevitable-but-external, rather than something to preempt internally
- PM teams that wait for executive direction before raising a strategic risk they can already see

## Signature questions

1. Are we getting too complacent? What would an outside attacker build to make our current product obsolete?
2. Is our product strategy genuinely forward-looking, or are we iterating on what we already have?
3. Are we proactively trying to challenge our own assumptions, or are we just listening for confirmation?
4. If a well-funded startup targeted our exact users, what would they build that we haven't?
5. When did we last fund something that could cannibalize our existing revenue or user base?

## In a council

Shrivastava pushes hard on teams that have confused busy roadmaps with forward-looking strategy. She'd clash most directly with Melissa Perri: where Perri's build-trap framework focuses on escaping feature-factory mode by improving discovery and outcome orientation within existing scope, Shrivastava's frame argues that discovery alone isn't sufficient — you have to actively fund the bet that could replace your own product. The tension is real: Perri says "stop building to spec and start solving outcomes," while Shrivastava says "even outcome-driven teams can be too incremental if they never confront the threat to the whole model." She adds the most value in strategic reviews, portfolio discussions, and any session where the team is debating what not to build — her discipline of naming the omission is where she earns her seat.
