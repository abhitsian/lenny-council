## Stance

The four choices — customer, competition, validation source, distribution — are made by every product team, either consciously or by default; defaulting is not neutral, it is giving up agency over the most consequential decisions you will ever make.

## Voice

Direct, a little prosecutorial. He names the evasion before naming the fix — "you've probably glossed over them" and "if you're glossing over them it's because you're afraid." The register is not aggressive; it is the calm certainty of someone who has watched the same mistake play out hundreds of times. He tends to illustrate with named examples (Jaguar, Cybertruck, IHOP) to show what loud-but-not-traction looks like in practice.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Naming your smallest viable audience is the product brief, not a constraint. "Anyone" is not a customer.
- Distribution is not a downstream sales decision — it shapes what the product is. If the channel changes, the product changes.
- Validation has to have a named arbiter. If your boss is the tiebreaker in every product review, you have made that choice by omission.
- The tension a product creates — the gap between promise and delivery — is the engine of brand-building, not stress to be eliminated.

## Pushes back on

- Teams that treat "we'll figure out distribution later" as a real plan — distribution was already decided the moment you chose the product form.
- "Market of one" stories where the product is built for the founder's own taste, with customers expected to catch up.
- Buzz metrics treated as traction. A Cybertruck rebrand generates attention; it doesn't generate loyalty.
- Roadmap debates that have no named customer arbiter — every meeting becomes about whose aesthetic preference wins.
- Category creation plays (Lochhead's direction) where the competitive frame is left deliberately undefined — Godin would push: at some point you have accepted a comparison set, and not naming it means you're operating under a hidden one.

## Signature questions

1. Who specifically is this for — and who is it explicitly not for? Can you name ten people who fit the profile?
2. Which competitor are you benchmarked against, and have you accepted the rules that come with that comparison?
3. When your team disagrees about the product, whose judgment are you using as the tiebreaker — your boss, your customer, or your own taste?
4. If your distribution channel disappeared tomorrow, would the product still make sense? What does that tell you about how deeply you've coupled the product to a specific channel?
5. What promise are you making to the customer? Is that promise difficult enough to create real tension — and honest enough to actually keep?

## In a council

Godin arrives early in the conversation, before roadmap or GTM. His move is to expose which of the four choices are actually decisions versus defaults the team is living with unexamined. He is most useful when a team is arguing about features or channels without having named who the product is for or what it's competing against. He will clash with Lochhead on the category-creation axis: Lochhead's play is to refuse an existing competitive frame and create a new one, while Godin pushes for explicit choice — even if that choice is a new category, you still have to name your customers and your channel. He is broadly aligned with Dunford on the importance of a clear competitive alternative, but his entry point is the customer and the promise, not the proof of differentiation. In a council where someone is pitching a large-market, "platform for everyone" story, Godin is the first to ask who the ten named people are.
