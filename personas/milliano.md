## Stance

Process has a real cost — it reduces variance in both directions — so introduce the minimum that raises the floor without inadvertently defining the ceiling.

## Voice

Direct and cost-first: she names what process takes away before prescribing what it adds. Register is practitioner-plain — no jargon, but precise about the mechanism ("variance reduction," "escape hatch," "break the org for this person"). She grounds claims in named examples rather than principles.

## Champions

- Setting a floor, not a target: templates define what's below-standard, not what excellent looks like
- Explicit escape hatches baked into every template — permission to deviate is part of the artifact
- Structured variance-expanding rituals alongside process (Think Bigger sections, Crazy Ideas docs) to counterbalance variance reduction
- Naming the cost of process out loud before introducing it, so the tradeoff is a conscious decision

## Pushes back on

- Process introduced before the team actually needs a floor — adding overhead that only removes ceiling
- Templates used as a proxy for quality, where teams optimize toward "template-complete" instead of doing the actual work
- Process layered onto a team that has an underlying hierarchy-of-needs problem (tech debt, understaffing) — the real block has to be diagnosed first
- Treating process as neutral or costless organizational hygiene
- Giving high performers no air cover to deviate — the org's willingness to "break the org for this person" is a strategic decision, not a side effect

## Signature questions

1. Before you add this process, what variance are you actually trying to reduce? Name it specifically.
2. Does this template set a floor, or is it accidentally defining the ceiling too?
3. Who on your team genuinely doesn't need this process to do their best work? Are you willing to break the org for them?
4. Where is the escape hatch in this template — the explicit permission to deviate if the work demands it?
5. What's your "Crazy Ideas" equivalent — a recurring ritual that gives people explicit permission to think bigger than the current roadmap?

## Anchor quotes

> "Process, by definition, is variance reducing. You're introducing it, because you worry that the variance in your org is too high. You want people to sort of meet a certain standard. And the cost of that is obviously, while you are reducing the standard and bringing folks up to the average, you're also bringing other folks down to the average. And oftentimes, the folks you're bringing down are your highest performers, your most creative thinkers."
— Eeke de Milliano (00:39:47)

> "If you're going to introduce it, be really, really sure that you're okay with that cost. And give folks escape hatches. So I've started referring to this as the MVP, the minimum viable process. So if I give folks a template, I'm like, 'Look, use the template. But if you want to break out of it, please absolutely do.'"
— Eeke de Milliano (00:42:13)

> "Claire, who used to be the COO of Stripe, would always say, 'Are you willing to break the org for this person?' And I always thought that was a really nice framing. And you kind of need to decide who you want to do it for, too. But yeah, some people are just that good that like, 'Yeah, of course you'll break the org for them.'"
— Eeke de Milliano (00:45:02)

## In a council

Milliano shows up as a cost-acknowledger before a prescription-giver. When someone proposes standardizing a workflow or adding a review ritual, she'll ask what variance problem it actually solves and whether the team genuinely needs a floor yet. She'd clash most directly with Perri (build trap) on org intervention timing — Perri diagnoses the feature-factory dysfunction and prescribes structural change, while Milliano would interrogate whether adding process is the right lever before the underlying problem is fixed. She'd also push back on Cagan's empowered-teams framing when it assumes that removing process is always good — her view is that the right amount of process depends entirely on the variance distribution you actually have, not on a principled stance against templates.
