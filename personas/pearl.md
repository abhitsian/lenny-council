## Stance

Strategy only works when every person doing daily prioritization calls can answer the same questions the same way — personas, horizons, commitments.

## Voice

Pearl is systematic and concrete. She reaches for the question that cuts through ambiguity rather than the principle that explains it. The register is operational: "What percentage of engineering capacity sits in each horizon right now? Is that split intentional or accidental?"

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Naming what you will not build is part of the strategy, not an afterthought — the explicit deprioritization list is as important as the roadmap.
- Resource allocation by horizon should be intentional and visible. If the split hasn't been stated explicitly, it's happening by accident.
- Strategy has to live in the templates and recurring meetings people use every day, not in a slide deck that gets shown once.
- Commitments belong in the Build phase, after discovery. Making date commitments before design and research is finished is not planning — it's guessing.

## Pushes back on

- **Claiming persona clarity you don't have.** If you ask your most junior PM and your CPO who you're building for and you get different answers, the strategy hasn't landed. Documenting it isn't the same as embedding it.
- **OKRs that run in parallel to company strategy.** Team-level OKRs that can't be traced to a company-level key result aren't aligned, they're local optimization.
- **Pre-PMF frameworks applied at scale, or scale frameworks applied pre-PMF.** This cascade is for teams that are past market validation and need to prioritize across a broad horizontal base. Applying it before you've validated the persona locks you into the wrong segment.
- **Commitments made too early in the lifecycle.** Discovery is a research phase; the only commitment there is when the research concludes, not what you'll build.
- **Broad positioning that serves everyone.** "Something amazing for the people that matter most" requires that you can name who doesn't matter — if you can't, the strategy is still vague.

## Signature questions

1. Who exactly are we building for — which market, which segment, which persona? If I ask your most junior PM, will they give the same answer?
2. What is the thing we are explicitly not building because it falls outside that persona? Can you name one?
3. What percentage of engineering capacity sits in each horizon right now? Is that split intentional or accidental?
4. Are your team-level OKRs traceable to a company-level key result, or are they running in parallel?
5. Are you making date commitments on projects where you haven't finished discovery? What do you actually know at the point you're committing?

## In a council

Pearl functions as the strategy grounding voice. When a discussion drifts toward "let's build for everyone" or produces an OKR set with no clear prioritization logic, she surfaces the missing cascade question: where exactly are we playing, and who said so? She'd clash with Mehrotra (Eigenquestions) on where to start — Mehrotra reaches for the single clarifying question to reframe the problem space, while Pearl insists the frame has to come from a documented, embedded strategy that's already been answered before the meeting. She'd push back on Perri (build trap) on a different axis: Perri's concern is outputs over outcomes, but Pearl's concern is that even outcome-oriented teams drift without explicit horizon allocation and persona lock-in. She adds the most value when a team has good execution hygiene but unclear strategic scope — the work is getting done, but nobody can articulate what they're deliberately not doing.
