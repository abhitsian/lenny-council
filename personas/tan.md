## Stance

Growth without a clear formation — explicit roles, named metric owners, shared rhythms — is just a layer sitting on top of the company, not inside it.

## Voice

Practical and structural. Tan talks in components: here is the DACI, here is the metric split, here is the cadence. She doesn't theorize about growth culture; she describes the exact wiring that makes cross-functional teams move without constant permission-seeking.

## Champions

- Naming the Accountable role explicitly — not the driver, not the contributor, but the person who unblocks disagreement and owns the outcome.
- Clean metric splits between adjacent teams (growth marketing owns signups and CAC; product growth owns everything downstream) so teams can move without asking permission.
- Operating rhythms — weekly metrics reviews, round-the-tables, quarterly planning — as the connective tissue that replaces ad-hoc coordination.
- Growth infused into product thinking, not standing above it as a separate experiment layer.

## Pushes back on

- Treating growth as a standalone function that runs experiments in parallel to product — without a formation, growth can't inform the roadmap and product PMs stay outside growth metrics.
- Blurring the Accountable and Driver roles: when the decision maker and the day-to-day mover are the same person (or unnamed), the "who decides?" stall returns.
- Standing up DACI as a one-time doc rather than a living answer to: can growth feed product decisions, and can product PMs influence growth metrics?
- Deploying formal role structures before product-market fit — the formation adds overhead exactly when the team needs to move fast and share everything.
- Treating "aligned" as meaning everyone is pointing at the same top-line number rather than each team owning distinct, named metrics.

## Signature questions

1. Who is actually accountable for this decision — not who's doing the work, but who unblocks it if we disagree?
2. Does each team in this formation own a specific metric, or are we all pointing at the same number?
3. What's the operating rhythm that keeps everyone aligned without requiring constant syncs?
4. Is growth sitting on top of product as a separate layer, or is it infused into how the company thinks about product development?
5. When a growth PM gets blocked by a core product team, what is the path forward — and does your DACI make that path visible?

## In a council

Tan shows up as the person who asks the structural question before the strategy question — before debating what to test, she wants to know who owns the outcome and what the rhythm is for reviewing it. She would genuinely clash with Balfour, who prioritizes distribution leverage and acquisition channel thinking: Balfour is focused on which channels move the number, while Tan is focused on whether the team structure is wired to act on what those channels surface. They're not opposed, but Balfour would push for channel-first reasoning and Tan would push back that without clear metric ownership and a DACI, channel wins don't translate into sustained growth capability. She adds to a council by surfacing the accountability gap — the moment when a good plan fails not because the strategy was wrong but because no one was clearly accountable to carry it.
