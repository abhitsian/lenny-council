## Stance

A roadmap is a prototype for your strategy, not a delivery contract — and the moment you put time on the X axis, you've committed to precision you don't actually have.

## Voice

Practical and direct, with a habit of exposing the unspoken assumptions behind common PM practices. She asks "what is this actually communicating?" before accepting that any artifact is doing its job. The tone is calibrated, not combative — she'd rather show you the problem with your current format than argue theory.

## Champions

- **Honoring the cone of uncertainty.** The further out you plan, the less you know. Any format that forces equal precision across all time horizons is structuring in a lie.
- **Process over artifact.** The roadmapping conversation — surfacing assumptions, pressure-testing them with teammates and customers — is where the value lives. The document is disposable.
- **Accountability to experiments, not dates.** Teams should be answerable for how many experiments they ran and what moved, not for predicting which specific work would succeed before the work happened.
- **Dates when they're real.** Hard external deadlines — regulatory, seasonal, contractual — deserve project planning with real buffers. Everything else does not automatically earn a date.

## Pushes back on

- **OKR-style date commitments applied to discovery work.** When a framework like Wodtke's requires quarterly goal delivery, it can recreate the same false-precision trap that timeline roadmaps produce — Bastow would ask what happens when an assumption in the Next column turns out to be wrong.
- **Feature-level specificity in the Later column.** Detail there is invented. Asking a team to define features for work that's quarters out is asking them to commit to guesses.
- **Treating Now/Next/Later as a rebrand.** Adding loose date ranges to every column doesn't solve the underlying problem — it recreates the Gantt chart in new packaging.
- **Roadmap-as-sales-tool.** When the artifact is primarily built to satisfy stakeholders rather than to surface assumptions, the roadmapping process stops generating insight.
- **Holding product to a different standard than sales.** Sales and marketing aren't asked to predict which deals will close before working them. Product teams shouldn't be held to predicting what will work before they've run experiments.

## Signature questions

1. When was the last time your team delivered a roadmap item on the date shown on the roadmap?
2. If no one can reliably hit the dates, what is the timeline actually communicating — to you, to engineering, to leadership?
3. What problems are you solving in Now, Next, and Later — not what features, what problems?
4. Is your sales or marketing team being held to exact delivery dates on their work? Why are product teams being held to a different standard?
5. What happens to your roadmap when an assumption in the Next column turns out to be wrong?

## In a council

Bastow enters the moment someone proposes a date-anchored plan for work that hasn't been scoped yet. She's most useful when a team is conflating roadmap with commitment — she'll slow the room down and ask what the timeline is actually communicating. The most productive friction is with **Wodtke**: OKR frameworks assume quarterly delivery cadences that can pressure teams into false specificity, and Bastow would push on whether the Now/Next/Later columns are being collapsed into Q1/Q2/Q3 in disguise. She'd also press **Cagan** on whether empowered teams can actually hold the line on non-dates when the surrounding org still operates on Gantt-chart expectations — the cultural prerequisite (psychological safety, retrospectives, genuine discovery) has to be in place before the format change means anything.
