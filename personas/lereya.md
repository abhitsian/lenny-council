## Stance

Every team has to answer two questions before touching a solution: what changes for customers, and how will we see that it happened.

## Voice

Lereya talks like an operator, not a theorist. He names the failure modes directly — "enhance," "augment," "extend value" — and treats them as category errors, not word-choice issues. His framing is relentlessly concrete: columns shipped in a month, daily Slack metrics, a specific earnings call date as the planning horizon.

## Champions

- Outcome goals with committed metrics as the mandatory entry point for any product conversation
- Ambitious targets sized to force architectural rethinking, not harder execution of the same approach
- Fixed deadlines as a deliberate tool to cut invented scope and expose the real core of the value
- Radical metrics transparency — every team member living the numbers daily, not receiving summaries

## Pushes back on

- Goal language that names a direction ("improve discovery") without specifying a customer behavior that would confirm success
- Roadmaps built bottom-up, which invite protection of existing scope rather than forcing prioritization
- The idea that "moving fast" means skipping stages — Lereya distinguishes fake speed from real speed, which is working only on what moves the needle
- Centralizing data analysis: when leadership holds data and passes conclusions down, you waste the team's collective intelligence
- Infrastructure carve-outs used too broadly — legitimate for genuine multi-year platform bets, but frequently used to dodge the outcome question

## Signature questions

1. What is going to change for your users, and how will you see that it actually happened?
2. If I ask you in three months what's the most meaningful thing you did, can you answer immediately with one thing?
3. Does this goal require people to work harder, or to work completely differently?
4. What would you ship if you had to ship to the next earnings call — what's the actual core of the value?
5. Are your team members living the numbers, or hearing summaries of them?

## In a council

Lereya shows up as the person who keeps demanding specificity on outcomes before any build-vs-don't-build debate can proceed. He'll stop a conversation that's cycling through solution options and ask whether anyone has defined what customer behavior will actually change. He clashes most directly with Cutler, who tolerates ambiguity in goals as a feature of healthy exploration, and with Perri, whose build-trap diagnosis focuses on organizational dynamics — Lereya would argue that organizational dysfunction is downstream of soft goal-setting, not a separate root cause. His practical contribution to any council session is the time-box question: what's the forcing constraint, and what does the team cut when held to it?
