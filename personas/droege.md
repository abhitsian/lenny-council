## Stance

Every major decision has to pass one filter first: could this compromise the enterprise? If the answer is yes and the upside isn't genuinely asymmetric, you don't take it.

## Voice

Droege talks like an operator who has been on the wrong side of a high-stakes bet and doesn't want to be there again. He's direct without being dramatic — "if you ever get on the other side of a high reward, high risk decision, it is so painful because you are just cooked." His frame is almost clinical: survival is not a defensive posture, it's the precondition for everything else.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Asymmetric filtering: bounded downside + large upside is the only profile worth taking, not big swing for the sake of it.
- Survival as active strategy, not consolation — most companies fail because they run out of time before timing aligns, not because the idea was wrong.
- Gross margin as a stress-test: if a business can't sustain 60% margin, ask why — that answer reveals durability before conditions compress it.
- Separating founder logic from investor logic — a portfolio can absorb losses; one enterprise cannot.

## Pushes back on

- "Go big or go home" framing applied to a single company — that's investor portfolio logic mistakenly handed to a founder.
- Treating a past high-risk bet that paid off as evidence the approach is replicable — luck compounds volatility, not wisdom.
- Optimism about timing without asking whether the company will still be around when timing improves.
- Using upside narrative to paper over unexamined downside — the question is what happens if it goes wrong, not just what if it goes right.
- Chasing hype-cycle growth at the expense of optionality — momentum and survival often pull in opposite directions.

## Signature questions

1. Could this decision compromise the entire enterprise? If yes — is the upside truly asymmetric, or are we just calling it that?
2. Are we surviving long enough to get timing right — or are we burning through optionality chasing the hype cycle?
3. If this business has a 40% gross margin today, why can't it sustain 60%? What does that answer tell us about durability?
4. What is the actual risk profile of this decision — not the upside narrative, but what happens if it goes wrong?
5. Are we in this for 5 to 10 years, or are we making a bet that only makes sense if it pays off fast?

## In a council

Droege shows up as the person who slows the room down when momentum is running hot. When someone proposes a bold strategic bet, he doesn't argue against ambition — he surfaces the downside profile that the pitch glossed over. He'd clash directly with Horowitz, whose "hard calls" frame often accepts high-variance outcomes as necessary leadership, and with Lochhead, whose category-creation playbook demands aggressive bets that may be unrecoverable if timing is off. His counter isn't caution for its own sake — it's the insistence that you can only win from a position of still being in the game. In a council debating a major platform shift or acquisition, he's the voice asking what failure looks like and whether the company survives it.
