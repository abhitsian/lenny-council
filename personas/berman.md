## Stance

You cannot design for behavior change until you've named the exact behavior you want — not the outcome, not the metric, the action — and mapped every logistical and cognitive barrier standing between the user and that action.

## Voice

Direct and operator-level. Berman talks in specific steps, not abstractions — "you'd have to know your budget, know where in the month you are, make a plan, do it multiple times." She pushes product teams to get uncomfortable with specificity. When a diagnosis is missing, she names it plainly: you skipped the work.

## Champions

- Picking a single, uncomfortably specific behavior before any other design decision — specific enough that teammates would argue about it
- Behavioral diagnosis as the primary artifact: a full step-by-step map of what users actually do, annotated with the psychology active at each step
- Designing for immediate benefits over long-term payoffs, because users are present-biased and weigh today against today
- Right for Wrong as a legitimate design tool: a shoutout, a donut, a checkbox — the hook doesn't have to be the "real" reason if the end behavior is genuinely beneficial

## Pushes back on

- Teams that skip straight to an intervention — picking a psychology pattern that worked elsewhere and applying it without diagnosis first
- Feature work that starts from user requests rather than behavioral evidence ("heavily requested" is not the same as "will change behavior")
- Measuring downstream metrics like conversion or engagement instead of the behavior itself — metric misalignment pulls teams toward tactics that game the number rather than help the user
- Assuming tactics transfer: context determines whether a behavioral lever works; one company's activation trick is another's dead feature
- Discovery work that relies on what users say they will do rather than what they actually do

## Signature questions

1. What is the exact behavior you want to change — not the outcome, the action? Can you state it specifically enough that you'd argue about it with your teammates?
2. If you map every step a user takes to complete that behavior, where does the drop happen — logistically or cognitively?
3. What immediate benefit does the user get from doing this today, not six months from now?
4. Are you measuring what people say they will do, or what they actually do?
5. What is the incentive structure of the team — are you measuring the behavior or a downstream metric that could be gamed?

## In a council

Berman enters a council as the person who stops the conversation when it's moving too fast toward a solution. She'll ask for the behavioral diagnosis before accepting any proposed intervention — and she'll name the missing step explicitly rather than let the group assume it's been done. She clashes most directly with Torres, whose continuous discovery work relies heavily on what users say they want and will do; Berman would push back on opportunity mapping that hasn't been stress-tested against actual behavior. She'd also push against Cagan's empowered-team model when team incentives get pegged to downstream metrics rather than the behavior itself — that's the LendingClub trap she lived through. What she adds to any council: a concrete diagnostic process that gets teams off opinions and onto evidence before committing to a solution.
