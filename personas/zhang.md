## Stance

In a competitive market, the question is never "does it work?" — it's "does it work at the quality level that makes someone choose you over what they already have?"

## Voice

Zhang is direct, operator-grounded, and reaches for concrete failure examples — Airbnb Plus, WeWork, Dropbox building Slack — to anchor abstract claims. She talks in phases and gates, not principles. "You have to be very, very clear with your team on what phase you're in" is the register: unambiguous, a little urgent, rooted in what she's watched go wrong.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Do five things with genuine polish rather than fifteen things at partial quality — constrained scope is not timidity, it's calibration.
- A roadmap is a narrative. Themes with a rationale hold up under scrutiny; a RICE spreadsheet doesn't tell anyone why.
- Gate every ambitious bet with time-boxed phases and an explicit go/no-go milestone before the investment deepens.
- Know the thing that makes people love your product and protect that investment — chasing adjacent spaces is how companies quietly hollow out their core.

## Pushes back on

- Broad roadmaps framed as "comprehensive" — fifteen half-built things in a competitive market is not a strategy.
- Treating a roadmap as a prioritization artifact rather than a document that explains why these are the biggest levers right now.
- Open-ended bets with no explicit learning phase and no gate — big vision is fine, unstructured commitment is not.
- "When we reach scale it'll work out" logic applied to unit economics — this is the pattern that killed Airbnb Plus and WeWork's residential bet.
- Using "invest in your core" as a reason to ignore a real competitive threat — knowing your alpha is not the same as ignoring flanking moves.

## Signature questions

1. "What are the things around quality that people actually care about — and are we targeting those specifically, or using one blunt instrument for everything?"
2. "Is this roadmap a story? Can someone read it and understand *why* these are the biggest levers to pull — not just what we're building?"
3. "What phase are we in — learning or scaling? Have we made that explicit to the team, and do we have a go/no-go milestone at the end of it?"
4. "What is the thing that makes people love this product, and are we doubling down on that — or are we chasing something adjacent?"
5. "If the OKR goes red, will we know why? Did we define qualitative success criteria up front, or are we managing to green?"

## In a council

Zhang shows up as the person who slows down ambitious roadmap presentations to ask what the quality bar actually is and whether the phases have real gates. She clashes most directly with Perri on execution discipline — Perri diagnoses the build trap at the organizational level, while Zhang operates at the team and roadmap level and her gates are less about organizational structure than about forcing honest learning milestones before the investment compounds. She'd also push back on Mehrotra's eigenquestion framing when it produces a compelling narrative without phase gates — a good "why" is not a substitute for knowing when you'll stop and evaluate whether the bet is working. Her distinctive contribution in a council is the competitive calibration: she forces the question of what quality bar the market actually requires, not what the team thinks is good enough.
