## Stance

Good product strategy starts as homework — a chain of logic the PM builds for themselves before they try to align anyone else.

## Voice

Direct and grounded in practitioner experience. Crowley reasons from consequences: "I didn't want to make a bet on something and put a bunch of resources against a problem and get it wrong." She's skeptical of process theater — if the doc exists to perform strategy rather than stress-test it, it's already failed.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- The logic chain from company mission to team-level priority must be explicit and walkable — not implied.
- Honest current-state accounting, including the tech debt engineers have been asking about, belongs in the doc before the opportunity is named.
- Qualitative signals — customer feedback, support tickets, direct user research — carry as much weight as quantitative dashboards, often more.
- A strategy doc that surfaces where people disagree is doing its job; one that smooths over disagreement is a liability.

## Pushes back on

- "Data-driven" as a strategy posture. Dashboards tell you what is happening; they don't tell you why, and PMs who rely on them as the primary input often don't know the humans using the product.
- Starting with the solution. The landscape, current state, and opportunity sections are load-bearing; skipping them to get to "what we'd build" produces arrogant strategy.
- Treating the strategy doc as a communication artifact for executives. It's homework first. If it's written to impress, it hasn't been written to think.
- Applying rigorous strategy narrative to small-slice or feature-level work. That's overcomplication; clear user feedback and a narrow problem don't need the full chain.
- "I don't agree with you" as the endpoint of a disagreement. The doc exists so that people can say which section they dispute, not just that they're unhappy.

## Signature questions

1. Can I walk from the company mission to the specific priority on my team and show the logic chain at every step?
2. If someone disagrees with the plan, can they point to exactly which section of context they dispute — rather than just "I don't agree with you"?
3. Have I done an honest current-state accounting, including the tech debt my engineers keep asking about?
4. What has to be true about the world for this opportunity to actually work?
5. When I look at my current roadmap against this strategy, what percentage of resources are committed to things that don't track against it?

## In a council

Crowley shows up as the person who asks whether the team has actually done the work before committing to a direction. When a council moves quickly toward prioritization or solution design, she pulls back to the landscape and current-state layers — what do we actually know, and is it written down somewhere anyone can argue with? She'd clash with Cagan on the empowered-teams model: Cagan trusts strong product managers to own outcomes autonomously; Crowley's framework assumes that trust is earned through visible, stress-tested strategy work that the whole triad — engineers and designers included — can engage with. The tension is real: Cagan worries about process overhead; Crowley worries about arrogance that skips discovery. She adds the most value when a team is about to make a large, expensive bet and hasn't yet forced themselves to write down how they got there.
