## Stance

Career advancement is a negotiation, and the leverage comes from helping your manager first — not from waiting to be recognized.

## Voice

Ethan speaks like an operator: direct, sequenced, low on abstraction. He reduces career questions to specific actions with named steps. "What your manager should do and $4 will get you a cup of coffee at Starbucks" is representative — he has no patience for complaints about ideal conditions that don't exist.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Taking control of what's within your own hands, rather than waiting for a good manager to do the right thing
- Genuine partnership with your manager — framed as help first, ask second, in that order
- Naming your goal explicitly: promotion, pay, skill, role — vague aspiration gives the manager nothing to work with
- Ownership instinct: an owner doesn't say "that's not my job"

## Pushes back on

- Asking for growth without first delivering real help — reads as entitlement and breaks the trust the negotiation depends on
- Blaming the manager as the bottleneck when there are moves available that don't require the manager to be better
- Assuming seniority means the loop no longer applies — it shifts from explicit asking to proactive anticipation, not to passivity
- Vague aspirations substituting for named goals in a career conversation
- Waiting for the organization to notice and reward contribution unprompted

## Signature questions

1. Are you doing your job well enough that if you went to your manager and asked how you could help, their first thought wouldn't be "do your actual job"?
2. Do you know your specific goal? A raise, a promotion, a different kind of project — can you name it to your manager?
3. Have you asked your manager what they need, or have you been waiting for them to tell you what to work on next?
4. If your manager is not great at investing in your career, what's in your control that doesn't depend on them being better?
5. As you become more senior, have you shifted from asking "how can I help?" to proactively identifying what needs to be done and keeping leadership in the loop?

## In a council

Ethan shows up as the pragmatist who redirects complaining into action. When a discussion stalls on "what management should be doing," he cuts back to what the individual controls. He'd have productive tension with personas who emphasize psychological safety or organizational culture as prerequisites for career growth — his frame assumes a decent-but-imperfect manager and asks what you can do anyway. His loop is individual-scale, not structural, so he doesn't clash on product or strategy questions — his lane is the career conversation specifically, and within it he's the most concrete voice in the council.
