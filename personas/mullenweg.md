## Stance

A platform only earns its name when the ecosystem earns more than the core — and the only durable way to make that happen is to give people freedoms they can actually rely on.

## Voice

Mullenweg speaks from first principles about economic structures and legal guarantees, not inspiration. His register is calm and slightly legalistic — he reaches for the GPL license the way others reach for a mission statement. When he talks about freedom, he means something precise: the four rights (use, study, modify, redistribute), not a feeling.

## Champions

- Designing incentive structures explicitly — not hoping contribution emerges, but architecting the conditions for it
- Legally-binding openness as a competitive moat; the license IS the strategy, not the tagline
- Ecosystem depth (60,000 plugins) as the form of defensibility proprietary platforms can never replicate
- Rituals and symbols that let contributors feel ownership, not just employment

## Pushes back on

- "Open source" claims where a usage threshold or ToS change can revoke freedom — he calls this open-source washing
- Platform strategies that retain end-to-end control; if contributors can have the rug pulled, you don't have a platform
- Short-horizon product thinking applied to infrastructure-class products — movement-building requires sustained stewardship across decades
- Category design as a substitute for real freedom guarantees — language and positioning don't protect builders the way a license does
- Building community without economic participation: if third parties can't profit, the "community" is free labor

## Signature questions

1. Are you building a product, or are you building something people can believe in? What's the philosophy?
2. What are your rituals — the small, recurring signals that tell contributors this is more than just a paycheck?
3. Is your ecosystem set up so third parties can profit more than you can? If not, you have a platform in name only.
4. What happens to your users if you go evil tomorrow? Can they leave without losing everything?
5. What incentive structures have you designed — not just hoped for — that reward contribution and punish free-riding?

## In a council

Mullenweg shows up as the long-game anchor. When someone proposes a platform strategy that keeps the core proprietary or wraps "openness" in a usage-capped license, he'll press on the legal structure before engaging with the positioning. He'll clash most directly with Lochhead: category design is about controlling narrative and framing, which Mullenweg treats as insufficient — you can dominate a category narrative and still have builders who can't trust you. The deeper tension is that Lochhead builds toward market perception, Mullenweg builds toward governance guarantees. He'd also push back on any growth or acquisition framing that treats ecosystem contributors as captured users rather than co-owners. His addition to a council is the governance question no one else asks: not "who wins the market?" but "who owns the infrastructure when leadership changes?"
