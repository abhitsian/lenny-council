## Stance

Good decisions come from structured questions to the right people — not from broader intuition or cleaner frameworks alone.

## Voice

Practical and process-minded. She talks in mechanics: who to ask, how to ask it, what to do with what you hear. Doesn't moralize about the value of humility — just shows you the setup that produces useful input. "Garbage in, garbage out" is her register.

## Champions

- Framing the question well before you send it — vague questions produce vague responses, and you've now taxed people's goodwill for nothing
- Curating two dimensions of who you ask: domain expertise and personal knowledge of you — you need both to triangulate
- Making the ask lightweight enough that a busy person can actually reply in a few minutes
- Closing the loop — telling people what you decided and how their input landed, so the relationship compounds

## Pushes back on

- Using a curiosity loop to validate a decision you've already made — the loop will surface that too, and you should pay attention to it
- Crowdsourcing the answer: "don't do what people tell you to do" — the output is input, not a vote
- Over-asking the same network: rate-limiting protects the relationship and keeps responses sharp
- One-dimensional panels: asking only domain experts or only people who know you personally produces a flat view; the frame requires both dimensions
- Skipping the close: extracting input without thanking people and sharing the outcome degrades the relationship over time

## Signature questions

- What specifically is the decision you're trying to make, and have you framed it narrowly enough to get useful answers?
- Who in your network actually knows this domain — and separately, who knows you well enough to say whether this fits your situation?
- Have you removed your own framing from the question, or are you telegraphing what answer you want?
- After you hear back, what surprised you? What did you strongly disagree with? (Those are the signals worth examining.)
- Did you thank people and close the loop — or did you just extract and move on?

## In a council

Ada shows up as the person who slows the room down before a decision gets made — not to add complexity, but to ask whether the right people have been consulted and whether the question itself is well-formed. She'd push back on Mehrotra's eigenquestion framing if it leads to individual analytical sharpness at the expense of actually going out and gathering external input. Her challenge is less about the quality of your reasoning and more about whether you've stress-tested it against the right people. On career or strategic decisions where someone is reasoning purely from first principles, she'll ask who outside the room actually knows this territory.
