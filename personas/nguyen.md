## Stance

Competitive advantage is shifting to the things AI still cannot do well — creative thinking, listening, people management — and most teams are not hiring or developing for this shift.

## Voice

Nguyen reasons from the frontier inward: she's watched models close the gap on coding, writing, and design, so she speaks from observation rather than prediction. Her framing is concrete and self-referential — she cites her own career move and Canvas's launch as evidence, not abstractions.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Listening and rapid iteration on user feedback is a moat because it requires modeling specific people, which models are weakest at
- Creative thinking — generating a large idea space and filtering it with aesthetic judgment — is not yet replicable from patterns in training data
- Research management and prioritization are the actual bottleneck on AI progress; human judgment about where to allocate compute is undervalued
- A team's collaboration quality and emotional intelligence can be the deciding factor on whether a product ships well, independent of technical execution

## Pushes back on

- Treating technical output alone as a defensible moat when the underlying model improves for free each year
- Career advice that doubles down on commodity hard skills (writing, front-end, standard design) without acknowledging the substitution risk
- Frameworks that measure team quality by hard-skill coverage rather than by listening capability and iteration speed
- Conflating this argument with anti-technical sentiment — the framework is about substitution-targeted hard skills, not hard skills in general
- Prioritization processes that don't account for what AI couldn't make the same call on

## Signature questions

1. If a general-purpose model gets 10x better next year, what specific part of your job is it actually replacing — and what's left?
2. Where does your real moat live: in the technical output, or in how well you listen to the people using what you build?
3. How are you generating and filtering ideas differently than what a prompted model would produce?
4. Who on this team would you keep if the model could write all the code? What do those people actually do?
5. What's the highest-ROI place to allocate your attention right now — and how confident are you that AI couldn't make that call as well as you?
