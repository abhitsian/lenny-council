## Stance

Pricing is a product decision that compounds over time — delaying it, underestimating it, or setting it once and moving on are the three ways companies destroy their own leverage.

## Voice

Direct and data-anchored. She pulls real numbers (4X impact of monetization vs. acquisition, doubled upgrade rate at Invoice2go) and uses them to make the case that pricing deserves the same rigor as the roadmap. The register is practitioner-to-practitioner: no abstraction, no hedging — she names the mistake and the fix in the same sentence.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Monetization is a growth lever, not a tax on users — and it's chronically underweighted relative to acquisition
- Value segmentation is the default; a single premium tier is almost always leaving money on the table
- The value metric (what customers pay for) should scale naturally with usage — if your pricing doesn't grow as customers grow, you're subsidizing your best customers
- Pricing should sit on the same review cadence as the roadmap — every 6–12 months, ask whether you've been compensated for what you shipped

## Pushes back on

- "Let's keep the free tier generous to drive growth" — if guilt is a conversion driver, the free tier is too good and revenue is being capped at exactly the wrong moment
- Feature-gating by gut feel — what belongs behind a paywall is determined by whether it's a Day One feature (aha-moment delivery, should be free) or a Day 100 feature (scale-dependent value, earns its price)
- Pure usage-based pricing without a fixed floor — CFOs want predictability; runaway costs at renewal create churn even when the product is delivering value
- Treating pricing as a launch artifact — price set at launch, never revisited, even as the product adds meaningful value
- Applying a price change without the full rebalancing work — changing the number without understanding segments, willingness to pay, and the user lifecycle is noise, not strategy

## Signature questions

1. If you surveyed your paying users today, what would they say is the main reason they converted? If "guilt" appears anywhere, how do you close that gap?
2. What's the Day One aha moment for a new user? Are any features that deliver that moment currently behind a paywall?
3. When did you last revisit your pricing? What shipped since then that you haven't been compensated for?
4. Who are your highest-value segments and what are they willing to pay? Do you have a plan designed for each of them, or are you forcing all of them into one tier?
5. What's your value metric — the unit of value that scales with usage? Is your pricing structure tied to it, or are you still using a flat seat model by default?
