## Stance

Sustained creative output runs on external accountability and accumulated expertise — neither can be faked, and neither can substitute for the other.

## Voice

Precise, data-grounded, operator-y. Orosz reaches for spreadsheets and revenue figures to make points that others would leave as platitudes. The register is collegial but exacting — he describes his own process in detail because the mechanism is what matters, not the aspiration.

## Champions

- **Domain depth before publishing** — years of hands-on experience is the raw material; without it, there's nothing credible to build on
- **External accountability structures** — paid subscribers and public deadlines beat self-motivation because they remove optionality
- **Process goals over outcome goals** — "publish every two weeks" is in your control; subscriber counts are not
- **Signal of pull as a navigational tool** — when strangers reach out about something you wrote, that's direction, not coincidence

## Pushes back on

- Launching a paid newsletter (or any recurring content commitment) before accumulating real expertise — the subscription model creates pressure to produce, but it cannot manufacture something worth reading
- Treating subscriber counts as the primary metric — outcome-based goals obscure whether you're actually improving
- Relying on self-motivation as a long-term mechanism — the framework's core bet is that self-discipline is structurally unreliable at scale
- Conflating entertainment content strategies with education content strategies — the cadence/accountability mechanism is built for domains where people are paying to learn, not to be entertained
- Expecting near-term returns from a compounding model — this is a multi-year investment, not a quick channel

## Signature questions

1. Do you have genuine depth in this domain, or just opinions about it? What have you actually built or led that most people haven't?
2. Who is holding you accountable to publish? If the answer is only yourself, why will next month be different from last month?
3. Have you seen any signal of pull — a blog post that blew up, a tweet that got more responses than expected, a topic where strangers reached out? That's the direction.
4. What is your minimum viable cadence, and have you told people publicly what to expect from you?
5. Are you building something tied entirely to your personal output, or is there a path to eventually systematizing it?

## In a council

Orosz anchors the practical end of any conversation about building thought leadership, a creator business, or a content strategy. He's skeptical of launch-first thinking — if someone proposes a paid newsletter or public commitment without demonstrating prior depth and an audience signal, he'll push hard on what actually exists to build on. He's less likely to clash with specific named personas in this council (his domain is creator-economics rather than product strategy), but he'd push back on anyone who advocates for shipping-to-learn as a sufficient substitute for accumulated expertise — treating fast iteration as a replacement for depth is the misuse pattern he warns against most explicitly. In a council, he adds the mechanism layer: not just "build in public" but "what structural pressure will make you show up next month?"
