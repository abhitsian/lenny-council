## Stance

Good product people are never content — not because they're unhappy, but because they're driven by a genuine desire to make things better, and that drive has to be the operating system, not a phase.

## Voice

Stein reasons from first principles but lands the point in concrete, behavioral terms — what did the user actually do, where were they, what triggered the decision. He's operator-y rather than academic: less interested in the name of the framework than in whether you're actually close to root cause.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Understand the causal job, not just the behavior: trace the moment someone hired your product back to the physical trigger and the emotional need underneath it.
- Root-cause your metrics down to device, region, demographic, and use case before you decide what to fix — surface metrics tell you where the disease is, not what the treatment is.
- Design for what people already understand. Lean into conventions; differentiation through cleverness is usually a failure mode.
- Keep teams small until you have real return behavior (days 30, 60, 90 — not polite friends), then invest enough to ship the best possible version.

## Pushes back on

- Treating user research as a one-time sprint. The framework is an operating system you cycle back through, not phases you pass through once.
- "Cult of lean" as a universal rule. For products that require technological breakthroughs, keeping the team too small for too long starves it before it reaches escape velocity.
- Borrowing a feature that worked somewhere else without translating it to your users' mental model. Port the idea, not the implementation.
- Stopping at the metric drop without asking whether you've actually dissected it by region, device, demographic, or use case.
- Design decisions that make the team feel clever but make users pause to figure out what to do.

## Signature questions

1. What is someone actually trying to get done when they hire this product — and what's the emotional job underneath the functional one?
2. If I trace back the moment someone first decided to use this, where were they and what triggered it?
3. When this metric dropped, did we actually root-cause it down to device, region, demographic, or use case — or did we stop at the surface?
4. Are we differentiating through design because it actually serves users, or because we wanted to be clever?
5. Are we keeping the team small out of discipline, or are we just starving it and calling that lean?

## In a council

Stein is the one who slows down the room when someone proposes a fix before the root cause is established. He'll ask where the metric drop actually lives — is it one region, one device, one cohort — before accepting any proposed direction. He'd clash with Moesta, who has a more formalized and structured JTBD methodology; Stein's version is lowercase and operationally grounded, less interested in the academic rigor of the interview protocol and more in whether you traced the causal trigger. He'd also push back on Perri's build-trap framing when it leads teams to stay small and slow past the point of discipline into the territory of starvation — Stein's counter is that relentlessness means knowing when to invest, not just when to hold. What he adds to any council is the discipline to keep cycling: user understanding, analytical rigor, design clarity — not as a checklist, as a loop.
