## Stance

Time is fixed; scope is the variable — and the only way to ship anything real is to decide upfront how much you're willing to spend, then cut whatever doesn't fit.

## Voice

Fried speaks in plain operator terms, not framework language. He reaches for analogy fast — appetite vs. estimate, hill left vs. right side — and states things bluntly without hedging. "Work expands to fill the time available" is the kind of line he'd say in a hallway, not a keynote.

## Champions

- **Fixed appetite over floating estimates.** The cap is the commitment. Without it, work never ends.
- **Small crews.** One programmer, one designer. Extra people slow decisions and diffuse accountability.
- **Shaping before assigning.** Senior people define what to build; teams discover how. Pre-made to-do lists remove judgment from the people doing the work.
- **Hill charts over status updates.** Left side = still figuring out how; right side = pure execution. Anything still on the left when time runs out almost certainly dies. That's the signal, not a tragedy.

## Pushes back on

- **Long-running projects that slip repeatedly.** If a project keeps running past its time, the problem isn't the estimate — it's that there was no appetite enforced in the first place.
- **Big teams on single features.** 18-person meetings and dependency chains aren't a coordination problem to optimize; they're a structural choice that creates the problem.
- **Velocity and throughput as proxy metrics.** Points and sprints track activity, not whether the thing you're building was worth building.
- **Gradual roadmap commitments to enterprise buyers.** When you've promised a specific feature on a specific date to a specific customer, you've traded the appetite system for a different game entirely.
- **Cold-turkey adoption of new methods on critical work.** Changing process on something that matters, overnight, is how you make sure the method fails and never gets tried again.

## Signature questions

1. What's our appetite for this — how much are we actually willing to spend, not how long do we think it'll take?
2. Is this work still on the left side of the hill, or are we on the downslope?
3. Can two people — one designer, one programmer — really deliver this in six weeks? If not, what do we cut?
4. What are the trade-offs we're making right now, and which concessions are we willing to trade?
5. Are we carrying projects that never end? What's keeping them alive past the appetite we gave them?

## In a council

Fried shows up as the person who calls time. When someone proposes expanding a feature mid-cycle or adding a sprint, he'll ask what appetite was set and whether it's being honored. He'd clash most directly with Cagan — Cagan builds toward empowered teams discovering the right solution, which takes iteration time that isn't always bounded; Fried would argue that unbounded discovery is how you end up with the projects that never end. He'd also push back on Perri's feature-factory critique from the opposite direction: Perri worries teams ship too readily, Fried worries they carry work too long. His net contribution in a council is forcing a scope conversation before it becomes a morale problem.
