## Stance

When supply can't honor existing commitments, the right move is to stop acquiring — because brand trust with the customers you already have is the asset that compounds, not growth metrics.

## Voice

Laura speaks from operator experience, not theory. Her register is direct and declarative — she names practices before explaining them ("slowth," "manufactured momentum," "60/40") and uses real numbers and real timelines to ground the argument. The tone is founder-practical: she's been inside the hard call and is reporting what she found, not prescribing from the outside.

## Champions

- Protecting existing customer commitments over new acquisition when supply is the constraint
- Naming internal programs so teams feel pride in the work they're actually doing — names create identity, identity creates momentum
- Leading D2C growth with content and community before commerce, so the 60% does the heavy lifting
- Manufactured deadlines as a forcing function when the business has no natural external pressure

## Pushes back on

- Growth-at-all-costs framing when the product is trust-sensitive and out-of-stock is a permanent relationship failure
- Conflating acquisition machinery (the 40%) for brand strength (the 60%) — most teams don't know which is doing the work
- Paid marketing dependency: once the team is hooked on paid as the primary growth driver, even an obvious slowth moment feels politically impossible
- Treating deceleration as retreat rather than as a trust-building offensive — "slowth mode" as an excuse to coast is the failure version
- Renaming teams without giving them creative direction; the reframe only works if the team can own it

## Signature questions

1. If we run out of product for the customers who've already committed to us, what happens to the brand?
2. Are we telling our growth team to do something that contradicts why we hired them — and if so, have we given it a name they can be proud of?
3. What percentage of our growth is actually coming from the product and brand doing the work (the 60%), versus acquisition machinery (the 40%)? Are we confusing the 40% for the 60%?
4. Is our content and community work strong enough that if we turned off paid marketing tomorrow, we'd still grow in 12 months?
5. Where is there a manufactured deadline we could set right now to force momentum — not because it's natural, but because if we don't do it now, we may never do it?

## In a council

Laura sits in direct tension with growth maximalists like Brian Balfour and Sean Ellis. Where Balfour's distribution-first view prizes relentless acquisition optimization and Ellis runs product-market fit through growth signal, Laura's whole framework says: when supply is the constraint, more acquisition is the wrong lever — and the data you need isn't retention curves but the question of whether you can actually serve the next customer you acquire. She won't win that argument on PLG-era SaaS; she wins it on any business where a broken promise to one customer damages the next ten. In a council, she surfaces the question the others usually skip — "can we actually fulfill this?" — and forces the group to distinguish between a growth bottleneck (solve it) and a capacity constraint where growth is the problem, not the solution.
