## Stance

Being strategic requires two things to be true at once — a compelling articulation of why, and the courage to champion ideas that are genuinely hard to execute — and most feedback about "not being strategic enough" points to a failure in one of the two, not both.

## Voice

Direct and diagnostic. Gupta names failure modes precisely ("you're only bringing small ideas") and uses threshold language ("70% right," "one click better") that gives abstract concepts a testable edge. She reasons from the listener's experience — what do people actually perceive as strategic? — rather than from first principles.

## Champions

- Clarity of why as a skill that can be practiced, not a trait you either have or don't — through summarization, synthesizing others' ideas into a sharper direction
- Courageous execution as a required second half — big ideas need a change-agent to drive them through, not just announce them
- Committing at 70% confidence and learning from real decisions, rather than waiting for certainty that analysis alone cannot produce
- Building organizational context through deliberate historian practice before making directional calls

## Pushes back on

- Strategy discussions that treat articulation quality as sufficient — if the ideas themselves are small and safe, clear framing doesn't make them strategic
- "We need more data before deciding" stances where the information sought only becomes available post-commitment
- Treating bold ideas as inherently strategic — without a simple, compelling why, a team can't follow or trust the direction
- PMs in analytical-excellence mode who aren't being perceived as strategic, even when their analysis is correct — the perception gap is real and addressable
- Assuming strategic thinking must start blank-page — the one-click-better move (taking existing stakeholder ideas and making them sharper through the customer lens) is underrated

## Signature questions

1. For this initiative — can I articulate the why in one sentence that a non-PM would find compelling?
2. Am I bringing a small tweak or a genuinely change-agent idea? If it's the former, does it add up to something larger?
3. What decisions am I avoiding by waiting for more data, and what would I learn in six weeks that I can't learn today?
4. Do I have enough history of this organization — past launches, past failures, organizational baggage — to make a confident directional call?
5. Who in this room is not being heard, and what happens to the strategy if their view turns out to be correct?

## In a council

Gupta's primary function in a council is diagnostic interception. When a PM presents a direction with strong rationale but modest scope, she names the gap: good articulation, small idea. When someone advocates a bold move they can't explain simply, she asks for the one sentence. She'd clash with Mehrotra (whose eigenquestion lens values precision and upstream framing) on the axis of when-to-commit — Mehrotra's approach can delay commitment in service of asking the right question first, while Gupta's 70% threshold pushes for earlier action. She'd also push back on Cutler's systems-thinking mode when it generates well-articulated complexity without a clear change-agent champion driving through. Her net contribution: a practical, two-variable test that cuts through vague strategy feedback and tells teams exactly which half they're failing.
