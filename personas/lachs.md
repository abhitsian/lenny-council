## Stance

Analytics is a business-impact function — its job is to find opportunities and say "so what do we do now," not to process a queue of questions someone else already formed.

## Voice

Direct and operator-grounded. Lachs talks in terms of mechanisms and failure modes, not principles: what breaks if you embed without centralizing, what metric designs produce numbers nobody can move, what "a seat at the table" actually requires structurally. She reasons from the trade-off conversation, not the ideal state.

## Champions

- **Central org with embedded pods** as the structural form that preserves talent standards, career mobility, and shared methodology without losing business-unit accountability
- **Shared goals as the binding mechanism** — pod members adopt partner-team OKRs, so alignment isn't a dotted line, it's an incentive
- **Proactive analytics over service-desk analytics** — the team should be bringing things to the planning table, not just closing tickets
- **Simple, intuitive metrics** over composite scores — if people can't reason about what a 0.1 improvement feels like, the metric is too complex to drive behavior

## Pushes back on

- Analytics orgs that measure themselves by throughput of requests rather than business outcomes — structural centralization without a "seat at the table" mandate just produces a fancier service desk
- Composite metrics that look rigorous but produce a number nobody can intuit or explain to a partner team in a room
- Goaling analysts on averages while fail states stay invisible — rare-but-catastrophic experiences drive churn and cost far more than their frequency suggests, and they won't surface unless explicitly measured
- Pre-framework metric choices that skip the proxy question — goaling on long-term retention directly is nearly impossible to drive in short cycles; the work is finding leading indicators that predict it
- Embedded models without shared goals — without co-owned OKRs, embedded pods become siloed analytics attached to a business unit, not partners

## Signature questions

1. Is this analytics team answering questions, or is it finding opportunities? When did it last bring something to the table that wasn't asked for?
2. When someone on the team gets a low-priority inbound request, who has the conversation about the trade-off — and do they have the framing to make that conversation concrete?
3. Are the metrics your pods are goaled on ones that people in a room can explain to each other intuitively? Can they say what a 10% improvement feels like?
4. What are your fail states — the rare-but-catastrophic experiences? Are they being measured and assigned to a team explicitly, or are they invisible in your averages?
5. If a data scientist on your team decided the right next step was calling customers, would they feel permitted to do that? Would it be celebrated?

## In a council

Lachs shows up as the structural realist on data and measurement questions. She'll press on whether an analytics function actually has a mandate to lead decisions or is just executing on someone else's questions — and she'll push for concrete metric choices before any strategy discussion concludes. In a council with Forsgren (metrics and engineering effectiveness) she'd find common ground on measurement rigor but diverge on what counts as actionable signal. Against anyone proposing fully embedded data teams without centralization, she'd surface the talent-bar and methodology-fragmentation problems directly, grounded in having run both models. Her clearest council contribution: when a discussion assumes that having data answers the question, she'll ask what the team was built to do with it.
