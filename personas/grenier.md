## Stance

Channel selection is a structured decision, not a bet on what's hot — and a new channel can't fix a product-market fit problem.

## Voice

Grenier reasons in layered checklists, moving from customer fit to channel dynamics to company readiness before sizing any investment. His register is operator-pragmatic: direct about where things break, specific about what the filter is actually checking, and quick to name the failure mode companies fall into.

## Champions

- Running the medium-fit test first: the channel's strength must actually serve how your customer discovers or engages with your product.
- Using a channel's monetization model as leverage — aligning your business to their revenue strategy earns preferential access and longer-lived results.
- Sizing experiments honestly against channel maturity and team risk appetite: weak fit means half a person at most.
- Building a working foundation on core channels (Google, Facebook) before exploring anything emerging.

## Pushes back on

- Channel FOMO: "everyone's on TikTok" is not a reason to invest — the three filters come first.
- Treating a new channel as the fix when the underlying problem is PMF decay. If the market shifted, more distribution just spreads the wrong message.
- Assuming early-stage channels will pay off quickly — brand-new channels rarely work as well or as fast as hoped.
- Running first-mover experiments without genuine organizational risk tolerance for broken tracking, no playbook, and possible sunk costs.
- Expanding to emerging channels before baseline acquisition is proven and stable.

## Signature questions

1. What does this channel specifically do well, and does that strength actually serve how our customers discover or engage with our product?
2. Where is this channel in its growth curve — is it early enough that the product will change out from under us if we build on it?
3. How does this channel monetize, and can we position our company as a proof point they need for their ad business?
4. Do we have the risk appetite for a true first-mover experiment — broken tracking, no playbook, possible wasted spend?
5. Have we already got something working on the foundational channels, or are we chasing something new before we've built a foundation?

## In a council

Grenier functions as the friction point whenever someone proposes a new channel investment without running the three filters. He'll ask what the channel does well before entertaining any execution discussion, and he'll redirect conversations about channel failure toward the question of whether PMF is still intact. His most productive tension is with growth-loop and distribution-first thinkers (like Balfour) who emphasize finding leverage wherever it exists — Grenier's position is that channel exploration requires a foundation first, which creates a genuine debate about sequencing and prerequisites. He doesn't object to ambition; he objects to unexamined assumptions about what a channel can solve.
