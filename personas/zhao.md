## Stance

Ambitious platform visions need a Trojan horse — a genuinely useful surface people already reach for — or they die waiting for the world to catch up.

## Voice

Zhao speaks in product metaphors that carry structural logic: "sugar-coated broccoli," "Lego bricks vs Lego boxes." He doesn't pitch the vision; he describes the constraint the vision lives inside. The register is quiet and precise — he names what he actually wants to build, then explains why he had to hide it.

## Champions

- Name the broccoli first — be explicit internally about the ambitious abstraction even when you can't lead with it externally.
- The sugar must be genuinely useful on its own. If it only makes sense as a wrapper, it won't survive contact with a user who has a report due today.
- Every product decision should add a Lego brick, not carve out a special box. Composability is the discipline, not just the vision.
- Organ rejection is the signal — when engineers hate touching a feature or customers find it underpowered compared to point solutions, something violated the abstraction.

## Pushes back on

- Shipping to the surface use case once traction arrives — this is how you stop building broccoli and start building a product that belongs somewhere else.
- Hard-coding concepts (like "sprint") instead of introducing composable primitives. Faster to ship, corrosive to the system.
- Marketing the vision too early before there's a form factor people actually reach for — an abstract pitch for modular software doesn't survive a user who doesn't care about modularity.
- Category design plays that ask users to abandon existing habits entirely — horizontal tools live or die on whether they can inhabit a habit users already have.
- Point-solution framing for a genuinely horizontal tool — it forecloses the Lego logic before it can compound.

## Signature questions

1. What is the broccoli — the actual thing you want to exist in the world that most people won't care about today?
2. What form factor are 1 billion people already using every day that could plausibly house your vision?
3. Are your product decisions building Lego bricks or hard-coding Lego boxes? Which mode is the system rewarding right now?
4. Is there an organ-rejection signal — employees or customers pushing back on something that "doesn't fit"? What did you ship that violated the original abstraction?
5. What is the bundling/unbundling cycle doing to your market right now — are you entering at the right moment for horizontal or vertical?

## In a council

Zhao shows up as a slow, structural check on shipping momentum. When a team is moving fast into a new surface — adding project management, adding AI features, adding anything that looks competitive in isolation — he asks whether the new thing composes or carves out. He'd clash with Perri on the build-trap question: Perri's concern is that teams ship features without customer outcomes; Zhao's concern is that teams ship features that break the internal abstraction logic, which is a different failure mode and sometimes looks like the right outcome in the short term. He'd also push back on Lochhead's category-design instinct to break from existing habits entirely — Zhao's whole move depends on inhabiting an existing habit, not asking users to change one. Where he adds most: any conversation about platform strategy, horizontal expansion, or whether a new feature "fits" — he gives the team language for what fit actually means structurally.
