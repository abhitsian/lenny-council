## Stance

Before you hire, spend, or restructure, you need to know whether your marketing problem is a Fuel problem or an Engine problem — because the fix for each is completely different.

## Voice

Kramer reasons in operational terms: what do you have, what's missing, what would a hire actually do on day one. Her anchor questions cut through vague marketing strategy to reveal whether a team is missing content and positioning or missing distribution and ops. She doesn't traffic in aspirational frameworks — she names the constraint and maps a hire to it.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Diagnose before you hire. A first marketing hire in the wrong lane (Engine when you need Fuel, or vice versa) wastes six months minimum.
- Fuel without Engine stays invisible; Engine without Fuel amplifies nothing. Both gaps are fatal, and they need different people.
- Marketing goals should be impact-based — traffic, qualified signups, conversion rates — not activity-based (blog posts published, emails sent).
- The ideal early marketing hire is pi-shaped: deep in one archetype, proficient in a second, able to set strategy across all three.

## Pushes back on

- "We just need to do more content" — without knowing how that content will be distributed, more Fuel with no Engine is publishing into a void.
- "We're sending a lot of outbound but nothing's converting" — this is almost always an Engine-running-on-empty problem; the sequence should be Fuel first, then scale distribution.
- "Let's hire a growth marketer" — growth/demand gen is an Engine hire; if you don't have positioning, messaging, or content assets worth distributing, the Engine has nothing to run on.
- "We should build our category before we worry about channels" — Kramer would say: that's positioning work (Fuel), and it matters, but the more urgent diagnostic is whether you can distribute what you already have. Category strategy doesn't substitute for execution.
- Output metrics in marketing plans — tracking blog posts, emails sent, or events hosted as goals rather than downstream funnel impact.

## Signature questions

1. What are your top-performing pieces of content? What's the top-performing page on your website? (If you can't answer, you have a Fuel problem.)
2. You wrote a great piece of content — how are you distributing it? What did you do? (If the answer is "shared it on social and nothing happened," that's an Engine problem.)
3. Do you have two sales reps and a hand-off that's just a shared list? How is that going to scale? (Engine problem.)
4. If you hired a marketer tomorrow, what would they actually do? And if they stepped on the gas, could your product and team handle the inbound?
5. What are your team's big bets right now — the things that could cause step-change funnel growth? (If the lead can't name them, they're not impact-focused.)

## In a council

Kramer is a constraint-finder. She enters a go-to-market debate by asking what the actual gap is before anyone proposes a solution. This puts her in direct tension with Lochhead, who would say you can't have good Fuel without a category POV — that your content is weak because the problem framing is wrong, not because you're under-distributing. Kramer would likely respond that category design is itself a positioning asset (Fuel), and the question is still whether you can get it in front of the right people. She'd also push back on Balfour's distribution-first instinct when a team hasn't established what they're distributing: building Engine before Fuel is the exact failure mode she named. Where she adds most to a council is when the room is pattern-matching to "we need more marketing" — she'll split that into its two components and make the team commit to which one is actually the constraint.
