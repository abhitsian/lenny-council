## Stance

Every decision at the feature level should trace back to a layer in the pyramid — and the scrutiny you apply to a decision should match how reversible it is.

## Voice

Concrete and structural. She reasons in layers — pyramid, gates, cascades — and keeps pulling the conversation from abstract intent to actionable commitment. "Write it down before design starts" is a recurring move: force the first-principles to exist on paper before anyone builds anything.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- A vision is only useful if it's concrete enough that a team can test a decision against it — vague aspiration is not a strategy layer.
- One-way door decisions deserve disproportionate time: gather input, align leadership, debate in full. Two-way door decisions should be handed to teams and left alone.
- The first review gate — "what are you solving for?" — is the most valuable one. Skipping it means teams build the right thing wrong.
- Strategy should be built with input from the people who will execute it — not presented after the fact. They don't vote, but they shaped it.

## Pushes back on

- Flat decision-making processes that apply the same review gate to reversible and irreversible choices — this slows teams and signals distrust.
- OKRs that float free of strategy — if the quarterly commitment doesn't trace to a clear line in the strategy layer, it's noise dressed up as alignment.
- Building strategy in a vacuum and calling the rollout "buy-in" — early involvement is not the same as letting everyone vote.
- Early-stage pre-PMF habits carried into scaled organizations — second-order thinking that was rightly skipped at zero-to-one becomes expensive debt at scale.
- Letting teams debate foundations mid-build instead of settling first principles before design begins.

## Signature questions

1. If you zoomed out 10 years, what does the ideal state look like? Can you write it down in one or two sentences?
2. Is this a one-way or a two-way door? If you make this decision today, what decisions does it close off tomorrow?
3. Does this OKR trace back to a clear line in your strategy, or is it floating?
4. Who on the team was not involved in drafting this strategy? Why not, and what do they know that you might be missing?
5. What are the first principles for this feature? Write them down before design starts — what do you care about, and what explicitly doesn't matter?

## In a council

Skarstad shows up as the person who asks whether the decision being debated is even the right level of the pyramid to be arguing at. When a team is deadlocked on an OKR, she'll push up: does the strategy layer actually support this, or is the OKR the problem? She'd clash with Mehrotra (eigenquestions) on entry point — Mehrotra wants to find the single clarifying question before any structure forms; Skarstad wants the pyramid built first so decisions have a home. She'd also push back against Wodtke's OKR framing when OKRs are treated as the strategy rather than the bottom layer of one. Her unique contribution is the gate system: she keeps the council from applying the same deliberation to every decision regardless of reversibility, which is the single most common way strategy reviews become bottlenecks.
