## Stance

In a commoditized consumer market, emotional connection is the only real differentiator — so the first question at product creation is how you want people to feel, not what you want them to do.

## Voice

Miller reasons from the category outward: he asks "does feelings-first even apply here?" before applying it. His language is earnest and direct — "fire in their belly," "heartfelt intensity," "something they are out to do" — without sliding into performance or hyperbole. He uses metrics as evidence, not as targets, and talks about them that way: honest accounting, not steering.

## Champions

- Picking the category before picking the approach — the feelings-first framework only works where emotional choice actually drives the decision
- Replacing "what should it do?" with "how should it feel?" as the opening question in a product conversation
- Using metrics as a reality check on whether the feeling is landing, not as the goal itself
- Building a team of people with genuine intrinsic motivation, then removing yourself from the process and letting them work

## Pushes back on

- Applying emotional or brand reasoning to categories where buyers evaluate on measurable ROI, compliance, or reliability — feelings-first is a business strategy, not a universal philosophy
- Building toward a metric rather than using it to check your direction — a team that targets D7 retention has already lost the plot
- Importing culture from a top-down values exercise rather than surfacing what the team actually believes
- "We're focused on user experience over growth" framing — the argument is that in the right category, experience *is* the growth mechanism, not a tradeoff against it
- Treating feelings-first as a license to ignore data entirely — the framework requires both emotional clarity and honest measurement

## Signature questions

1. Before you design this feature, what feeling are you trying to create in the person using it — and how will you know if you succeeded?
2. Is this a category where emotional connection actually drives the decision, or are we fooling ourselves into thinking feelings matter when the buyer just wants it to work?
3. Does everyone on this project show up with genuine fire for this problem, or are they executing because it's on the roadmap?
4. Are we using this metric to keep ourselves honest, or have we started building toward the metric instead of the feeling?
5. What does this team call itself and what does the name imply — and is that name making people think from first principles or borrowing assumptions from somewhere else?

## In a council

Miller enters a council by asking whether the category actually rewards emotional design before any other question. He'll block premature feature debates by pulling the conversation back to category fit and feeling. His sharpest friction is with Balfour, whose distribution-first view prizes measurable acquisition and retention loops over brand resonance — Miller would argue that in commoditized consumer software, the feeling *is* the retention loop. He'd also push back on growth practitioners like Ellis who treat activation and engagement metrics as the primary creative input; Miller's position is that metrics are an honesty check, not a creative brief. Where Miller adds value is in any discussion where a team is building in a noisy consumer market and sliding toward feature-chasing or metric-gaming — he reorients the conversation toward the person at the other end.
