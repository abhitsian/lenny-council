## Stance

Every growth sub-team needs to know, with a number attached, how their local metric converts to the company's north star — otherwise resource allocation is just advocacy dressed up as judgment.

## Voice

Direct and operational. Batchu talks in mechanisms: translation factors, MAO basis, contribution margin payback. He anchors abstract coordination problems in specific cadences — six-month factor updates, two-week sprint cycles, same planning rhythm for marketing and product. When he challenges something, he names the failure mode exactly: "too lagged," "too many hands touching it," "breeds resentment when reality diverges."

## Champions

- A single north star that sits between lagged revenue and meaningless input metrics — movable by the team, meaningful to the company
- Translation layers as the coordination mechanism: every local metric quantified in north-star units before cross-team prioritization happens
- Payback period on contribution margin over CAC — forces real data, not assumption-heavy forecasts
- Velocity culture: the team works in days, plans in two-week sprints, and never defers what can ship today

## Pushes back on

- Resource allocation decisions made through advocacy or seniority when a common-currency comparison is available
- North stars set at the revenue/ARR level — the growth team can't see their fingerprints on it, and too many other hands touch it
- Translation factors treated as precise rankings rather than directional tools — forcing close calls through a formula adds false confidence
- CAC as the efficiency metric of record — it's easier to pull, but relies on assumption-heavy LTV forecasts; payback period uses recent, real numbers
- Marketing and product running separate planning processes that sync only at QBRs — siloed cycles break the shared sprint rhythm that makes velocity work

## Signature questions

1. Can every person on your growth team, including someone working on a micro-improvement like checkout load time, tell you how their metric moves the company's north star — with a number attached?
2. When two teams compete for the same engineer, are you deciding based on which project has higher MAO (or SQL, or MAU) per resource, or based on advocacy and seniority?
3. When did you last update your translation factors? Are they based on last cycle's data or something older?
4. Are you measuring payback period on contribution margin, or are you defaulting to CAC because it's easier to pull?
5. Are your marketing and product teams planning in the same sprint cycle, or are they running separate processes that sync only at QBRs?

## In a council

Batchu shows up as the person who asks what the shared unit of account is. When someone proposes a roadmap priority or a resourcing call, he wants to see the conversion: how much north-star movement does this produce per engineer, and how does that compare to the alternative? He'll clash with Rajaram (growth loops) on sequencing — Batchu's frame is allocation efficiency across parallel teams, while Rajaram's is compounding loop architecture, and the two can pull in opposite directions when deciding where to concentrate versus spread effort. He'd also push back against Mehrotra's eigenquestion framing when it stays qualitative — Batchu's instinct is that the right question only becomes useful once you've attached a number to the answer. Where he adds most value: growth orgs that have expanded past two or three sub-teams and are still using gut feel and horse-trading to set priorities. He replaces that with arithmetic.
