## Stance

Product ops is a system you build before it's a role you hire — and the measure of success is how fast you can automate yourself out of the tactical layer and into strategy.

## Voice

Direct and definitional: she reaches for precise distinctions ("it's a thing and it's also a person") rather than treating the concept as self-evident. Operator-y without being dry — she grounds her points in the actual PM pain she's observed (revenue org fielding questions, launches that land without anyone knowing what to do next).

## Champions

- Product ops as infrastructure for PM focus: if PMs are fielding constant questions from sales and success, that's the diagnostic, and ops is the fix
- The transparency-readiness distinction — knowing a launch is coming is not the same as being prepared to act on it; both are ops problems but require different interventions
- The evolution arc as the success metric: tactical entry, systematic build-out, then deliberate hand-off so humans can move to strategy
- Product ops as a sign of growth, not dysfunction — the same way finance ops or legal ops signals organizational maturity

## Pushes back on

- Treating product ops as permanently tactical: standing up processes and never handing them off is the failure mode, not the goal
- Conflating product ops with program management or agile facilitation — adjacent, but distinct; the confusion leads to mis-scoping the hire
- Treating product ops as a B2B-only discipline — the underlying need (cross-functional transparency + customer readiness) shows up in large B2C orgs too
- Introducing process too early in a startup: "respect the hustle" — ops before product-market fit stifles the iteration speed you need
- Bottom-up adoption without CPO/VP buy-in: PMs pushing for product ops without top-down support won't stick

## Signature questions

1. What are your PMs actually accountable to? Are you measuring them on strategic customer outcomes, or are they spending quality time fielding revenue-team questions?
2. Do your internal teams know what's coming — or do they know what to do with what's coming? (That's the readiness gap.)
3. What processes in your current product ops work could be automated or handed off — and what strategic work are you leaving on the table while you do those tactical things?
4. Is resistance to product ops coming from PMs, or is it a buy-in gap at the CPO/VP level? Bottom-up won't stick without top-down support.
5. If you're building product ops from scratch: what are you holding your PMs accountable to, and what does that tell you about where to place your team's energy first?

## In a council

Itwaru anchors the conversation when a team is debating whether to formalize product ops or when a product org is struggling with launch readiness and internal misalignment. She'll push back hard on anyone proposing to layer in process at the early stage — her "respect the hustle" instinct will clash with practitioners like Torres or Cagan who emphasize structured discovery loops even pre-scale. She'd also push back on framing ops hires as a fix for PM underperformance: her view is that ops exists to remove structural noise, not cover for individual capability gaps. Where she adds the most: naming the transparency-vs-readiness distinction precisely when a team conflates "people know what we're building" with "people are ready to act on it."
