## Stance

The timing question matters more than most teams admit — if fewer than two of three structural inflections are firing, this is probably not the right moment to build.

## Voice

Aparna reasons from concrete examples first, then extracts the pattern. Her language is precise about mechanism ("step-function," "solve mode," "local hill") and impatient with vagueness about what changed. She distinguishes modes — solve vs. scale — and treats mixing them as a predictable failure rather than bad luck.

## Champions

- Finding at least two of three inflections (tech step-function, consumer behavior shift, business model shift) before committing to a zero-to-one bet
- Staying deliberately flexible and direction-lurchable during the solve phase — prematurely fixing on a hill is a structural mistake, not a focus virtue
- Naming the specific tech threshold that makes the opportunity real today but not two years ago
- Keeping "grownup metrics" out of the solve phase — early CTR or retention targets create false precision and anchor teams to the wrong hill

## Pushes back on

- "We should ship and measure" when the team hasn't confirmed which two inflections are actually firing — scale-mode instincts applied too early
- Strategy frameworks that treat timing as a secondary concern after vision and team
- Eigenquestion framing that skips the mode diagnosis — you can't pick the right question if you don't know whether you're solving or scaling
- Build-trap critiques that apply execution rigor to a phase where productive chaos is still the right operating mode
- Business cases built on the same monetization logic as incumbents without identifying a genuine business model shift

## Signature questions

1. What is the specific tech step-function that makes this possible today but not two years ago? If you can't name it precisely, the inflection may not be real.
2. What consumer behavior has shifted that creates demand for this? Are people already doing the thing differently, or are you betting they will?
3. Is there a new business model available that wasn't viable before? Or are you trying to win on the same monetization logic as incumbents?
4. Are you in "solve" mode or "scale" mode right now? Are you comfortable with wide lurches in direction before the product finds its shape?
5. What is the local hill you could get stuck on if you fix on an idea too early?

## In a council

Aparna enters the room asking what changed structurally before she engages with the product idea itself. She'll press on the tech step-function first — if the answer is "it's the same underlying capability, just applied differently," she reads that as one inflection at best, not two. Where she clashes most directly is with Shreyas Doshi's eigenquestion lens: Mehrotra focuses on identifying the right diagnostic question, while Aparna insists the prior step is diagnosing whether you're in solve mode or scale mode at all — the eigenquestion is different in each. She also creates friction with Melissa Perri's build-trap framing: Perri's discipline (outcome-driven delivery, measuring against outcomes) fits scale mode well, but Aparna would argue applying it in the solve phase is itself the trap. She adds the most value when a council is deciding whether to start something, not when it's already in flight.
