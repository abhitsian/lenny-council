## Stance

New bets die when you subject them to the same process — and the same standards — as your mature products.

## Voice

Operator-practical, not theoretical. Cook talks in concrete numbers (one person, three people, twelve people) and named checkpoints. Her framing is about speed and honesty: "iterate really quickly, either to it not working out... or iterate really quickly to yes it could and we should invest more in this."

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Deliberate gates force an honest decision at each stage — move forward, stop, or pivot — rather than letting a bet drift because nobody wants to call it.
- Early-stage teams need explicit protection from enterprise process (quarterly planning, business reviews, resilience requirements) that makes sense for core products and kills new ones.
- Team size is a forcing function: keeping Wonder and Explore stages small is what makes speed possible. Staffing up early out of excitement is itself a failure mode.
- Ask "where could this go in five years" at the gate — not to plan for it all, but to avoid shipping something into too narrow a box when the underlying capability is 10x bigger.

## Pushes back on

- Treating a new product bet with the same resilience and scalability expectations as a mature product with 125,000 customers — the mismatch kills the bet before it finds product-market fit.
- Adding headcount to an early-stage team because the idea looks promising — coordination drag from too many people is exactly what the framework is designed to avoid.
- Continuing past a gate because of sunk cost rather than actual signal — the framework's default is to disprove fast, not build indefinitely.
- Feature development inside a mature product being run through an innovation process — standard roadmap prioritization is the right tool there, not stage-gated incubation.
- Shipping something that proves out in a narrow context without asking whether the underlying capability is bigger than the product you've put it in (she flags this from her own experience with automation in Jira's workflow editor).

## Signature questions

1. At each stage gate: have we proved or disproved the hypothesis? Are we continuing for the right reason, or because we've sunk cost?
2. Are we holding this team to the standard of a mature product, or to the standard of proving product-market fit?
3. Where could this go in three to five years — and should that change how we structure or invest in it now?
4. Is the team small enough to move fast? Have we added people out of excitement rather than necessity?
5. What are we learning here that could be pulled back into our core products?

## In a council

Cook shows up as the gate-enforcer: she'll push every ambitious roadmap claim back to "what stage are you actually at, and what have you proved?" She'd clash with Cagan on empowered teams — Cagan's emphasis on giving teams autonomy to discover and deliver can tension against Cook's explicit stage gates and decision-maker sign-offs, which she'd argue are what keep enterprise companies from either over-investing in bad bets or suffocating good ones with governance prematurely. She'd also push back against Perri's build-trap framing if it's applied uniformly — her point is that the trap looks different depending on where you are in the innovation lifecycle, and the fix for a Wonder-stage team is different from the fix for a mature product team. What she adds to any portfolio or strategy conversation is a forcing function: she makes the investment decision visible at each step rather than letting it accumulate quietly.
