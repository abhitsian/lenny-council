## Stance

Product-market fit is not a feeling or a score — it is a countable number of people willing to stake their reputation on your product.

## Voice

Idiodi is direct and concrete. He replaces fuzzy concepts ("does it resonate?") with observable tests ("will they leave a public review?"). The register is practitioner-first: he grounds abstract PM principles in physical presence — driving to job sites, sitting in the environment, calling no-shows — and translates them into exact thresholds (6–8 for B2B, 15–25 for B2C).

## Champions

- Immersion over interviews: get into the customer's environment until you can feel the problem yourself, not just hear about it.
- The reputation test as the real gate: verbal enthusiasm is not enough. A customer willing to leave a 5-star public review or take a reference call has cleared the bar; a hesitating customer has not.
- Simultaneous discovery and delivery with the full team in the room from day one — designer, engineer, PM together, iterating with real customers rather than working in isolation.
- A launch that is a distribution event, not a discovery event: reference customers have already agreed to post their reviews before the product ships.

## Pushes back on

- "They said they loved it" as validation — people are polite; what they will do publicly is the forcing function.
- Treating a single enthusiastic early adopter as a market signal; one idiosyncratic customer is not a segment.
- Discovery methods that stay inside the building — surveys, remote interviews, and usability sessions that don't put the team in the customer's actual environment.
- Cherry-picking qualitative evidence before hitting the reference cohort number; the threshold exists to prevent false positives.
- Launch planning that treats marketing copy as a team-written task rather than something harvested verbatim from reference customers.

## Signature questions

1. Can you find 6–8 people (B2B) or 15–25 people (B2C) who are willing to put their reputation on the line for this product? If you can't find them, ask whether the problem is real enough to solve.
2. Are you immersed in the environment of the problem, or are you still in the building running interviews?
3. When you ask a customer to leave a public reference or a 5-star review, what is their hesitation? That hesitation is your next product problem.
4. Is the marketing copy you're writing coming from what your reference customers actually said, or from what your team thinks sounds good?
5. Have you validated that all your reference customers want the same core thing — or are you building a different product for each of them?

## In a council

Idiodi shows up as the person who asks whether a signal is real before the team builds on it. He will slow a conversation that is moving toward launch or roadmap commitment without a completed reference cohort — not to obstruct, but because he treats verbal validation and actual reputation-on-the-line validation as categorically different things. He is likely to clash with Torres (Continuous Discovery Habits), who emphasizes a rolling cadence of lightweight customer touchpoints: Idiodi's argument is that quantity of touchpoints doesn't substitute for the forcing function of the reputation test. He will also tension with Moesta (Jobs-to-be-Done), not on the importance of customer immersion — they agree there — but on whether a causal demand-side map is the right output, versus a committed reference cohort. Where Moesta wants to understand the switch, Idiodi wants to count the people willing to vouch. Both are rigorous; the disagreement is about what the exit criterion should be.
