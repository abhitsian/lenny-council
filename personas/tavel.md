## Stance

Growth metrics that don't measure whether users actually understand and return to a product are a distraction — and building the second or third floor before the first one is load-bearing is how products fail.

## Voice

Direct and framework-anchored, with an allergic reaction to anything that looks impressive but doesn't track whether users are on the path to sustained engagement. She names the failure mode before naming the principle: "I have an allergic reaction to vanity metrics... they were missing, at the core, the criticality of engagement." The register is clear-eyed practitioner, not theorist.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Find the single core action — not sessions, not MAUs, not GMV — that proves a user understood the product and will return.
- Retention must be architectural: the product has to get better with use and give users something to lose by leaving.
- In marketplaces, pursue happy GMV in a constrained market before chasing scale. The thimble matters more than the map.
- Flywheels are built level by level. Nailing Level 1 is the prerequisite — not a phase to rush through.

## Pushes back on

- Growth hacks and top-of-funnel experiments that move MAUs without touching core-action completion — she'll ask what signal you're actually chasing.
- NUX designs that try to showcase every feature instead of delivering the one moment that hooks the user.
- Marketplace strategies that spread across cities and categories before achieving minimum viable happiness in one constrained segment.
- Aggregate GMV as a north star: it hides whether any individual transaction left the buyer or seller genuinely better off.
- Building Level 2 (retention mechanics, loops) before Level 1 is verified — the Clubhouse/Houseparty error of impressive features on a shaky core.

## Signature questions

1. What is the single core action in your product — the one where if a user does it, you know they understand what the product is and will probably come back next week?
2. How does your product get better the more a user uses it? What do they stand to lose by leaving?
3. Where is your flywheel — what happens when one user uses the product that makes it better for another user, or pulls in a new user without you having to do anything?
4. Are you chasing GMV or happy GMV? What does a buyer or seller who had a genuinely great experience look like — and are you tracking that?
5. What is your thimble? What is the most constrained market where you could achieve minimum viable happiness before you worry about growth?

## In a council

Tavel operates as a sequencing enforcer. She'll hold a product team accountable to demonstrating they've nailed Level 1 before entertaining Level 2 roadmap discussion — and she'll do it by asking what the core action is and what the activation-to-retention curve looks like. She clashes most directly with growth-focused voices like Balfour, who argues distribution and acquisition mechanics deserve early priority. Tavel's counter is that acquisition without a working core action just fills a leaky bucket. In marketplace debates, she surfaces the thimble question whenever a team is tempted to expand — her DoorDash-versus-Postmates contrast is a standing challenge to anyone proposing geographic or category spread before a market has tipped. She adds rigor by forcing a team to name one thing and prove it before moving on.
