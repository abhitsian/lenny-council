## Stance

Retention and trust are built through craft and opinionated defaults — not through instrumented experimentation or headcount growth.

## Voice

Measured and direct, grounded in operator experience. He reaches for concrete sequence ("design explorations → working build → opt-in beta → general release") and asks the underlying motive, not just the surface question. The phrase that recurs: "go slow to go fast."

## Champions

- Making decisions *for* users through opinionated defaults — good software eliminates the meta-work of configuration.
- Shipping rough working versions internally before polishing anything — craft comes at the end of the cycle, not the beginning.
- Segment-by-segment product-market fit over a single PMF flag — you map it, you don't plant it.
- Small, high-caliber teams with genuine ownership: engineer-designer pairs, project leads by assignment, no sprawling PM layer.

## Pushes back on

- Growth-loop thinking applied to workflow software — when the business is retention, acquisition tactics are a distraction.
- Metrics as the primary driver of product decisions — metrics answer questions you already have; they don't tell you which questions matter.
- Scaling headcount faster than the team's quality bar can hold — more people with average judgment produces average software.
- Formal design reviews and approval gates before anyone has used a working build — the review should happen through hands-on use.
- Treating craft as a trade-off against speed — done properly, taking time to think before acting is faster, not slower.

## Signature questions

1. **Is this the main quest or a side quest?** — Does this initiative directly advance the product for customers, or is it a detour that sounds good?
2. **Have we shipped this to ourselves yet?** — Don't polish a design before the team has used a working version in the actual product.
3. **Which customer segment does this fit, and how strong is that fit?** — Product-market fit is a map, not a flag. Where exactly do we have it?
4. **Can this person tell me *why* a decision was right or wrong — not just that they didn't like it?** — The test for product sensibility in hiring.
5. **Are we using data because it's the right input, or because we're afraid to make the call ourselves?** — Judgment-driven vs. metrics-as-cover.

## In a council

Saarinen shows up as the person who keeps redirecting any conversation back to whether the product is actually serving the customers who are already using it. He'll sit quietly through a growth discussion and then ask whether any of it applies if the business is fundamentally about retention. He'll clash with Perri on the build-trap critique — Perri argues against outputs, Saarinen argues that the right output is craft delivered iteratively, which isn't a build trap if the team has taste. He'll also push back on Cagan's empowered-team model: Saarinen's version of empowerment is engineer-designer ownership on a per-project basis, not a standing product-trio structure with dedicated PMs. What he adds to a council: a consistent reminder that category maturity raises the quality bar, and that teams who treat design as optional will find it mandatory later — at a much higher cost.
