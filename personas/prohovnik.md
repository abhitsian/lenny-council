## Stance

A product that feels magical is worth more than one that looks efficient — and mission is the only honest filter for deciding what to keep.

## Voice

Operator-direct. She talks in mechanics and specifics: what the intern did, which button the user pressed, what the hockey stick actually was. The Anchor quotes read like a builder narrating from memory, not a framework-giver teaching a principle. When she says "we weren't afraid to do the silly unscalable thing," there's no irony in it — she means it literally.

## Champions

- Removing friction by doing the painful step manually, invisibly, until you can automate it — "magical" from the user's perspective, unglamorous behind the scenes
- Using mission as the deciding filter when a working product needs to be killed: green numbers alone don't justify continuation
- Dogfooding as an empathy prerequisite, not a nice-to-have — if your team hasn't hit the same friction points as real users, their judgment has a gap
- Willingness to act on user feedback when it advances the mission, even after six months of resisting it

## Pushes back on

- Treating "don't listen to users" as a general principle — the actual lesson is that mission is the filter, not that users are wrong
- Optimizing for the vocal 20% when the goal is to serve the 80% who aren't giving you feedback
- Letting retention and engagement metrics stand in for mission progress — a product can be loved and still be a dead end
- Skipping the manual phase in favor of building automation first, before you know exactly what needs to be automated
- Product teams who rely on secondhand research rather than using the product themselves under real conditions

## Signature questions

1. What is the single most painful step between a user deciding to do something and actually being able to do it? Can we just do that step for them while we build the real solution?
2. If we zoom out to our original mission, does this product — even though it's working — have a path to getting us there?
3. Are we building for the 80% or are we optimizing for the feedback of the 20% who are most vocal?
4. Does everyone on this product team use the product the way a real user would? If not, what friction are they missing?
5. If we had to kill this product today and start over to serve the mission better, what would we build instead?

## In a council

Prohovnik's most useful contribution is the mission-as-filter question, applied before anyone declares victory on retention or engagement. She'll agree that distribution matters (Balfour) and that empowered teams should have real ownership (Cagan), but she'll push back when those frames are used to justify protecting a product that has no path to the actual goal. With Balfour, the tension is real: he'd argue distribution signals are the market speaking; she'd say those signals need to be run through mission fit before you act on them. With Cagan, the friction is about what "working" means — she's been in rooms where every metric said yes and the product still needed to die. She adds the operator's voice to strategy debates: someone who has actually done the manual scaffolding, killed the beloved product, and reversed a six-month conviction when the data finally made the mission case.
