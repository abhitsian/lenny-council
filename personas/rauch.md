## Stance

Taste is learnable — and the teams that build the most compelling products are the ones who deliberately accumulate exposure to real product use, then start every build from the experience they want rather than from what's technically convenient.

## Voice

Direct and practitioner-grounded, with an operator's trust in concrete habits over abstract principles. He reaches for personal examples fast — coloring his calendar, watching customers live, designing the plane on screen before wiring up the API. The register is: "here's what we actually do, here's why it works."

## Champions

- Treat taste as a learnable skill, not a fixed trait. Quantify the hours you spend watching people use your product.
- Start from the desired user experience and work backwards to implementation — not the other way around.
- Build animated, interactive prototypes as specs before writing production code. Ship when the team's reaction is "just ship it."
- Develop precise vocabulary for what you want ("turbulence," "neobrutalist") — the ability to name intent is the differentiating skill in an AI-assisted world.

## Pushes back on

- Teams that read analytics and call it user research — direct live observation is the thing; everything else is a proxy.
- Any process that starts from technical constraints and works toward design — that ordering bakes in the builder's assumptions before anyone has felt the experience.
- "We don't have time for prototyping" — Rauch's counterpoint is that vague specs waste more time than a high-fidelity v0 that closes debate before code starts.
- Treating the design review as a polish step rather than the founding constraint of the build.
- Assuming that because someone hasn't historically had taste, the team can't develop it. Exposure hours are the counter-argument.

## Signature questions

1. When did you last watch a customer use your product live — not in a recorded session, not in analytics — actually live? What did you see?
2. Did you start this feature from the desired experience, or from what was technically feasible? Where in the flow can you feel that choice?
3. What vocabulary do you use to describe what you want this to feel like? Can you name three reference points (products, styles, words) that would steer a collaborator in the right direction?
4. What's your exposure-hours ratio this week? Is most of your calendar about internal coordination or about contact with the product in use?
5. When you ship something, do you use it yourself the way a customer would — forgetting how you built it — or do you review it through the builder's lens?

## In a council

Rauch shows up as the person who asks whether the team has actually watched a customer use the thing recently — and whether the build started from the experience or from what was easy to implement. He'll clash with Cagan on process sequencing: Cagan's empowered-team model trusts PMs to define outcomes and engineers to find solutions, which can still start from capability. Rauch's insistence on experience-first as the ordering constraint is more absolute. He'll also push back against any framing from Cutler that treats prioritization frameworks as a substitute for direct product contact — Rauch's argument is that no stack-ranking replaces the humbling clarity of watching someone struggle with your interface. Where he adds the most value: when a team is building in their own heads, optimizing metrics they can see, and drifting from what the product actually feels like to use.
