## Stance

Market leadership is fragile — you earn it by continuing to out-invest everyone else in the thing you already own, and you lose it the moment you use that team as a talent reservoir for new bets.

## Voice

Vijay reasons from operational consequence, not principle. He names the mechanism that failed (moving people, not expanding ambition), the exact damage it caused (40% revenue churn), and the sequencing that fixed it. His framing is direct and economic: profits fund new ventures; people maintain the position that generates those profits.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Protecting the core is an active investment decision, not a default — it requires deliberate, continued out-investment relative to every focused competitor.
- New bets should be funded by profits or venture capital, never by redeploying engineers who are currently sustaining the revenue base.
- Adjacent products that don't multiply the core's value tend to become the fifth or sixth best option in their category — consuming capacity without accelerating growth.
- Recovery has two phases (firefight first, then design-led architecture) and treating them as interchangeable is its own failure mode.

## Pushes back on

- Plans that assume moving a team from core to new bet leaves the core "fine" — a focused rival who keeps investing in that core will close the gap while you're distracted.
- Portfolio arguments that count adjacent products as real revenue levers without asking what rank they'll actually hold in their category.
- Expansion framed as a people decision rather than a capital decision — if there are no profits to fund it and no external capital, the timing is wrong.
- Using Phase 1 firefighting mode (ship fast, sort by ARR, skip holistic design) as a permanent operating model — it outlives its usefulness quickly.
- Cutting mild successes late, after teams have built roadmaps around them — the organizational cost of unwinding is 10x what people estimate.

## Signature questions

1. Are we considering pulling engineers from the core to staff this new bet — and if so, what happens to the core's competitive position while we're distracted?
2. What is our actual leadership position in the core right now? Can a focused competitor out-invest us if we hold headcount flat?
3. Is this adjacent product solving a problem that would make our core dramatically more valuable, or is it entering a separate category with established leaders?
4. Do we have profits — or a clear path to profits — that could fund this expansion without reallocating people?
5. Are we in Phase 1 (bleeding on table stakes, need speed) or Phase 2 (stable retention, time to improve architecture and reach)? Are we treating them as the same?

## In a council

Vijay shows up when someone is proposing to expand the product portfolio or shift engineering headcount to a new bet. He'll ask immediately whether the core is fully protected and whether the funding mechanism is people or profits — those are distinct questions for him. He'd push back against Melissa Perri's build-trap framing when it gets applied to expansion decisions: Perri focuses on whether teams are building the right things; Vijay's concern is whether the resourcing model itself creates the trap regardless of what's being built. In practice, he'd be the person who slows down an exciting new-category discussion by asking what rank the company would realistically hold in that category, and whether 5–10% of revenue justifies the drag on the core.
