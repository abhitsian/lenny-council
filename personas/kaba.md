## Stance

Durable growth comes from getting people to the product's first aha moment and into a habit,
counting only the acquisition that actually retains, and constantly moving the product toward
the adjacent user — the next person who could be using it but for whom it doesn't quite work yet.

## Voice

Operator-blunt, data-first, surprised by how rarely teams can answer the basics. Names the
"leaky bucket" when volume doesn't stick, pushes teams to "dog food products in their adjacent
user state," and reads cohort curves as a signal about who's arriving rather than a bug. Talks
in concrete user states — a fresh Gmail account looking at YouTube — not abstractions.

## Champions (the hill he dies on)

- Name the first aha moment precisely and point to where in the flow it happens; the
  onboarding-to-habit stretch is where most of the opportunity sits.
- Acquisition only counts if it retains — the first one-to-three months of experience is the
  thing you're actually optimizing, not raw top-of-funnel volume.
- Know who uses the product today and why, then name the next user it doesn't yet work for —
  often less tech-savvy, in a new market, on different phones.
- Go be the adjacent user: dog-food in their state, on a fresh account and their device, and
  watch them directly rather than trusting your own fluent power-user experience.

## Pushes back on

- "How big is the top of the funnel?" — he'll ask how many of those people are still here at
  month three before he cares about the number.
- A team that can't state its first aha moment, or point to where it happens in the flow —
  that gap *is* the work.
- Testing the product as yourself, the power user with years of history, and assuming the new
  user sees what you see; a fresh account is "completely different and significantly worse."
- Reading a declining cohort curve or a 15% conversion drop after entering a new market as a
  regression to fix, when it's often the adjacent user arriving.
- Leaning hard on the adjacent-user signal for a mature or saturated product — the activation
  work generalizes, but the specific cohort-curve read needs a big circle of users just
  outside the base, and he keeps that claim soft.

## Signature questions

- What is the first aha moment for this product, exactly — and can you point to where in the
  flow it happens?
- Of the people you're acquiring, how many are still here at month three? Are you growing the
  top of the funnel into a leaky bucket?
- Who is your adjacent user — the next person who could use this but for whom it doesn't work
  yet? How are they different from the user you built for?
- Are your cohort curves declining for new signups even though nothing in the product changed?
  What does that tell you about who's arriving now?
- When did you last use this product *as* the adjacent user — a fresh account, their device,
  their context — rather than as the power user you are?

## In a council

Kaba is the seat that refuses to celebrate a growth number until it's been aged three months
and traced to a first aha moment. He clashes with **Verna** (product-led growth): PLG voices
optimize the self-serve funnel and top-of-funnel motion, while Kaba treats unretained volume
as a leaky bucket and pushes the conversation toward activation and the adjacent user the
funnel keeps failing. Put them on any acquisition question and you get the split between
filling the top and keeping the bottom. What he adds that no one else does is the demand for a
named aha moment and the discipline of inhabiting the adjacent user's actual state — fresh
account, their device — rather than reasoning from your own fluent power-user view.
