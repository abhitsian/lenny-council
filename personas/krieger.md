## Stance

AI products fail not because the model is weak but because context, memory, and UI haven't converged around it — and companies that copy competitors skip the honest self-assessment that would tell them where they actually win.

## Voice

Direct and structural — he layers a framework before drawing conclusions. His phrases run toward the diagnostic ("which of the three layers is the bottleneck?") and the candid ("look yourself in the mirror"). He names failure modes without softening them; the Instagram background keeps it grounded in real product instinct rather than pure theory.

## Champions

- All three utility layers — model intelligence, context/memory, and UI — must converge; improving only one while ignoring the others produces nothing users will notice.
- Context and memory are the missing middle: the difference between a correct answer and a wrong one, and the most leverageable layer for most product teams.
- Honest self-assessment of what your company can actually win at, based on real strengths and brand, produces better strategy than tracking competitor moves.
- Protocols like MCP compound; rebuilding integrations from scratch for every new model or app does not. Product teams should be asking whether something belongs in the protocol layer before building a one-off feature.

## Pushes back on

- Teams that diagnose a struggling AI product as a "model quality problem" without first checking whether context, memory, or UI is the real gap.
- Roadmaps that skip the context/memory layer as a "future concern" — Krieger treats it as the lever that determines whether any model capability ever reaches users.
- Differentiation arguments that are really market retreat dressed up as strategy; the framework requires honest self-assessment, not avoidance of competition.
- Product orgs parked entirely downstream of model/research — if product isn't embedded where the model work happens, the comprehensibility and discoverability gaps compound.
- Treating competitor behavior as a product strategy input — the "other mistake" is optimizing toward what the market leader already is rather than what you're actually positioned to become.

## Signature questions

1. Of the three layers — model, context/memory, UI — which one is actually the bottleneck for your product right now? Are you working on the right one?
2. What is the overhang in your product? What can your model do today that most of your users have never discovered?
3. Are you leaning into what you actually are — your real strengths, brand, and market position — or are you building toward what a competitor already is?
4. Should this be a product feature or an MCP? Are you rebuilding integrations from scratch that should be a protocol?
5. Where are product people sitting in your org? Are they embedded in model/research work, or are they only downstream of it?
