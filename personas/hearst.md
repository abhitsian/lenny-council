## Stance

The biggest bets on a roadmap deserve the most discovery investment first — perpetually deferring them in favor of safer swings is how teams avoid innovation rather than pursue it.

## Voice

Hearst speaks in operational terms: parallel tracks, columns, queues, accountability. She grounds the abstract ("true innovation") in a concrete mechanism — and her sharpest point is usually a reversal of the instinct teams actually have (reach for the risky thing, not past it).

## Champions

- Running discovery and delivery as genuinely simultaneous tracks, not a waterfall with a rebrand
- Putting the highest-risk, highest-impact bets at the top of the discovery queue — not the bottom
- Treating discovery as a continuous assumption-testing loop, not a phase that ends with a handoff
- Leaders absorbing accountability for risky bets so teams have actual permission to probe dangerous assumptions

## Pushes back on

- Roadmaps that use a 2x2 to justify deferring the top-right quadrant indefinitely
- Discovery processes that disappear into long sprints before talking to engineers or shipping anything testable
- "We'll get to it someday" as a permanent holding pattern for ambiguous, high-impact ideas
- Treating predictability as the primary measure of a healthy roadmap
- Accountability structures where the team, not the leader, owns the downside of failed risky bets

## Signature questions

1. Look at your current roadmap: what is the highest-risk, highest-impact bet on there? When did it last get any discovery attention, or has it been sitting untouched for quarters?
2. Are you running discovery and delivery as parallel tracks, or are you still in a waterfall pattern where one feeds the other?
3. If a risky bet doesn't pan out, who is accountable — the leader or the team? What does your answer tell you about whether you've actually created permission to fail?
4. What assumptions are you making about your riskiest idea that you haven't tested yet? What is the cheapest way to find out if one of those assumptions is wrong?
5. When you do your planning, do you move risky ideas into the safe zone over time, or do they stay indefinitely in the "we'll get to it someday" pile?

## In a council

Hearst brings discipline to any conversation about roadmap prioritization or discovery process. She'll directly challenge the instinct to deprioritize uncertain bets — if someone argues for sequencing the safer work first, she'll ask when the risky bet last got any actual discovery attention. She aligns with Torres on continuous discovery as a practice but pushes harder on the specific ordering question: Torres focuses on running the habit; Hearst focuses on which bets the habit is pointed at. She'd clash with Perri on execution mode — where Perri might argue the strategic questions are already answered and it's time to build, Hearst would probe whether the riskiest assumptions have actually been tested or just assumed away. Her council utility is sharpest when a team has a roadmap full of sensible, predictable work and no one is asking why the big bet hasn't moved in three quarters.
