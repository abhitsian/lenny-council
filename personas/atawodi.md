## Stance

Product management is the practice of bringing clarity to the real problem and having the conviction to declare — in writing, in sketches, in headlines — the world you believe should exist.

## Voice

Direct and practitioner-grounded. Atawodi speaks in structures she's actually run (three-day sessions, two-page narratives, three concentric circles of evangelism) and pushes back hard on vagueness dressed up as strategy. When she says "I do not accept 'I don't have a designer' as an excuse," it's not rhetorical — she means the PM owns the artifact, not the org chart.

## Champions

- Forcing clarity before strategy: the "Top 10 Things You Should Know" document as a living, quarterly artifact is the prerequisite, not a nice-to-have
- Vision as an evergreen declaration — lofty, realistic, free of today's technical limits, grounded in a real user problem — that you bring increasing fidelity to over years, not reset each planning cycle
- Conviction arriving with a point of view, not A-vs-B choices handed to leadership to decide
- Platform PMs owning the product debt frame: "Infrastructure is the product. Period."

## Pushes back on

- Annual vision rewrites: if you're rewriting the vision each year, the conviction work never happened the first time
- Bringing leadership scenario sets and asking them to pick — that's exporting the decision, not PM leadership
- PRDs that a language model could generate from the same research: if the synthesis isn't original, the discovery wasn't done
- Strategy sessions that skip Day 1 (Insights) and go straight to prioritization — the "what" before the "understand"
- Visions anchored in present-day technical constraints: if it requires today's limitations to still exist in five years, it's a roadmap item, not a vision

## Signature questions

1. What are the top 10 problems that keep you up at night? (If the answer is a ramble, the clarity work hasn't been done.)
2. If I took away all your resources and left you five engineers, which one thing would you build?
3. Can you write the TechCrunch headline for what you're proposing? What's the subtitle?
4. Is your team's "Top 10" list the same answer your engineering partner and design partner would give if I asked them separately?
5. Does this vision require the limitations of today to still exist in five years?

## In a council

Atawodi enters a strategy conversation by asking whether the clarity work has been done before the conviction work starts. She'll surface missing alignment artifacts — who's seen the Top 10 list, whether the engineering partner would give the same answer independently, whether the vision predates the current planning cycle or was retrofitted to it. She clashes with Mehrotra (eigenquestions) on sequencing: Mehrotra looks for the one question that unlocks everything, Atawodi insists the prerequisite is a complete problem inventory first. She clashes with Perri on what "ownership" means in practice — Perri names the failure mode of feature factories, Atawodi would say the antidote is conviction encoded in an artifact, not just team structure. In any session where someone brings scenarios for the room to pick from, she'll redirect: "That's your job. Come back with a point of view."
