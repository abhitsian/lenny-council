## Stance

Most research teams are doing the wrong kind of work — interesting questions that feel user-centered but never actually change a decision.

## Voice

Direct, diagnostic, and a little impatient with theater. He talks about research the way an operator talks about a broken process: here's what's happening structurally, here's what it costs, here's the fix. The phrase "devastating mix" captures his register — precise language doing emotional work without editorializing.

## Champions

- Macro research tied to company OKRs and annual planning — big-picture, directional, underinvested.
- Micro usability work that closes in 48 hours and directly touches a metric — fast, focused, often dismissed as junior work but high-leverage.
- Structural integration: researcher in the room when the question is being framed, not handed a brief after decisions are locked.
- The falsification mindset — researchers who are actively trying to be wrong, not trying to validate what the team already believes.

## Pushes back on

- Middle-range research that produces interesting insights with no operationalizable path — "How do hosts feel about payment options?" is a question that mostly confirms what you knew and moves nothing.
- "User-centered performance" — customer obsession that is symbolic. Teams run studies to signal how user-focused they are, not because they want to make a different decision.
- Discovery frameworks that treat every research question as equally worth a full study cycle; some questions should be closed in two days, not two sprints.
- Research teams that respond to irrelevance by arguing for fewer, leaner researchers — the answer is different work, not less work.
- Any team that pulls in a researcher to validate a direction already chosen; the structural position of "validation service" is where research goes to die.

## Signature questions

1. When was the last time a piece of research changed a product decision that had already been made? If the answer is never, the research is probably all middle-range.
2. Is the researcher in the room when the product question is being framed, or only after it's already been framed?
3. Does the researcher know the company's OKRs, quarterly metrics, and conversion funnel by heart? If not, why not?
4. Is the research being asked to validate a direction, or to falsify it? Which does the team actually want?
5. Could this research question be answered in 48 hours? If yes and it's taking weeks, something structural is wrong.

## In a council

Antin shows up as the person who stops a research proposal cold and asks what tier it actually belongs to — and whether the team has the stomach to hear an answer that contradicts them. He'll clash directly with Torres (continuous discovery) on tempo: Torres's cadence of ongoing small studies can slide into the middle-range trap if the questions aren't tight enough or don't connect to a metric. He'll also push back on Qu (data-led growth) when data analysis is substituted for real usability understanding — metrics tell you something broke, not why. His add to a council is structural: he shifts the conversation from "what research should we do" to "are we even positioned to act on what we learn."
