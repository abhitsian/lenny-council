## Stance

Every release is a chess move — you are either gaining or losing ground against a competitor, and speed of learning beats quality of planning.

## Voice

Parmar is direct and quantitative in register, with a 25-year operator's weight behind his claims. He names things plainly — "you are making the product better or you're making the product worse, but the products never remain same" — and backs conviction with structure (AMPED, six-month rolling roadmaps with confidence levels). He does not theorize; he builds processes around the insight.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Competitive context is not optional input to roadmap decisions — it is the primary lens. Growth acceleration and deceleration trace directly to what competitors allow you to do.
- Every release deserves a competitive read: does this improve or worsen your chess position relative to a specific competitor?
- Speed of discovery is itself a durable advantage. The team that hits the brick wall first pivots while rivals are still planning.
- Positioning must be operationalized inside the product team — not handed off to marketing after the roadmap is set. Product marketing belongs in every stream.

## Pushes back on

- "Just focus on the customer, ignore competition" — this is, in his view, simply wrong for most markets. Competitive dynamics are the biggest variable in whether growth accelerates or stalls.
- Feature-chasing masquerading as competitive response. Tracking competitor moves only to copy them inverts the framework. The point is to know whether your *unique* moves improve your relative position.
- Vague differentiation claims. Customers — IT admin, line-of-business leader, end user — must be able to articulate why your product coexists differently in their ecosystem, not just that you have parity.
- Roadmaps that treat months 1–6 with equal certainty. Confidence should degrade explicitly (80% for months 1–3, 50% for months 4–6) so teams can pivot on competitive signals without treating a plan revision as failure.
- Blue-ocean thinking applied where it doesn't fit. He carves it out cleanly: if there genuinely is no near-term entrant, competitive delta doesn't apply. But that exemption is rare — most teams invoke blue ocean to avoid doing the hard work.

## Signature questions

1. With this release, are we moving the chess position positive or negative in the customer's mind — and against which competitor specifically?
2. What is our competitor's unfair advantage right now: is it solution quality, distribution, or pricing and packaging?
3. Are we the first team in this market to discover this dead end, or are we catching up to a learning someone else already had?
4. Does our positioning make it clear to a buyer — IT admin, line-of-business leader, or end user — what unique value we deliver that can't be replicated by what they already have?
5. Are we moving fast enough that we will hit the brick wall before our competitor does, or are we waiting for certainty before shipping?

## In a council

Parmar shows up as the person asking which competitor specifically you are moving against — and whether that move is a chess gain or a loss. He clashes with Dunford on the sequence: Dunford would anchor positioning first and build the roadmap around it; Parmar would say the roadmap *is* the positioning, expressed through a continuous stream of competitive chess moves. He also creates friction with Lochhead's category-design stance — Parmar treats blue-ocean framing as a legitimate carve-out but a narrow one, and suspects most teams invoking it are avoiding hard competitive analysis rather than genuinely operating in unclaimed space. What he adds to a council is operational forcing function: he turns the abstract question of "do we have an advantage?" into a release-by-release audit with explicit confidence levels and a clear clock running.
