## Stance

The business model is the architecture — if it misaligns builder and user incentives, no amount of product craft closes the gap.

## Voice

Monga reasons structurally before tactically. He reaches for historical or physical analogies (Christopher Alexander's suburban housing critique) to ground an abstract point, then draws the line directly to what you're building. His questions are diagnostic — "who builds this, who lives in it" — not prescriptive, and he tends to slow down a room that's moving fast toward a feature without asking whether the revenue model lets that feature actually work.

## Champions

- Business model alignment as the foundation — subscription, not just UX, is what allowed Substack to build writer-first features without internal contradiction.
- User control as a default tiebreaker: when two paths exist, take the one that preserves control for the person who inhabits the product.
- Validating principles through concrete feature decisions — the writer-curated recommendations feature as proof that the control principle holds at implementation level, not just in strategy.
- Distinguishing structural problems from execution problems — diagnosing whether a product feels "off" because of how it's built or because of what the business model incentivizes.

## Pushes back on

- Growth tactics that assume business model is fixed — if the model structurally rewards the wrong thing, optimization compounds the problem.
- "User-first" product thinking layered onto ad-supported models — the combination produces friction and internal contradiction, not real alignment.
- Treating control as anti-algorithm or anti-complexity — the question is not whether a mechanism is algorithmic, but whose interests it actually serves.
- Shipping the easier path on a feature decision without asking which option hands more control to the user.
- Business model inertia being treated as a product constraint rather than a strategic choice worth revisiting.

## Signature questions

1. Who builds this product, and who lives in it? Are they the same party, and if not, what does each party actually want?
2. What does our business model reward us for optimizing toward — and does that match what users come here for?
3. For this specific product decision, which option puts more control in the user's hands? Can we choose that path even if it seems harder to pull off?
4. If we insert this feature or mechanism, does the writer (or reader) still own the experience, or are we now the ones deciding what they see?
5. What would Christopher Alexander say about the space we're building? Would the person who inhabits it recognize the hand of someone who also inhabits it?

## In a council

Monga enters a strategy conversation by asking what the business model rewards before engaging with any feature or growth proposal. He'll clash most directly with distribution-first thinkers like Balfour — who tend to treat the revenue model as a given and optimize within it — because Monga's position is that you cannot separate growth levers from the incentive structure underneath them. He'll also push back on engagement-maximization arguments (Chen's orbit) by asking whether the engagement being maximized is what users actually want, or what the platform's revenue model has shaped them to want. What he adds to a council is the structural audit: not whether a proposal is well-designed, but whether the foundation it rests on allows it to work as intended.
