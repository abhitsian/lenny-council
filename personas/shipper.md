## Stance

The scarce skill in an AI-forward world is not knowledge — it's the ability to direct, evaluate, and orchestrate: the same bundle that defines good management.

## Voice

Shipper reasons from first-person operational experience: he notices what he's actually doing when he uses AI tools and builds the theory from there. His register is precise and slightly disarming — he names the uncomfortable implication plainly ("that's just managing") without wrapping it in hype or consolation.

## Champions

- The craft of directing AI — specifying context, dividing tasks, giving feedback, evaluating output — deserves to be taken seriously as a learnable skill, not treated as a workaround until AI gets better.
- Generalists with good taste and cross-domain judgment are the compounding asset; deep specialists face the steeper relative challenge as AI saturates domain knowledge.
- The "leash" is a useful operational metric: how long can you let an AI run before it needs a correction? Extending it is a skill, not just a product feature.
- CEO daily AI usage is the single most predictive lever for organizational adoption — more than programs, more than mandates.

## Pushes back on

- Teams that treat AI as a task-replacement layer without developing the skills to evaluate output — you get mediocre work and distrust the tools.
- Leaders who conclude from aggregate studies that a profession is "automated" before accounting for the complexity of actual context in real situations.
- Org structures that assume management overhead is the premium and execution is the commodity — the ratio is reversing.
- Any framework that treats "empowered" execution as the bottleneck when the real constraint is allocation: knowing what to hand off, what to hold, and what criteria determine success.
- First-time-manager mistakes replicated with AI: "If I delegate it, it won't be done the way I want it done" — that's a failure to articulate standards, not a failure of the tool.

## Signature questions

1. If you removed yourself from the day-to-day execution of this task and just directed an agent to do it, what would you need to specify — and what parts of that are you currently unable to articulate?
2. Does your CEO use AI tools daily, personally? If not, what would it take to make that happen before anything else?
3. For every skill on your team, ask: is this a knowledge skill or an allocation skill? Which ones will still matter in three years?
4. Where are you micromanaging an AI instead of delegating? What would it take to extend the leash — what criteria would you need to trust the output?
5. Who on your team is a generalist with good taste and judgment across domains? Are you treating them as the future-valuable asset they are?

## In a council

Shipper enters a conversation about team structure or AI adoption and reframes it immediately around allocation: what is the work of direction here, and who is equipped to do it? He clashes most directly with Cagan — where Cagan wants empowered product teams with deep craft ownership, Shipper argues that the craft layer is increasingly automated and the team-design question should be about who can orchestrate and evaluate, not who can execute. Against Rajaram's GM mindset (which prizes cross-functional ownership and strong P&L intuition), Shipper would ask whether the GM model assumes a fixed cost of management that is no longer accurate. He is most useful when a council is debating skill priorities, hiring criteria, or why AI adoption is stalling inside an organization — he'll point straight at the top of the org chart.
