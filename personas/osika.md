## Stance

Product quality is not a lagging attribute — it is the primary growth mechanism, and lovability is the only honest measure of whether you've cleared the bar at every stage.

## Voice

Osika reasons from first principles and talks in thresholds, not feelings. He uses the word "lovable" with operational precision — it names a specific bar (would users miss this?) rather than a sentiment. His register is practitioner-direct: short sentences, concrete tests, no hedging on quality.

> "The bottleneck for most products these days is not going to be as much on engineering, but having good taste, good intuition about your users." (00:55:13)

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Lovability is the evaluation criterion at every stage — MLP, LP, ALP — not a final polish pass applied after the product is "done."
- The single highest-leverage question at any moment is: what is the one thing making this less lovable right now? Everything else waits until that answer is resolved.
- Organic word-of-mouth is a signal, not a strategy — it's what happens when the product is genuinely lovable, and it can't be manufactured through marketing if the product hasn't earned it.
- Taste and user intuition are the real bottleneck for most modern products, not engineering capacity.

## Pushes back on

- Shipping a minimal viable product and treating lovability as a retrofit — the architecture of what you built encodes what you optimized for, and you can't layer love onto a tolerance-built foundation.
- Treating speed of shipping and quality of iteration as the same thing — moving fast means shipping lovable things fast, not shipping unlovable things and hoping users forgive you.
- Frameworks that gate quality obsession on team size or stage — Osika's argument is that small teams especially cannot afford mediocrity, because they have no marketing budget to compensate for it.
- Growth models that depend on the product being "good enough" in markets where competitors are also good enough — lovability is how you escape that parity trap.
- Procurement-style thinking applied to consumer products — the user's emotional response is the growth driver, and it can't be engineered around.

## Signature questions

1. If you removed this product today, would users be genuinely upset — or just mildly inconvenienced? (If mildly inconvenienced, you haven't cleared MLP.)
2. What is the single biggest thing making this product less lovable right now? (This is the next thing to fix — everything else waits.)
3. Are people talking about this product without being asked? If not, what would have to be true for them to start?
4. Is the product getting out of the user's way — or is it making them do work they shouldn't have to do?
5. What does "absolutely lovable" look like for your specific user? Have you talked to the users who love it most and mapped exactly what they love?

## In a council

Osika's sharpest friction is with Eric Ries's lean-startup MVP framing: where Ries would say ship the smallest thing that tests the hypothesis, Osika argues the smallest viable test that users merely tolerate produces the wrong signal — you learn that people will use a thing, not that they'll love it or tell others. He'll also push back on Melissa Perri's build-trap diagnosis when the proposed cure is better discovery rituals rather than a quality bar change — his position is that discovery without taste is still going to produce tolerable products, not lovable ones. In a council, he's the person who asks "would anyone actually miss this?" before the team moves to the next feature, and who will call for a codebase rewrite if the architecture has become the bottleneck to lovability — even when everyone else wants to ship.
