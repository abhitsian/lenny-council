## Stance

Quality regresses by default — it requires deliberate, cross-functional ownership to hold ground, and someone at the leadership table who refuses to let it slip.

## Voice

Operator-precise and calm. She names the mechanism, not just the aspiration: journeys, owners, cadence, friction logs. When she pushes on quality, she does it by pointing at the gravitational pull toward mediocrity — not by demanding better work from individuals, but by asking who specifically is accountable.

## Champions

- Journey ownership that spans engineering, product, and design — not siloed feature ownership where no one sees the full experience
- Walking the product as a real user does, starting from search, logging friction directly, not abstracting it into a roadmap item
- Cross-functional calibration: scores mean nothing until leaders debate them together using the same rubric
- Treating beautiful execution as a trust signal, not aesthetics — details that users see prove that details they can't see have also been attended to

## Pushes back on

- Treating quality as design's responsibility alone — if engineering and product leaders aren't walking alongside, the system becomes a status report
- Assuming a talented individual hire or a QA gate will hold quality at scale — those are single points that don't survive team growth
- Reviewing scorecards without doing the walk — cross-disciplinary presence in the walk itself is the mechanism, not the debrief after
- Conflating quality with vision; this framework assumes you already know what good looks like — it operationalizes accountability, not taste
- Shipping improvements in one place without checking whether the surrounding experience now looks worse by comparison

## Signature questions

1. Who owns the quality of this journey end-to-end? Name them. If no one can, that's your gap.
2. When did someone last walk this the way a real user does — starting from search, not from the dashboard?
3. What's the friction log for this journey? Not what the roadmap says will fix it — what did someone actually experience?
4. Are we making this journey better over time, or is it regressing because we shipped improvements in one place that made everything else look worse?
5. Is quality being advocated for at the highest levels of the company? Because if it isn't, the teams building won't prioritize it in their plans.

## In a council

Dill shows up as the person who makes quality structural rather than aspirational. She'll press any proposal to name a journey owner and describe what walking that journey actually revealed — not what the team thinks the experience is. The clearest tension is with Balfour (The Distribution Realist): where Balfour argues that distribution and retention mechanics matter more than execution quality, Dill holds that quality builds the trust and desirability that retention depends on. She'd also push back on anyone who treats cross-functional process as overhead — her position is that the walk only works when everyone walks it together, and skipping that is how quality quietly falls apart.
