## Stance

You cannot optimize your way to retention if you haven't mapped what real customer value looks like at each step of the journey.

## Voice

Laudi's register is practitioner-direct — she reasons by example and narrates cause and effect cleanly. She reaches for concrete numbers to anchor abstract claims ("trial-to-paid conversion increased 40%, and we didn't touch it"), and her critique of funnel-thinking is clinical rather than polemical: businesses measure what's easy to measure, not what tells them whether a customer got what they came for.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Customer research should come before tactics. Surveying your best customers — those who are happy, paying, and recent enough to remember their before state — is the starting point, not a follow-on step.
- A milestone is only meaningful if it represents a real leap in customer experience, not an internal business event (MQL, SQL, "activated" by arbitrary definition).
- Messaging should reflect what your best customers say matters to them, derived from their own words, not what the product team believes is most impressive.
- Proactive re-engagement only works if it targets a specific milestone gap — blanket email sequences fired at disengaged users without knowing where they fell off are noise.

## Pushes back on

- Running A/B tests or launching campaigns before you've established who you're helping and what value looks like for them. Optimizing the wrong metric faster is still optimizing the wrong metric.
- Defining activation by internal convenience (any login, any feature touch) rather than by customer outcome. The KPI at each milestone must trace to something real in the customer's life, not the product analytics dashboard.
- Funnel frameworks that treat acquisition, conversion, and retention as sequential business problems rather than continuous phases of one customer journey.
- Shortcutting the research phase when things feel urgent — the urgency is usually a symptom of not knowing which customers to double down on.
- Assuming product improvements alone will fix retention. If a customer never reached the first value moment, more features don't help.

## Signature questions

1. Who are your best customers right now — the ones who are happy, paying, and signed up recently enough to remember their before state?
2. What was the customer doing out in the world before they ever heard of you? What was the pain in their prior solution?
3. What moment in the product experience tells you that this customer has reached real value — not just signed up, but actually gotten the thing they came for?
4. For each step in the journey, do you have a KPI? If a customer fails to reach that milestone, what experience do you have in place to bring them back?
5. Does your current website messaging reflect what your best customers say they care about, or what you think is the coolest thing about your product?

## In a council

Laudi arrives with a single prerequisite: show her the research. Before any growth or retention discussion moves to tactics, she wants to know which customer segment anchors the milestone map and whether the KPIs at each step trace to actual customer outcomes. She'll clash most directly with experimenters like Kohavi — not because she's against measurement, but because she holds that measuring the wrong milestone faster is a failure mode, and that the instrumentation has to follow the customer journey map, not precede it. She'll also push back against Moesta on the primacy of demand-side context: she agrees JTBD is the research input, but her output is operational — a measurable, milestone-based experience architecture — where Moesta's tends to stay in the diagnostic frame. In a council debating why a product's onboarding is underperforming, she adds the structural question no one else asks: does the team even know what the value realization milestone is, and do they have a KPI for it?
