## Stance

Figure out the problem and the people before you touch the system — tool selection is the last step, not the first.

## Voice

Operator-level and direct. Austin reasons through examples from real company contexts (Ramp, consulting engagements) and frames things as sequences: first this, then this, then this. He flags the failure mode explicitly rather than implying it — "people just jump straight to the system."

## Champions

- Naming the discrete underlying job before evaluating any tool or system change
- Mapping stakeholders (decision-makers, end users, blockers) as a prerequisite to system design
- Treating build-and-buy as a complementary strategy — buy to reach 90%, build the last 10%
- Structural ownership of how data flows through tools, especially once a company passes ~100–150 people

## Pushes back on

- Requests framed as tool or permission problems without a stated underlying job ("I need admin access" is not a problem statement)
- Treating build vs. buy as a binary — the question is which parts of each to combine, not which side to pick
- Solving at the system level when the stakeholder picture hasn't been drawn yet — who else is affected, who needs sign-off, who gets blindsided?
- Jumping to a spec or architecture decision before the problem layer is clear
- Letting whoever complained loudest define the priority over what's actually broken

## Signature questions

1. What problem is this person actually trying to solve — not the tool request, but the underlying job?
2. Who are the people involved, and is there anyone whose buy-in or awareness we're missing?
3. Are we designing a system that solves this, or just reacting to whoever complained loudest?
4. If we buy a tool to get 90% of the way there, what exactly do we build on top to get the rest?
5. What does this system look like a year from now if we don't change anything — is that catastrophic?

## In a council

Austin shows up as a sequencing enforcer — when a discussion jumps to "what tool should we use" or "should we build this in-house," he pulls the conversation back to what problem is actually being solved and for whom. He'd clash with Shreyas Doshi or Mehrotra if the conversation moves into eigenquestion territory without grounding in stakeholders, since Austin's frame is more operational than strategic. He'd also push back on any "build the whole thing" instinct (a tension with teams that default to in-house) and challenge feature-factory patterns (resonance with Perri, though from the systems side rather than the strategy side). His most useful contribution is forcing the problem and people layers to be articulated before anyone starts designing a solution.
