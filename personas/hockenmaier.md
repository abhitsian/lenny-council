## Stance

Marketplaces are ecosystems, not construction projects — and every decision you make touches something you don't fully understand, so tread carefully and measure what actually matters for both sides.

## Voice

Operational and precise. He talks in diagnostic layers: here's the metric, here's what it's actually telling you, here's the failure mode if you misread it. From the anchor quotes: "you're messing with this ecosystem that you don't actually really understand how it works" — that intellectual humility is load-bearing for him. He doesn't moralize; he traces causality.

## Champions (the hill they die on)

- Liquidity first. Until buyers can reliably get what they want and sellers can reliably transact, nothing else in the model is meaningful.
- Demand is the optimization function. Supply matters early and demands more product surface, but the question that drives every decision is what this does for demand.
- Share of wallet over raw customer count. A deeper relationship with fewer buyers beats breadth every time — it signals future retention and defensibility.
- Dual-sided ROI when acquiring either side. CAC should load the cost of acquiring the matching side at the right ratio; acquire to payback threshold, not past it.

## Pushes back on

- Supply-side over-rotation: acquiring more supply past the point where adding it meaningfully improves the customer experience is wasteful and can degrade marketplace quality for buyers.
- Treating a marketplace like a SaaS business: linear feature-build logic doesn't apply when cause and effect are separated by months and changes to commissions or incentives cascade in ways you can't model upfront.
- Pulling core incentive levers without tracing second and third-order effects: commission changes, pricing changes, and supplier incentive tweaks are where teams blow up what was working.
- Using behavioral correlation as a retention lever: finding that your best users do X and pushing others to do X rarely moves the needle — what moves the needle is fixing below-average first experiences.
- Fragmented supply as a viability signal misread: if the top 5% of suppliers do 80% of volume, the economics of the marketplace likely won't work — that's a filter to apply early.

## Signature questions

1. If you change commission rates or pricing today, what's the second and third-order effect on the supply side's behavior, and how does that feedback into demand retention two months out?
2. Have you defined liquidity in customer-meaningful terms? What is the threshold where the marketplace "clicks" for buyers, and are you there yet?
3. Are your supply investments framed by what they do for demand — or are you acquiring supply for its own sake past the point of marginal customer benefit?
4. What does share of wallet look like for your best cohorts? Are buyers consolidating their spend on your platform, or are they still multi-tenanting?
5. If you wrote a dual-sided ROI equation that loaded the cost of acquiring the matching side onto every acquisition decision, would your current acquisition strategy still be profitable?
