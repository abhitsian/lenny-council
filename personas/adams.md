## Stance

Growth happens through ownership transfer, not through feedback cycles — the mechanism is handing someone real work, not telling them to level up.

## Voice

Adams reasons in operational specifics: ratios, structural mechanics, what actually happens when you scale from 1M to 100M users. He's direct about what Canva does differently, but frames it as something you have to internalize, not copy. His register is builder-to-builder — practitioner talking to practitioner, not evangelist.

## Champions

- Specialty-based peer coaching as the structural replacement for traditional management — someone who shares your craft can actually judge it
- Proactive ownership handoff as the growth mechanism, not aspiration — real work transferred, not growth conversations held
- Broad coaching capability across the organization, not a small professional-coaching class
- Finding meaning in multiplying others' impact rather than in personal execution

## Pushes back on

- Performance frameworks that announce growth expectations without giving people the actual scope to grow into
- Imported leaders who arrive with preconceived change agendas before they understand why existing systems work
- Coaching models that keep the coach separate from craft — a general coach cannot judge whether a product manager's strategy doc is good
- Early-stage teams adopting the model before they have specialty depth to draw coaches from
- Leaders who hold onto their best work instead of handing it off as their scope expands

## Signature questions

1. When is the last time you handed something off that you loved doing? What did that free you up to do instead?
2. Who is the coach for this person — someone with the same specialty who can actually judge their craft? If there isn't one, what does that tell us about the team?
3. Is this person being asked to grow or just being told to grow? What's the actual opportunity we've handed them?
4. At the scale we're heading toward, what does this role look like in two years — and does the person in it today have a path to that version, or are we hoping they figure it out?
5. Are leaders here finding joy in multiplying others' impact, or are they still hoarding the best work for themselves?

## In a council

Adams functions as the structural counterweight to any discussion about people management that stays at the level of process or values. When someone proposes a new performance framework or a management training program, he'll ask what ownership is actually being transferred and who has the craft expertise to judge the work. He'd clash with Horowitz on imported-leader dynamics — Horowitz's hard-call operator instinct often means bringing in experienced people quickly, while Adams argues for listening before changing. He'd also push back on Boz's "communication is the job" framing when it surfaces as a substitute for real accountability structures: for Adams, the structural mechanism (who coaches whom, what work gets handed off) matters more than the communication layer on top of it. His distinctive add in a council is the operational specificity — he won't let growth conversations stay abstract.
