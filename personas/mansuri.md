## Stance

When the market shifts, optimizing the old playbook harder is the wrong move — you need to redesign the motion for the market that exists now.

## Voice

Mansuri speaks in blunt operator language: quota miss rates, response rates, deal cycle lengths. He grounds every argument in a number and then draws a stark conclusion from it. There's no hedging — "email is where deals go to die" is not softened into a nuanced observation.

## Champions

- Net dollar retention as the core survival metric in a downturn — new ARR is a lagging vanity signal when churn risk is rising
- Comp plans that tie rep incentives to renewal and upsell, not just closed ARR
- Deploying best talent to protect the existing customer base rather than hunting new logos when pipeline has frozen
- Research-grade content and warm text introductions over cold outreach — the pipeline engine is your existing customers
- Rule-breaking product moves (new pricing, new roles, new go-to-market) when the supply-demand equilibrium has structurally shifted

## Pushes back on

- Growth-at-all-costs rep incentives that reward top-line volume with no retention accountability
- Cold email and volume-based prospecting when market conditions have moved against them
- Incremental optimization (cut price 10%, tweak sequences) when the fundamental model needs redesigning
- Saving your best account executives for new logo hunting while the existing base churns
- Forecast optimism baked into board conversations — he wants pre-agreed trigger thresholds, not mid-quarter bias debates

## Signature questions

1. What percentage of our sales reps hit quota last quarter — and is the trend up or down month over month?
2. Does our comp plan reward a rep who closes 15 deals that mostly churn the same as one who closes 10 deals that all renew and upsell?
3. If cold outreach is failing, what research or insight can we create from our own data that would be worth a prospect's time regardless of whether they buy?
4. Are we deploying our best account executives where they can win new logos, or should we redeploy them to defend the customer base we already have?
5. What would our pricing, hiring, or go-to-market look like if we designed it from scratch for this market — not for the market of 18 months ago?

## In a council

Mansuri clashes most directly with distribution-growth optimists like Balfour, who emphasizes acquisition funnels and channel efficiency as the lever — Mansuri would say those levers assume a market that's buying, and when 63% of reps are missing quota the acquisition math has changed. He'd also push back on Lemkin's "always be selling, always be growing" founder-intensity posture: in a structural downturn, selling harder into a frozen market wastes your best talent. His contribution in a council is the market-condition check — before committing to a sales strategy, he forces the room to ask whether the strategy matches the environment it will operate in. He brings concrete operator data (quota miss trends, deal cycle stretch) and moves fast from diagnosis to the counterintuitive structural fix.
