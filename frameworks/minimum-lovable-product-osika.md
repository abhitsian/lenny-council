## One-liner

Replace the MVP mindset with a lovability ladder: ship a Minimum Lovable Product first, then a Lovable Product, then an Absolutely Lovable Product — where lovability, not viability, is the measure of progress at every stage.

## The framework

Osika runs product development through three ascending stages, each with a distinct quality threshold:

1. **Minimum Lovable Product (MLP)** — the smallest version that people genuinely want to use, not just tolerate. The bar is love, not bare functionality. If users wouldn't miss it, it hasn't cleared MLP.

2. **Lovable Product (LP)** — the product works reliably end-to-end, gets out of users' way, and feels polished. Bugs and rough edges are removed. This is the stage where organic word-of-mouth becomes a real growth driver.

3. **Absolutely Lovable Product (ALP)** — the product is so good it creates pull. Users recommend it, talk about it, and come back without being prompted. This is the state the company name itself encodes as the north star.

The progression is not a checklist — it's a continuous quality obsession. At each stage, the team identifies the biggest bottleneck (what is making this less lovable than it should be), solves it completely, then moves to the next. Speed of iteration matters: weekly planning, weekly demos, and a bias toward shipping over speccing.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are building a consumer or prosumer product where emotional resonance drives retention and word-of-mouth
- Your growth model depends on organic spread — the product has to be worth talking about
- You have a small team and can't afford to ship mediocrity that requires expensive marketing to compensate
- You are in a market where competitors are shipping "good enough" — lovability becomes the differentiation

**Does not apply when:**
- You are selling into a procurement process where buyers don't use the product themselves — lovability of the end-user doesn't close the deal
- You are in a regulated category where compliance gates shipping, making rapid quality iteration hard
- Your product is infrastructure — developers use it because it works, not because it's lovable

## Signature questions

1. If you removed this product today, would users be genuinely upset — or just mildly inconvenienced? (If mildly inconvenienced, you haven't cleared MLP.)
2. What is the single biggest thing making this product less lovable right now? (This is the next thing to fix — everything else waits.)
3. Are people talking about this product without being asked? If not, what would have to be true for them to start?
4. Is the product getting out of the user's way — or is it making them do work they shouldn't have to do?
5. What does "absolutely lovable" look like for your specific user? Have you talked to the users who love it most and mapped exactly what they love?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Osika explicitly warns against is treating lovability as a final polish pass — something you add after the product is "done." He frames it as the evaluation criterion at every stage, including the earliest. Shipping a minimal viable product and then retroactively trying to make it lovable almost never works; the architecture of what you built encodes what you optimized for.

A related misuse: confusing speed of shipping with iteration quality. Lovable shipped fast — but the team also paused new features entirely to rewrite their codebase when the architecture became the bottleneck. Moving fast does not mean shipping things that aren't lovable and hoping users forgive you. The bottleneck question ("what is making this less lovable right now?") has to be answered honestly before you ship, not after.
