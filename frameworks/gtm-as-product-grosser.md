## One-liner

The buying experience itself is a differentiator — when products converge, how you sell becomes as important as what you sell, and that requires treating GTM as a designed product with a unified segmentation layer across every revenue-touching function.

## The framework

Grosser's GTM-as-Product framework has three interlocking components:

**1. Buyer journey as designed experience**
Map the customer lifecycle from first awareness to multi-year retention as a sequence of deliberate experiences — not a funnel of transactions. At each touchpoint ask: does this feel human and personalized, or transactional and flat? The example from Stripe: replace a standard discovery call (interrogating the prospect) with a whiteboarding session where the customer co-creates their own payment architecture diagram. They leave with an asset; you leave with deep contextual knowledge.

**2. Unified segmentation**
GTM orgs default to a Venn diagram where marketing, sales, CS, and support each operate with slightly different segmentation frameworks that overlap but don't align. The fix: define one shared segmentation model and own it as a company-wide asset, not a GTM-only one. Grosser delivers the segmentation framework herself in every new hire's first week at Vercel as part of KYC (Know Your Customer).

The segmentation model itself is multi-axis:
- Size (SMB / mid-market / enterprise) — baseline buying complexity
- Growth potential — matters especially in consumption-based models (a company growing 200% YoY is worth more sales investment than one growing 8%)
- Business model (B2B, B2C, marketplace, platform) — dictates which product features are relevant
- Observable signals (e.g. Crux/traffic rank at Vercel, transaction volume at Stripe) — push a company into a higher segment when their footprint demands it even if headcount doesn't

**3. Consultative GTM, driven by risk-avoidance psychology**
80% of buyers purchase to avoid pain or reduce risk — not to capture upside. GTM messaging and sales motion should center on "here's the risk you're running" and "here's what happens if you don't act," not "here's the art of the possible." The consultative shift is structural: move from transactional selling to acting as a thinking partner who helps the customer understand their own architecture, gaps, and competitive exposure.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Products in a space where technical differentiation is narrowing or competitors are proliferating
- The sales cycle is long enough that multiple touchpoints exist to design
- The company is past founder-led sales and has a repeatable process to encode
- There is a consumption-based or usage-based revenue model where customer growth directly compounds your revenue

**Does not apply when:**
- You are selling a single eight-figure enterprise product from day one and every deal is fully custom (e.g. Sierra-type AI enterprise) — PLG and "buyer journey design" are lower leverage than executive relationships and procurement navigation
- You haven't yet documented what a repeatable sale looks like; you can't design an experience around a process that doesn't exist yet

## Signature questions

1. At each step of your buyer journey — first touch to five-year customer — does the buyer leave with something of value, or just a checkbox completed?
2. Do your marketing, sales, CS, and support orgs all segment customers the same way? If not, where does the Venn diagram break down and what does that cost you?
3. What is the actual risk your buyer is trying to avoid? Are you selling to that, or are you still selling the art of the possible?
4. If I put your top account executive in front of 10 engineers at your company, how long before they figure out she isn't a product manager?
5. Where in your sales process are you being transactional when you could be consultative — and what asset could the buyer walk away with from that moment instead?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Grosser explicitly warns against is treating segmentation as a GTM tool rather than a company-wide framework. When product, engineering, and GTM operate with different segmentation models, product builds for one audience, sales sells to a different one, and CS retains a third. The fix isn't a better CRM view — it's making segmentation a company value (Vercel calls it KYC: Know Your Customer) and having GTM leadership deliver it personally to every new hire. Skipping that unification step means all the experience design on top of it is built on a misaligned foundation.
