## One-liner

Organizational clarity — knowing exactly what problem you're solving and ensuring resources actually reflect your stated priorities — is more decisive than being right.

## The framework

Tomer Cohen organizes this around two equal parts:

**1. Clarity of thought**
Define the problem at the level of specificity where you can almost see the solution. Not "build a video product" but "short-form video for this audience, with these unique criteria, trading off these vectors." Once the problem is that crisp, you can see the mountain peak and the base camp — the middle stays blurry, but the destination is unambiguous.

Solutions built on first-principles opinions beat generic best practices. The test: does your principle have a trade-off? "We should build a simple product" is useless — everyone says that. "I'm willing to sacrifice X to make it simple" is an actual opinion worth debating.

**2. Clarity of execution**
Stated priorities must show up in resource allocation. If your engineers are all on a migration, the migration is the priority — call it that. When top talent is assigned to moonshots that aren't the stated number-one priority, the confusion lives in execution, not strategy.

**The minus-one-to-one transformation pattern**
For turnaround products, Cohen adds a third move: carve out a small cohort (he used two million LinkedIn members), run the new experience in isolation, prove behavior change with evidence, then bring it to the full product. This sidesteps the organizational immune response that fires whenever experiments touch everyone's metrics.

**The mountain metaphor**
Set the peak clearly and ambitiously — start from market potential, not today's numbers. Share the peak and the base camp. The middle will resolve as you climb. Never set the goal from the existing baseline.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A team has alpha-type members who hedge after a decision is made, running parallel bets against the agreed direction
- An organization has declared a top priority that isn't reflected in where engineers actually spend time
- You're running a turnaround product where entrenched metrics and flows make big experiments politically untenable
- AI systems are core to the product and no product leader owns the algorithm's objective

**Doesn't apply when:**
- The problem genuinely isn't understood yet — pre-clarity, you need exploration, not conviction
- The decision is truly reversible and low-stakes (no need for full alignment on every call)
- You're in a diverge phase (Cohen explicitly ran a weeks-long diverge period before converging on AI bets)

## Signature questions

1. "What is the exact problem you're trying to solve — not 'launch a video product,' but for which audience, with what unique criteria, and what are you willing to trade off?"
2. "If I look at where your engineers are spending their time right now, does it match what you told me was your top priority?"
3. "What is the objective of the algorithm — can you write it on a board for me?"
4. "Are you disagreeing with me or misunderstanding me? If you're misunderstanding, let's spend time until you can articulate my point of view in your own words. If you're disagreeing, let's stop pretending it's a clarification question."
5. "If you're successful, what change would happen in the world?"

## Common misuse

Cohen explicitly warns against using the framework as permission to stay vague about your actual opinion. "Saying that we should build a simple product for me is useless. Who doesn't want to build a simple product? But saying that I'm willing to sacrifice or trade off this — that's where I get excited." The failure mode is declaring clarity while holding a position with no teeth: no trade-off named, no contrarian stake, just consensus language dressed up as conviction.

A second misuse: treating clarity as a reason to skip the diverge phase when exploring genuinely new technology. Cohen ran weeks of open-ended AI exploration before converging. Clarity of execution comes after exploration, not instead of it.
