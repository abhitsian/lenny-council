## One-liner

Start with a concrete outcome you want to prove you've learned, work backward to identify the right questions and frameworks, find the best practitioners to answer those questions, apply the learning in context, then circle back with results — and repeat.

## The framework

Walter's approach has five repeating steps:

1. **Name the outcome.** Pick a concrete, measurable result that will prove you've learned the skill. Not "understand growth" but "ship experiments that move activation by X% in six months."

2. **Work backward to questions.** Ask: what frameworks, best practices, and examples would I need to answer in order to drive that outcome? Read just enough to make your questions sharp, not to learn the whole domain.

3. **Find the best practitioner.** Identify who has done this most rigorously — company, team, or person. Reach out with the smallest possible ask: a specific question answerable in two minutes via email, not a request for a meeting.

4. **Apply in context.** Run the thing. Pull in researcher, data, experiments. Do the actual work toward the outcome.

5. **Close the loop.** Go back to the mentor. Show what happened — worked or didn't. Use that as the launch point for the next question and the next iteration.

Two skill categories frame where to apply the model:

- **IQ skills** (execution, product sense, strategy, interviewing) — learnable faster, more overlap across people, typically first-priority early career.
- **EQ skills** (communication, leadership, management, listening, self-awareness) — harder to learn, highly personal, best addressed one at a time over longer horizons (three to six months per skill), require a mirror from someone who will be honest.

For EQ specifically, the loop depends on building trust deep enough that people give you the subjective feedback — how you *come across*, not just what you did.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're entering a domain you know nothing about and need to get useful fast (Walter's situation joining Slack as a growth PM with no growth experience).
- You have a specific outcome on the line — a job, a team metric, a promotion.
- You have access to practitioners, even loosely through events, intros, or cold outreach.

**Doesn't apply when:**
- The skill is so contextual or personal that no external framework exists (deep EQ patterns like "how stress affects you" require coaching and years, not a six-month outcome loop).
- You're in an environment without visible high-quality artifacts to reverse-engineer — the model depends partly on osmosis from strong practitioners and their work.

## Signature questions

1. What is the concrete outcome that would prove you've learned this skill — not just read about it?
2. What questions, if answered, would get you to that outcome? Have you narrowed those questions enough that a practitioner could answer one in two minutes?
3. Who has done this most rigorously, at what company? Can you get a small foot in the door with them?
4. After you tried the thing, did you go back to the mentor and close the loop — or did you move on?
5. For EQ: what's something people keep telling you you're good at, but you yourself think is not a big deal? That's likely your core strength — and it has a shadow side you need to understand.

## Common misuse

Walter explicitly warns against treating learning as consumption — reading articles, watching talks, processing content — and calling that skill-building. The failure mode is mistaking input for practice. The model only works if you apply it toward a real outcome and close the feedback loop. People who skip the "apply in context" and "circle back" steps get the mental models without the muscle. For EQ skills specifically, the second common misuse is working on too many things at once: the model requires going all-in on one skill at a time, for months, with deliberate weekly practice.
