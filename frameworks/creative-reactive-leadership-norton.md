## One-liner

Product leaders who get stuck aren't missing frameworks — they're operating from fear and external approval rather than from internal purpose, and no new tactic fixes that.

## The framework

The framework draws on Anderson & Adams's Leadership Circle research, which identifies two modes of operating:

**Reactive leadership** — responding to the world from fear. You see threats and problems, want to be right, want to be liked, or want to control. Three postures:
1. **Complying** — seeking approval, needing to be liked, meeting everyone's expectations. Effective early on; costs you power and decisiveness as you get more senior.
2. **Protecting** — needing to be right, retreating into your own ideas, critical of others.
3. **Controlling** — my-way-or-the-highway, needing dominance and winning.

**Creative leadership** — responding from openness, curiosity, passion, purpose, and growth.

The research finding: 75% of leaders are primarily operating reactively. Creative leadership correlates positively with every measurable dimension of success (revenue, brand, profitability, team capability). Reactive leadership correlates negatively.

The shift is not a skill upgrade. Norton calls it redesigning your internal operating system — interrogating the underlying belief systems that keep you locked in reactive postures. He uses a driving metaphor: when you move into a leadership role, the complexity doesn't just increase, your place in the world shifts such that the self-complexity required demands a complete reboot of how you make meaning.

**How the unlock actually works:**
- Identify which reactive posture you default to
- Surface the underlying belief driving it (e.g., "the only way to be decisive is to be domineering")
- Challenge whether that belief is actually true
- Redefine the underlying value in a creative direction (e.g., from "I need people to like me" to "I want to earn long-term admiration")
- Use coaching, 360 assessments, and values work to see when the creative mode works — the dopamine hit of seeing it perform is part of the rewiring

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** A leader has mastered the skills and frameworks, keeps hitting walls anyway, and the stuckness has a "what got me here won't get me there" quality. Also applies when leaders report burnout, existential uncertainty about whether they still love the work, or a pattern of giving away so much power that execution and decisiveness suffer.

**Does not apply when:** Someone is still early in their career and genuinely needs the tutorial — frameworks, tactics, how to hold the controller. Asking "what's important to you?" when someone needs to learn how to run a sprint is the wrong intervention. Mentorship and advice fit that stage better. The framework is for people who have already learned and mastered the tools.

## Signature questions

1. What are you optimizing for in your leadership right now — and is that driven by what the world expects of you, or by what matters to you internally?
2. Which of the three reactive postures (complying, protecting, controlling) do you recognize yourself defaulting to when under pressure?
3. What is the underlying belief that's keeping you in that posture? Is the belief actually true?
4. What would it look like to keep the underlying value (care for people, rigor, drive) but express it from a place of purpose rather than fear?
5. When you imagine yourself operating creatively at your best, what becomes possible that isn't possible right now?

## Common misuse

Norton explicitly warns against treating this as a skills swap — going from one reactive posture to another. His own example: he was a complier (approval-seeking), and people told him to push back more. His only mental model for pushing back was the controlling autocrat. He rejected that too, because it's equally reactive. The trap is believing your only alternative to a reactive style is a different reactive style. The actual move is finding how your underlying value (e.g., care for people) can be expressed creatively rather than replaced by something incompatible. Coaching that just tells you to "be more assertive" without interrogating the belief system underneath will produce that same mistake.
