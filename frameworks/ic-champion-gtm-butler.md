## One-liner

A two-part bottoms-up motion: first get individual contributor practitioners to deeply love the product, then give them the conditions and support to spread it through their organization until it becomes a revenue event.

## The framework

**Part 1 — Get ICs to love you**

1. **Credibility first.** Technical practitioners have a high bullshit meter. Don't market; produce content they could not have written themselves. Hire from within the user base (designer advocates, developer advocates) — practitioners talking to practitioners carries weight that marketers never will.

2. **Build with users.** In the early days, every person who tries the product is a collaborator. That means direct support from engineers, live debugging, and fixing bugs the same day they surface. Nothing scales here; do it anyway.

3. **Go where they already are.** Don't build a destination and ask people to visit it. Find the community that already exists — Twitter for Figma's designers — and engage there. Use technical content and personal handles, not brand handles, to interact.

4. **Transparency builds the relationship.** When things go wrong — downtime, acquisitions, pricing changes — say so publicly and specifically. Post-mortems, open forum sessions, and letting users see the technical rationale all compound into trust over time.

**Part 2 — Enable champions to spread it**

1. **Remove gates before the advocacy moment.** Free access to sharing (unlimited collaborators, generous free tier) must exist before someone is ready to champion the product internally. Cutting off the growth vector at three collaborators broke the model; switching to unlimited collaborators and gating on file count instead fixed it.

2. **Designer (practitioner) advocates in the sales process.** Embed a technical practitioner — someone who was a passionate user before they joined the company — into sales conversations. Never give them a quota. Their job is to speak peer-to-peer with customers. This became known internally as "the Tom Factor": deals closed at a significantly higher rate when a DA joined the call.

3. **Turn the biggest adoption blocker into a growth lever.** For Figma, design systems were the reason enterprise buyers stalled. Rather than working around that, Figma invested heavily in design-system features, content, meetups, a dedicated conference (Schema), and a whole website (DesignSystems.com). The thing blocking org-wide adoption became the reason to upgrade from Pro to Org.

4. **Maintain champion relationships permanently.** Internal champions don't disappear once a deal closes. When they're frustrated, they post publicly. Invest in making champions visible: speaking slots at Config, amplification on social, platform to grow their own careers. The champion's career advancement and Figma's platform growth reinforce each other.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Works well when:**
- The IC uses the product for a significant portion of their working day (e.g., a designer in a design tool eight hours a day).
- The target audience cares about craft and tools deeply — engineers and designers fit this profile; many knowledge workers do not.
- A community around the practice already exists somewhere (Twitter, Slack, forums) that you can show up in rather than build from scratch.
- The product is valuable to a single user before any collaboration features exist — it must be worthwhile solo.
- The IC role is naturally cross-functional, making them a natural super-spreader within their org.

**Does not fit well when:**
- The product has no single-player value (pure network-effect products like Slack are different).
- The target audience does not have deep attachment to their tools and would switch without friction.
- There is no practitioner community to tap into — you'd need to build distribution from zero.

## Signature questions

1. Does the individual contributor get meaningful value from this product before anyone else on their team uses it? If not, the bottoms-up motion has no starting point.
2. What is the one thing inside the product that a practitioner would want to talk about with peers — the technical or craft detail, not the collaboration story?
3. Who is the internal champion profile, and what does spreading this product do for their career? Are we helping them grow or just using their effort?
4. What is the biggest operational blocker to org-wide adoption, and have we leaned into that as a product investment rather than around it?
5. Are our metrics measuring signal or noise? At small numbers, can we find the handful of people who genuinely love it rather than optimizing conversion rates by five percent?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Butler names explicitly is applying standard top-down or outbound motion on top of this framework and assuming both can coexist without protecting the bottoms-up model. As the company adds a sales team, quota-driven motions, and programmatic marketing, the instinct is to let the IC-champion work atrophy because it doesn't show up cleanly in attribution. The practitioner advocates, community programs, and free-tier generosity all look expensive and unmeasurable relative to a direct sales funnel. Butler's warning: the bottoms-up motion is what built the business, and abandoning it to scale faster is how companies lose the thing that differentiated them.
