## One-liner

To get acquired on your own terms, identify 2–3 strategic buyers, find the area where you have a competitive advantage, and make it impossible for them to ignore you — while staying openly friendly throughout.

## The framework

Julia describes M&A readiness as two complementary moves:

**1. Inflict Pain (Get Noticed)**
- There are only ever 2–3 buyers who will find what you're building truly strategic. More than that is noise.
- Find the specific area where you have a competitive edge over each buyer.
- Be vocal, loud, and technically credible in exactly that area — make them feel the competitive pressure.
- Do this while staying friendly and positioning as a partner, not a threat. Avoid shutting doors prematurely.
- The goal: their ears perk up and they ask, "What's going on with this company?"

**2. Value Creation > Value Capture (Operating Philosophy)**
- Charge a fraction of the value you deliver — by design.
- At dbt Labs: customers value dbt at 20–35% of their cloud data warehouse spend. dbt charges far less than that fraction.
- This builds community, flywheel, and long-term platform position. Capturing less now accelerates the ecosystem that creates more total value later.
- Corollary: "Worse is Better" and "Tech Debt is a Champagne Problem" — ship early, learn from users, and let scale create the incentive to rebuild properly.

**M&A Timing Rule**
The time to start thinking about an M&A strategy is when you don't need one. A strong independent path gives you negotiating leverage in every conversation.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have 12–18 months of runway and time to build relationships before a formal process.
- You're a startup in a space with 2–3 obvious incumbents who should find you threatening.
- You have a genuine technical or distribution advantage you can demonstrate publicly.
- You're building an open-core or platform product and need to decide what to give away vs. charge for.

**Does not apply when:**
- You're out of runway — in that situation, be transparent and cast a wide net. "Everyone understands 'strategic alternatives' means you're looking to sell."
- You're trying to inflict pain without staying friendly — that just closes doors permanently.
- You have no real competitive advantage to make visible. Visibility without substance reads as noise, not threat.

## Signature questions

1. Of all the companies that could potentially acquire you, which 2–3 would find your specific capability truly strategic — not just interesting?
2. Where exactly are you causing competitive pain for those buyers right now? Is that pain visible to them?
3. Are you staying friendly and open with potential acquirers, or have you taken a stance that closes those doors?
4. What fraction of the value you create are you actually capturing — and is that intentional?
5. Have you planted seeds with the corp dev team, product sponsor, or GM at your target acquirers before you needed anything from them?

## Common misuse

Julia explicitly warns against two failure modes:

**Taking too competitive a stance with buyers.** Many founders shut down conversations with incumbents or potential acquirers because they want to signal independence. This eliminates option value. Inflicting pain and staying friendly are not in conflict — the playbook requires both simultaneously.

**Starting M&A conversations only when you're desperate.** By then the leverage is gone. The playbook only works when you have a viable independent path — that's what gives you power in every conversation. If you're out of time, skip the playbook entirely and be direct about your situation.
