## One-liner

Validate the problem obsessively before building anything; the problem becomes your North Star, your fundraising story, your hiring filter, and your PMF signal — all at once.

## The framework

Levine organizes the startup journey into a sequence of phases, each with one governing question. The phases are not stages on a timeline — they're modes of focus. Doing two at once means you fail at both.

**Phase 1 — Problem validation**
Start with personal frustration, then speak with 20 people you don't know. The signal is not "yes, that's interesting" — that's a non-signal. The signal is when someone interrupts to correct your framing: "No, the real problem is..." That correction is the thing you want.

**Phase 2 — Product market fit**
PMF has one metric: retention. If users come back, you created value. If they don't, you didn't. For high-frequency products, watch three-month retention; conversion usually happens on the third or fourth use. For low-frequency products (tax filing, travel), the signal takes longer and requires a different go-to-market approach.

**Phase 3 — Growth or business model (choose based on frequency)**
If usage is high-frequency, word-of-mouth growth comes naturally — prioritize growth before locking in a business model. If usage is low-frequency, you're sentenced to paid acquisition forever; figure out the business model first or you'll run out of money growing.

**Cross-cutting: the 30-day hire/fire rule**
Every time you hire someone, mark your calendar 30 days out. Ask one question: "Knowing what I know today, would I hire this person?" Yes → give more equity and buy their loyalty. No → fire immediately. The failure mode is not bad hires; it's CEOs who don't act on what they already know.

**User adoption bell curve**
Users split into: innovators (2%, use because it's new), early adopters (use because there's value), early majority (30%, afraid of change — this is the group that wins you the market), late majority (use only if forced), laggards. You win by designing for early majority, who won't try if it feels complex or risky. The solution is always simplicity.

**Fundraising: the dance of 100 nos**
Investors invest in 1% of what they see. The no is not feedback — arguing with it is like arguing after a rejected date. Start every pitch with your strongest point; the first slide stays on screen longest. Finish with your second-strongest. Stories need details to feel real — specifics create credibility.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** starting a company, choosing what to build next, evaluating team fit, pitching investors, or redesigning a product that isn't retaining users.

**Doesn't apply well when:** the product creates a new behavior category where no existing problem awareness exists (Levine acknowledges this himself, citing the iPhone). In those cases, a solution-first approach may be necessary — but he treats it as the exception, not the default.

## Signature questions

1. What is the problem you're solving — not what your product does, but why the world is worse without it?
2. If you talk to 20 people who don't know you, do they interrupt your problem description to correct it? Or do they just nod?
3. What is your retention at 30 days, 60 days, 90 days — and does that number tell you whether you created value or not?
4. Are your users high-frequency or low-frequency? Because your entire growth strategy and sequencing decision hinges on that one question.
5. Thirty days after your last hire: knowing what you know today, would you hire this person?

## Common misuse

The most common failure Levine warns against: starting with the solution and reverse-engineering a problem to justify it. Phrases like "our company is..." or "our AI system is..." signal solution-first thinking. So does pitching a product before establishing that the listener has the problem. The second failure is delaying hard people decisions. Founders who knew within a month that a hire was wrong, then waited six months to act, lost their best performers in the interim — the top people always leave environments where hard calls don't get made.
