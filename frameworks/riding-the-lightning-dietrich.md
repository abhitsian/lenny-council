## One-liner

Hypergrowth companies compress five to ten years of normal growth stages into months, which means leaders must constantly hire people who have already lived through the next stage — before it arrives.

## The framework

Dietrich names three pillars that together produce hypergrowth. She calls the combination "riding the lightning":

**1. Amazing product people love to use**
The product must generate organic excitement. Virality is a product quality signal, not a marketing tactic. ChatGPT, Miro, and 1Password all spread because the experience itself made people tell others.

**2. Organic, inbound, and viral word of mouth**
You cannot buy your way to hypergrowth rates and keep unit economics viable. The viral motion has to be structural — users naturally pulling in other users (Atlassian's user group meetups, Miro's whiteboard shares, 1Password's family plan expansion).

**3. Hire 2X–3X leaders ahead of your current stage**
Hypergrowth companies jump from 10 to 50, 50 to 100, 100 to 200 employees in rapid succession. Internal talent often can't keep up. Leaders need to proactively hire — or hire advisors — who have already run a team or function at the next order of magnitude. Homegrown talent is valuable but must be paired with people who have seen what "great" looks like at the next scale.

The supporting practice Dietrich adds: **wait as long as possible to hire salespeople**. Atlassian spent the money it would have spent on a sales org on R&D instead, producing a product that sold itself. Sales-assisted motions make sense when the product requires context, customization, or serves a buyer who doesn't self-serve — but adding sales too early is expensive and obscures whether the product has real pull.

**Press-release-first product ideation** (secondary framework, drawn from Amazon): before building, write the press release for what you're shipping. Forces the team to answer "will this resonate?" before it becomes a bag of features. Dietrich calls this "start with the end in mind."

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- The company has found product–market fit and is now in the scaling phase ($30M–$500M ARR is Dietrich's advisory sweet spot)
- The product can deliver time-to-value in days, not weeks — enabling product-led growth
- The buyer segment is willing to research and self-serve (dev tools, collaboration, ML ops)

**Does not apply when:**
- Product–market fit is still unclear — at that stage, the priority is depth with early customers, not stage-jumping
- The product requires heavy customization or integration before delivering value (then a sales-assisted motion is appropriate from the start, see Retool as her counterexample)
- Bundling is the land strategy — Dietrich found that landing with multiple products slows PLG conversion; bundle only after the single-product land has worked

## Signature questions

1. At your current growth rate, which stage of growth are you 6–12 months away from hitting — and does anyone in your leadership team have direct experience running at that scale?
2. Is your product spreading because people are excited to use it, or because you're paying for awareness? What does your organic/inbound ratio look like?
3. If you removed your sales org tomorrow, would customers still find and adopt your product? What would break?
4. Are the growth blockers you're experiencing a marketing or sales problem — or a company strategy problem that marketing and sales are being asked to paper over?
5. When you land a new account, are you landing with one focused product or asking the buyer to evaluate a bundle? What does the conversion data say?

## Common misuse

Dietrich's explicit warning: the growth levers — moving upmarket, expanding globally, adding sales channels — are **company strategy problems**, not individual department problems. The failure mode she sees repeatedly is a CEO or board blaming marketing or sales for weak growth when the real issue is that product, engineering, sales, and customer success haven't aligned around a shared strategic move. Swapping out the CMO or CPO doesn't fix a strategy misalignment. Her other pointed warning: adding salespeople early feels like adding predictable revenue but actually creates cost drag and masks whether the product has genuine pull. Test before you scale any channel.
