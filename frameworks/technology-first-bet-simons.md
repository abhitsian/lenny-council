## One-liner

Build deep proprietary technology without a predetermined market problem, stay alive long enough at minimum burn, and let actual user behavior reveal where product-market fit lives — conviction over consensus.

## The framework

Eric describes this as the hand he played with StackBlitz over seven years, leading to Bolt. The framework has four interlocking moves:

**1. Make the technology the bet**
Pick a substrate technology that is both technically novel and directionally important — not because you have a product in mind, but because you believe the platform shift will force a valuable application into existence. For StackBlitz, this was WebContainer: a WebAssembly operating system that runs inside a browser. The team modeled this directly on Figma's WebGL-first bet in 2013 — build the rendering engine from scratch, and the product follows.

**2. Run minimum viable burn**
Until users are "pulling the product out of your hands," spend nothing that doesn't extend runway. Every vendor gets a half-price ask. Headcount stays small and senior. Resist every external pressure to scale spend or headcount during market exuberance — the rounds you don't raise and the hires you don't make in the up-cycle are what keep the company alive in the down-cycle.

**3. Take shots on goal, not grand slams**
Because you don't know which application will connect, structure the company to run multiple bets cheaply. Small headcount with high context per person is the mechanism: everyone can act front-to-back without approval chains, which lets you pivot a product line in weeks not quarters.

**4. Watch for emergent pull, then run toward it**
PMF is discovered, not engineered. When Bolt launched with a tweet, the team watched for two signals: day-over-day ARR acceleration that didn't fade after launch, and users from outside the expected audience (in their case, non-developers making up 67% of users). Those were not planned outcomes — they were observed outcomes. The framework treats this discovery phase as anthropology, not strategy execution.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have a technically novel substrate that is clearly ahead of what the market can absorb today.
- The founding team has prior experience with low-burn operations and can psychologically tolerate years without a clear product hit.
- You are operating in a domain where the underlying platform is shifting (browser capabilities, LLM code quality thresholds, etc.) and you can identify why now may be a tipping point.

**Does not apply when:**
- You have a clear customer pain point and a reachable distribution path — in that case, the lean startup / customer discovery approach is faster.
- Runway is short or investor pressure demands near-term revenue proof.
- The technology bet requires hardware or regulatory access that limits how many shots you can take.

## Signature questions

1. What is the underlying platform shift that makes this bet possible now that was not possible two years ago?
2. What is your burn rate, and how many distinct product shots on goal can you take before cash runs out?
3. Are users pulling the product out of your hands, or are you pushing it at them? What's the honest evidence?
4. Who is actually showing up to use this? Are they the audience you expected, or is someone else discovering value you didn't anticipate?
5. What is the consensus view on your approach, and why do you believe the consensus is wrong today even if it will be right later?

## Common misuse

Eric warns against mistaking this framework as a license to build technology indefinitely. The failure mode he explicitly names is allowing the "nerd side" of you to convince yourself that building cool technology is enough, separate from building a venture-scale company. He spent years post-WebContainer struggling to find a product that converted technical adoption into willingness to pay. The framework only works if the team also takes genuine product shots (not just polishes the tech), and if burn is so low that each failed product attempt does not kill the company. The other warning he gives explicitly: raising and spending aggressively during exuberance years (2020–2021) is the thing that would have killed StackBlitz. Consensus pressure to grow headcount is the enemy of this approach.
