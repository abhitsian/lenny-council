## One-liner

Separate the metrics that change your decisions (signal) from the metrics that fill dashboards (noise), and build your paid growth operation around that discipline — from platform selection to hiring to team cadence.

## The framework

Davis draws the framework from Nate Silver's *The Signal and the Noise*, then operationalizes it across four layers:

**1. Signal identification**
The first question for any metric: does it change what you do? Impressions, reach, and frequency are noise when the campaign goal is conversions. Click-through rate, click share, and conversion rate are signal. Quality score signals account-level health. Impression share is ego marketing — "I want to be number one, I want to be there all the time" — click share is the signal.

**2. Signs-of-life testing**
Before scaling any platform: load your customer list, build a 1% lookalike, and put a small amount of spend in. If there's positive signal, then build a campaign. Do not go "a hundred miles an hour" on a signs-of-life result. First confirm: right creative, right messaging, right campaign structure for that platform's user behavior.

**3. Ops cadence (buckets → rocks → pebbles → cadence)**
A spreadsheet where column A holds big rocks (finance, performance, structure, keywords, ad copy, quality score, targeting) and each rock breaks into pebbles with a review frequency: weekly, biweekly, or monthly. This is what agencies skip — they touch the obvious things, not the full depth.

**4. Forward-thinking, backwards-planning**
Before any engagement: what is the endstate you need to reach? Then work backwards to milestones. If the endstate is $100M in spend, one person cannot do that — build the hiring plan into the milestones now.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're deciding which platform metrics to act on and which to report-to-management
- You're running signs-of-life experiments on a new channel
- You're evaluating agency work or spotting where an inherited account is underperforming
- You're hiring: the signal-vs-noise interview question (throw data at a candidate, ask what they'd optimize) surfaces data thinkers vs dashboard readers

**Does not apply when:**
- You don't have product-market fit yet — don't run paid to an unconverted market. You'll annoy users who later won't give you a second chance.
- You're spending under ~$50K/month on a platform — incrementality testing won't have enough signal, and the ops overhead exceeds the return.
- Your goal is awareness, not conversion — in that case, reach and frequency become the signal, not the noise.

## Signature questions

1. What is the actual goal of this campaign? Work backwards from there to decide which metrics are signal and which are noise.
2. Where are users already finding you organically? That tells you which platform to test first.
3. What does your ops cadence look like — how often are you actually touching keywords, ad copy, search query reports, quality score?
4. When you run a signs-of-life test, are you giving yourself permission to fail and learn, or are you under pressure to succeed immediately?
5. If a candidate says "reach, frequency, CPMs, CPC, conversion rate, cost per MQL" — which of those would you cut, and why?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Davis names repeatedly is **ego marketing** — optimizing for impression share, top-of-page placement, or high CPM numbers because they look impressive internally, not because they drive conversions. Agencies run this by default: they have playbooks, they hit the obvious account levers, and they move on to their next 50 clients. The ops cadence exists precisely to prevent this — weekly and biweekly checks on the things that actually change account performance (keyword granularity, negative lists, quality score, search query reports) rather than the monthly vanity review that most agencies deliver.

A second misuse: importing creative from one platform directly to another. User mindset differs by platform — what works on Meta (customer testimonials, value props) can fail on TikTok. Run platform-specific creative tests; don't assume a winner travels.
