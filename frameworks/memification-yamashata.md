## One-liner

PMs must distill insights into memorable, distributable units that travel through the org without them, and own the purpose layer so that teams can make great local decisions without waiting for top-down specs.

## The framework

**Memification** is the practice of taking a data insight, research finding, or product truth and compressing it into something so clear and repeatable that leaders cite it unprompted in unrelated meetings. You know you've done it when an exec quotes your insight in a meeting you weren't in. The test: can a busy person with 30 seconds of attention carry this and use it correctly?

Steps Yamashita describes:
1. Synthesize disparate inputs (research, data, opinions from reviews) into a single distillation — a thesis, not a summary.
2. Strip context until the insight stands on its own. Escaping the curse of knowledge is the active work, not just a nice-to-have.
3. Check memorability: would someone in a hallway conversation reach for this? If not, compress further.

**PM Owns the Why** is the companion framework. The PM does not own the spec, the what, or the how — those are shared with designers and engineers. The PM owns the problem framing so completely that every team member can make a good local call without escalating.

Yamashita traces this to the shift from Microsoft (spec everything, tables for every error state) to Google/YouTube (large team, you cannot specify everything, how do you make it so a great decision gets made?). The answer: if everyone understands why we're doing this and what problem we're solving, people can make really great decisions on their own.

The two frameworks interlock: Memification is how the why travels. A well-memified "why" propagates through the org and surfaces spontaneously at the right moments.

**OKR corollary — three tests for a good goal:**
Yamashita applies the same logic to goal-setting. A good OKR passes three tests:
- *Legibility* — people look at it and understand what it is without decoding.
- *Actionability* — it stirs action. Looking at it makes someone want to do something differently.
- *Authenticity* — it honestly depicts what the team is actually doing day-to-day, not a post-rationalization of projects already in flight.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- The team is large enough that the PM cannot be in every decision.
- Work is moving fast and engineers/designers are making constant small calls.
- You need insight or strategy to self-propagate through an org without you as the messenger.
- You're trying to make OKRs feel real rather than performative.

**Does not apply when:**
- The team is small enough that tight-loop spec culture works (very early stage, single-PM teams).
- The problem is still being defined and there is no stable "why" to memify yet.
- Regulatory or safety contexts require exhaustive spec discipline regardless of team size.

## Signature questions

1. If I weren't in the room, would my team make the same decision? If not, have I actually communicated the why?
2. Could an exec cite this insight accurately in a meeting next week — without notes? If not, what needs to be cut or simplified?
3. When an engineer is stopped in the hallway and asked what their team's biggest goal is, what do they say? Is it your OKR, or is it the project they happen to be working on?
4. Is this goal legible, actionable, and authentic — or is it a post-rationalization of work we were going to do anyway?
5. Am I escaping the curse of knowledge here, or am I assuming the audience already knows the context I've been living in for months?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Yamashita names explicitly is OKR theater: writing goals that are either (a) a secondary metric no one actually cares about, technically measurable but not connected to anything real, or (b) an ambitious top-level metric where you have too many hops to actually prove you moved it. Both produce the same result — people check the box but aren't actually thinking about the goal day-to-day.

The memification failure mode is the inverse: assuming that because you communicated something once, it is now shared knowledge. Real memification requires compression to the point of spontaneous reuse, not just clarity at the moment of presentation.
