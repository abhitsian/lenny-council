## One-liner

Tenets are opinionated, memorable decision rules — not aspirational platitudes — that resolve recurring team debates once and for all, enabling smaller, faster, more coherent product organizations.

## The framework

Baxley draws a sharp line between **principles** and **tenets**:

**Principles** are words no one would argue against — simple, clear, beautiful, fast. They're fine as aspirations but useless as decision tools. Nobody ever sat in a meeting and said "let's try to make it as confusing as possible," so "be clear" tells the team nothing when they're in an actual disagreement.

**Tenets** resolve real forks. They name which direction you go when two legitimate approaches compete. The test: a good tenet makes a bad alternative thinkable. If you can't imagine someone arguing the other side, it's a platitude, not a tenet.

**How to write them:**
- Identify debates that keep coming back with the same two camps
- Have that debate once, decisively, at the organizational level
- Write the decision as a short, memorable rule — everyone needs to be able to recall it without a handbook
- Cap at three or four; more than that and they won't stick

**Baxley's three tenets at ThoughtSpot:**
1. *Documentation is a failure state* — if the user needs a manual to use it, the design failed
2. *Start simple; users opt into complexity* — the baseline experience is approachable; power lives behind a deliberate choice
3. *The whole product looks and feels like it came from a single mind* — combats the natural enterprise tendency to fragment across teams

**The Keynote origin story:** Steve Jobs, when asked how to think about building Keynote, gave three rules: (1) make it difficult to produce ugly presentations, (2) prioritize cinematic transitions, (3) optimize for innovation over PowerPoint compatibility. That third one particularly mattered — without it, the team would have spent years fighting over compatibility vs. originality.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- The team is relitigating the same design or strategy debate repeatedly
- Different teams are making independent decisions that fragment the product
- You need to scale decision-making beyond what command-and-control allows — moving from control to choreography
- An enterprise product serves many teams in many geographies and the center can't review everything

**Does not apply when:**
- You're at a stage where the product vision itself isn't clear yet (tenets won't stick if the underlying direction is undefined)
- Feature-level decisions — Baxley tried writing tenets for individual features and found they didn't work; tenets operate at the design strategy level
- The "debate" is actually just a real tradeoff that requires fresh judgment every time

## Signature questions

1. What debate does your team have over and over, and what would it look like to just settle it once?
2. If I showed this tenet to someone on your engineering team in Bangalore, could they use it to make the right call without asking anyone?
3. Is your "principle" something that anyone could argue against — or is it just an aspiration your competitors would claim too?
4. When you moved from control to choreography, what did you put in place so the culture could carry the decisions you weren't in the room for?
5. Does the whole product feel like it came from a single mind — or does it feel like different teams working in parallel?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Baxley explicitly calls out: writing tenets that sound like tenets but function as platitudes. The word "tenet" doesn't do the work — the substance does. If your tenet reads "design for the user," it's still a platitude. The test is whether it actually resolves a real fork. Can you point to an argument that a tenet ruled out? If not, you haven't written a tenet, you've written an aspiration.

A secondary misuse: applying tenets at the feature level. They work at the product strategy or organizational culture level. Baxley tried feature-level tenets and found they didn't hold — the unit of analysis is too small for this kind of standing rule to help.
