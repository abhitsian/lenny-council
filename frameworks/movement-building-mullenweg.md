## One-liner

Give people something to believe in — a philosophy, a worldview, rituals — and the community becomes a moat no proprietary competitor can replicate.

## The framework

Mullenweg's movement-building approach rests on four reinforcing layers:

**1. A philosophy worth defending**
Anchor the project to a set of principles people can hold as their own. For WordPress, this is the four GPL freedoms: use, study, modify, redistribute. The philosophy isn't marketing — it's the actual license. People who adopt it are not just users; they're co-owners of an idea.

**2. Symbolic rituals that carry soul**
Small signals — a tagline, a naming convention, an annual tradition — signal that the project isn't purely utilitarian. "Code is poetry" appears in the WordPress.org footer. Every release is named after a jazz musician. These are low-cost rituals that attract people who want to belong, not just consume.

**3. Incentive structures that invite contribution**
Economics is all about how you set up the incentive structures. The architecture must allow people to build on top of the project in ways that benefit them — plugins, themes, extensions. A true platform is when the ecosystem earns more than the core. That ecosystem depth becomes the moat: 60,000 plugins can't be replicated.

**4. Open governance as a freedom guarantee**
Proprietary platforms can pull the rug. Open source cannot — the code belongs equally to everyone. Even if leadership becomes misaligned, contributors can fork. This guarantee is what makes builders choose WordPress over Shopify's ecosystem: you don't need permission.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're building a platform where third-party builders create the long-term value
- You're playing a decades-long game where community defensibility matters more than near-term control
- You want to attract contributors who are intrinsically motivated, not just employed

**Doesn't apply when:**
- Your competitive advantage depends on proprietary code staying hidden
- You need tight control over the user experience end-to-end
- You're building for a short-term exit; movement-building requires sustained stewardship

## Signature questions

1. Are you building a product, or are you building something people can believe in? What's the philosophy?
2. What are your rituals — the small, recurring signals that tell contributors this is more than just a paycheck?
3. Is your ecosystem set up so third parties can profit more than you can? If not, you have a platform in name only.
4. What happens to your users if you go evil tomorrow? Can they leave without losing everything? Open source means the answer is yes.
5. What incentive structures have you designed — not just hoped for — that reward contribution and punish free-riding?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Mullenweg explicitly warns against is **open-source washing** — using the language of openness without the actual freedoms. He called out Meta's Llama model as "fake open source" because it requires a license from Meta once you exceed 750 million monthly active users. The moment you can have the rug pulled — by a usage threshold, a terms-of-service change, an API shutdown — you don't have freedom, you have the appearance of freedom. The framework only works when the open license is non-negotiable and legally enforced, not a branding choice.
