## One-liner

A 2x2 framework for feedback and leadership built on two axes — care personally and challenge directly — where most leaders fail not by being too harsh but by being too empathetic to speak up.

## The framework

The two axes form four quadrants:

- **Radical Candor** (high care, high challenge): what good looks like. You care enough about the person to tell them hard things.
- **Ruinous Empathy** (high care, low challenge): the most common failure mode. You like the person, you don't want to hurt their feelings, so you say nothing. They suffer for it later.
- **Obnoxious Aggression** (low care, high challenge): saying hard things without showing you give a damn. It's not just unkind — it's inefficient, because it triggers fight-or-flight and people can't hear you.
- **Manipulative Insincerity** (low care, low challenge): the worst quadrant. Pretending to agree when you don't.

The framework applies to both praise and criticism — and Kim Scott is explicit that praise matters at least as much as criticism.

**How to give feedback (HIP CORE):**
- **H** — Humble (you might be wrong; this is a dialogue, not a monologue)
- **H** — Helpful (state your intention to help, not to dominate)
- **I** — Immediate (don't wait for a better moment — it means you'll never say it)
- **I** — In person or synchronous (phone over video; you need to gauge how it lands)
- **P** — Private (for criticism; praise can be public)
- **P** — Not about Personality
- **CORE** — Context, Observation, Result, next stEp (e.g., "In the meeting / when you said um every third word / it made you sound stupid / go to the speech coach")

**How to solicit feedback:**
1. Ask a go-to question (not "do you have feedback?" — that gets nothing). Kim's version: "What could I do or stop doing that would make it easier to work with me?"
2. Count to six in silence after asking. Almost nobody can hold six seconds; they'll fill it.
3. Listen to understand, not to respond. Manage your defensive reaction.
4. Reward the candor — fix the problem, show what you did, then ask if you over- or under-corrected.
5. If you disagree: find the 5-10% you can agree with, name it, then say "as for the rest, I want to think about it and get back to you" — and actually follow up.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You manage people and want to build real trust, not false harmony.
- You're a people-pleaser defaulting to silence to avoid discomfort.
- Your team's culture is stuck in ruinous empathy — good intentions, chronic avoidance.
- You want to create conditions where your direct reports give *you* feedback.

**Doesn't apply when:**
- You use it as permission to be blunt without relationship. Kim's explicit warning: saying "in the spirit of Radical Candor" and then acting like a jerk is obnoxious aggression, not the framework.
- You try to apply it uniformly — the right words for one person will silence another. Candor has to be calibrated to the individual.
- Respect is absent. Kim's follow-on book Radical Respect addresses this directly: if you don't respect the person, care and challenge won't land.

## Signature questions

1. What is the person losing by not hearing this? Is your silence actually kind?
2. Are you holding back because you care about *them* — or because you care about *your own* comfort or reputation right now?
3. Is the team's culture closer to ruinous empathy or obnoxious aggression — and what specific conversation are you avoiding that's holding that in place?
4. When you got feedback from someone, did you reward their candor with a real change, or just said thank you and moved on?
5. What is your go-to question for soliciting feedback — and does it sound like *you*, or like something you'd never actually say?

## Common misuse

Two failure modes Kim explicitly warns against:

**1. Weaponizing the label.** People say "in the spirit of Radical Candor" and then act aggressively. The framework does not license bluntness without relationship. The care dimension is not optional.

**2. Applying a script.** Kim repeatedly refuses to give word-for-word language because if you sound like Kim Scott and not like yourself, people won't believe you want the answer. The framework requires authenticity — same intent, different words per person and per relationship.

The Bob story is the canonical misuse case on the other side: 10 months of ruinous empathy, no real feedback, until the only option left was termination. Bob's question — "Why didn't you tell me? I thought you all cared about me." — is the consequence Kim returns to as the reason the framework matters.
