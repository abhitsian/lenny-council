## One-liner

An empowered product team is handed a customer or business problem to solve and measured on whether it solves it, while a feature team is handed a roadmap of features to ship and measured on shipping them — and only the first kind of work actually needs a product manager.

## The framework

1. **Give teams problems, not features.** An empowered team gets one or two hard customer or business problems a quarter (on top of keep-the-lights-on work), not a list of features to design, build, and ship by a date. The team figures out the solution.
2. **Measure outcomes, not output.** Shipping the thing earns no points; solving the problem does. Cagan reframes it for execs as "time to money" rather than "time to market" — the techniques for shipping fast are well-known, but proving the value landed is the hard part.
3. **Own value and viability, not just usability and feasibility.** Engineers own feasibility (can it be built), designers own usability. A real product manager owns value (will customers choose it) and viability (does it work for the business — legal, ethical, financial, brand). A feature team only has to produce something usable and feasible, which is why it doesn't need a PM.
4. **Strategy is the leaders' job; the bets get handed down.** Product teams don't do product strategy — product leaders do. Leaders decide the focus and the bets (Cagan agrees this looks top-down), then give those bets to teams with the latitude to figure out the how.
5. **Three dimensions of the product operating model.** (a) How you decide what to work on — that's product strategy, done in annual planning. (b) How you solve problems — that's product discovery, finding solutions that work for the customer and the business. (c) How you build, test, and deploy — that's delivery, done reliably and instrumented so you can prove the outcome. Discovery and delivery are different work, not the same loop.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** when the business needs more than optimization — finding solutions to genuinely hard problems where the answer isn't known up front. This is where the PM role earns its keep.
- **Doesn't (or needs less):** pure optimization work — low-risk A/B tests like changing a call-to-action — is real work but it's not discovery, and Cagan says it doesn't require the full empowered-team setup. He also concedes some B2B sales-driven companies genuinely know what to build, though he's openly unsympathetic to teams that *choose* to stay feature factories.
- **Boundary on "empowered":** empowered does not mean bottom-up free-for-all. Strategy still comes from leadership top-down; the empowerment is in how the team solves the assigned problem, not in choosing which problems matter.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- Were you handed a problem to solve, or a feature to ship? If it's a feature with a date, you're on a feature team and you probably don't need a PM for it.
- What outcome does this move, and how will you prove it moved? If the answer is "we shipped it," that's output, not outcome.
- Who owns value and viability on this? If nobody can name the business and customer case beyond "it can be built," the hardest part is unowned.
- Is this discovery or optimization? A call-to-action A/B test is fine, but don't call it product discovery.
- Where did this problem come from — a leader's strategy, or an exec's pet feature? Strategy is leadership's job; a roadmap of pet features is the top-down failure mode.

## Common misuse

Calling a team "empowered" while still handing it a roadmap of features and dates — what Cagan calls product management theater. The tell is a PM whose self-description is "I facilitate, I communicate, I herd the cats": on a real empowered team the PM is a creator responsible for value and viability, not a backlog administrator. He also warns against the inverse mistake — reading "empowered" as fully bottom-up. Strategy stays with leadership; teams get latitude on the solution, not a vote on the bets.
