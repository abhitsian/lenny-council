## One-liner

Before hiring a marketer or allocating resources, split all marketing activity into two buckets — Fuel (everything you create that adds value) and Engine (how you distribute it) — then identify which bucket is your biggest constraint.

## The framework

**Fuel** is everything you make: website copy, blog posts, templates, webinar recordings, positioning, messaging. The words and assets that add value to an audience.

**Engine** is how you get Fuel to the right people: email segmentation and drip logic, SEO infrastructure, paid distribution, social channel strategy, marketing ops, HubSpot setup, and reporting. The mechanics that move Fuel through the funnel.

Some things are both. A Slack community, for example, generates user-created content (Fuel) while also giving you an audience to distribute to (Engine).

**The diagnostic move:** Ask where you have the biggest gap right now, or where doing more would drive the most growth. Then hire and allocate toward that constraint.

Common patterns:
- Top-down sales teams often have an SDR engine already running but no Fuel to put in it. Hire a content or product marketer first.
- PLG teams often have people discovering the product but dropping out everywhere. They have Fuel; they need Engine — lifecycle emails, better web conversion, funnel ops.

**Three archetypal hires** mapped to the framework:
1. **Content/community marketer** — Fuel specialist
2. **Growth/demand gen marketer** — Engine specialist
3. **Product marketer** — straddles both; lays positioning groundwork (Fuel) while understanding channel strategy (Engine)

**Pi-shaped hiring:** The ideal early marketer is expert in one of the three archetypes, proficient in a second, and able to set strategy across all three. The rarest combination is content + growth (opposite sides of the brain). The most common and useful combos are product marketing + growth or product marketing + content.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** You are trying to figure out who to hire as a first or early marketer, or why a current marketing effort isn't working. Also useful for diagnosing resource allocation on an existing team.

**Does not apply when:** Your business is still pre-product-market-fit or in active discovery mode with design partners. At that stage, founders should handle marketing and contractors should fill gaps — a full-time marketer will sit on their hands.

## Signature questions

1. What are your top-performing pieces of content? What's the top-performing page on your website? (If you can't answer, you have a Fuel problem.)
2. You wrote a great piece of content — how are you distributing it? What did you do? (If the answer is "shared it on social and nothing happened," that's an Engine problem.)
3. Do you have two sales reps and a hand-off that's just a shared list? How is that going to scale? (Engine problem.)
4. If you hired a marketer tomorrow, what would they actually do? And if they stepped on the gas, could your product and team handle the inbound?
5. What are your team's big bets right now — the things that could cause step-change funnel growth? (If the lead can't name them, they're not impact-focused.)

## Common misuse

Building an Engine before you have Fuel — sending outbound email campaigns or standing up paid distribution before you have positioning, clear messaging, or quality content. The Engine amplifies what you put into it; if the Fuel is empty or weak, the Engine just accelerates nothing.

The mirror misuse: creating lots of Fuel (blog posts, content pieces) without any distribution, then concluding that content doesn't work. The content was never the problem.

Emily also warns explicitly against activity goals in marketing ("write 10 blog posts this month"). Goals should be impact-based — traffic, conversion rates, qualified signups — not output-based.
