## One-liner

Use data to reveal what is actually happening in your product (the diagnosis), then use design as the creative act that figures out how to fix or improve it — data shows you where the problem is, design invents the solution.

## The framework

Zhuo names this framework explicitly and returns to it repeatedly. It has two distinct roles:

**Data as diagnostic tool**
Data captures reality. It reflects back what users are actually doing versus what you believe they are doing. This includes quantitative metrics, A/B test results, customer interviews, TikTok comments, social chatter — all of it is data. The job of data is to surface the gap between your internal story ("people love us, we're growing") and what is genuinely happening. Data tells you if a problem exists and roughly where it lives. It does not tell you what to build.

**Design as the treatment**
Once data surfaces the gap, design is the creative, inventive process of solving it. There is no formula. You still have to dream, invent, experiment. A/B tests help you try more things and understand short-term effects, but they don't replace the creative act. The solution comes from people, not from the metric.

**The failure mode data cannot prevent**
Zhuo adds a second layer: even within the diagnostic role, choosing which metrics to look at is an art, not a science. Interpreting whether a 5% lift is good or bad is still judgment. Numbers feel precise but the selection and interpretation remain human acts.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A team is stalled on whether to trust gut or trust the data — this reframes data's role so the tension dissolves
- A product stops growing and nobody knows why — this is exactly the diagnosis scenario Zhuo describes
- Designers push back on data-drivenness as an attempt to control or dictate the solution

**Does not apply when:**
- A product is so new that no meaningful behavioral data exists yet; Zhuo acknowledges fast-growing companies often run on instinct and good vibes early on and that is fine
- The question is purely executional (how to build something already well-defined), not diagnostic

## Signature questions

1. What is our current story about how users feel about this product — and what does the data actually say?
2. Are we using data to find a problem, or are we asking data to tell us the solution? Which of those two questions are we in right now?
3. When growth slowed (or if it slows), do we have the instrumentation and observability to root-cause it quickly?
4. Which metrics did we choose to look at, and why? Is that selection itself being examined or treated as neutral?
5. After the diagnosis, are we giving the design team real creative latitude to invent the treatment, or are we constraining them with the data prematurely?

## Common misuse

Zhuo's explicit warning: treating data as prescriptive rather than diagnostic. When a team asks "what does the data say we should build?" they have crossed the line. Data can show you retention dropped among 25–34 year olds in the first week. It cannot tell you whether the fix is a better onboarding flow, a feature change, or a pricing shift. Outsourcing the creative judgment to the metric is the failure mode. The metric surfaces the gap; a person still has to invent the treatment.

A second misuse she names: using the false precision of numbers to shut down design intuition entirely. Numbers create a feeling of certainty that the underlying phenomenon does not support. The best designers she has worked with are obsessed with understanding reality — they use data the same way a good diagnostician uses test results: to sharpen their understanding, not to replace their judgment.
