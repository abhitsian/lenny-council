## One-liner

A lower activation rate (5–15%) is a feature, not a bug — it signals tight correlation with long-term retention; pair it with a multi-metric system (north star + guardrail + sophistication) and revisit the metric every 6+ months as the business opportunity shifts.

## The framework

Isford structures PLG growth around four funnel stages she names **Join → Evaluate → Upgrade → Expand**:

- **Join**: How a user enters the product — via organic discovery or a teammate invite. Different entry paths warrant different treatment.
- **Evaluate**: The user determines whether the product will deliver the value they're looking for. Isford is deliberate about this word: it's not "onboard" or "activate" — it's the user's assessment, not the company's. This is where onboarding investment has the biggest leverage.
- **Upgrade**: The user has built enough familiarity and perceived value to convert to a paid plan, or raise their hand for a sales conversation.
- **Expand**: More people in the same organization adopt the product, driving net dollar retention, referrals back to Join, and compounding usage.

On top of the funnel, Isford adds three activation-specific principles:

1. **Low activation rate heuristic**: Target a metric that only 5–15% of users reach. High rates (e.g., 40%) mean the bar is too low — retention at month 12 or 24 will be weak. A tighter metric that fewer users reach, but with higher fidelity, produces bigger downstream impact when you improve it incrementally.

2. **Multi-metric tracking**: Don't run on a single north star. Isford's Airtable activation team used three: their team activation metric (north star), individual week-2/week-4 retention (retention signal), and a "Build" sophistication score (depth-of-use signal). Each metric can move independently — a new onboarding treatment might improve sophistication without improving team adoption, and that's useful signal.

3. **Dynamic north star**: Stability for at least six months matters, but no metric should be permanent. Revisit when the biggest opportunity for the business has shifted. Airtable moved its growth org north star from revenue to user growth when they decided a decade-long view on monetization was more strategically sound.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Your product has any self-serve or freemium element — a free plan, a sandbox, a trial.
- You are deciding which activation metric to target, or suspect your current one is too easy to hit.
- You're building or restructuring a PLG growth team and want a common language across acquisition, activation, and monetization.
- You're running onboarding experiments and want guardrail metrics so you don't optimize activation at the cost of revenue.

**Doesn't apply when:**
- The product has no self-serve component and all adoption is sales-led with heavy handholding. (Isford acknowledges this; she just says freemium and trials work best when users can reach some value without a human next to them.)
- You're in the first weeks of a product before you have enough behavioral data to determine correlation between any activation event and long-term retention.

## Signature questions

1. What percentage of new users hit your activation metric? If it's above 20–25%, is the metric actually predictive of 12-month retention?
2. Are you building onboarding for what the customer needs, or for what your pricing and packaging suggests you should push?
3. What is your sophistication or depth-of-use metric — not just whether someone activated, but how far they went?
4. Has your north star metric been stable for six-plus months? If so, is it still tracking the biggest opportunity in the business, or has the opportunity shifted?
5. When a new onboarding treatment moves your north star, do you also check whether it moved retention and sophistication independently, or just the composite?

## Common misuse

Isford warns explicitly against two failure modes:

**Building onboarding for the business instead of the customer.** Teams map onboarding to pricing tiers — pushing premium features because users who adopt them are more likely to pay, not because the feature is what the user needs right now. She calls this "sitting in the priors of what your packaging scheme might suggest." The fix: guardrail metrics that make visible when onboarding changes hurt revenue signals, paired with a team culture anchored in customer value, not conversion proxies.

**Naming features instead of driving outcomes.** Tooltips that say "This is Automations" tell the user what the company built. They don't tell the user whether that feature is relevant to their job, how to activate it, or what outcome it delivers. Isford's standard: describe the contextual application, or even better, give the user a one-touch action that gets them to value — do some of the work for them.
