## One-liner

Product ops is both a system you build and a role you hire — and mature product ops teams should deliberately automate their tactical work so they can transition into strategic advisors to product leadership.

## The framework

Christine breaks product ops into two distinct definitions that often get conflated:

**1. Product ops as a system (the thing)**
Any VP, head of product, or PM can create a system that helps their team thrive in product management. This is not a person — it's the structures, processes, and transparency mechanisms that remove noise for product people. Every product team has some version of this whether they name it or not.

**2. Product ops as a role (the people)**
The more common definition: a person or team who are strong partners to product managers. For more mature product orgs, they evolve into strategic advisors to the CPO or VP of Product — surfacing qualitative and quantitative data to inform better strategic decisions.

**The evolution arc** — where product ops matures over time:
1. **Entry point: process streamlining** — The first ask from immature product orgs is usually "help us plan the same way across teams." This is where most product ops starts.
2. **Tactical responsibilities** — Voice of customer synthesis, tooling optimization (PM's tool stack, not planning tools), content strategy, and cross-functional transparency (especially revenue org ↔ product team).
3. **Diagnostic signals that you need product ops** — Two: (a) lack of transparency across the organization, and (b) lack of readiness — people know something is coming but don't know what to do with it.
4. **Endgame: strategic advisory** — Mature product ops automates or hands off tactical work, then redirects human energy to strategy: retention, growth, in-app experience, voice of customer at a higher level.

**The two signals — transparency and readiness**
- Transparency: stakeholders (revenue, product, exec) aren't aligned on what's happening and why
- Readiness: teams know a launch is coming but can't prepare customers or position it correctly — this is distinct from just knowing the date

**What product ops is not**
- Program management or agile facilitation (though there's overlap in immature orgs)
- Product marketing (PMM positions and sells; product ops educates and enables internal teams to understand the value)
- A Band-Aid for dysfunction — ops at any mature function is a sign of growth, not inefficiency

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- The product team is growing fast and PMs are fielding constant questions from sales/success that crowd out customer time
- Internal alignment is breaking down — revenue org and product are talking past each other
- A launch failed because people knew it was coming but weren't prepared to act on it
- PMs are stuck in planning facilitation instead of customer discovery

**Doesn't apply when:**
- The product team is small enough that a strong PM can carry the system themselves without a dedicated role
- For early-stage teams still finding product-market fit: "Respect the hustle" — introducing process too early stifles the speed needed to iterate

**Important boundary:** Product ops leaders should have hands-on product experience. The day-to-day role can come from consulting, customer success, or technical success — but leadership-level product ops hires should have felt the PM pain directly.

## Signature questions

1. What are your PMs actually accountable to? Are you measuring them on strategic customer outcomes, or are they spending quality time fielding revenue-team questions?
2. Do your internal teams know what's coming — or do they know what to do with what's coming? (That's the readiness gap.)
3. What processes in your current product ops work could be automated or handed off — and what strategic work are you leaving on the table while you do those tactical things?
4. Is resistance to product ops coming from PMs, or is it a buy-in gap at the CPO/VP level? (Bottom-up won't stick without top-down support.)
5. If you're building product ops from scratch: what are you holding your PMs accountable to, and what does that tell you about where to place your team's energy first?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Christine warns against explicitly: building a product ops team and then locking them into tactical execution permanently. If you stand up processes and never automate or hand them off, you waste the humans who could be doing strategic work — and you create a team that's uncomfortable with change. She made one point "abundantly clear" to every product ops hire: get into this role if you're comfortable letting go of things and moving on. The company will change, processes will be automated, and the job will evolve. Product ops people who are attached to their tactical ownership become obstacles rather than multipliers.

The secondary misuse: treating product ops as a B2B-only role. The common thread is cross-functional transparency and customer readiness — not the B2B business model. Large B2C companies with complex internal stakeholder structures need this too.
