## One-liner

Strategy is an integrated set of choices that compels desired customer action — and the cascade gives you five sequenced questions to build those choices from the ground up.

## The framework

Roger Martin developed the Strategy Choice Cascade between 1987 and 1995 while at Monitor Company, answering the practical question Porter left open: *how* do you actually build a winning strategy? The five questions must be answered together, with each layer reinforcing the others:

1. **Winning aspiration** — What are you trying to accomplish? Not a mission statement, but a clear articulation of the outcome you want customers to choose you for. The aspiration should be framed in terms of customer benefit, not market share.

2. **Where to play** — On what playing field will you compete? This covers customer segments, geographies, product categories, distribution channels, and vertical stage (where in the value chain you'll operate). You are not going to play everywhere; the choice of where you *won't* play is as important as where you will.

3. **How to win** — Given where you've chosen to play, how will you be either better at creating customer value than competitors, or lower cost? These are the only two routes. "Being stuck in the middle" — neither differentiated nor low-cost — leaves you with no protection.

4. **Must-have capabilities** — What specific capabilities do you need that your competitors don't have, or can't replicate easily? The how-to-win is a theory; capabilities are what make the theory defensible. They should be hard to copy (see: Westlaw's 100 years of lawyer-annotated case law, Four Seasons' low-turnover staff model).

5. **Enabling management systems** — What recruiting, onboarding, career development, measurement, and operational systems do you need to build and *maintain* those capabilities? This is where moats become durable. Four Seasons' 10% staff turnover (vs. the industry's 80%) is a management systems outcome, not luck.

The cascade is iterative, not linear. You toggle back and forth between the five questions until they form an internally consistent, mutually reinforcing set.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** You're defining or re-evaluating strategy at any level of an organization — from a brand manager to a CEO. Martin explicitly argues this is as relevant for a P&G brand team as it is for the executive suite. Also useful when you sense you're "playing to play" but can't articulate why the strategy feels hollow.

**Doesn't apply well when:** You're solving an execution problem, not a strategic one. If the five questions are already answered and mutually consistent, the cascade won't tell you how to run the operations — that's a different problem. Also, the cascade won't tell you *which* capabilities to build if you haven't first nailed where-to-play and how-to-win; the order matters.

## Signature questions

- What is the customer action we are trying to compel? (Not "what do we want to achieve" — what do we need *them* to do?)
- Are we actually differentiated in the customer's mind, or would they flip a coin between us and a competitor?
- If a well-resourced competitor saw our margins, would they copy our strategy? What would stop them?
- Are our capabilities the result of deliberate choices, or just what we happened to be good at?
- Do our management systems (hiring, training, measurement) actively build the capabilities we say we need to win — or are they inherited from a different strategy?

## Common misuse

Martin's most explicit warning: framing winning aspiration as "there's a big market over there, we'd like to get some." That is not a winning aspiration — it's an opportunity observation with no theory of customer value. The aspiration has to tie back to a customer problem: *why would a customer choose us?* A pitch that starts with market size and ends with "we'll capture X%" has skipped question one entirely.

He also warns against treating capabilities and management systems as afterthoughts once where-to-play and how-to-win are set. Moats are not built by having a clever positioning — they are built by the accumulated specificity of what you do internally. The more multifaceted the capabilities and systems, the less likely a competitor bothers trying to replicate them. Simple advantages (one large plant, one patent) get competed away fast. Layered, systemic ones (Southwest's entire operating model) do not.
