## One-liner

Product management is the practice of bringing clarity to problems and having the conviction to declare the world you believe should exist — translated through narrative, not just data.

## The framework

Ebi structures the PM's job around two interlocking concepts: **clarity** and **conviction**.

**Clarity** means removing everything that pollutes the core problem. Practically, it shows up as:
- A living "Top 10 Things You Should Know" document — a running list of the biggest user and infrastructure problems, updated quarterly by each PM and paired with data partners
- A three-day strategy session structure: Day 1 is **Insights** (understand work: dog-fooding, competitor teardowns, stakeholder 10-things-you-should-know inputs), Day 2 is **Strategy** (which problems to prioritize and in what order), Day 3 is **Big Rocks** (3–5 things anyone can remember, not a laundry list)
- A two-page narrative document (Insights → Approach → Big Rocks) that acts as an evergreen team-facing explainer

**Conviction** is the vision — a feeling of what you believe the world should look like. Not certainty. A feeling made legible. The vision has four properties: lofty, realistic, devoid of today's technical limitations, grounded in a clear user problem.

**Three ways to make the vision tangible:**
1. **Once Upon a Time** (narrative Mad Libs): "Once upon a time [the problem]. Then one day [the change]. And because of that [new state]. And finally [what you left the world with]."
2. **Future headline / article**: Write the TechCrunch headline (and subtitle, and eventually the full article) that would exist if the vision came true. Drop it into a realistic page layout to make it feel real.
3. **Mock or sketch**: Draw the future state — post-it notes, app store screenshots with blank rectangles, or a lo-fi sketch handed to a designer. "I do not accept 'I don't have a designer' as an excuse."

**Three concentric circles of evangelism:**
- Core team first (present multiple times, leave the doc open for comments, not view-only)
- Stakeholders next (the people who gave you their 10-things input — they already feel heard)
- Leadership last, and go as high as possible; let them pull you back rather than self-censoring

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies** when: you're entering a planning cycle and need to align a team around a 3–5 year direction; you're a platform PM who needs to build for use cases that don't exist yet; you're onboarding a new team or new partners and want a repeatable onboarding artifact.

**Does not apply** when: you need a six-month execution plan — for that, Ebi says, "do the tweet" or write the mock. The full empathize–create–evangelize cycle is for macro visions that are genuinely three-plus years out and won't need to be redone annually.

**Boundary the owner draws explicitly**: if you need to re-create the vision every year, you didn't do the work the first time. Vision is evergreen. What changes is the fidelity you bring to parts of it.

## Signature questions

1. What are the top 10 problems that keep you up at night? (If the answer is a ramble, the problem clarity work hasn't been done.)
2. If I took away all your resources and left you five engineers, which one thing would you build? (Forces conviction.)
3. Can you write the TechCrunch headline for what you're proposing? What's the subtitle?
4. Is your team's "Top 10" list the same answer your engineering partner and design partner would give if I asked them separately?
5. Does this vision require the limitations of today to still exist in five years? (If yes, it's not a vision — it's a roadmap item.)

## Common misuse

The failure mode Ebi calls out most explicitly: **treating vision as an annual planning exercise**. If a PM recreates the vision every year, it means neither the problems nor the conviction were ever settled — just deferred. A real vision is an evergreen artifact you bring increasing fidelity to over time (low-fi sketch → hi-fi mock → sizzle reel), not a document that gets replaced.

A second misuse: **coming to leadership with A vs. B scenarios and asking them to pick**. That's offloading the conviction work. The PM's job is to arrive with a point of view, surface the remaining uncertainties, and ask for help mitigating specific risks — not to export the decision upward.
