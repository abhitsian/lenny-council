## One-liner

Scaling a company well starts with knowing yourself, not your product or your team: write down your own operating principles, build a few founding documents and a repeatable operating cadence, and that shared structure becomes the thing everyone holds onto when the company gets chaotic.

## The framework

Two layers — the personal layer (your operating principles) and the company layer (the structures you put in place). Johnson insists the personal one comes first.

**Personal operating principles (her four):**
1. **Build self-awareness to build mutual awareness.** Seek feedback, understand your motivators, strengths, blind spots, and tendencies, then expose them to others. She frames this as the most fundamental thing to crack before you can manage or build a company. To crystallize your own: take a list of 70–80 values, force-rank down to ten, then five, then three, and ask *why* each survived — there's usually a formative story behind it. Then plot your work-style tendencies (introvert/extrovert, task/people oriented) using assessments as raw data.
2. **Say the thing you think you cannot say.** The hard, true observation everyone is avoiding. Her tactics for people who find this hard: ask a question ("is there something we're not talking about?"), and own the observation as *your* perception, not a judgment. She also calls this being an **explorer, not a lecturer** — hold up a mirror, don't pass a verdict.
3. **Distinguish between being a leader and a manager.** Different jobs; she names this as something she did badly early in her career. A manager's job is to enable people to be their best, not to be the expert who tells them what to do.
4. **Come back to the operating system.** When things spin out, return to your rituals — how you set goals, make decisions, run launches. The same bare-bones structure scales from one team to many functions; it's how you "scale yourself."

**Company structures (the house metaphor — foundation, posts/beams, mechanicals):**
- **Founding documents:** a one-line **mission**, **long-term goals** (the longer-distance "why we exist," which she expected to be 3–5 year goals), and **company values / operating principles**. Sign you need to invest here: new hires keep asking "why do we do it this way?"
- **Supporting structures:** OKRs / goals, quarterly business reviews, planning — common structures that replicate up and down the company "stack."
- **Operating cadence (mechanicals):** the rhythm of the work — calendar-driven and event-driven (e.g. a customer event as an internal forcing function).

The discipline: do very few things, consistently, and actually use them. Revisit maybe once a year.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** companies past the all-in-one-room stage that are starting to hire at speed — the moment you can't just tell everyone everything. She argues this structure work matters as early as finding product-market fit, and that founders who skip it end up with "a house that got added on to 17 times and it's not even two years old," forcing a costly teardown later.
- **Doesn't / use sparingly:** pre-PMF and small enough that everyone's in one room — you don't need the packet or the onboarding yet; lightweight hiring process matters more. And the failure mode at the other end is over-structuring: mandating too much, or swapping in a new vehicle every time you hear what another company does. Her stance is explicitly anti-perfectionist — "there's no perfect org structure... but having one and committing to it is good."

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- If management starts with you, what are your three real values — and what's the story behind each? Have you written your operating principles down, or are they still just in your head?
- What's the thing in this situation that everyone's thinking and nobody is saying? Can you say it as a question you own, not a judgment?
- In this meeting are you exploring with the person, or lecturing them?
- Is this a leadership move or a management move — and are you doing the one the moment actually calls for?
- When it gets chaotic, what's the ritual you come back to? Do you have founding documents and a cadence people can hold onto, or are you inventing the process each time?
- Are you adding the *few* structures that scale, or piling on a grab-bag of things you heard other companies do?

## Common misuse

Two failure modes she names directly. First, treating the structures as bureaucracy to be optimized into perfection — leadership teams "experiment with different vehicles," don't follow them, or throw them out before they've been tried, "and it creates a lot of chaos." Her fix: do very few things consistently, use them, and revise at most once a year. Second, skipping the personal layer entirely — jumping to team and company structure while never doing the self-awareness work, which leaves you unable to give the kind of owned, non-threatening feedback the whole system depends on.
