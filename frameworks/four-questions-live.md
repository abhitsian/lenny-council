## One-liner

A self-diagnostic framework for product leaders built around four questions Shreyas wished he had asked himself earlier: Why am I so busy? Do I have good taste? Why is my job so frustrating? Am I really listening? — each surfaces a hidden layer of leadership work that productivity tricks alone can't fix.

## The framework

Shreyas presents four questions as a sequence of honest self-examination. Each question has a diagnostic layer and a move that follows from honest answers.

**Question 1 — Why am I so busy?**
Efficiency tools (LNO, to-do lists, calendar routines) hit a ceiling as scope grows. The real culprits are: (a) no real product strategy, which turns planning into 4–6 week rituals with false precision; and (b) poor product decisions — treating one-way doors as two-way doors, accumulating feature debt that compounds into permanent busyness. The move: invest upfront in a real, pre-aligned product strategy, and pause before committing to any feature, because "most doors that look like two-way doors are actually one-way doors" for a PM leader.

**Question 2 — Do I have good taste?**
Taste isn't just about pixels or product quality. It's about the beliefs you choose to form — and how you form them. Most PMs mistake authority bias (Bezos said it), alliterative catchiness (fail fast vs. fail quickly), or impressive-sounding charts for genuine insight. Real taste means evaluating an idea separate from its social proof, authority proof, and framing. The move: actively strip away these proxies and evaluate ideas on first principles.

**Question 3 — Why is my job so frustrating?**
Product work happens at three levels: impact, execution, and optics. Everyone has a natural level. Frustration compounds when you spend most of your time forced to operate outside that level — especially at the optics level as you climb the corporate ladder. The move: identify your superpowers, make career decisions based on where you do your best work, not on LinkedIn-visible progression or external expectation.

**Question 4 — Am I really listening?**
Surface-level listening (recap, eye contact, "what I heard") is not real listening. There is an entirely different level of listening that enables world-class leadership. Shreyas points to Rick Rubin, Peter Drucker, and others as references for what genuine listening actually means.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** A product leader is chronically stressed, frustrated, or feels stuck despite working hard. Also useful during career inflection points or after recognizing a pattern of poor decisions that didn't get caught until QBRs.

**Doesn't apply when:** The problems are structural (bad org, underfunded team, leadership that won't align) rather than internal. These questions are self-diagnostic — they won't fix a broken external environment, and applying them there can lead to misdirected self-blame.

## Signature questions (council field)

1. "Why are you busy — is it scope, or is it that you haven't made a real product strategy that removes the need for all this planning ritual?"
2. "That belief you're operating from — are you holding it because it survived first-principles scrutiny, or because someone impressive said it, or because it has a catchy name?"
3. "Look at the last five features you shipped — how many were genuinely two-way doors versus one-way doors you decided were reversible in the moment?"
4. "What level of product work is your natural home — impact, execution, or optics — and what percentage of your actual week is spent there?"
5. "When you're in a conversation and you think you're listening, what are you actually doing?"

## Common misuse

Treating this as a one-time checklist rather than ongoing honest self-examination. Shreyas is explicit that he asked himself these questions for years but was not honest with himself about the answers. The framework only works if the answers are brutally honest — not the version you'd share at a QBR. The other failure mode: using the "identify your superpowers" insight to justify avoiding hard things rather than to make deliberate career choices about where to invest.
