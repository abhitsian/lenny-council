## One-liner

Before betting time on an emerging acquisition channel, score it against three filters: does the channel's medium match your customer need and business goal, where is the channel in its lifecycle and how does it monetize, and does your company have the risk tolerance and channel maturity to actually run it.

## The framework

Adam Grenier calls this the **Exploring Emerging Channels Framework**. It has three ingredients evaluated in sequence:

**1. Medium-fit**
Identify the overlap between (a) what your customer needs, (b) what your company's growth goals are, and (c) what the channel does well. If those three don't intersect, the channel is at best a yellow light no matter how hot it is. Example: Clubhouse maps cleanly to Spotify's audio-first discovery goals; a photo-first platform like Paparazzi does not.

**2. Channel DNA**
Two sub-questions:
- *Lifecycle position:* Where is the channel on its growth curve? Early-stage channels require ongoing re-investment to keep pace with product changes and carry a real risk of going away. Being a true first mover means tracking is broken, content standards don't exist, and you may need to accept sunk costs.
- *Monetization model:* How does the channel make money? If your business model can support or amplify their monetization strategy, you get preferential access, custom arrangements, and longer-lived results. Grenier used this logic to get Hotel Tonight into Facebook's mobile ads alpha by positioning as a non-gaming case study the platform needed.

**3. Company DNA**
Two sub-questions:
- *Risk profile:* Does your team genuinely have comfort with true first-mover chaos — broken tracking, offensive content adjacency, no playbook? Be honest.
- *Channel maturity:* Companies without a working baseline on Google and Facebook are rarely in a good position to run experiments on emerging channels. "I've got a good foundation" is the right precondition for riskier exploration.

**Sizing the investment**
Run the three filters together to calibrate headcount and time. Strong medium-fit + mature channel + large team = 3 dedicated people. Weak channel DNA + small team = half a person, maximum. Cap most experiments at one quarter; fishing-level signal should appear in a month or less.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have at least one baseline channel working (Google, Facebook, or equivalent).
- You are evaluating whether to add a new channel layer, not replace a broken one.
- You are facing channel FOMO ("everyone's on TikTok, should we be?") and need a structured filter to cut through noise.

**Does not apply when:**
- Your core problem is product-market fit, not channel selection. If the market itself has shifted, launching a new channel won't fix it — you have to re-establish PMF first.
- You are at the very earliest stage before any acquisition channel is working at scale.

## Signature questions

1. What does this channel specifically do well, and does that strength actually serve how our customers discover or engage with our product?
2. Where is this channel in its growth curve — is it early enough that the product will change out from under us if we build on it?
3. How does this channel monetize, and can we position our company as a proof point they need for their ad business?
4. Do we have the risk appetite for a true first-mover experiment — broken tracking, no playbook, possible wasted spend?
5. Have we already got something working on the foundational channels, or are we chasing something new before we've built a foundation?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Grenier explicitly calls out: companies chase channel hotness without ever asking whether the channel's strengths map to their customer or business needs. The second failure is treating a new channel as the fix for a PMF problem. If the market has shifted, adding a channel just distributes the wrong message more broadly. The framework is a filter for channel selection, not a substitute for confirming you still have product-market fit in the current market.
