## One-liner

A behavioral observation system for evaluating early-stage founders that reads subtle social signals — defensiveness, earnestness, co-founder dynamics, and scrappiness — rather than judging idea merit alone.

## The framework

Jessica's framework consists of six observable behavioral signals she evaluated in 10-minute YC interviews while her technical co-founders assessed the product:

1. **Non-defensiveness / intellectual openness** — When challenged, do founders engage like a tennis match or close up? Defensive founders rarely pivot well; open founders are ready to learn and adapt their idea toward what users actually want.

2. **Earnestness / authenticity** — Are founders solving this because they genuinely care about the problem, or because a startup seems like a cool thing to do right now? Earnestness shows up as honesty about what they don't know, not smoke-and-mirrors.

3. **Domain expertise with personal connection** — Did this person discover the problem by living it? Fixing a broken industry you know from the inside is a strong signal.

4. **Co-founder relationship health** — Do they finish each other's sentences or block each other from speaking? Founders with shared history (school, work, family) carry more trust into the hard moments.

5. **Scrappiness / making things happen** — Does their past behavior show they will go to unreasonable lengths to move forward? The Obama O's cereal box story is the canonical example: effort that proves desperation and commitment regardless of idea quality.

6. **Commitment / burning the boat** — Are they actually leaving their job, or keeping a safety net? Founders who stay on salary at Google rarely survive when it gets hard.

Jessica validated the framework by tracking gut predictions over years and checking outcomes. No founder she strongly opposed has gone on to major success.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** evaluating founders at the earliest possible stage, before there is meaningful traction data. The product idea is almost irrelevant — YC funded Airbnb despite hating the idea. This is a people-bet, not an idea-bet.

**Does not apply when:** there is significant traction, an operating company, or enough product signal to evaluate the business on its merits. The framework is explicitly designed for a 10-minute window with no data.

**Charisma is not a signal.** Charismatic founders who are "full of baloney" doing a startup because it's cool will pass on charm alone. Substance has to underlie any charm, or the signal is false.

## Signature questions

1. When we push back on your idea, do you engage with the challenge or defend your position?
2. Why this problem — is there a personal or professional experience that connects you to it, or did you pick it because it seemed like the right market?
3. How long have you and your co-founder known each other, and have you worked together before?
4. Are you going to quit your job? When?
5. Tell me about something you built before — something scrappy, even if it failed.

## Common misuse

Mistaking charisma for substance. Jessica explicitly warns against getting fooled by a compelling presenter who hasn't earned their conviction through domain expertise or personal connection to the problem. The framework is designed to detect authentic commitment, not performance of commitment. A highly polished, charismatic founder should trigger more scrutiny, not less.
