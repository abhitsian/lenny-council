## One-liner

Remove friction by doing manually—and temporarily—whatever would make the experience feel magical, and stay willing to kill any product that stops serving the actual mission.

## The framework

Maya articulates two practices that worked together at Anchor:

**1. Friction obsession with manual scaffolding**
Identify the single biggest barrier blocking users from getting value. If you can't automate it yet, do it by hand—completely hidden from the user—until you can. The goal is to make the experience feel complete and magical, not to seem efficient. At Anchor, this meant hiring college interns to manually submit podcasts to Apple Podcasts on behalf of users pressing a single "distribute" button. Users got a magical outcome; the manual work happened backstage.

**2. Kill your darlings when the mission demands it**
If a product is working—users love it, retention is good, numbers are green—but there is no path from here to the actual mission, move on. Anchor did this twice: killing a beloved social audio product (1.0) to build creator tools (2.0), then reversing course on their "we're not podcasts" stance when export-to-RSS turned out to be the hockey stick trigger. Each move was painful. Each was right.

The two practices connect: both require prioritizing the mission and the 80% over the vocal 20%, and both require ignoring short-term signals that feel like success.

**Supporting discipline: dogfooding as empathy infrastructure**
Maya requires her product team to create their own podcasts. Not as an exercise—as a prerequisite to understanding creator pain. Secondhand user research doesn't generate the same visceral understanding as hitting the same friction points yourself, especially when building tools for people whose livelihood depends on them.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have a clear mission that the current product may not reach at scale
- A user experience requires navigating steps that are technically doable manually before they can be automated
- Your user base is vocal and retained, but growth is plateauing in a way that feels structural
- You're building tools for creators, professionals, or anyone whose frustration is rooted in operational complexity

**Doesn't apply when:**
- Your mission is narrow enough that the vocal 20% *is* your target market
- Doing things manually creates compliance, legal, or trust risks that outweigh the UX benefit
- You don't have a clear mission to use as the deciding filter—without it, "kill your darlings" becomes arbitrary pivoting

## Signature questions

1. What is the single most painful step between a user deciding to do something and actually being able to do it? Can we just do that step for them while we build the real solution?
2. If we zoom out to our original mission, does this product—even though it's working—have a path to getting us there?
3. Are we building for the 80% or are we optimizing for the feedback of the 20% who are most vocal?
4. Does everyone on this product team use the product the way a real user would? If not, what friction are they missing?
5. If we had to kill this product today and start over to serve the mission better, what would we build instead?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Maya explicitly warns against: learning "don't listen to users" from the Anchor 1.0 story, then applying it reflexively to Anchor 2.0 when users clearly told them what they needed (podcast export). She did exactly that—dug in for six months on "they don't know what they want"—and it delayed the hockey stick. The lesson is not "users are wrong." The lesson is that the mission is the filter. When a user request advances the mission, act on it. When it deepens a profitable niche that won't scale to the mission, don't.
