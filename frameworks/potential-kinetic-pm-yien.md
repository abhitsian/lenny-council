## One-liner

Product management is the practice of converting a team's potential energy into realized customer value — through constraint-setting, decision logging, direct customer exposure, and obsession with the final deliverable.

## The framework

Kevin Yien uses a physics metaphor as the organizing principle: a team of engineers, designers, and others represents **potential energy**. The PM's job is to minimize loss in the conversion from potential to kinetic — getting as much of that talent into actual customer value as possible.

The model has four concrete practices:

**1. Draw the perimeter (constraint-setting)**
The PM's primary job when working with eng and design is to define the boundaries of the problem space — not to solve it. The best word for this is *constraints*: who is the customer, what job are they trying to do, what tradeoffs have we already made (e.g., speed over consistency). Once constraints are set, engineers and designers can push against the edges and come up with the most creative solutions. Underdefined space produces underdefined solutions.

**2. Obsess over the final deliverable**
Drawing the perimeter is not enough. PMs also need to be present at the other end — paying attention to whether value is actually reaching the customer. Yien spent a full week tuning the milliseconds of a menu animation on Square's restaurant POS because it materially affected adoption by experienced bartenders. A PM who delegates that detail is abdicating a core responsibility.

**3. Keep a decision log**
Product sense — the ability to make good decisions with insufficient data — is a learnable skill. It improves through reps: make a decision, document the rationale, track the outcome. PMs who wait to only log decisions made inside their job miss the majority of available reps. You can log bets on external companies, competitor moves, anyone's product roadmap — the outcome will find its way back to you.

**4. Direct customer exposure (not filtered input)**
Research reports, sales summaries, and customer success notes are processed by someone before they reach you. Yien calls this "bent glass." PMs need raw exposure — direct conversation with actual users — as the foundation for the mental model they build of the customer. The automation tactics (Gong alerts → Zapier → Calendly) exist to make this consistent, not to replace it.

A hiring extension of the model: the **unsell email**. At offer stage, Yien sends candidates a blunt list of the things that will probably reinforce their fears about the role. Candidates who remain equally excited are the right hires. The goal is to match people to environments where they can convert their own potential.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are working on a team that builds for customers you are not yourself
- You have enough ambiguity that the perimeter hasn't been drawn and the team is spinning
- You are trying to improve PM craft over time, not just execute in the current quarter
- You are hiring for culture and environment fit, not just skill

**Does not apply when:**
- The team is building for themselves (Stripe, Figma, Twilio) — in those cases the builders *are* the customer and the PM role may be minimal or unnecessary
- The decision log is used as a substitute for building real products — Yien explicitly warns it is a complement, not a replacement

## Signature questions

1. What constraints have you actually drawn for your team? If you removed them, what would change?
2. When did you last spend a full day on a detail that felt like "the engineer's job"? What did you find?
3. What decisions have you logged in the past 90 days, and how many outcomes have you tracked?
4. How much of your customer knowledge comes from direct exposure versus filtered reports? What's the ratio?
5. When you're hiring, what are the three things most likely to make this person regret joining — and have you said them out loud?

## Common misuse

Yien explicitly names two failure modes:

**Perimeter without presence.** Some PMs interpret "draw the perimeter" as license to stay high-level and hands-off once the box is set. Yien's counter-example — spending a week on animation timing at Square — makes the point directly. The PM who says "I defined the requirements, now figure it out" is skipping the second half of the job.

**Decision log as armchair analysis.** The log is a reps-building practice, not a substitute for shipping. Yien's warning: "If you think that you can just sit back in your chair, look out at the market, make a bunch of calls and be like, 'Look at how smart I'm getting without actually being hands-on with building a product,' you're not actually going to get any better." (00:42:12)
