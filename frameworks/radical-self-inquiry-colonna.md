## One-liner

Leadership development through three inputs — practical skills, radical self-inquiry, and shared honest experience — produces enhanced leadership and, more importantly, greater resilience without sacrificing yourself in the process.

## The framework

Colonna's equation, sketched on a dry-erase board at Naropa University:

**Practical skills + Radical self-inquiry + Shared experiences = Enhanced leadership + Greater resilience**

**Practical skills** — the "how" people usually show up asking for: communication, decision-making, delegation, execution. This is the smallest part of the work.

**Radical self-inquiry** — asking questions that take your breath away. The core move is a single question: *How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?* The word "complicit" is precise — you are driving the getaway car, not sticking up the bank teller. The question surfaces agency, not guilt. Other questions in this category: What am I not saying that I need to say? What am I saying that's not being heard? What's being said that I'm not hearing?

**Shared experiences** — sitting in a circle with people who just have your back, telling the truth about what's actually going on, without fixing or judging. The entrepreneurial community is socialized to bullshit everyone including themselves. A women's circle, a CEO boot camp, a good podcast — anything that breaks the "all our companies are moving up and to the right" performance.

**Greater resilience** — this is the real output, not a bonus. Colonna circles it on the whiteboard. The goal is not peak performance; it is not killing yourself in the process of becoming a leader.

The underlying mechanism: childhood patterns and unsorted baggage don't disappear when someone becomes a CEO. Team dysfunction almost never comes from lack of talent, strategy, or execution — it is the leader's unexamined patterns made organizational. "Until you make the unconscious conscious, it will direct your life and you will call it fate" (Carl Jung, via Colonna).

Growth mindset is held loosely. When you nail a mindset to the floor as the correct way, you have become fixed. Stay attached to the growth; hold mindset loosely.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A leader or team keeps recreating the same dysfunction despite tactical interventions
- Someone is outwardly succeeding but internally exhausted, anxious, or numb
- A team can't make decisions independently of the founder
- Busyness has become a persistent identity rather than a phase

**Does not apply when:**
- The actual problem is genuinely a skills gap or resource shortage (rare but real)
- Someone is in acute crisis and needs clinical support, not coaching questions
- The leader is unwilling to examine their own role — the whole framework depends on the person with the most power being willing to do the work first

## Signature questions

1. How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?
2. How does it serve me to feel completely busy to the point where I feel exhausted — and is there a more conscious way to get that feeling?
3. What am I not saying that I need to say? (And its corollaries: What am I saying that's not being heard? What's being said that I'm not hearing?)
4. Who would you be without the story of who you are?
5. At the end of my days, what would I like the people who come after me to say about me?

## Common misuse

The framework is heard as "sort yourself out or you'll fail." Colonna is explicit that this is not the intent. The complicity question is not an accusation of responsibility — it evokes agency. People deflect into spiritual bypassing (ayahuasca, meditation retreats, growth mindset as a fixed rule) instead of sitting with the actual question. The other misuse: treating radical self-inquiry as a one-time clearing event. It is a daily practice. You ask the same questions every day because you are always a work in progress. Tacking self-inquiry onto a leadership skills curriculum as a module misses the point — it is the center, not the supplement.
