## One-liner

Build retention-focused B2B software by making opinionated products, using taste and empathy over metrics, keeping teams small and high-ownership, and treating craft as the condition for customer trust — not a trade-off against speed.

## The framework

Karri Saarinen calls this the **Linear Method** — a published set of principles for how Linear builds product, hires, and operates. It rests on four interlocking pillars:

**1. Opinionated software by default**
Productivity software should make decisions for users, not expose flexibility. Every feature should have a clear "right way" that eliminates the meta-work of configuring the tool. Karri's design mantra: "design something for someone." Designing for everyone produces generalized solutions; designing for someone produces optimized ones.

**2. Consistent cycles over infinite backlogs**
Teams work in focused cycles (similar to sprints, but auto-scheduled and without the connotation of sprinting). The cycle establishes a committed list for a window of time, gives the team permission to ignore everything else, and creates an audit trail when priorities shift. Cycles are optional and can be turned on or off per team.

**3. Craft through fast iteration, not upfront perfection**
Quality doesn't come from polishing before you build — it comes from shipping to yourself early, then to a small cohort of opted-in customers, then to everyone. The sequence: design explorations → working build in the app (internal only) → opt-in beta with one to ten customers → general release with full polish. Design reviews happen through hands-on use (Karri tries the product in different states himself), not formal approval gates.

**4. No metrics-driven goals; all-company customer empathy instead**
Linear doesn't set per-feature metrics targets. The substitute is continuous customer proximity: shared Slack channels with customers, weekly calls, recorded user sessions shared across the team, and direct support engagement by everyone including founders. The goal is for every person in the company to have enough context to use judgment — what Karri calls the "magic" side of the magic-and-science balance.

**Supporting practices:**
- **One PM (head of product)**: Project ownership sits with engineer-designer pairs. A project lead — engineer or designer, assigned per project, not by title — owns scoping, communication, and delivery.
- **Segment-specific PMF**: Product-market fit is treated as a segment-by-segment map, not a binary. Linear sequenced from early-stage startups → growth companies → larger enterprises, getting fit in each segment before expanding.
- **Paid work trials**: Every hire does a paid multi-day trial alongside the team before an offer, working on a real vague problem statement with codebase access. This surfaces product sensibility and lets candidates evaluate the company.
- **Never more than double headcount per year**: Smaller, higher-caliber teams outperform larger average teams. Karri's rule of thumb from experience at Coinbase and Airbnb.
- **Go slow to go fast**: Taking time to think before doing beats rushing and reworking. Karri's personal operating motto.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're building B2B workflow software where retention and long-term trust matter more than acquisition volume.
- Your team is small enough (under ~100) that direct customer empathy can substitute for instrumented metrics.
- Your founders have strong product taste and can credibly set quality bars without process scaffolding.
- You're competing in a crowded category where design and craft can be a differentiator — Karri's argument is that once a category matures, high-quality design becomes table stakes, not a luxury.

**Doesn't apply when:**
- You're in a high-velocity transactional business (e-commerce, growth-loop consumer apps) where rapid experimentation and per-feature metrics are the right feedback mechanism.
- You don't have founders or senior people with genuine product taste to anchor quality decisions — the method requires taste-holders, not just process.
- You need to scale headcount rapidly; the "never more than double" principle depends on being selective, which gets harder under hypergrowth pressure.

## Signature questions

1. **Is this the main quest or a side quest?** — Before taking on any initiative, does it directly advance the product for customers, or is it a detour that sounds good but doesn't?
2. **Have we shipped this to ourselves yet?** — Don't spend time polishing a design before the team has used a working version in the actual product.
3. **Which customer segment does this fit, and how strong is that fit?** — Product-market fit is a map, not a flag. Where exactly do we have it, and how much territory do we need before expanding?
4. **Can this person tell me *why* a decision was right or wrong — not just that they didn't like it?** — The test for product sensibility in hiring: articulated reasoning, not instinctive reaction.
5. **Are we using data because it's the right input, or because we're afraid to make the call ourselves?** — The question that separates judgment-driven decisions from metrics-as-cover.

## Common misuse

The failure mode Karri explicitly calls out: treating craft as **upfront perfection**. Teams that won't ship anything until it's polished never get real feedback. The Linear Method pushes things into the product fast — janky, internal-only — and iterates from there. The polish comes at the end of the cycle, not the beginning. Separately, the method gets misread as anti-metrics; it isn't. Linear does track weekly active users at the company level and pulls usage data when investigating a specific question. The distinction Karri draws: metrics answer questions you already have; they don't replace the judgment about which questions to ask.
