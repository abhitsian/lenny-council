## One-liner

Before touching a tool or system, name the problem and the people it affects — only then design the solution.

## The framework

Austin calls it **PPS: Problem → People → System**. Three questions in sequence:

1. **Problem** — What is the discrete issue this person is trying to solve? Not the symptom ("I need admin access"), but the underlying job ("I want onboarding to be less manual for new sales hires").
2. **People** — Who is involved? Does the decision-maker need sign-off from the CRO? Do the end users need training? Are there stakeholders who would block or be affected if you skip them?
3. **System** — Only after the first two are clear: what system change, tool configuration, or build actually solves it?

The framework runs alongside a second principle Austin applies together with PPS: **Build AND Buy** (not build versus buy). Once the system answer is clear, the question is never "should we build or should we buy?" but "what part can we buy to get 90% there, and what do we build on top to get the last 10%?" The vendor is committed to you; building on top of a third-party tool means you can get accelerated outcomes that a pure in-house build wouldn't get.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies** when someone (a stakeholder, a report, a vendor) arrives with a request that sounds like a tool or permission problem. The urge to jump straight to the system is the signal that PPS is needed.

**Applies** at any company size, but Austin says it becomes structurally important around 100–150 people, when the "village approach" to systems breaks down and someone has to own how data flows through tools.

**Does not replace** product-market fit decisions or technical architecture choices that require deeper domain expertise. PPS is a framing tool, not a full spec process.

## Signature questions

1. What problem is this person actually trying to solve — not the tool request, but the underlying job?
2. Who are the people involved, and is there anyone whose buy-in or awareness we're missing?
3. Are we designing a system that solves this, or just reacting to whoever complained loudest?
4. If we buy a tool to get 90% of the way there, what exactly do we build on top to get the rest?
5. What does this system look like a year from now if we don't change anything — is that catastrophic?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Austin explicitly warns against: **jumping directly to the system**. Someone says "can you give me admin access to this tool" and the MarTech person says yes or no without ever asking what they're trying to accomplish. Solving at the system level without understanding the problem and stakeholders leads to permission sprawl, duplicate tools, and tech debt that compounds as the company scales. Austin also flags the flip side: building an entire solution in-house when a third-party tool would get you 90% there faster — treating build vs. buy as an either/or rather than a both/and.
