## One-liner

A 2x2 matrix that predicts B2B SaaS outcome potential by plotting workflow frequency (daily vs. infrequent) against workflow scope (everyone in the company vs. a single department), with high-frequency-niche as the richest starting ground for billion-dollar outcomes.

## The framework

Two dimensions define the quadrant:

**Dimension 1 — Scope**: Does the workflow apply to every department ("everyone workflows") or just specific departments ("niche workflows")?

**Dimension 2 — Frequency**: Is the workflow executed daily / multiple times a week (frequent) or once a month or less (infrequent)?

This produces four quadrants:

| | Frequent | Infrequent |
|---|---|---|
| **Everyone** | Hardest to enter; dominated by Microsoft, Google, Atlassian (email, calendaring, Slack, Notion, Confluence) | Moderate challenge; e.g., expense management — nearly universal but rare (Concur territory) |
| **Niche** | **High-NI** — richest zone for B2B SaaS: Jira, Salesforce, Calendly, recruiting tools, martech | Hardest to monetize; low-frequency, single-department workflows like FP&A planning tools |

**High-NI (High-frequency Niche)** is Oji's target zone: a specific department cares deeply, uses the tool daily, and the workflow is mission-critical to how they earn money or do their job. Examples: Jira for engineering teams, Calendly for salespeople and recruiters, Salesforce for sales orgs.

**The framework is not static.** Strategy is navigating from a lower quadrant toward a higher one. Typeform (low-frequency, near-everyone) aims to become high-NI for marketers. Atlassian (high-NI with Jira) reaches for high-everyone with Confluence. Companies below find a sub-department that uses the workflow daily, then expand from that foothold.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Evaluating a new B2B SaaS problem space before committing years of work
- Deciding where to deploy early investment as a VC or operator
- Shaping product strategy around which customer segment to deepen into
- Assessing whether a market has natural ceiling on outcome potential

**Does not apply when:**
- Building consumer software (frequency and scope map differently)
- The startup is built on technical or scientific innovation that precedes market validation (e.g., OpenAI-style bets)
- The company is already at scale and the question is execution, not market selection

Oji explicitly cautions against applying any framework without understanding its underlying logic: "You have to go deeper. Because as situations change... if you understand the fundamentals, you'll be able to use frameworks in a much more productive way because you'll adapt it to your stage."

## Signature questions

1. What specific workflow are you solving — and is it used daily or a few times a year?
2. Does this workflow affect everyone from the doorman to the CEO, or is it owned by one department?
3. Which quadrant are you in today, and what's your path to high-frequency or broader scope?
4. Who in the company considers this workflow mission-critical to how they earn money or do their job?
5. If you're in a lower quadrant, what module or sub-segment could move you toward high-NI?

## Common misuse

Treating the quadrant as destiny. Founders look at their workflow, land in a lower quadrant, and either abandon the idea or treat the framework as a verdict. Oji's actual point is that quadrant position is a starting assessment — the work of strategy is active navigation toward better quadrants. The framework predicts probability, not outcome. He also warns explicitly against blind framework application without understanding the underlying logic: companies at different stages (early, middle, scaling) need different approaches, and misapplying a framework to the wrong stage is as harmful as ignoring it.
