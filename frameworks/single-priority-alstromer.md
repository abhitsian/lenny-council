## One-liner

Early-stage startups can only work on one thing at a time, so the moment you start talking strategy, you've already lost track of what your single priority actually is.

## The framework

Gustaf distills execution discipline into a few concrete moves:

**The priority stack rule.** At any point in time, a startup has a ranked list of things to do, and only the top item matters. Not two items. Not a portfolio. One. Everything else waits until that one is done.

**The Office Hours probe.** In regular one-on-ones, Gustaf opens with: "What's holding you back from moving faster?" Not "give me an update." Not "what's your strategy?" This forces founders to name the actual blocker — and they usually don't know until they say it out loud.

**The accountability loop.** In Group Office Hours, every company answers: "What were your goals for the last two weeks? Did you hit them? What got in the way?" Founders are competitive. Not wanting to show up with nothing creates real forward pressure.

**The strategy flag.** Whenever a founder wants a "strategy conversation," Gustaf reads it as a signal they don't actually know their priorities. Strategy assumes you can run multiple things simultaneously. Small startups cannot.

**The indifference reframe.** Most non-customers are not rejecting you — they're indifferent. They signed up and forgot. Indifference means you have another shot. This frees founders from fear of rejection, which is the main reason they avoid talking to users.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies:** Pre-product-market fit. Any company under 20 people where every week needs tangible progress. Moments when founders are debating resource allocation across multiple workstreams simultaneously.

**Doesn't apply cleanly:** Companies past PMF that genuinely need to allocate across product lines. Gustaf acknowledges that growth-team thinking (scalable, parallel experimentation) is exactly right *after* something is working — it's just the wrong model before.

## Signature questions

1. What is the single most important thing you're working on right now — not a list, just one?
2. What's holding you back from moving faster on that?
3. What were your goals for the last two weeks, and did you hit them? If not, what got in the way?
4. Are you talking to customers this week, or are you building based on what you already believe you know?
5. When you say "strategy," what specifically are you hoping to decide that you can't decide by just ranking your priorities?

## Common misuse

Founders hear "one priority" and interpret it as permission to stop thinking about everything else. Gustaf's point is the opposite: you need to keep re-ranking constantly, because the top item changes. The trap is locking in on a priority that's no longer the real blocker while telling yourself you're being focused. The probe question — "what's holding you back from moving faster?" — is how you catch this. If you stop asking the question, the discipline collapses.
