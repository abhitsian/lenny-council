## One-liner

Getting press is a human persuasion game, not a service transaction — it only works when you understand what each editor is trying to do for their audience, then shape your story around that.

## The framework

Feifer structures the process in three steps:

**Step 1 — Prep**
- Define what press is actually *for* before chasing it. Growth? Investor credibility? Positioning? If you can't answer that, stop.
- Inventory what parts of your story would be relevant to different publication missions. The same company may have entirely different angles for a trade pub vs. a lifestyle roundup.
- Know that press is unpredictable. Treat it as an add-on to growth strategy, not a primary channel.

**Step 2 — Find (who to pitch)**
- Target the publication whose audience matches your goal — not the most prestigious outlet, the most relevant one. A hot dog food truck doesn't belong in Entrepreneur; it belongs in the local food section.
- Read what the publication actually publishes to infer their editorial mission. No about page will tell you this directly. The coverage pattern will.
- Go to the writer, not the editor-in-chief. Find whoever covers your category by reading bylines, not org charts.
- Prioritize freelancers over staff writers. Freelancers are hungrier for stories because they only get paid when they find and place them.

**Step 3 — Pitch**
- Send a short, clearly personalized email (three paragraphs max). Signal that you know this person's work — but only if you actually do.
- Frame the story for their audience, not for your brand. Don't lead with your product's value proposition; lead with the story the editor can tell through you.
- Be human. Press releases and marketing copy fail because they read as non-human. A real email to a real person beats a polished pitch template every time.
- Consider a fourth path: you don't have to be the feature. You can be part of a trend story, provide a data-driven report, or position yourself as a quotable expert hooking onto breaking news.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have a clear, tactical reason for press (awareness of a launch, investor validation, reaching a new customer segment, building founder authority).
- Your story has a counterintuitive angle, a visible problem-solving moment, or a place in a broader trend — something the editor can use to serve their audience.
- You're at a stage where coverage would actually move something for you.

**Doesn't apply when:**
- You're too early for press to have any effect.
- Your goal is local sales but you're chasing national publications (or vice versa).
- You're B2B in a niche space where consumer/business publications won't reach your buyers — in that case, trade publications, expert positioning, or data-driven surveys may work better.
- You want press because you feel you deserve it. That's not a tactical reason.

## Signature questions

1. What do you actually need press for right now — and if you can't answer that in one sentence, should you even be pursuing it?
2. Who is this publication actually trying to serve, and does your story help them serve that audience?
3. Are you pitching the editor-in-chief out of visibility, or the person who actually covers your category?
4. Is there a problem-solving story buried inside your journey that an editor's audience would want to extract lessons from — not just the product, but the ingenuity moment?
5. Are you pitching yourself as the feature, or could you be part of a trend/context story where you provide the entry point?

## Common misuse

Feifer's explicit warning: treating press as a primary growth strategy. Coverage is unpredictable — it might drive thousands of signups, or it might do nothing except give you a logo to put on your website. Both outcomes happen. The founders who get burned are those who bank on press to solve a growth problem, then feel betrayed when nothing happens. The founders who use it well treat it as validation currency they deploy on their own terms — in ads, on websites, in investor emails — not as a direct traffic driver they depend on.

A related failure mode he calls out: emailing the most visible person at a publication (the editor-in-chief) because they're easiest to find, rather than finding the person who actually assigns or writes stories in your category. Visibility in the masthead is inversely correlated with bandwidth to source your pitch.
