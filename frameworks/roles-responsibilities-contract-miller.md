## One-liner

Each member of the product/design/engineering/data quartet writes mutual role expectations independently, then the group debates and commits to a shared contract — revisiting it when execution or velocity breaks down.

## The framework

Nikita's exercise has four concrete steps:

1. **Individual writing.** Each function (PM, designer, engineering manager, data scientist) writes two things: what they believe their own role is, and what they expect from each counterpart. Done independently before any group session.

2. **Group review and debate.** The group reads each other's documents. This surfaces where assumptions diverge — who owns experiment briefs, who tracks sprint execution, what "shared responsibility" actually means for a specific deliverable.

3. **The contract.** The group converges on an explicit written agreement about roles, responsibilities, and shared ownership. Atlassian's playbook format is one vehicle; the specific document matters less than the act of committing together.

4. **Cascade.** Leaders run the same exercise throughout the org, not just at the leadership tier. Every team establishes its own version of the contract.

**Revisit trigger:** The contract isn't a one-time artifact. Nikita recommends reviewing every 3–6 months if the team is disciplined, but in practice it gets revisited when execution or velocity visibly breaks down — when something falls off the rails and nobody is sure whose it was.

**The velocity diagnosis built in.** When reviewing the contract for execution failures, Nikita uses two specific breakdowns to locate the problem:
- *Decision velocity* (time from "we need to do a thing" to a committed decision) — typically a PM ownership gap
- *Development velocity* (ticket decomposition speed, cycle time) — typically a shared PM + engineering manager gap

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A new team forms or major re-orgs shift functional boundaries
- A team is shipping slowly but doesn't know where the bottleneck is
- "Who owns this?" conversations keep recurring across sprints
- A remote or distributed team has trouble coordinating asynchronously on complex decisions

**Doesn't apply when:**
- A team is 2–3 people and the overhead exceeds the ambiguity (early-stage startups often don't need this level of formality)
- Roles are genuinely novel or undefined — the exercise requires some working vocabulary for what each function does

## Signature questions

1. What do you expect from your engineering manager that they might not know they're responsible for?
2. When we shipped slowly last quarter, where specifically did the bottleneck sit — decision-making or execution?
3. Is your data scientist embedded in the team or borrowed from a central pool? How does that affect your velocity?
4. If something falls through the cracks on this team, who finds out first — and is that the right person?
5. Are we confusing outcome clarity with execution clarity? We know the goal — do we know whose job it is to get us moving?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Nikita explicitly names: swinging too hard on outcomes and forgetting output as the indicator. Teams that do this contract exercise but then only track OKRs and goal clarity end up with everyone aligned on *what* they're optimizing for but no one accountable for *how fast* they're getting there. The contract needs to include ownership of execution velocity — sprint delivery, cycle time, ticket decomposition — not just strategic responsibilities. If the contract is all strategy and no shipping cadence, it won't catch the most common breakdown.
