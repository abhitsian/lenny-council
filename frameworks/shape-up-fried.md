## One-liner

Replace time estimates with fixed appetites, cap every feature at six weeks with two people, and kill anything that doesn't fit — because work expands to fill the time you give it.

## The framework

Shape Up is 37signals' method for building products, documented at basecamp.com/shapeup. Its core parts:

**Appetites, not estimates.** An appetite is the maximum time you're willing to spend on a feature. Six weeks is the absolute ceiling. "An estimate is like how long do we think it's going to take? And however long it takes is however long it takes... We instead have appetites."

**Two people max.** One programmer, one designer. No 18-person meetings, no slow decision chains. Most features run shorter than six weeks; the cap just prevents runaway scope.

**Shape the work before assigning it.** Senior people write a brief pitch describing what to build and the rough approach. The team then gets latitude to figure out how — no pre-made to-do lists or tickets. They create the work that needs to happen.

**Hill charts track progress.** Work is not linear; it's a hill. Left side = still figuring out how. Right side = pure execution. If work is still on the left side of the hill when time runs out, it dies. "If there's any work that's left over that's still on the left side of the hill, meaning we're still pushing it up, we don't know how we're going to do it and we're at our time limit, it almost certainly dies."

**Six-week cycles, two-week cool downs.** After each cycle, two weeks of unscheduled time. People tighten loose ends, fix bugs, explore side interests. Meanwhile, shapers prepare the next round. Not stopping work — doing different kinds of work.

**Trading concessions.** Scope gets adjusted continuously during the cycle as surprises surface. The team and shapers negotiate what stays in and what drops to fit the appetite.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

Works best for small, focused software teams where scope is the variable and time is fixed. Most useful in product orgs that keep getting trapped in never-ending projects or whose estimates keep slipping.

Harder to apply wholesale in large organizations with enterprise sales commitments, dependency-heavy roadmaps, or when the product itself requires long-running R&D cycles. It can be adopted partially — even just switching from estimates to appetites carries value. Fried's advice: try it on a low-criticality project first. "Keep working the way you're working, but some future project, some low criticality project, try this new method on that."

The biggest failure mode is attempting it overnight on something that matters. "People trying to make a rapid, fundamentally, 180 degree changes quickly is a great way to fall flat on your face."

## Signature questions

1. What's our appetite for this — how much are we actually willing to spend, not how long do we think it'll take?
2. Is this work still on the left side of the hill, or are we on the downslope?
3. Can two people — one designer, one programmer — really deliver this in six weeks? If not, what do we cut?
4. What are the trade-offs we're making right now, and which concessions are we willing to trade?
5. Are we carrying projects that never end? What's keeping them alive past the appetite we gave them?

## Common misuse

The most explicit failure Fried warns against: adopting Shape Up cold-turkey on an important project when your team has never worked this way. The first cycle will go badly. Teams that try it on critical work and fail conclude the method doesn't work, and they'll never try again — "because everyone knows how bad it went last time on something that really mattered."

The second misuse: treating six weeks as a soft guideline. If you let projects run seven, eight, or nine weeks, you don't actually have a system. The appetite only works if you're willing to kill scope — or the whole feature — when time is up.
