## One-liner

The human data market has permanently shifted from cheap generalist labor to scarce expert knowledge; the only defensible moat is trusted access to the right audience at zero acquisition cost.

## The framework

Lord describes a four-part structure for winning in the expert data market:

**1. Recognize the market shift.**
Pre-training gains asymptoted because the internet ran out of novel data. Post-training now drives all frontier model improvement. Post-training requires domain experts — people who can break models in advanced STEM, law, finance, medicine — not generalists drawing bounding boxes. Companies still optimizing for low-cost generalist supply are operating on an obsolete thesis.

**2. Build CAC/LTV structural advantage.**
Most competitors acquire experts through LinkedIn recruiters, Instagram ads, and performance marketing — paying millions per month with poor conversion. Lord's model: partner with 1,600 universities (92% of top 500 schools), inherit a decade of brand trust from Handshake's core consumer product, and acquire experts at near-zero CAC. LTV compounds when you treat contributors as professionals with community, cohort-based onboarding, and a clear training path — not as transactional piece-workers. High retention on projects follows.

**3. Community and instructional design over transaction.**
Experts expect to be treated differently from gig workers. Lord's team built: (a) cohort onboarding with instructional designers so contributors actually learn how to break models, (b) community groups and peer feed so contributors can see how others are learning, (c) graduated project tracks — internal Handshake projects first, then premium customer projects for top performers. This is the experience differentiation that justifies trust from both contributors and frontier labs.

**4. Data quality as the only currency that matters.**
Labs care about three things in order: quality, volume, speed. Internal post-training teams, GPU capacity, and per-unit data assessment let Lord's team sit directly with researchers and say "here's what we're seeing in the data." Treating data production like a movie production — pay for a unit, verify quality, then sell the same package to multiple labs — is how you build gross margin and trust simultaneously.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You already have a community or audience that includes high-credential specialists (PhDs, licensed professionals, domain experts)
- Your supply acquisition is organic or near-zero CAC — an inherited network, not a paid acquisition engine
- The market you're entering has shifted from volume metrics to quality metrics
- You can differentiate on contributor experience, not just on pricing or volume capacity

**Does not apply when:**
- The domain still rewards scale over precision (early-stage generalist training tasks, annotation work that doesn't require expertise)
- You're entering the market without an existing trusted audience — the structural advantage Lord describes is not replicable from scratch through performance marketing
- Your market is verifiable-commoditized (e.g., rubric scoring where model-as-judge can fully automate quality assessment)

## Signature questions *(council field)*

1. What is your actual CAC for acquiring one PhD-level contributor? How does that compare to the LTV of their data output over 12 months?
2. Are you treating contributors like professionals or like gig workers? What does your onboarding look like, and what's the drop-off rate before first completed project?
3. Where does the audience trust already exist? What relationship are you inheriting, and could a competitor replicate that relationship with money?
4. What specific capability domain are the labs investing in right now, and do you have concentrated supply in that domain — or just general access to smart people?
5. Is the data you're producing improving model benchmarks? Do you have your own post-training capacity to actually measure the quality of your output before selling it?

## Common misuse

Lord explicitly warns against replicating the scale-through-transaction model: hiring hundreds of recruiters, spending tens of millions on performance marketing, and dropping experts directly into projects with no training. The failure mode is treating expert supply like commodity supply — you will have high churn, low data quality, and no defensible brand. The model only works if the audience trust is inherited, not purchased. Companies that try to shortcut this with LinkedIn outreach and paid ads will have structurally worse unit economics than any incumbent with an organic community.
