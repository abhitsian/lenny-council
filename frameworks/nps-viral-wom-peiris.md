## One-liner

Use NPS not as a satisfaction metric but as a predictor of exponential referral behavior — each band-jump doubles the viral coefficient — then use verbatim NPS comments to derive the product pillars that get customers to a nine or ten.

## The framework

Peiris built this in stages over a decade at Wise, where 70% of customer acquisition comes through word of mouth.

**Stage 1 — Measure word of mouth directly.** Ask customers how they heard about you, integrated into the signup flow, not bolted on after. Attribution models + direct sampling gives the true word-of-mouth percentage.

**Stage 2 — Overlay NPS on referral behavior.** The key discovery at Wise: when you overlay NPS score against actual referral invite rates and conversion, each one-band move roughly doubles the behavior:
- Scores 1–6: very low invite rates and low conversion of invitees
- Scores 7–8: doubles vs. the 1–6 group
- Scores 8–9: doubles again
- Scores 9–10: doubles again

This means improving NPS from 30 to 50 does not produce a linear bump — it increases the viral coefficient of your entire customer base exponentially.

**Stage 3 — Mine NPS comments for product pillars.** The numbers tell you the score; the comments tell you what to fix. At Wise, reading thousands of NPS comments week over week produced three repeating themes: make it cheaper, make it faster, make it easier to use. These became the three product pillars — price, speed, ease of use — with dedicated KPIs and team ownership under each.

**Stage 4 — Target 10x, not incremental improvement.** Customers only advocate when they've had an experience they didn't know was previously possible. Entering a market at 5.9% when the alternative is 6% gets you users but not advocates. Entering at 0.35% — eight to ten times cheaper — triggers word of mouth. The bar for recommendation is not "better"; it's "I didn't know this was possible."

Peiris operationalizes this as: what is the theoretical minimum cost, the theoretical maximum speed, for this product? Then work backwards from there over two or three years, rather than iterating towards it incrementally.

**Stage 5 — Build conviction over split tests.** Once the pillars are clear, switch from testing-to-validate to conviction-driven investment. "You can't split test your way to love." Experiments are useful for not breaking things and for understanding negative impact (holdouts, not significance tests), but not for deciding whether price or speed is worth investing in. Teams build conviction through qualitative insight and deep customer conversation, then build.

**Supporting loop — Product marketing within the product.** Closing the perception-reality gap is its own discipline. Customers may have received instant, cheap service but not *know* they did. The Wise comparison graph — showing visually what the bank charged vs. what Wise charged, with a share button — tripled referral rates on the post-transfer success screen. Making the value visible is part of the word of mouth engine.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- The product has a strong utilitarian value (financial, time, effort savings) that can be made quantifiably visible
- The business has thin margins per user and limited paid-acquisition budget — word of mouth is not a nice-to-have, it's the only scalable channel
- The product operates in a category where the existing experience is genuinely terrible (negative NPS category, like banking)
- The team has the patience and organizational runway to pursue 10x improvements over multi-year horizons

**Does not apply when:**
- The product category is low-stakes or low-frequency enough that users don't have occasion to recommend (they use it, they don't talk about it)
- You're optimizing for conversion-rate growth in an already-crowded, parity market — NPS optimization has high ROI only when there's a real advocacy gap to close
- The team is in early discovery mode and doesn't yet have enough NPS volume to make comment analysis meaningful

## Signature questions

1. What percentage of your new users last month found out about the product from a friend — and how exactly are you measuring that?
2. When you plot your NPS score distribution against actual referral invite rates, do the bands double? If not, what's different about your product category?
3. What are the three things customers keep saying in your NPS comments? Have you made those the named pillars of your roadmap?
4. When you entered your last market or launched your last major feature, were you 10x better than the alternative — or just better? What would "10x better" actually require?
5. Where in the product do customers receive real value but don't *know* they've received it? What would it take to close that perception gap?

## Common misuse

Peiris is explicit about the failure mode: teams read NPS scores, feel good about the number, and then optimize for conversion rate — which gives a one-off lift without changing the underlying viral behavior. The trap is treating NPS as a satisfaction KPI rather than a leading indicator of exponential referral behavior.

A related misuse: entering markets with a product that's marginally better and assuming word of mouth will follow. Peiris found that advocacy only kicked in at 8–10x price improvement; anything below that produced customers but not advocates. Teams confuse "people are using it" with "people are recommending it" — they are different thresholds, and the recommendation threshold is much higher.
