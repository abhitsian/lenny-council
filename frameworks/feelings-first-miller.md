## One-liner

Pick a product category where emotional connection drives switching — then build by asking how you want people to feel, use metrics only as an honesty check, and hire people with heartfelt intensity who can do the work without being managed.

## The framework

Josh Miller's framework has five interlocking moves:

**1. Choose the right category first.**
The feelings-first approach is not universally applicable. It works when the market is commoditized consumer software — where all competitors are functionally interchangeable and emotion is the only real differentiator. Miller chose the web browser explicitly because of this: everyone uses it, all browsers are essentially the same code (Chromium), and winning means getting people to choose yours emotionally rather than rationally. In cybersecurity software sold to government agencies, emotional resonance doesn't move the needle. Pick a category where it does.

**2. Define the feeling before defining the feature.**
At The Browser Company, product conversations start with: "How do we want to make someone feel?" — joy, speed, organization, focus, lightness, surprise. For the Peek feature, the conversation was about making tab-hopping feel "airy and effortless." This replaces the spec's opening question from "what should it do?" to "how should it feel?" Metrics are then used to check whether the feeling is being achieved, not to define the goal.

**3. Use metrics as guardrails, not drivers.**
Miller tracks D5/D7 retention (how many people use Arc at least five days a week) because it captures retention, engagement, and growth in one number and can't be gamed. But the team doesn't build toward that number — they build toward feelings, and check whether the number is moving. The distinction is: metrics tell you if you're lying to yourself; they don't tell you what to make.

**4. Hire for heartfelt intensity, then get out of the way.**
The single most important input is people who show up with genuine intrinsic motivation — something personal they are "out to do." Miller says this matters more than any other factor. Once you have that team, give them an ambitious problem and remove yourself from the process. He credits removing himself from Arc's product development process in early 2023 as the biggest positive shift that year.

**5. Derive culture from the team, not the top.**
Company values at The Browser Company came from interviewing everyone on the team and extracting what they naturally said about why they loved working there. No one was told what the values were; they emerged. The output was a narrative essay ("Notes on Road Trips") rather than a corporate values page — a rhetorical tactic to force first-principles thinking rather than importing preconceptions.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- The product category is a commodity — functional alternatives exist and are freely available
- The buyer or user is a person making an emotional or habitual choice, not a procurement officer running a checklist
- Switching costs are low enough that delight can drive adoption
- The brand is customer-facing and trust is a meaningful competitive variable

**Does not apply when:**
- You are selling on measurable ROI, compliance, or risk reduction (cybersecurity, enterprise infrastructure)
- Performance or reliability is the primary differentiator — no amount of beautiful UI survives bad latency in a video call tool
- The market is won by lock-in, network effects, or switching costs that have nothing to do with how the product feels

## Signature questions

1. Before you design this feature, what feeling are you trying to create in the person using it — and how will you know if you succeeded?
2. Is this a category where emotional connection actually drives the decision, or are we fooling ourselves into thinking feelings matter when the buyer just wants it to work?
3. Does everyone on this project show up with genuine fire for this problem, or are they executing because it's on the roadmap?
4. Are we using this metric to keep ourselves honest, or have we started building toward the metric instead of the feeling?
5. What does this team call itself and what does the name imply — and is that name making people think from first principles or borrowing assumptions from somewhere else?

## Common misuse

Miller explicitly warns that optimizing purely for feelings is just as flawed as optimizing purely for metrics — the framework requires both. The failure mode is using "feelings-first" as a license to ignore data entirely, or applying it to a category where feelings don't actually drive the decision. He also cautions that the approach is conditional on category selection: choosing to work in a commoditized consumer market is what makes feelings-first a business strategy rather than romantic idealism. Skip the category selection step and the framework collapses into a creative philosophy with no business logic behind it.
