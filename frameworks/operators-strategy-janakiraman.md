## One-liner

A learnable, repeatable process for building product strategy — it forces choice, builds alignment through collaboration, and produces no business value until tested through execution.

## The framework

### What strategy is

Strategy sits between mission/vision and the plan (roadmap). It forces choice to deploy scarce resources toward maximum impact. Chandra uses a resonance analogy: like finding a frequency that causes disproportionate vibration in an object, strategy is selecting the frequency that achieves resonance between product and market.

It has three components:
1. A handful of areas to focus on — called **strategic pillars** (ideally three)
2. Areas that are explicitly **not** the focus
3. The **why** behind both

### Small-S strategy (present-forward)

Used when you have a product with problems to solve. Two-year horizon, 8–12 weeks to complete, led by product management. Five stages:

**Stage 1 — Preparation (4 weeks)**
Form a strategy working group (at minimum: PM, engineering, design, data). Assign six parallel work streams:
- Behavioral insights meta-analysis (data)
- UXR insights meta-analysis (design/research)
- Leadership interviews: ask what success/failure looks like, what their pet ideas are, what principles to keep in mind
- Competitive analysis: what are competitors releasing, what areas are they investing in?
- Adjacent roadmaps: what are neighboring teams building?
- User observation: each working group member watches or interviews one user and writes down key learnings

Output: a single master preparation readout deck combining all six work streams.

**Stage 2 — Strategy Sprint (up to 1 week)**
Day 1 — Share out: everyone presents their preparation findings. Team takes notes on problems.

Day 2 — The most important day: generate all problems observed, cluster into 10–15 problem clusters, flip each cluster into an opportunity framing (e.g., "difficulty finding things" → "discovery"). Score each opportunity on four criteria:
- Expected impact
- Certainty of impact (quality of evidence)
- Clarity of levers (do you know how you'd solve it?)
- Differentiation (are these levers unique to your team/company?)

Down-select to three strategic pillars. Generate "how might we" questions for each pillar (2–3 per pillar).

Day 3 — Winning aspiration: each working group member writes a newspaper headline describing what progress looks like in two years. Combine all headlines into a single winning aspiration statement.

**Stage 3 — Design Sprint (1 week)**
Led by the design lead. Input: the three pillars and their how-might-we questions. Output: illustrative concepts (mocks, sketches) that make the strategy tangible. Goal is not feature-ready designs — it's giving people something to visualize.

**Stage 4 — Document writing (1–2 weeks)**
PM writes a 3–4 page strategy doc. Sections: leadership context, key insights and analysis, strategic pillars with why (including the full scoring table in the appendix), winning aspiration with embedded illustrative concepts, alignment questions. Strategy doc is separate from the roadmap — do not include a detailed roadmap in it.

**Stage 5 — Rollout (2–3 weeks)**
Pre-flight with 2–3 gatekeepers (senior decision-makers) one-on-one to get alignment before broader sharing. Then async or group review with key stakeholders. Then team roadshows in groups of 8–10 people to keep it conversational. Purpose is to land the strategy, not to re-litigate it — defend the pillars using the scoring criteria if challenged.

### Big-S strategy (future-forward)

Used when you want aspirational, long-term product vision. 3–10 year horizon, up to 6 months, led by design and UXR — not PM. Steps:
1. Start from company mission/vision; map long-term cultural, social, competitive, and technology trends
2. Leadership interviews focused on futures: "What does a day in the life of a user look like in five years? Why is the world better in 10 years?"
3. Generate three distinct, cohesive futures (like concept cars — never commercialized, but a source of inspiration and components)
4. Build prototypes with learning goals; test with users through concept/prototype testing
5. Push winning elements into live product testing
6. Combine with small-S roadmap — they run as parallel work streams that merge into one

At VRChat, both run simultaneously: PM leads small-S, design leads big-S.

### Why this process works (Chandra's three reasons)

1. **Alignment is built in** — the strategy comes from the working group, not the PM alone; leadership inputs are baked in early; people accept what they helped create
2. **Better problem articulation** — more minds find better strategic pillars
3. **Defensible criteria** — the scoring table makes every pillar choice justifiable and every change traceable

Strategy only accumulates business value through execution and results. A strategy that doesn't get tested is just a document.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies to:** Growth-stage companies; product verticals within larger companies. Works at company level (small company) or team level (within a large company).

**Doesn't apply when:** You need a strategy in under four weeks (you can take shortcuts, but quality degrades). Big-S doesn't fit if you need near-term horizon clarity — that's small-S territory. Resource allocation belongs in roadmapping, not in the strategy doc.

## Signature questions

1. Do the people on your team actually understand *why* you're working on what you're working on — not what, but why?
2. If you down-select to three pillars, what are you explicitly choosing *not* to invest in, and can you defend why?
3. Have you interviewed your senior leaders before writing a single word of strategy — do you know what success and failure look like to them?
4. For each opportunity area you're considering: do you know what levers exist, and are those levers unique to your team or company?
5. When strategy produces results different from what you expected, are you willing to call it honestly and pivot — or are you rationalizing to protect the doc?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Chandra explicitly warns against: treating strategy as the PM's solo work product, then rolling it out for buy-in. When you do this, leaders reject it like unwanted fruit — you show them a mango, they don't like mangoes; you try an apple, they stopped eating apples. Interview leaders *before* building strategy, not after, so their inputs are already baked into the pillars. The fruit story is the whole point of leadership interviews in the preparation phase.

A second misuse: conflating strategy with roadmap. Strategy says what areas to focus on and why. Roadmap says what to build and when. Including detailed roadmap in the strategy doc muddies both.
