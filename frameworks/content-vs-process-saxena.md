## One-liner

What you build and when (content) is the core driver of startup success — process is just the vehicle to execute it, and hiring for content first is the only move that scales.

## The framework

Sanchan draws a hard line between two things that often get conflated:

**Content** — knowing what to build, for whom, and when. This is the substance: the roadmap decisions, the customer insight, the product judgment. A content person can answer "what should we do?" without needing process to prompt the question.

**Process** — how you execute against content. Scrum, sprints, review cycles, A/B test frameworks. Process is scaffolding; it doesn't generate the right decisions, it just structures execution once you know what you're doing.

The failure mode he diagnoses: companies keep hiring for process fluency (people who can run a great sprint or build a dashboard) because it's measurable and feels rigorous. Over time, the process becomes the product — leaders spend 80% of their calendar moving the machinery rather than asking what the machinery should be building.

His prescription:
1. Hire people who deeply understand the content of your specific domain — the customer problem, the competitive terrain, what winning looks like.
2. Teach them whatever process the company needs. Process is learnable; content judgment isn't easily transplanted.
3. As a leader, stay in the content yourself. If your whole team disappeared tomorrow, you should still be able to say what to build and why — "maybe not with the same capacity, but at least be able to tell you this is what we should build, this is when, this is how."
4. Evaluate for content in interviews with real work challenges, not polished presentations. Ask candidates to solve a real problem and watch the depth of their answer, not the format of their slides.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're hiring at an early-stage startup where every person sets the product direction
- You're scaling a product organization and noticing decision velocity slowing
- You're a founder who suspects the company is drifting toward process-for-process's-sake
- You're evaluating exec candidates and need to distinguish those who can execute from those who can only manage execution

**Doesn't apply when:**
- The company is mature enough that consistency and reproducibility (manufacturing, compliance, regulated infra) are the actual product — process *is* the value
- You need to hire for a role that is genuinely process-centric (legal ops, compliance, certain engineering infra roles)

## Signature questions

1. If your entire team disappeared tomorrow, could you still tell me what to build and why — or would you first need to rebuild the process to figure that out?
2. Walk me through a product decision you made where data pointed one way and your judgment pointed another. What did you do and what happened?
3. What was the last thing you said "no" to on your roadmap, and what was the actual reason?
4. When you joined this company, how long did it take before you had a strong opinion about what the product should become? What fed that opinion?
5. What process do you run today that you'd eliminate if you could — and what's stopping you?

## Common misuse

Sanchan is explicit that this isn't an argument against process entirely — "I'm not saying process shouldn't exist. You absolutely need some process. That's why you can scale." The misuse is treating the framework as license to run chaotic, unstructured teams. The point is sequencing and prioritization: when hiring, optimize for content judgment first. Process can be installed; the ability to know *what* to build in a specific domain is much harder to train in after the fact. Leaders who read this as "process doesn't matter" will end up with talented individuals who can't coordinate — which is a different kind of failure.
