## One-liner

As AI handles knowledge work, the scarce skill shifts to allocation — knowing what to delegate, when to step in, how to evaluate output, and how to set vision — the same bundle of skills that defines effective management.

## The framework

Shipper's framework describes a shift from a **Knowledge Economy** to an **Allocation Economy**. The argument runs in four parts:

**1. Management skills are now the bottleneck**
Knowledge work — writing, coding, analysis — is being automated. What remains is the work of directing that automation: setting direction, gathering the right context, dividing tasks, choosing which model/agent to use, giving feedback, and judging quality. That is exactly what a manager does with humans.

**2. The leash as a measure of progress**
You can tell how capable an AI system is by how long a leash you can give it. Tab-complete = short leash. A 20–30 minute autonomous agent task = longer leash. AGI, by Shipper's definition, is when it becomes economically profitable to run agents indefinitely — the leash never needs to go back on.

**3. Management skills are not yet broadly distributed**
Right now only about 8% of the workforce are managers. The reason is cost — management was expensive to do at scale. AI makes managing cheap, so more people will have to learn those skills. Entry-level workers who learn both the craft and how to direct AI to do that craft will accelerate faster than any prior generation.

**4. Generalists gain relative to specialists**
AI holds deep specialist knowledge in every domain. That shifts the advantage toward people who can move across domains and orchestrate, rather than people who go deep in one lane. Small generalist teams can now do what previously required large specialist organizations.

**The CEO usage heuristic (applied version)**
The single best predictor of successful AI adoption inside a company: does the CEO use ChatGPT (or equivalent) daily? Daily CEO usage drives excitement, sets realistic expectations, and signals that adoption is real rather than a memo. Without it, no one else can lead the charge.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are thinking about which skills to develop over the next 3–5 years
- You are deciding how to structure a small team around AI tooling
- You are a leader trying to drive AI adoption inside a company
- You are evaluating whether a candidate or team member will thrive in an AI-forward environment

**Does not apply when:**
- You are deep in a technical domain where the craft itself still requires human judgment and years of embedded context (e.g., complex legal strategy, clinical diagnosis in ambiguous cases)
- Your team is so early-stage that management overhead is not yet the constraint
- You need to think about job design at the execution level rather than the organizational level

## Signature questions

1. If you removed yourself from the day-to-day execution of this task and just directed an agent to do it, what would you need to specify — and what parts of that are you currently unable to articulate?
2. Does your CEO use AI tools daily, personally? If not, what would it take to make that happen before anything else?
3. For every skill on your team, ask: is this a knowledge skill or an allocation skill? Which ones will still matter in three years?
4. Where are you micromanaging an AI instead of delegating? What would it take to extend the leash — what criteria would you need to trust the output?
5. Who on your team is a generalist with good taste and judgment across domains? Are you treating them as the future-valuable asset they are?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Shipper explicitly warns against is treating AI as a tool that replaces thinking rather than one that requires better thinking. The allocation economy does not mean you stop engaging — it means you engage at a higher level: vision, taste, criteria, evaluation. Managers who just dump tasks at AI without developing those skills get mediocre output and distrust the tools. The same mistake a first-time manager makes ("If I delegate it, it's not done the way I want it done") will repeat itself with AI unless you learn to articulate standards, divide tasks well, and know when to step in.

A related misuse: concluding from one study or one domain that AI is ready to replace a class of professionals wholesale. Shipper's point is that using AI is itself a skill, and most professionals haven't had time to develop it yet. Context complexity is routinely underestimated.
