## One-liner

A mental model for managers: regularly ask yourself whether you'd fight to keep each person on your team, and if the honest answer is no, have the tough conversation now rather than later.

## The framework

**The Keeper Test** — At Netflix, managers are expected to ask themselves with some frequency: "If this person came to me today and said they had another opportunity and were leaving, would I do everything I could to keep them at Netflix?" If the answer is no — if you'd feel even a little relieved — that's a signal to act, not defer.

The test has three interconnected parts:

1. **Talent density as the prerequisite.** High talent density isn't the output of a good culture — it's the input. Candor, freedom, and responsibility only work when you have a team of people with strong judgment. Maintaining that density requires managers to hold themselves accountable to an uncomfortable standard on a continuous basis.

2. **Timely, ongoing feedback instead of annual reviews.** Netflix has no performance reviews. The keeper test is operationalized through regular 1-on-1s, not a formal cycle. When team members ask "Am I passing your keeper test?" in a 1-on-1, it creates a direct opening for the kind of real-time feedback that most companies defer until an annual review — by which point the feedback is stale and the conversation feels loaded.

3. **Acting on the answer.** The test only works if the answer triggers action. If you would not fight to keep someone, the next step is either a candid conversation about what needs to change or a decision that Netflix and this person are not the right fit. The goal is that no one is surprised by that conversation — because feedback has been running continuously.

**The feedback model** (Stone's three-part approach to closing the gap):
- Set explicit expectations — don't assume they're obvious
- Give specific, direct feedback when something falls short, delivered in private ("not live in the big meeting")
- Jump in and help close the gap — the manager gets in the document, not just tells someone to fix it

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** You are managing people, want to maintain a high-performance team culture, and are willing to have uncomfortable conversations in a timely way. Works best when feedback is already normalized as part of the operating rhythm.

**Does not apply when:** The surrounding culture has no tolerance for direct feedback or when managers lack the support to have difficult talent conversations. Stone is explicit that this requires holding yourself to "a lot of stuff that doesn't feel like natural human behavior." Freedom-and-responsibility practices that accompany the keeper test are hard to replicate without the talent density that makes them safe.

## Signature questions

1. If this person told me today they were leaving, would I fight to keep them — or would I be a little relieved?
2. Does this person know what I actually expect from them, or am I assuming they know?
3. Am I giving feedback close to when it happens, or am I waiting for a review cycle that doesn't exist?
4. When something isn't at the bar, am I stopping at the feedback, or am I helping them get there?
5. Are there gaps in skill or behavior on this team that I'm tolerating because the conversation is uncomfortable?

## Common misuse

Stone explicitly warns against mistaking the keeper test for a source of anxiety. The failure mode is a "hunger games mentality" where people live in constant fear of being cut. The test is designed to create *less* stress, not more — because people know where they stand. The version that goes wrong is when managers use the test as a latent threat without pairing it with the continuous, specific, private feedback that makes the evaluation feel mutual and expected.

A second misuse: treating it as a periodic exercise (like a quarterly review) rather than an ongoing mental model. Stone is clear that Netflix has no performance reviews precisely because the keeper test is supposed to live in the day-to-day, not be triggered by a process calendar.
