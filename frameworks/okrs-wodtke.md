## One-liner

Pick one objective for the quarter, define a few key results that answer "how do we know we
succeeded," then run a weekly rhythm where Mondays you commit to what moves the ball and
Fridays you celebrate wins — the cadence is what actually makes it work.

## The framework

1. **One objective per quarter.** Don't run 20 OKRs. There should be no question what the
   single most important thing in the company is. The objective is "that vision for the
   quarter" — the strategy made real at a one-quarter level — and it should be motivating
   without being ridiculous.
2. **Roughly three key results, answering "how do we know?"** Key results are how you know
   you succeeded. Wodtke triangulates: one hardcore number, one squishier quality measure so
   you don't forget it, and usually one with a dollar sign. They must be outcomes, not tasks.
3. **Keep the template dead simple.** Objective + ~three key results. Nothing more. Simple
   gives you room to fiddle; complicated methodologies make people obsess over rules instead
   of what they're actually trying to do.
4. **Run the weekly cadence — this is the heart of it.** Every Monday you commit ("what am I
   going to do to move the ball forward this week"); every Friday you celebrate ("what was the
   most awesome thing that happened this week"). These two bookends, even alone, put you in
   good shape. The rituals borrow directly from agile.
5. **Use a short weekly status email/Slack.** Wodtke points to the Zynga format: your
   confidence level on each key result, what you did last week, what you're doing next week
   (plus a few notes). The "last week / next week" is where the learning lives — you start
   noticing what stops you from getting things done. Share it as widely as you can, ideally
   the whole company.
6. **Grade at the quarter's end — but make retrospective primary.** Set ~70% as a good
   target: a goal that feels uncomfortable but not doomed. The grade matters less than the
   retro and the learning cycle it feeds into the next quarter.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** any team that needs focus and a learning cadence over a quarter — and it scales
  down to one person. Wodtke runs her own life on it. It's strongest when there's a clear
  single priority to rally a team around.
- **Doesn't (or needs care):** the 70%-is-success rule comes from Google, which prints money
  and can afford to miss. Lenny raises (and Wodtke engages with) the point that for most
  companies setting goals you only expect to 70%-hit may not make sense. The framework also
  assumes you already have a strategy above it — Wodtke notes many companies don't, and OKRs
  can't substitute for one. Key results that are really just tasks (the Figma spreadsheet
  failure mode) are the sign it's busted.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- What's the *one* objective this quarter? If you're naming several, which single thing has to
  move?
- For each key result: how do we know we succeeded? Is that an outcome, or is it really a task
  in disguise?
- Do you have three-ish key results that triangulate — a hard number, a quality measure, and
  something with a dollar sign?
- What did you do last week, what are you doing next week, and what stopped you? (The learning
  is in the things that didn't work.)
- Are you actually running Mondays-commit / Fridays-celebrate, or just writing the doc once and
  forgetting it?

## Common misuse

Writing key results that are really tasks. Wodtke calls this the biggest mistake people make:
"it's always going to be tasks. I mean, people put tasks in there all the time." The failure
mode she and Lenny name explicitly is the Figma story — meetings spent reviewing spreadsheets
of hundreds of IC tasks until everyone loses sight of why any of it matters. The fix is to keep
key results as real outcomes ("how do we know we succeeded?"), keep the meeting at the right
level (top three initiatives, ~10 minutes), and make the retrospective — not the grade — the
point.
