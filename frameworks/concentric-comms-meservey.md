## One-liner

Structure your message distribution from the inside out — start with employees and co-founders, expand in concentric rings to power users, influencers, and media — each outer ring trusts the inner ring, so you can't skip a circle.

## The framework

Lulu's approach to underdog communications is four interlocking principles:

**1. Concentric Circles**
List your five or six key audiences and rank them by how much they can influence your success and how much credibility outer groups assign them. Work outward in order:
- Innermost: self (get clear on message, pick 1–3 facts from the 1,000 in your head)
- Circle 2: co-founders, executives, employees, investors
- Circle 3: power users / early adopters
- Circle 4: influencers in your space
- Circle 5: relevant media

Each circle assumes the inner circle knows better. If employees aren't on board, nobody further out will be either. You cannot skip a circle.

**2. Cultural Erogenous Zones**
People have things they care about passionately — and you won't change that. Map each audience ring's existing passions, then build the bridge (the "API") from their passion to your message. You're not creating new passions; you're connecting your thing to the ones that already exist.

**3. Pressure = Force / Area**
A physics equation applied to comms: pressure equals force divided by area. Startups have limited force (dollars, hours, credibility). To maximize impact, reduce surface area — narrow your message and audience until you're hitting the cultural erogenous zone bullseye. Better to be food coloring in a cup than food coloring in the ocean.

**4. Ideas Spread Framework**
To make a message repeatable, use one or more of: a joke/meme-ready line, an analogy, a vivid mental image, or a story. Avoid adjectives — they're subjective and forgettable. A story can be repeated over a dinner table; an adjective cannot.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are a startup or underdog without institutional backing or deep media relationships
- You are launching something new and need earned rather than paid distribution
- You have a mission-driven product where founder voice is a competitive asset
- You are fighting status quo rather than riding it

**Does not apply when:**
- You are a large incumbent that already has institutional distribution — the rules of engagement are different, even if some principles (erogenous zones, going direct) still hold
- Your audience is so undifferentiated that concentric rings can't be meaningfully separated
- You have so much brand recognition that message sharpening matters less than message consistency at scale

## Signature questions

1. Who are your five to six key audience rings, ranked by how much they influence your success — and have you actually done that ranking in a doc?
2. For each ring: what do they already care deeply about, and what is the bridge (the API) from their passion to your message?
3. Are you spreading your surface area too wide? What happens to your pressure if you cut it to the top two rings only?
4. Is a human voice actually speaking for this company, or are you cosplaying a corporation? Would anyone repeat what you just said at a dinner table?
5. Have you started building your own direct distribution channel — and have you picked the one medium that your spokesperson is naturally best at?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Lulu explicitly warns against: trying to grow or go viral for the sake of it, detached from a real message and a real audience match. Attracting a large but mismatched audience (the "hamster tweet" problem) is worse than a smaller right audience — it dilutes trust, reduces engagement quality, and pulls your messaging toward the average of too many people's tastes, which makes you unremarkable. The pressure formula breaks down when you widen surface area to chase vanity metrics.

A second failure mode: treating going direct as a one-time sprint. Lulu is explicit that you need an ongoing cadence — posting regularly so that when you need to draw on your audience (defending against an attack, launching something), the relationship is already primed. Showing up only in a crisis is readable and ineffective.
