## One-liner

Identify your superpowers and kryptonite by mining your own past stories, validate the stories against real data (treat colleagues as customers), then run small experiments to change behavior — working from the inside out.

## The framework

Lichaw calls this story-driven leadership. It has four interlocking moves:

**1. Start with yourself (inside-out)**
Leadership development begins with self, not team. Borrowing Ken Blanchard's concentric-circles model: lead yourself first, then one-on-one relationships, then groups, then the business. Trying to lead outward before you know who you are produces an "eggshell that's just ready to crack."

**2. Mine your stories for superpowers**
Strength tests give you a list; stories give you context. The method: pull three peak experiences — one from childhood, one from the last ten years, one explaining how you got into your current work. Lay them on top of each other and look for what shows up at the key moments. Those recurring themes are your superpowers.

**3. Treat self-stories as hypotheses, not facts**
The stories you tell yourself ("I'm too nice," "nobody listens to me") may or may not be true. Run customer discovery on yourself: go talk to the people around you the same way you'd talk to users. Treat what they say as data that either validates, debunks, or rewrites the story. The CEO who thought people didn't listen found out they actually wanted vision and autonomy, not someone to stop talking.

**4. Reframe kryptonite as functional**
Traits you think are liabilities often serve a purpose. Ask: "How is this serving me?" Imposter syndrome drives harder work; dyslexia correlates with big-picture spatial thinking; being quiet in meetings can mean deep listening. Kryptonite is only a problem when used in excess. The goal is finding the right dose, not elimination.

**5. Run small experiments, then get bigger**
Borrowed from Gestalt coaching and lean methodology: never leave a session without a small in-room experiment first. Test the hypothesis in 30 seconds before you try it for an hour. Then evaluate through three filters — head (thoughts), heart (emotions), hands/body (physical sensation). The body often registers the truth before the brain does.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A leader is stuck in a story about themselves that is limiting their effectiveness
- There's a gap between how someone sees themselves and how their team experiences them
- A leader knows they need to change but is forcing change rather than growing into it
- Someone has strengths they're apologizing for or hiding rather than deploying

**Does not apply when:**
- The problem is structural or contextual (wrong role, wrong organization) — Lichaw explicitly says she's not a career coach and won't tell you to stay in a job that's draining you
- Someone needs skills training, not narrative reframing
- The team dysfunction is about systems or incentives, not self-awareness

## Signature questions

1. When you were a kid, what's a project or experience that completely lit you up — what were you doing, and what made it feel that way?
2. What story are you telling yourself about this situation — and how is that story serving you right now?
3. If you went out and talked to your team the way you'd talk to customers, what do you think they'd actually say about you?
4. What's your kryptonite — and when does it help you, even if you think it's hurting you?
5. Imagine it's three years from now and things went exactly as you'd hoped. What are you doing, and what have you built?

## Common misuse

Lichaw warns against skipping to the outside before grounding in the inside. Many leaders apply "user-centered" thinking to leadership — focus on the team, make them heroes, don't make it about you. That instinct feels correct but produces shallow change. The framework only works if you do the inner work first; otherwise you're performing leadership rather than embodying it.

A second failure mode: using the stories exercise as an affirmation exercise. The point is not to tell yourself a better story and believe it harder. The point is to go get real data — talk to your colleagues — so the new story is grounded in evidence, not just in self-perception.
