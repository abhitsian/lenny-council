## One-liner

Notice the feeling a design gives you, then reverse-engineer *why* — because reaching visual perfection requires deliberately breaking mathematical rules to correct for how the eye perceives things.

## The framework

Hische describes a three-layer process she applies both when evaluating a client's brand and when coaching non-designers to develop taste:

**1. Intuition Capture**
Look at something — a logo, a typeface, a layout — and register the feeling before analyzing it. Don't name the parts. Don't reach for vocabulary. Just ask: what does this feel like right now? Hische argues people consistently underestimate this capacity because pattern absorption happens automatically as we move through the world; the feeling arrives before the brain catches up.

**2. Intuition Justification**
Once you have the feeling, walk it back. "Why does it feel this way? What does this remind me of? When have I seen something that made me feel this?" The goal is to trace the sensation to its source — a decade-old flyer, a wedding invitation, the ink bleed of printed paper. Hische calls this "Song Exploder-ing your intuition." The sources are personal but the reactions are not unique; adjacent audiences feel similar things for adjacent reasons.

**3. Optical Correction**
Perfect geometric execution produces imperfect-looking results. Where letter strokes join, optical weight accumulates and reads as a dark smudge. So expert typographers narrow strokes at joins, shave bowls into verticals, and round hard edges — not to make things look imperfect, but to make them *look* perfect despite being mathematically imperfect. The rule: you end up creating something exact, then have to make it not exact so it's perceived as exact.

**Supporting exercise — the blur test:**
Before getting into detail, blur your eyes (or take your glasses off if you have bad vision) and look at the brand as a whole. What's the overall color story? Does everything feel like it came from the same hand? If it reads as coherent at blur, then you start zooming in. If it doesn't, no amount of detail work will fix it.

**On the "good enough" threshold:**
Nothing is ever 100% perfect. The most you can realistically reach is 99.8%. That last 0.2% is infinite recursion — you could spend a career on it. Knowing when the work is done is itself a skill.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A logo or brand "feels off" but no one can name why — the framework provides a path from sensation to diagnosis
- Non-designers (founders, PMs, engineers) need to participate in design decisions without pretending to have technical vocabulary
- A logo refresh is scoped around fixing utilitarian problems (doesn't scale, falls apart in context, elements feel disconnected)
- Typography is being evaluated and the question is whether it was drawn by an expert or someone with a hobby interest

**Does not apply when:**
- The brand genuinely has no voice yet — the blur test can't find coherence that isn't there; the framework is for *reading* existing work, not generating direction from scratch
- Stakeholders need to align on brand strategy first; intuition justification works on designs, not on business positioning

## Signature questions

1. What feeling does this give you right now — before you name any of the parts?
2. Walk it back: what does this remind you of? Where have you seen something that made you feel this way?
3. If you blur your eyes and look at the whole brand together, does everything feel like it came from the same universe?
4. Where are the joins — the places where strokes or elements accumulate weight — and have those been compensated for?
5. Are you chasing the last 0.2%? Is that worth the time, or is the work done?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Hische warns against explicitly: jumping to detail before establishing the big picture. Clients bring her a list of specific complaints — "the way the R is drawn" — when the actual problem is the overall vibe is incoherent. Addressing minutiae before the top-level read is wrong will produce a polished version of the wrong thing. Start at blur-eyes level. Go tight only once the broad picture holds.

A secondary misuse for non-designers: treating intuition justification as a way to override the designer's judgment. The framework is for building shared language and understanding *why* you feel what you feel — not for producing a brief that tells the designer what to do technically.
