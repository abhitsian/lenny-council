## One-liner

Every product release moves you forward or backward in a customer's mind relative to competitors — treat each delivery as a chess move and track the delta, because products never stay the same.

## The framework

Parmar's competitive delta framework has three interlocking claims:

**1. Products never remain the same.**
Every time code ships, the product is either better or worse in the customer's eyes. There is no neutral release. This applies to your releases and your competitors' releases simultaneously.

**2. The success of a company is a direct relation of what the competition allows you to do.**
Three variables determine how much room you have to grow: how good the competitor's solution is, how strong their distribution is, and how aggressive their pricing and packaging is. All three must be tracked — not just feature quality.

**3. Be the first to hit the brick wall.**
In competitive markets, speed of learning beats quality of planning. The team that discovers dead ends first can pivot faster than anyone else. Being one to three steps ahead in uncovering what doesn't work is itself a durable advantage.

**Operationally**, Parmar embeds this through:
- The AMPED structure (Analytics, Marketing, Product, Engineering, Design) — product marketing sits inside every stream to maintain competitive positioning as a first-class concern, not a handoff
- Formal product reviews where anything large gets pressure-tested for competitive differentiation before it ships
- A six-month rolling roadmap with explicit confidence levels (80% for months 1–3, 50% for months 4–6) so teams can pivot on competitive signals without being penalized

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are in a market with at least one credible competitor (direct or indirect)
- You are making recurring release decisions across a product team at scale
- You need a forcing function to keep positioning and roadmap in sync

**Does not apply when:**
- You have genuinely found a blue ocean with no near-term entrant — Parmar explicitly carves this out: "If you are a player where there's no one else in the market, that's one thing. Kudos to you for identifying a blue ocean strategy."
- You are in the very earliest pre-product stage where the customer problem itself is still undefined

## Signature questions

1. With this release, are we moving the chess position positive or negative in the customer's mind — and against which competitor specifically?
2. What is our competitor's unfair advantage right now: is it solution quality, distribution, or pricing and packaging?
3. Are we the first team in this market to discover this dead end, or are we catching up to a learning someone else already had?
4. Does our positioning make it clear to a buyer — IT admin, line-of-business leader, or end user — what unique value we deliver that can't be replicated by what they already have?
5. Are we moving fast enough that we will hit the brick wall before our competitor does, or are we waiting for certainty before shipping?

## Common misuse

Parmar explicitly warns against two failure modes on opposite ends:

**Ignoring competition entirely.** The "just focus on the customer, don't worry about competition" advice is, in his view, simply wrong for most markets. Competitive dynamics are the biggest variable in whether your growth accelerates or stalls.

**Feature-chasing without a positioning anchor.** Tracking competitive moves only to copy them inverts the framework. The chess metaphor is about knowing whether your *unique* moves improve your relative position — not about matching whatever the competitor just shipped. Parmar pairs the competitive delta lens with a hard requirement for positioning clarity: customers must be able to see why you coexist differently in their tech ecosystem, not just that you have feature parity.
