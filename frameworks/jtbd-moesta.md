## One-liner

People hire a product to make progress out of a struggling moment, and whether they switch
is decided by four forces — the push of their situation and the pull of the new outcome
fighting the anxiety of the new thing and the habit of the old — so you study the demand
side (what people are trying to do) before you touch the product.

## The framework

1. **Start from the struggling moment, not the product.** Demand is caused by a struggle in
   some context, and that struggle exists before any product does. Study the struggle and
   the progress someone is trying to make; the product is what they wrap around it.
2. **See the demand-side competitive set.** Don't benchmark products that look like yours
   (the supply side — same tech, same business model). Ask what someone actually reaches for
   in that moment. A Milky Way competes with a glass of wine, a brownie, and a run — not just
   with a Snickers.
3. **The four forces decide whether anyone moves.** Moesta numbers them:
   - **F1 — Push:** the context that makes someone say "today's the day I have to do
     something different." It has nothing to do with your product. Without a push they can't
     even see you, because we're creatures of habit.
   - **F2 — Pull:** the attraction of the new outcome / new state.
   - **F3 — Anxiety:** the worry the new thing creates. Every time you show someone something
     new it generates anxiety — and adding features adds anxiety, it doesn't only add pull.
   - **F4 — Habit:** the pull of the present, the cost of leaving the old thing.
   If F1 + F2 are not greater than F3 + F4, nobody moves. Reducing F3/F4 friction can win the
   switch without changing the product at all.
4. **Map where they are in the timeline of buying.** Six phases: first thought, passive
   looking, active looking, deciding, first use, ongoing use. Meet people where they are in
   that timeline instead of where you want them (e.g. someone in passive looking wants a demo
   to learn, not to be closed).
5. **Interview for the story, then cluster into pathways.** Get people who recently switched
   to tell the causal story of "today's the day." Extract the pushes, pulls, anxieties,
   habits, trade-offs, and hire/fire criteria. Then cluster — don't segment — because pushes
   and pulls travel together as sets, and one product usually gets hired for 3-5 jobs that
   conflict (fast vs. thorough).

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** anything involving a switch or a purchase where you can find people who
  recently made the move and reconstruct the causal story — new products, repositioning,
  pricing, sales-process design, even job changes. Works on net-new categories too, by
  studying the competitors people switch from (Moesta studied eBay/Etsy to learn a
  marketplace's jobs before it existed).
- **Doesn't:** deep habitual behavior where there's no switch to study. Ask why someone has
  hired Tide for 20 years and they'll just make something up — the job is invisible until
  they change. Also doesn't work when a company wants you to confirm the jobs they wish were
  true; Moesta will tell you what the demand side asks for, not build the case you want.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- What's the struggling moment here — the specific context where someone says "today's the
  day"? If you can't name it, you're starting from the product, not the demand.
- What does this actually compete with on the demand side — including the status quo, a
  workaround, or doing nothing?
- What's the push and the pull, and what's the anxiety and the habit working against them?
  Are F1 + F2 really bigger than F3 + F4?
- Where is the person in the timeline of buying — passive looking, active looking, deciding?
  Does this artifact meet them there?
- This feature adds pull — but how much anxiety does it add? What could you reduce instead of
  add?
- What are you choosing to suck at, and do those trade-offs match the trade-offs the customer
  would make?

## Common misuse

Treating more features as more pull. Moesta is explicit that adding features also adds
anxiety ("can it do all those things?"), so piling them on can stall the switch instead of
winning it. The deeper misuse is building the sales or product process around how you want to
sell rather than how they want to buy — closing someone who is still in passive looking, or
asking what people say they want (granite, hardwood, Energy Star) instead of reconstructing
the trade-offs they actually made when they bought.
