## One-liner

Treat your career like a product: write a spec with success criteria, set measurable milestones, balance learning mode against impact mode, and maintain a portfolio of big bets alongside incremental growth.

## The framework

Deb Liu names five components that PMs routinely apply to products but neglect for themselves:

1. **Write the spec.** Define what success looks like in five years. Name the skills, roles, and milestones you want as "features." Without a spec, you have no benchmark to evaluate opportunities against.

2. **Set metrics.** You measure your product — do the same for your career. Track skills acquired, milestones hit, trajectory toward the north star. Without metrics you can't tell if a new role is moving you forward or sideways.

3. **Balance learning mode and impact mode.** You have the most impact in jobs you know well, but then you stop learning. You learn fastest in new territory, but early impact is low. Deliberately alternate: ladder back and forth between mastery (high impact) and exploration (high growth), rather than staying flat in one mode.

4. **Treat your career as a portfolio.** A great company building new products expects a 50% failure rate. Apply the same lens to your own career: some bets will fail and that is the design, not a defect. Core-product work builds craft; zero-to-one work builds stories. Both are legitimate portfolio positions.

5. **Measure every decision against the long-term north star.** Job opportunities arrive serially under time pressure. Having a measuring stick — "is this getting me closer or further from where I want to go?" — converts serial decision-making into deliberate progress.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

Applies when someone is at a crossroads (new job offer, promotion stall, considering a zero-to-one move) and defaulting to drift rather than direction. Most useful for mid-career PMs who have already built core craft and are ready to take bigger swings.

Less useful for someone genuinely early-stage (first 1-2 years) who needs to accumulate craft before having a meaningful spec to write. Also less useful for pure lifestyle optimization — the framework is about intentional trajectory, not work-life balance per se.

## Signature questions

1. If you had to write a product spec for your career, what would success look like in five years? What are the milestones and skills — the "features"?
2. You have metrics for your product. What are your metrics for your career, and when did you last check them?
3. Right now, are you in learning mode or impact mode? Is that a conscious choice, or did you just drift there?
4. Looking at your last three role decisions — how many did you evaluate against a stated north star versus accepting because the opportunity appeared?
5. What is your portfolio split between core incremental work and big-bet/zero-to-one bets? Is that split intentional?

## Common misuse

The most explicit failure mode Deb names: treating career planning as a one-time exercise in job selection rather than an ongoing measurement system. The framework breaks down when people write a rough north-star once and then stop checking their trajectory against it. The point is not to have a plan — it is to use the plan as a measuring stick for every serial decision that arrives under time pressure. A spec you never reference is no different from no spec at all.

A secondary misuse: applying the framework too early. Deb is direct that zero-to-one bets are high-risk for people who haven't yet built core craft. The spec and portfolio thinking presuppose enough experience to have something meaningful to bet with.
