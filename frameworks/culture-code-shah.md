## One-liner

Treat your company culture as a product you build for your team — survey it, find the
bugs, ship fixes, and keep iterating — because the alternative (trying to "preserve" the
culture) means building for needs the company has already outgrown.

## The framework

1. **Write the culture down.** Shah captured HubSpot's existing culture in a slide deck he
   called the Culture Code — named that because if you could write code to define the
   culture, this is what it would look like. The point isn't a code of conduct; it's an
   attempt to make the culture explicit and legible, like an operating system for the company.
2. **Treat the team as customers and the culture as the product.** Every company builds two
   products: one for customers, one for the team. Culture is the second product. Once you
   accept that, the product playbook applies directly.
3. **Survey it like a product.** HubSpot runs a quarterly NPS survey on the culture product —
   the same instrument you'd use to measure a customer-facing product.
4. **Treat feedback as bugs, triage in the open.** Survey responses are anonymous but every
   one is published. They get categorized and walked through at the all-hands (the
   "all-minds"): here are the bugs we'll fix, and here are the ones that are "working as
   designed" and we're not prioritizing. Same as a product review.
5. **Keep iterating — don't freeze it.** You'd never freeze the lines of code of a product,
   so don't freeze the culture either. The needs of employees change as the company scales,
   so the culture has to change with them.
6. **Label aspirations as aspirations.** When the team called out claims in the deck that
   weren't true yet, Shah kept them in but added "liner notes" marking them as aspirational.
   Over time, those aspirations became true because new joiners treated them as real and
   acted accordingly. The rule: it's fine to write what you want to be true, as long as you
   flag it and don't pass it off as already true.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** when you're scaling and worried the culture is "fading" — Shah's point is that
  change is the goal, not the threat. Also when culture work feels abstract or inauthentic;
  the people-as-customers framing gives you a concrete loop (survey → bugs → fixes) instead
  of vague values talk.
- **Doesn't override core values:** Shah is explicit that "culture is product" does not mean
  there are no fixed beliefs. There are intentional constraints the product holds constant
  (HubSpot's "building for SMB," "easy to use") that you deliberately won't iterate away
  from — the same way a product has values it won't trade off. Iteration happens around those
  constraints, not through them.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- If culture is a product, what's its NPS — and when did you last measure it?
- What are the open bugs in your culture right now, and which ones are you actually shipping
  fixes for versus marking "working as designed"?
- Are you trying to preserve your culture? If so, whose needs is it still built for — the
  company you were, or the one you're becoming?
- Which parts of your stated culture are aspirational, and have you labeled them as such — or
  are you presenting things that aren't true yet?
- What are the few core constraints you will deliberately never iterate away from, even as
  everything else changes?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Shah names directly is the "preserve the culture" instinct — leaders
treating their job as maintaining the culture as the company grows. He calls that the number
one mistake founders make, because it builds for needs the company has already outgrown. The
second misuse is the aspiration trap: putting things in the culture doc that aren't true yet
and presenting them as fact. His fix is to keep the aspiration but label it, so the team
trusts the doc and the aspiration has room to become real.
