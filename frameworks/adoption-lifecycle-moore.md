## One-liner

Four distinct market phases — Early Market, Bowling Alley, Tornado, and Main Street — each demand a completely different go-to-market playbook; running the wrong one in the wrong phase actively destroys growth.

## The framework

Moore names four inflection points in how technology gets adopted, each with its own buying persona, sales motion, and success metric.

**1. Early Market**
The buying persona is the visionary — someone who wants to leapfrog their peers and will fund you out of their own discretionary budget. No established budget exists for you. The model is project-based: you over-commit to making one marquee customer successful. The goal is a radiating reference — a well-known name that talks about you when you're not in the room. Product doesn't need to be complete, but the "magic ingredient" (the 10x effect) must work.

**2. Crossing the Chasm / Bowling Alley**
The chasm exists because pragmatists refuse to use a visionary as a reference. They only buy what their peers are already buying — and nobody has yet. The solution: pick a beachhead segment that is *big enough to matter, small enough to lead, and a good fit with your crown jewels*. Within it, target one geography, one industry, one profession, one use case. Become the dominant solution there so partners form around you, which unlocks the next adjacent segment (the bowling pin expansion). The sales model shifts from project to solution — the pitch is never about your product, it's about their problem.

**3. Tornado**
A category goes horizontal: budget appears across the entire market at roughly the same time, and pragmatists shift from "you're not doing that, are you?" to "we better do it." The playbook flips to broad coverage, standard product, competitive land-grab. Whoever captures market share fastest gets the ecosystem. This is where the gorilla is made.

**4. Main Street**
The product is commoditized; services and operational excellence are the new competitive layer. The model is land-and-expand. Product-led growth works well here because the next purchase carries low risk — there is no chasm to cross.

**The beachhead formula:** Target segment = big enough to matter + small enough to lead + good fit with crown jewels. A good early pond: if you hit high growth rates for two years, you could own 30–50% market share in it, which is enough for partners to start orbiting you.

**Fish-to-pond ratio:** The beachhead can't be a billion-dollar market — you can't be a big fish. It needs to be a segment where you can become the obvious standard within a venture funding window.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies to:** B2B software companies with disruptive technology, where buying decisions are high-risk and federated (multiple stakeholders, no existing budget line for you). Still the most reliable playbook for B2B SaaS, 30+ years after it was written.

**Does not apply to:** Consumer products, B2C apps, and companies in the digital transformation layer where B2C is driving innovation. Moore explicitly says Crossing the Chasm is not designed for consumer computing or mobile-first businesses. Product-led growth companies need this framework only at the moment they go upmarket to enterprise — until then, they're playing a different game.

**Tornado misread:** If you're in the Bowling Alley and you act like you're in the Tornado (broad coverage, budget qualification, horizontal sales), you won't scale — the ecosystem won't form. The reverse is equally fatal: running a solution/problem model when the category has already gone horizontal means leaving market share on the table.

## Signature questions

1. Do you have a marquee customer — a recognizable name that will talk about you when you're not in the room? If not, you're not ready to cross the chasm yet.
2. What is the compelling reason to *buy* (the customer's pain)? Is that different from your compelling reason to *sell* (your pitch)? Are you leading with theirs or yours?
3. In the segment you're targeting: same geography, same industry, same profession, same use case — can you realistically become 30–50% market share in two years?
4. What is the reference class your pragmatist customer uses? Is the reference you're offering another pragmatist (peer-valid), or a visionary (which they will discount)?
5. Which playbook is your sales team currently running — and does it match the phase the market is actually in?

## Common misuse

Moore's single most explicit warning: entrepreneurs confuse the **compelling reason to sell** with the **compelling reason to buy**. When chasm-crossing stalls, they improve their demo, redesign their deck, or sharpen their pitch — all of which are reasons to sell. What actually moves pragmatists is duress on their side: a problem so painful they're willing to take a risk on an unknown vendor. The sign you've confused these: your sales cycle is long, pragmatists keep taking meetings but never say yes, and every "fix" you try is about your product's attractiveness rather than understanding what's on fire for them.

A second explicit warning: using a visionary as a reference to sell to pragmatists. Pragmatists know the visionary made wild decisions and left messes behind. They will not accept that reference. The beachhead reference must be a pragmatist who looks and thinks like them.
