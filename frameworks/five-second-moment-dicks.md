## One-liner

Every story worth telling is built around a single five-second moment of transformation or realization — and you build the entire story backward from that moment, using 98% of the story as context to make the audience feel the flip.

## The framework

**1. Find the five-second moment first**
Before building any story, locate the singular moment where a flip happened: either transformation (I used to be one kind of person, now I'm another) or realization (I used to think something, and now I think a new thing). This can be one second long. It is non-negotiable. If you can't identify it, you don't have a story — you have a report.

**2. Start at the end**
For any true story you've lived (about yourself, your company, your product), you start at the end. The five-second moment you've already found forms everything else. It tells you what the beginning must be: the state that is in direct opposition to where you land. Beginning and end must operate in opposition to each other.

**3. Build context backward (the 98%)**
Everything before the five-second moment is context to bring that moment into the greatest clarity possible. The purpose is not to recount events. It is to bring the audience to the point where they can experience the flip alongside you.

**4. Layer in stakes throughout**
Stakes are what the audience worries about, wonders about, wants for you. Five tools:
- **Elephant** — plant something at the very start that makes the audience go "what's going to happen here?" It doesn't have to be what the story is actually about.
- **Backpack** — share your plan before you execute it, so the audience carries your hopes with them.
- **Breadcrumbs** — drop hints that something is coming without revealing it.
- **Hourglass** — when the audience is on the edge of their seat, slow down. Load the scene with detail. Make them wait.
- **Crystal ball** — predict a future (even a wrong one) to plant worry in the audience's mind.

**5. Apply the dinner test**
Tell stories the way you'd tell them at dinner — slightly elevated, but no performance-art conventions. No opening with unattributed dialogue. No fake sounds. No verbal detritus. If you wouldn't say "Bang, the door opened" to a friend at dinner, don't say it on stage.

**6. Make it your story**
Stories told about other people require no vulnerability from the teller, and the audience feels it. Vulnerability is the mechanism of connection. If the story belongs to someone else, find the angle that centers your own change or realization — the moment when their experience changed something in you.

**7. Match theme, not content (for business use)**
When deploying story in a business context, don't try to match the content of the story to the content of the problem. Match the theme, meaning, or message. The scientist who told an apple-shopping story to sell laboratory tubes was not talking about tubes — he was talking about people deserving to get what they need. The audience hears the topic, then snaps it over to the business point. That snap is the moment of connection.

**Homework for Life (the story-generation system)**
At the end of each day, write down — in one spreadsheet row — the moment that would've been worth telling as a story. Not the story itself. Just the moment. The goal is not to collect stories; it's to develop a lens for noticing what's worth remembering. Dicks started finding 1.8 moments per day; after 12+ years he finds 7.6. The lens sharpens with practice. This also slows down subjective time — days stop dissolving because you've made an account of each one.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're preparing a talk, keynote, pitch, sales narrative, or all-hands
- You're trying to make a business point memorable and not just logically clear
- You're coaching someone on communication and they're producing reports dressed as stories
- You want to build a reusable vault of stories (Boris's "bricks") rather than solving one-off communication problems

**Doesn't apply when:**
- You're writing fiction without a known ending — Dicks explicitly carves out novel-writing as self-discovery, not starting-at-the-end
- The "story" is actually an explanation or how-to — those are different genres with different structures
- You genuinely have nothing to say: no five-second moment means no story, and manufacturing one produces the forgettable corporate communication Dicks warns against

**Common failure mode:** People report on their days instead of finding the moment of change. Reporting — a chronological sequence of things that happened — does not connect to audiences even when the content is relevant to them. The universal appeal of a story comes from change, not from shared subject matter.

## Signature questions

1. What is the five-second moment — the exact instant when you thought one thing and then thought a new thing? Can you say it in one sentence?
2. What is the opposite of where you ended up? That opposite is where your story must begin.
3. Who is the audience worrying about, and from what moment in the story do they start worrying? Have you planted an elephant early enough?
4. Is this your story — are you expressing any vulnerability? Or are you presenting someone else's experience as a spokesperson?
5. If you stripped out all the context and kept only the five-second moment, would the audience understand why it matters? If not, the context isn't doing its job yet.

## Common misuse

Dicks's explicit warning: people front-load all their stakes at the beginning of a story because they're worried about losing the audience, then let tension flatline through the rest. Stakes need to build throughout — not peak at the opening and coast. The second failure he names is collecting the label of "storyteller" without accepting its cost: to tell stories is to separate from the herd, to be vulnerable, to be specific. Anyone who wants the brand of storytelling without the exposure is not actually doing it. The round-white-flavorless corporate talk is the direct result of people optimizing for safety over memorability.
