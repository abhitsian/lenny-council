## One-liner

Startup survival comes down to founders refusing to lose hope long enough to pivot toward genuine expertise — the companies that seem most doomed mid-batch often become the biggest successes.

## The framework

Caldwell draws on 21 YC batches to describe a consistent pattern with three observable components:

**1. Don't Die (the core mantra)**
Every startup — without exception — reaches a point where the founders seriously wonder if it's over. The ones that become large companies are those where founders maintained an irrational commitment to keep going despite all evidence suggesting they should stop. Caldwell's rule: as long as you still have ideas to try and still enjoy the work, you keep going.

**2. The Expertise Pivot**
A good pivot moves toward something the founders already know deeply — ideally something they'd been filtering out because they felt burnt out on it or didn't think it was glamorous. Caldwell's test: does the new direction feel "warmer"? Brex went from VR headsets to fintech (the founders had built a fintech product in Brazil). Retool went from a Venmo competitor to internal tools (the founders had been building internal dashboards for their own startup). In both cases, the pivot was a return to prior expertise, not a leap toward a hot trend.

**3. Tarpit Ideas (what to avoid)**
A tarpit is not just a bad idea — it's an idea that seems great, gets positive validation from friends and users, but has been repeatedly attempted since the '90s without success. The dangerous property of a tarpit is that you can get traction and early validation, which makes it hard to walk away. Examples: apps to coordinate plans with friends, music discovery, location check-in services. Caldwell notes he personally worked on a tarpit (music discovery with imeem) and still fell for the pattern.

**Pre-PMF growth hacking is actively harmful**
Caldwell's contrarian stance: analytics, A/B testing, and growth hacking are the wrong tools before product-market fit. When you have no users, staring at dashboards is a substitute for doing real customer work. The right measure is: what percentage of your calendar is actual customer conversations? Target 20-30%.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A startup is struggling mid-batch and the founders are considering shutting down
- A founder is evaluating a pivot — the question is whether to go toward an untested shiny idea vs. something with personal expertise
- A pre-PMF team is tempted to invest in experimentation infrastructure before finding customers

**Doesn't apply when:**
- A founder has run out of all genuine ideas and no longer enjoys the work or believes in it. Caldwell is explicit that "don't die" is not a prescription to keep going through misery indefinitely. If you're out of ideas, have co-founder conflict, and dread the work, that's a legitimate reason to stop.
- Growth tactics at post-PMF scale. His warning about A/B testing and analytics applies specifically to pre-PMF; at scale, these are exactly the right tools.

## Signature questions

1. Do you still have untried ideas for how to make this grow? If so, have you actually tried them all?
2. If you were to pivot, what's the closest thing to something you're already an expert at — even if you're burnt out on it or think it's boring?
3. How many in-person customer conversations have you had in the last 30 days? Pull up your calendar.
4. Is this idea something a lot of people have tried and gotten early validation on but never cracked? Or is it genuinely un-tried?
5. Are you avoiding talking to customers because of social anxiety or the fear of looking stupid?

## Common misuse

Founders treat "don't die" as an unconditional mandate to push through regardless of how they feel. Caldwell is explicit that the framework has a real off-ramp: if you genuinely don't enjoy the work, don't like your co-founders anymore, and have no remaining ideas — that's not failure of will, that's an honest signal. The pattern he warns against is shutting down purely to avoid the appearance of failure while the underlying love for the work and the problem is still there. The framework is about overcoming fear of embarrassment, not overcoming genuine misery.

The other misuse is pivoting toward a "cooler" untapped opportunity rather than toward existing expertise. Founders often pivot away from things they know (because they're tired of them) toward fashionable idea spaces they've seen in their information diet. Caldwell calls this going "colder" — toward the city where everyone else is already looking — rather than toward the mountains where your specific background gives you an edge no one else has.
