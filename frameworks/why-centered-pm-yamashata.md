# Why-Centered PM + Community-Led Growth

## One-liner

PMs own the "why" — not the what or how — so that teams can make great local decisions at scale, paired with intentional community building that turns passionate users into internal evangelists.

## The framework

### Part 1: Why-Centered Product Management

Yuhki's core PM philosophy has a single axis: **PMs are uniquely responsible for the why**.

The what and the how are shared — engineers, designers, and customers all contribute ideas and solutions. But the PM must be the person who can answer: *why are we doing this, and what problem are we solving?*

**Three layers of why:**
1. **Customer why** — why did the customer ask for this feature?
2. **Problem why** — back up one level: what underlying problem created that need?
3. **Root cause why** — go one more level: is there an underlying condition that created the problem in the first place? (Borrowed from engineering's Five Whys postmortem practice.)

**Why this enables scale:** At a small company like Microsoft in its spec-heavy culture, a PM could specify every error-handling state. At a large team like YouTube, that's impossible — engineers and designers are making dozens of local decisions every day without a PM in the room. If everyone understands *why* you're doing this, those local decisions trend toward great. Without it, you get drift.

**Storytelling as the delivery mechanism:** Owning the why means nothing if you can't communicate it memorably. Yuhki pairs the why-centered framework with a specific emphasis on *synthesis* (distilling many inputs into one clear takeaway) and *memification* (making an insight sticky enough that a CEO would cite it in the middle of a meeting without prompting).

### Part 2: Community-Led Growth

Yuhki reframes what Figma does as community-led growth rather than pure product-led growth.

**The model:**
1. **Cultivate irrational love** — the product has to solve real problems AND equip users with a philosophy about a better way of working. Both together create the emotional response that drives advocacy.
2. **Build intentional community** — Figma's "Friends of Figma" program gives passionate users across geographies a place to connect (Discord), meet regularly, and receive support (data, stories, talking points) to evangelize inside their organizations.
3. **Empower internal champions** — the real sales unit is not Figma's sales team; it's the designer inside a company who already loves Figma. The sales team's job is to find those people and equip them to make a stronger case internally.
4. **Create shared files as community currency** — Figma files shared publicly act as open-source community artifacts. When users share "here's how we do it at Company X," it creates insider access that draws others in.

**Origin discipline:** Dylan Field read customer feedback obsessively for a decade — including single-tweet signals with zero likes — not to act on every piece, but to maintain a close pulse. Yuhki and the CTO formalized this into a private "Concerning Tweets" channel: a small group that reviews low-engagement signals that carry a kernel of truth.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Your team is large enough that the PM can't specify every decision — you need scalable principles, not specs.
- Your product represents a new way of working, not just a new tool. That philosophical angle is what fuels community evangelism.
- Your growth model depends on word-of-mouth spread within and across organizations.

**Doesn't fit well when:**
- You're in a small team where the PM can and should be hands-on with the what/how.
- Your product doesn't have a philosophy worth championing — community-led growth needs something bigger than features to rally around.
- Your customer base is fragmented and not reachable through community channels (e.g., highly regulated industries with no online community).

## Signature questions

1. **"Does this PM own the why?"** — Can this person clearly articulate the problem being solved, and have they done the Five Whys to find the root cause underneath the surface request?
2. **"What story is this leader trying to create in their head?"** — When presenting to a stakeholder, what meme are they trying to form so they can remember and retell it?
3. **"If we stopped an engineer in the hallway right now, could they state the team's biggest goal?"** — If not, the OKR isn't real; it's a post-rationalization of work already in motion.
4. **"Are we equipping our champions, or are we selling?"** — Who is the internal advocate inside the customer's organization, and what do they need from us to make a stronger case?
5. **"Is there an almost irrational emotional response to this product?"** — Not just satisfaction — love. Is it there internally first? Does it exist in the community?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Yuhki explicitly names with OKRs applies directly to the why-centered model: **PMs who post-rationalize the why rather than genuinely owning it**.

Teams write OKRs (or frame the why) as a description of what they're already building, then reverse-engineer a "problem" to justify it. The test Yuhki applies: if you stop a random engineer in the hallway, can they state the goal? If not, the why never penetrated — it was documentation, not direction.

A second failure mode: treating community-led growth as a marketing program rather than a product-rooted commitment. Dylan's obsession with customer feedback was a decade-long discipline. Friends of Figma worked because it started with a product people genuinely loved, not as a growth hack layered on top of an average product.
