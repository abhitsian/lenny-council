# Fear and Anger Give Bad Advice

## One-liner

When fear grips your mind, it makes exaggerated predictions and stops you from doing the difficult-but-necessary thing — the fix is to recognize you're in fear, check with someone who isn't, and bet against the fearful prediction.

## The framework

Mochary identifies two emotional states that systematically produce bad decisions:

**Fear** distorts predictions. When a person is gripped by fear, their brain cannot see reality — it generates exaggerated worst-case scenarios. The person in fear cannot self-diagnose this clearly because they are inside it. The person who is not in fear (e.g., a coach, a trusted peer) can see clearly what the fearful person cannot.

The intervention is a bet: identify what the fearful person predicts will happen if they take the difficult action. The coach predicts the opposite. Run a lower-stakes version of the decision first, observe who was right, and use that win to build trust in the pattern. Mochary reports making this bet hundreds of times without losing once — not because he is exceptional, but because ungripped brains make more accurate predictions.

**Anger** destroys relationships. Anger is not a base emotion — it is a cover for pain. The brain externalizes pain as anger to avoid feeling it directly. The cost: the pain gets pushed onto the people nearest to you. The real resolution is to allow yourself to feel the pain rather than convert it into anger. The practical step is stopping before acting when in anger.

**Notifying someone they are in anger** requires precise language. "You're in anger" reads as accusation. "Are you in anger?" reads as passive-aggressive. The phrase that works: "I perceive you to be in anger." It is an I-statement, it is observation only, and it has no embedded judgment — which is what allows it to land.

Three seminal documents in Mochary's curriculum frame the entire coaching practice: (1) On Time, (2) Top Goal, and (3) Fear and Anger Give Bad Advice. The third is the crux — if a CEO doesn't resonate with it, coaching won't work.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

Applies when the reluctance to act is driven by perceived threat to ego, status, or relationships — not physical danger. The canonical cases: a CEO who hasn't told the board about a serious problem, a manager who is delaying a necessary firing, a founder avoiding a hard conversation with a co-founder.

Does not apply to literal physical danger. Mochary explicitly draws this line: "I don't mean physical danger, I mean things that we perceive to be danger to our egos."

The framework also applies at the organizational level — companies in fear of layoffs delay action, companies in fear of transparency hide problems from boards. The same pattern holds.

## Signature questions

1. What are you predicting will happen if you take this action — and is that prediction being made by a gripped brain or a clear one?
2. Who in your life is not inside this situation and could make a bet on the opposite outcome?
3. When you feel angry at someone, what pain underneath the anger are you trying not to feel?
4. Is there a lower-stakes version of this decision where we could test whether fear is giving you bad advice?
5. What's the difficult-but-necessary thing you've been avoiding — and how long have you been avoiding it?

## Common misuse

The framework gets misapplied when people treat "bet against the fear" as a universal heuristic and apply it to physical risk. Mochary warns directly against this. The bet is calibrated for ego-protective fear — the kind that prevents transparent board communication, necessary firings, difficult feedback conversations — not situations where caution is genuinely warranted.

A second failure mode: naming someone's emotional state in a way that triggers more of it. "You're in anger" backfires. The I-statement format ("I perceive you to be in anger") is not optional wordsmithing — it is load-bearing. The framing change is what makes the intervention land rather than escalate.
