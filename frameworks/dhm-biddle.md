## One-liner

A product strategy is a hypothesis for how you'll delight customers, in a way that's hard for competitors to copy, that also makes the business money — and any idea worth shipping has to clear all three at once.

## The framework

1. **Delight customers.** Aim for 10X-better, not "satisfied." Biddle frames this as the
   founder's job at the start: find the thing that's an order of magnitude better than what
   exists. At Netflix the proxy for delight was retention — if customers loved a change,
   fewer of them canceled.
2. **In hard-to-copy ways.** A delightful thing a competitor can match next week buys you
   nothing long-term (his "happy family on the couch" landing page would've been copied by
   Blockbuster within a week). The durable categories he names: unique technology
   (personalization), network effects, brand, economies of scale, and — for B2B — switching
   costs.
3. **In margin-enhancing ways.** A "fancy phrase for making money." A change can delight and
   build advantage and still fail the model if it loses money you can't justify. The whole
   point of margin is to fund the next round of delight.
4. **Resolve the delight-vs-margin trade-off explicitly.** The hardest part. He works it as
   literal math: value of customers saved (LTV × a word-of-mouth multiplier) vs. the cost of
   delivering the change. The Perfect New Release test barely moved retention, so the cost
   killed it — unless you believe a higher word-of-mouth factor, which is exactly where his
   CFO and product team fought.
5. **Align the org on a priority order with GEM.** Force-rank Growth, Engagement,
   Monetization and make leadership agree. Engagement is his stand-in for product quality.
   He calls misalignment on this rank "the number one source of misalignment" that wrecks
   startups.
6. **Pick a proxy metric for each, especially engagement.** The hardest one to define is
   "how do you measure product quality." At Netflix the engagement proxy was monthly
   retention. Start with a SWAG (stupid wild-ass guess), then get the team to refine it.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** consumer product strategy where you can run A/B tests and tie changes to a
  retention-style metric; cross-functional decisions where a feature looks delightful but the
  business case is contested; leadership alignment when growth and monetization teams pull in
  opposite directions. Biddle says the model also holds for B2B — the hard-to-copy categories
  just shift (switching costs matter more, the UI bar is different because users live in the
  product 10-14 hours a day).
- **Doesn't / breaks down:** when an idea only works for a sliver of users. He won't ship
  "two percenters" — features that 2% of customers use add complexity for everyone and can't
  move a retention metric, so they never earn their place even if delightful. He's also
  explicit that he's spent zero hours in enterprise, so treat his B2B claims as transfer, not
  experience.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- Is this 10X better, or just better? Delight works at order-of-magnitude, not "more satisfied."
- If you shipped this, could a competitor copy it within a week? If yes, what durable
  advantage are you actually building — tech, network effect, brand, scale, or switching cost?
- What's the margin story? Does this make money, or fund the next thing that does — and if it
  loses money, does the delight plus hard-to-copy advantage outweigh that?
- What share of customers will actually use this? If it's a two-percenter, you're adding
  complexity for everyone and it won't move retention.
- Have your leaders force-ranked growth, engagement, and monetization — and do they agree?
- What's your engagement proxy metric, the one that stands in for product quality?

## Common misuse

Treating DHM as a checklist where one box is enough. The failure mode Biddle keeps returning
to is shipping something delightful that's trivially copyable (the happy-family landing page),
or something delightful that loses money you can't defend (the Perfect New Release test that
barely moved retention but cost $5M). All three legs have to hold, and the delight-vs-margin
trade-off has to be worked out in actual numbers — not asserted. The related org-level misuse
is leaving growth/engagement/monetization unranked, which he names as the top source of
misalignment that breaks startups.
