## One-liner

Build fewer things at a high-polish bar that users genuinely love, pair that with narrative-driven roadmaps that answer "why" before "what," and gate execution in time-boxed learning phases so sunk-cost thinking can't trap you.

## The framework

Zhang's teaching is three interlocking practices:

**1. Minimal Lovable Product (MLP)**
MLP is the new MVP. In a competitive market, "barely meets a quality bar" is not enough. The right question is: what is the quality bar that actually resonates with your users given what they're comparing you to? Two practical moves:
- Do five things with genuine polish rather than 15 things with partial coverage. "People would actually respect this idea of 'You've given me minimal lovable in five areas as opposed to minimal viable in 15 areas.'"
- Scatter "pixie dust" deliberately — pick a few moments where you exceed expectations (keyboard shortcuts, pre-populated templates) rather than trying to delight everywhere. You can't pixie dust everything; something always gives.

The calibration: if users are replacing a manual spreadsheet workflow, your bar can be lower — you're already a step change. If they have polished alternatives, lovable is the floor.

**2. Story-First Roadmapping**
A roadmap is a narrative, not a spreadsheet. RICE scores and effort columns answer what gets built; they don't answer why. What humans need — and what holds up under scrutiny — is a set of themes with a clear rationale behind each one.

Zhang's format: a doc (not a deck, which needs a voiceover) with themes written out, the assumptions behind each, and links out to Jira or wherever work actually lives. The doc changes when major assumptions change, not when a cell value shifts. "What is the story that you're telling about your roadmap that these inputs can then go and influence?"

**3. Phased Execution with Go/No-Go Gates**
Big visions are fine. Open-ended bets are not. Break ambitious work into time-boxed phases with explicit milestones and a go/no-go at each gate. Before the phase starts, define what "crushed it" looks like qualitatively and quantitatively. This does two things: it gives teams permission to run at something unscalable in the learning phase, and it prevents the sunk-cost trap — "I invested a quarter in it, but I didn't invest two years."

**Understand Your Alpha (the through-line)**
Across Dropbox, Airbnb, WeWork, and Webflow, Zhang saw the same failure mode: chasing competitors or adjacent spaces while underinvesting in the core reason people love the product. Dropbox built a Slack competitor instead of fixing sync performance. Airbnb Plus tried to out-operate boutique hotel operators instead of doubling down on what made real homes trustworthy. The discipline is: when evaluating a new investment, trace it back to your core advantage. If it doesn't extend that, it's usually a distraction.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're in a competitive market where alternatives exist — lovable is the floor, not a stretch goal.
- Your team has a roadmap that's a prioritization spreadsheet with no narrative thread — themes will focus execution.
- You're in early phases of something ambitious and need a structure that lets you learn without overcommitting.
- You're a PM leader onboarding to a new company and need a fast context-building framework (40-50 conversations across levels and functions, not just your peers).

**Doesn't apply / watch out when:**
- You're replacing something truly terrible (a manual workflow with no alternatives) — MVP-level quality may be entirely sufficient.
- Your org needs a precise execution plan, not themes; themes can become a cover for vague direction if not paired with actual milestones and artifacts.
- You're using "we'll invest in what makes us special" to justify not addressing a real competitive threat — knowing your alpha is not the same as ignoring threats.

## Signature questions

1. "What are the things around quality that people actually care about — and are we targeting those specifically, or using one blunt instrument for everything?"
2. "Is this roadmap a story? Can someone read it and understand *why* these are the biggest levers to pull — not just what we're building?"
3. "What phase are we in — learning or scaling? Have we made that explicit to the team, and do we have a go/no-go milestone at the end of it?"
4. "What is the thing that makes people love this product, and are we doubling down on that — or are we chasing something adjacent?"
5. "If the OKR goes red, will we know why? Did we define qualitative success criteria up front, or are we managing to green?"

## Common misuse

Zhang's explicit warning: using a big vision as a reason to hire aggressively and build broadly before the unit economics are clear. The Airbnb Plus postmortem and WeWork are both examples of "magical thinking around unit economics — you're like, 'When we get to the scale of X, it's all going to work out.'" The MLP + phased execution framework is specifically designed to prevent this — lovable scope is constrained scope, and each gate forces the question of whether the economics work before the investment deepens. A team that treats MLP as "MVP but polished" without the phased gating and go/no-go discipline is only getting half the framework.
