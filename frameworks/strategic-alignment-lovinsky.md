## One-liner

Subordinate your immediate role and personal position to whatever move best serves the broader business; in healthy organizations, that bet consistently pays off — and unhealthy ones reveal themselves fast when you try it.

## The framework

Lovinsky names two moves that look like career risk but are actually alignment signals:

**1. Kill your own project early**
When you can see that the roadmap of the broader org makes your project a low-priority use of engineering headcount, say so — even if it shuts down the team you joined to build. The move forces a clear-eyed read of relative priority before sunk cost hardens into politics.

**2. Ask to be layered**
If a reporting structure is limiting your output, propose the change yourself — including one where you report to a peer. The bar is simple: will this lead to better results for the organization? If yes, a healthy culture will reward it.

**3. Internalize company strategy, not just your domain**
The operating stance is: "Having a broader view of the company strategy, having an instinct for what we should be doing and why and how I might prioritize all of these investments if I were given the opportunity to do that." You move from advocate-for-my-area to steward-of-the-whole.

**Secondary diagnostic: Growth Masks Problems**
A companion lens Lovinsky applies before and after alignment moves. Triple-digit growth means problems stay buried — there is no urgency to fix supply-side friction when demand is flooding in. The diagnostic question to run even during good times: "What would I do right now if growth went negative? How would I prioritize things?" Running this question proactively is what makes the alignment moves legible instead of panic-driven.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have enough context to form a genuine view of company-level priorities (not just your own team's metrics)
- You have read enough of the culture to trust it is healthy — you've developed a "nose for talent" and high-functioning teams from prior experience
- Growth is masking a structural issue you can already see (supply liquidity, single-channel dependency, wrong monetization model)

**Does not apply when:**
- You are too new to have formed a real view of company strategy — the framework requires internalized context, not guesswork
- The culture is not healthy; in that case the same moves confirm you should leave, not double down
- You have no foundational strengths to anchor on; Lovinsky pairs this with a rule that you should always bring 1-2 things you already own into any stretch role, so you are not entirely at sea

## Signature questions

1. If you were given full authority to prioritize all investments across the company right now, what would you change — and does your current work reflect that?
2. What would happen to this product if growth went to zero tomorrow? What problems would surface that are invisible today?
3. Is the incentive structure around this team actually compatible with what it needs to discover? (Performance management cycles designed for large orgs are wrong for 0-to-1 work.)
4. Are you making this move because it will produce better results for the organization, or because it feels safer for you personally?
5. Can you identify 1-2 things you genuinely own coming into this new role — things that will hold you steady while you learn everything else?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Lovinsky explicitly calls out is applying this in an unhealthy culture and interpreting the bad outcome as a personal failure rather than a culture signal. The framework includes an escape clause: "If it's not [a healthy culture], that's good to know too. That's good to know early." The move is also a culture diagnostic — not a universal self-sacrifice move that always pays off regardless of environment.

A second misuse: treating the "growth masks problems" diagnostic as a reason to slow down during growth. Lovinsky's use of it is the opposite — run the negative-growth scenario as a background process *during* strong growth, so you are not caught rebuilding the engine mid-flight.
