## One-liner

Growth comes from relentlessly optimizing the path to must-have value — first activation, then engagement/referral/revenue, then acquisition — rather than treating customer acquisition as the primary lever.

## The framework

Sean Ellis calls it a **value delivery engine**: a sequenced approach to building growth around the core must-have experience, not around top-of-funnel volume.

**Step 1 — Identify must-have value**
Run the Sean Ellis Test: ask active users "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" with options very disappointed / somewhat disappointed / not disappointed. Aim for 40%+ saying very disappointed. This is a *leading indicator* of PMF — actual retention cohorts are the confirmation.

Ignore somewhat disappointed users when diagnosing what to fix. They're signaling a nice-to-have. Tweaking product or onboarding based on their feedback risks diluting the experience for must-have users.

**Step 2 — Understand what makes it must-have**
Drill into the very disappointed segment:
- Open-ended: "What is the primary benefit you get?"
- Multiple-choice follow-up (different group): force them to pick one of four benefit statements
- Then: "Why is that benefit important to you?" — this surfaces the emotional context, not just the feature

**Step 3 — Define a North Star Metric that reflects value delivery**
Pick something that counts units of the must-have experience being delivered, can grow up and to the right over time, and correlates to revenue without being revenue. Monthly purchases (Amazon), nights booked (Airbnb), weekly rides (Uber) are examples. Revenue as a north star is a mistake — revenue should be a product of doing things right, not the guide for day-to-day actions.

**Step 4 — Build the engine in this sequence**
1. **Activation** — speed to value; get users to the aha moment in the first session; this is where you lose most people and most teams ignore it
2. **Engagement** — design the loop that brings users back at the right natural frequency
3. **Referral** — only build incentivized referrals once word of mouth is already working; incentives accelerate what's already there, they can't create it
4. **Revenue model** — optimize conversion to paid and unit economics
5. **Acquisition** — only then pour fuel on customer acquisition channels

**ICE prioritization** — to run a high-velocity experiment program across all these areas, score ideas by Impact (best case, how much?), Confidence (how sure are you?), and Effort (how hard?). Reach is already inside Impact; adding it separately is unnecessary complexity.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have an MVP and some active users — even at 7% very disappointed, the survey tells you what to fix
- You're trying to justify whether to invest in growth vs. continue iterating on product
- You're sequencing where to put engineering and growth effort

**Doesn't apply when:**
- The product is one-off by nature (a single workshop, a movie you just watched) — use NPS as the filter instead
- You have no users yet to survey — you need real usage data, not hypothetical
- The business is post-PMF at scale — referral and acquisition become more nuanced (e.g., Eventbrite's high score was partly switching-cost lock-in, not pure utility)

## Signature questions

1. Of the people who would be very disappointed without your product, what is the single primary benefit they're getting — and why does that benefit matter to them?
2. What percentage of users who sign up actually experience the core must-have moment? (If it's 5%, your growth ceiling is the 5%, not the channel.)
3. Is your North Star Metric a unit of value delivered to the customer, or is it a financial proxy that could be gamed without delivering value?
4. Are you trying to fix acquisition before you've fixed activation? What's your signup-to-usage rate?
5. Of your somewhat disappointed users who care about the same core benefit, what specifically is preventing it from being must-have for them?

## Common misuse

Ellis explicitly warns against two failure modes:

**Starting acquisition before fixing activation.** Most teams flip the sequence — they drive traffic, then try to fix the funnel. His LogMeIn example: 95% of signups never did a remote control session, capping profitable spend at $10K/month. After three months of full-team focus on activation (signup-to-usage went from 5% to 50%), the same channels scaled to $1M/month with 80% word-of-mouth growth.

**Treating the 40% threshold as binary.** The threshold is a forcing function for team alignment — "we don't scale until we hit this" — not a hard scientific cutoff. Culture matters (Brazilians run optimistic, Hungarians run pessimistic). A 37% score at a company with a clear acquisition angle is more actionable than a 70% score where you have no idea how to grow.
