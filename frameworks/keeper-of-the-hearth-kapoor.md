# Keeper of the Hearth: Zero-to-One Building Model

## One-liner

The person pushing a zero-to-one idea inside a company is the Keeper of the Hearth — responsible for stoking the flame, securing buy-in, and making the idea spread like wildfire through three concrete phases: right idea (empathy), buy-in (vision), and spread (hype).

## The Framework

Mihika draws on the Greek myth of Hestia — goddess of the hearth whose sole job is to keep the fire burning even when everyone else is off on their quests. The person championing a new product is that Hestia.

**Phase 1 — Right Idea (Empathy)**
Have non-stop user conversations, not just about the product you're building but about the person's full tooling stack and general perceptions of the company. Ask non-users why they're not using the product — those are often the most revealing conversations. The idea must also ladder into a company-level goal, not just feel personally compelling.

**Phase 2 — Buy-in (Vision)**
Create a single shared artifact the whole team builds together — research, design, and engineering cross-pollinated, not handed off sequentially. At Figma, the format is: pain point → solution → proof point → pain point → solution → proof point. Prototypes and mocks do more than words. Put out an A- idea early so people can react to it; an opinion is a better catalyst than a blank canvas. Calibrate your confidence signal explicitly ("I feel strongly" vs. "I have medium confidence") so teammates know when to push back.

**Phase 3 — Spread (Wildfire)**
Find every large company forum — hackathons, sales kickoffs, design conferences — and get the product visible there, even before it's polished. A scrappy demo in a company-wide keynote does more than months of internal advocacy. Put the product on staging early so colleagues can shape it; people evangelize what they helped build. Hype is not manufactured — it's getting people to see what you see.

**Underlying stance: Ruthless Optimism**
Zero-to-one requires optimism that borders on delusion. Every "no" gets translated into "not yet." First pitches will fail. Keep pitching.

## When It Applies / When It Doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're pushing a net-new product or category inside a larger company, not iterating on an existing one.
- You lack formal authority and are building from the bottom up.
- The idea requires sustained internal momentum over many months before it gets a greenlight.

**Doesn't apply when:**
- The product direction is already locked and validated — at that point you're executing, not flame-keeping.
- The idea is a two-week feature sprint. The hearth metaphor is for ideas that could die 10 times before they ship.
- Leadership has already declared the initiative a top priority. The model is for bottoms-up proposals, not top-down mandates.

## Signature Questions

1. What is the single artifact your team is building together — or are people each pouring their energy into their own deliverable?
2. Have you talked to non-users and asked them why they're not using the product? What did they say?
3. What's the next large company forum where you can put this product in front of people, even if it's rough?
4. When you hear "no" or "maybe someday," do you understand what assumption the other person is holding that you aren't? Have you addressed that assumption directly?
5. Do the people on your team understand what motivates them about this project — do they want a solution handed to them, or do they need to have shaped it to care about it?

## Common Misuse

Treating conviction as a substitute for user empathy. Mihika is explicit that high conviction without a trail of user proof points reads as pushing a personal preference rather than solving a real need. The ownership of the flame includes constantly surfacing external signal — from user conversations, non-users, and sales calls — so the idea doesn't live only in your head. Strong opinions must remain loosely held: "kill your darlings if you hear something that tells you otherwise."
