## One-liner

Find where your target audience is actively asking questions about their problems, answer those questions genuinely and completely first — then let your product surface as the natural next step.

## The framework

Meltem built Deel from under $1M to $300M ARR in three years using this sequence:

**1. Find where people ask questions**
Identify the digital spaces where your target audience asks questions peer-to-peer: Reddit subreddits, Quora, Twitter threads, closed Slack/Discord communities, VC portfolio networks. The question is literal: where are people typing this problem out and waiting for an answer?

**2. Answer authentically, problem first**
Show up in those spaces as a person with expertise, not as a company with a pitch. Answer the question completely. Explain the constraints, the tradeoffs, what might not work for them. Only after the question is answered do you mention your solution exists.

**3. Earn compounding reach via SEO**
Open digital answers (Reddit, Quora, public forums) have a long tail — one good answer keeps working. Apply the same logic to written content: use a traffic light system to prioritize keywords by buyer intent (green = high intent to buy, yellow = maybe, red = skip), then write content that ends the Google search. The test is: after reading your article, does the person go back to Google looking for more?

**4. Layer paid and partnerships only on top of the foundation**
Paid ads require a fast website, clear messaging, and validated positioning first. Partnerships work when the partner is genuinely who your audience turns to for guidance on this problem — shared audience alone is not enough. For Deel, VC partners worked because founders ask their VCs what tools to trust; random tech integrations with the same ICP did not.

**5. Hold growth teams to revenue, not leads**
Growth orgs at Deel commit to closed-won revenue goals, not top-of-funnel metrics. Leading indicators (leads, SQLs) are tracked, but success is defined at the bottom of the funnel.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're in B2B or any space where people actively search for a solution to a known problem.
- Early stage: the first 6–12 months before you have budget for awareness campaigns.
- The problem is specific enough that people type it into Google or ask it in forums.

**Does not apply when:**
- Consumer goods driven by aspiration and aesthetics (lipstick, fashion) — the audience goes to Instagram and influencers, not Google.
- Your market isn't actively searching; demand creation, not demand capture, is the job.
- You're a marketplace or tool built on user-generated content (Yelp, Glassdoor) — SEO works there but via a different mechanism.

## Signature questions

1. Where exactly are people typing this problem today — what subreddit, what search query, what Slack community?
2. If you removed all mention of your product from your answer, would it still be the most useful answer in that thread?
3. When someone reads your content page, is their Google search over — or do they bounce back for more?
4. Is the Google search intent for this keyword actually aligned with what your buyers need, or are you optimizing for traffic that will never convert?
5. If you had $0 to spend on growth, what would you commit to in terms of closed revenue — and does that answer change if you still can't commit to a revenue number with budget?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Meltem explicitly calls out: people jump straight to awareness campaigns or paid ads before the foundation exists. Running paid ads to a website that loads in 4+ seconds, has unclear messaging, or hasn't validated what resonates — that's money spent before the skeleton is built. The framework is sequential on purpose: fast website → people can find it → content answers their questions → paid ads amplify what's already working. Skipping steps because awareness campaigns look like the more ambitious move is what wastes the first six months.

A second misuse: treating any partner with audience overlap as a growth lever. The filter isn't shared ICP — it's whether your audience actually goes to that partner when they have this specific problem. Partners who aren't trusted advisors for your problem category don't move the needle regardless of how much the audiences overlap.
