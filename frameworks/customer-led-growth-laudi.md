## One-liner

Replace funnel-based growth metrics with a customer journey map built from jobs-to-be-done research — with a measurable KPI at each milestone — so you can guide customers to value and catch them when they fall off.

## The framework

Laudi's process has four sequential steps:

**1. Identify your best customers and their job to be done**
Survey customers who are happy, paying, and signed up recently enough (3–6 months) that they remember what life was like before. You are looking for: the trigger moment, the prior solution, the must-haves, and the desired outcome. From the responses, identify 1–2 customer jobs and pick one to lead with. Prioritization criteria: urgency of the problem (painkiller over vitamin), willingness to pay, retention/expansion potential, and any unfair advantage you have with that segment.

**2. Map the customer journey through three phases**
Build a milestone-based map — aim for roughly six milestones total — through:
- **Struggle phase**: Problem (experiencing the pain out in the world) → Interest (actively shopping for a solution)
- **Evaluation phase**: First Value (product activation, the moment they see it could work) → Value Realization (solving the customer job for the first time)
- **Growth phase**: Continued Value (frequency and habit) → Value Growth (expansion, advocacy, or upgrade)

Each milestone represents a meaningful leap in the customer's relationship with the product, not an internal business event.

**3. Assign a measurable KPI to each milestone**
For every milestone, define what "they got there" looks like in product or marketing data. Example from SparkToro: First Value = 5+ searches plus at least one list created. Value Realization = searches + lists + at least one export, within a meaningful time window. Without a KPI at each milestone, you cannot tell where customers are falling off or build proactive interventions to help them forward.

**4. Build messaging and operationalized experiences**
Create a 5–7 page messaging guide grounded in the voice-of-customer research: value prop, competitive advantages, major value themes, emotional and functional benefits tied to specific product attributes. Use this guide as the baseline for website copy, email onboarding, in-product checklists, and win-back campaigns. If a customer misses a milestone KPI, trigger a proactive re-engagement experience targeted at that specific gap.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A SaaS company has a working acquisition channel but conversion or retention is underperforming
- The team is running piecemeal campaigns without a shared understanding of what "customer value" looks like at each step
- There is no consistent post-acquisition customer experience (the "no man's land" of onboarding)
- Leadership from product, marketing, and CS needs a shared language and shared KPIs

**Does not apply when:**
- The company has not yet found product-market fit — the research will reflect a scattered customer base with no clear job
- There are not yet enough paying customers (ideally 3–6 months in) to run a valid survey or interview set
- The product changes so frequently that a milestone map would be obsolete before it is operationalized

## Signature questions

1. Who are your best customers right now — the ones who are happy, paying, and signed up recently enough to remember their before state?
2. What was the customer doing out in the world before they ever heard of you? What was the pain in their prior solution?
3. What moment in the product experience tells you that this customer has reached real value — not just signed up, but actually gotten the thing they came for?
4. For each step in the journey, do you have a KPI? If a customer fails to reach that milestone, what experience do you have in place to bring them back?
5. Does your current website messaging reflect what your best customers say they care about, or what you think is the coolest thing about your product?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Laudi explicitly warns against is jumping straight to tactics — A/B testing copy, launching campaigns, tweaking onboarding flows — before establishing who you are actually trying to help and what value looks like for them. Teams assign KPIs to internal events (MQLs, SQLs, "activated users" by some arbitrary definition) rather than to meaningful customer milestones grounded in research. The result is optimizing the wrong thing: better-looking numbers on a metric that does not correspond to real customer value. The fix is always to go back upstream — survey your best customers first, build the journey from their experience, then assign KPIs that map to actual value moments.
