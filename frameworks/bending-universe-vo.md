## One-liner

Know what you want, ask for it clearly, make it easy for your boss to help you get there — and recognize that org structures bend around talented, motivated people.

## The framework

Claire Vo describes three interlocking moves:

**1. Know and name what you want**
State your target role explicitly — to your boss, during interviews, before promo cycles. Not as a demand but as a shared project: "I want to be a CPO. Here's how I'm going to get us there. I want you to partner with me on it." This applies at every career stage, including inside a new role ("I want my next role to be a CEO role, so I want this role to fill in my gaps").

**2. Solve the organization's problem, not your career problem**
Frame every growth conversation around what the company needs, not what you want. Draw the org chart. Put your name on it. Show how the structure change solves a real gap. "That's solving a problem for the company. That's not solving a career growth issue for an individual."

**3. Operate in your zone of genius**
Do a calendar audit: categorize everything you did last month into (a) I hated this, (b) fine, (c) I loved this, (d) I would spend all my time here if I could. Use the top bucket to identify what only you can do that no one else in the organization can replicate. Protect that space. Breadth of responsibility is only sustainable when it sits inside the zone.

**The bending principle**
Organizations at growth-stage companies are not fixed. Talented, motivated people attract expanded scope. The org chart is a living entity — structure shifts around people who produce results. Treat it as a system you can influence, not a ceiling you work under.

**Supporting tactics**
- **Clock speed shift**: communicate to leaders that everything should move one iteration faster. Year → half, half → quarter, quarter → month, week → today. Don't let recurring meetings set the action cadence.
- **Clear is kind**: feedback-avoidant cultures degrade the talent bar. Saying "you are not meeting expectations, and I believe you can get there" is kinder than soft hedging.
- **Fast beats right**: when debating between a perfect decision and a fast one, fast wins at the end of the day.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're in a growth-stage or late-stage startup where org design is genuinely fluid
- You have a track record the conversation can stand on — Claire is explicit that the work has to speak for itself
- You're in a manager or executive role where you can influence how scope and teams form

**Doesn't apply when:**
- You're in a very early-stage role with no track record yet — the bending comes after the results
- You're in a large, rigid enterprise where promotion cycles and headcount are tightly controlled (she acknowledges: "I sometimes functionally cannot promote people inside larger organizations whenever I want")
- You're spending more time on growth edges than strengths — taking on more scope before the zone of genius is stable burns both you and the org

## Signature questions

1. What do you want out of your current role, and what do you want your next role to be?
2. What does you being in a different position do for the company — not for your career, for the company?
3. If you go through your calendar for the last month, what falls into "I would spend all my time here"?
4. What is the thing you do that no one else in this organization can replicate?
5. Where is the organization operating slower than its ambition? And are you a bottleneck in that?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Claire explicitly warns against is making career growth conversations about yourself. The tell is when the conversation is "I want to be a director because I want direct reports" rather than "your span of control has nine reports, you need leverage here, and I can solve that." Framing the ask as a career need rather than an organizational solution removes the lever that makes the thing actually work. She also warns against over-indexing on growth at the expense of the zone of genius: putting people (or yourself) into positions where you're spending most of your time on growth edges rather than strengths is bad for the individual and bad for the org.
