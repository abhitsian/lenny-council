## One-liner

Shift product leadership from threat-based, fear-driven decision-making to trust-based, co-creative collaboration by treating emotions as wisdom signals — not noise to suppress.

## The framework

Built from the 15 Commitments of Conscious Leadership (Diana Chapman, Jim Dethmer), the framework has two interlocking parts:

**1. The two states**

Every person in a meeting, review, or product decision is in one of two states:

- **Threat/fear/righteousness state**: lizard brain, blaming, arguing, needing to be right, career-anxiety-driven. Results in toxic OKR reviews, defensive prioritization, lowest-common-denominator decisions.
- **Trust/curious/co-creative state**: open-minded, gift-seeking, learning-oriented. Results in real feedback loops, better strategy, stronger team dynamics.

The job of a conscious leader is to notice which state they and their team are in — and shift.

**2. Emotions as data (whole-body intelligence)**

Rather than suppressing emotions at work, map each one to a signal:

| Emotion | Signal |
|---|---|
| Fear | Something wants attention |
| Sadness | Something wants to be let go |
| Anger | Something is not of service to me/my mission |
| Joy | Something wants to be celebrated |
| Creative energy | Something wants to be born into the world |

The practice: notice where in your body you feel each emotion. Acknowledge it to yourself. When safe, name it in the room — "I notice I feel fear around this" — to shift the group's state.

**3. Objective function clarity**

Most people are running on an implied objective function — values inherited from family, community, or career pressure — without ever consciously choosing it. Get explicit: what does future-you (5–10 years out) actually care about? That clarity filters what deserves your Sunday night.

**4. Vision through first principles + future imaging**

To develop strategy: close your eyes, imagine the world 5–10 years from now absent your company's influence. Build a vivid, emotionally real picture. Then apply first principles — what's physically/economically true regardless of who wins? That becomes the foundation for a product strategy that has pull, not just direction.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Team is stuck in blame cycles during reviews or retrospectives
- A leader is confusing presentation polish with actual impact
- Product strategy is stale and no one can articulate a compelling future
- You're sensing team misalignment but can't name it

**Doesn't apply when:**
- The team is in early execution mode with a clear, agreed direction — adding emotional processing to a fast sprint just adds friction
- The org culture has zero tolerance for this kind of language yet; introducing it without buy-in backfires
- The decision is purely technical with no interpersonal dynamics — first principles alone is enough

## Signature questions

1. "Which state are we in right now — threat/righteousness, or trust/curiosity — and what would it take to shift?"
2. "What emotion is present in the room that no one is naming?"
3. "What's your actual objective function — not the one your career pressure implies, but the one future-you in five years actually cares about?"
4. "If you close your eyes and imagine the world ten years from now absent your product, what do you actually see? What's going to be true regardless?"
5. "Are we in execution mode or vision mode right now — and is that a conscious choice or just the default?"

## Common misuse

The failure mode Nickels explicitly warns against: treating the emotional layer as an add-on to an already-packed meeting cadence. This framework requires protected time — you can't do genuine vision work in a 30-minute OKR review. The practice needs space: a long hike, an all-day whiteboard session with laptops down, a run. Squeezing it between back-to-back reviews produces nothing. The other misuse is going too far into vision-theory land — "all the PhDs locked in a room for two weeks and there's a beautiful whiteboard diagram" — without connecting to execution. The conscious part means being aware of which mode you're in and choosing deliberately, not camping permanently in either.
