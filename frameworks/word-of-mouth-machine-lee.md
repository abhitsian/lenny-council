## One-liner

Build a word-of-mouth machine first — obsess over the first 30 seconds of product experience until the product grows on its own, then amplify with influencer marketing and paid channels that multiply the signal rather than substitute for it.

## The framework

Grant Lee names four levers, applied in a specific order:

**1. The first 30 seconds**
Get the first 30 seconds of the product experience to feel magical — good enough that a new user will tell their friends. Only move to amplification after this is working. Gamma spent three to four months rebuilding onboarding post-Product Hunt launch because signups spiked but word-of-mouth never took hold. When the rebuilt experience shipped in March 2023, signups went from hundreds to 5,000 to 20,000 per day with zero marketing spend.

**2. The one-egg rule**
Give a new user one clear thing — not five features, not a tour of capabilities. "Throw them one egg, they can probably catch it. Throw them four or five eggs, they're probably going to drop all of them." The job is to earn the next 30 seconds, then the next 30 seconds after that.

**3. Micro-influencer echo chambers**
Work with thousands of micro-influencers across niche audience pockets rather than a handful of large creators. The pattern Grant calls an "echo chamber": teachers tell teachers, consultants tell consultants. Founders should personally onboard every early influencer so they can tell the product story in their own voice. LinkedIn outperformed all other platforms — 4–5x higher conversion rates than comparable channels.

**4. Performance marketing guardrails**
Only dial up paid acquisition after word-of-mouth is working. Never let paid acquisition exceed 50% of new sign-ups. If it does, the core growth engine is broken and paid spend just accelerates a leaky bucket. At $100M ARR, more than 50% of Gamma's new signups still come through word-of-mouth.

**Brand DNA as a prerequisite to scale**
Before creative output can scale to thousands of ad variants and influencer assets, the brand needs a replicable DNA — art direction, voice and tone, visual language that stays cohesive at volume. "You kind of have a feeling for, if I were to scale this up to a thousand pieces of creative, would it still feel cohesive or is it kind of all over the place?"

## When it applies / when it doesn't

Applies to: prosumer, B2C, and SMB products where the product is used and shared by the same person, and where onboarding is the choke point. Also relevant to B2B products with a self-serve motion — even in B2B, word-of-mouth from colleagues and former coworkers is a meaningful signal.

Less applicable to: pure enterprise B2B products with long procurement cycles where brand touch points before sign-up matter less.

The framework breaks down when a team skips step one — using influencer spend or paid ads to compensate for a broken onboarding experience. Grant is explicit that this is a trap: "The temptation would've been, hey, let's just spend more on ads or spend more on marketing. Because we'll just fuel the top of the funnel and everything else will work itself out. I think that would've been a trap."

## Signature questions

1. If you look at your new sign-up cohort today, what percentage found you through a direct or branded search — meaning someone told them about you? Is it growing?
2. Does your first 30 seconds of product experience earn the next 30 seconds? Would a complete stranger share it with a colleague without prompting?
3. Which niche communities use your product and also talk to each other regularly? Are you working with micro-influencers who are already part of those communities?
4. What share of your acquisition is coming from paid channels today? If it's over 50%, what's broken upstream?
5. If you gave a creator your brand assets right now and asked them to make 50 different posts — would they all feel like the same product?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Grant names most explicitly: treating influencer marketing as a turnkey, budget-and-forget program. Teams pick five large creators, hand over a script, and expect results. The script reads like an ad, the creator has no real connection to the product, and money gets wasted. The actual approach — personally onboarding each creator, helping them understand the product so they tell the story in their own voice, and working with dozens to hundreds of micro-influencers — is hard to scale in the short term. That difficulty is the point. It's what makes the downstream word-of-mouth feel authentic.

The second failure mode: skipping straight to paid or influencer spend before organic word-of-mouth is present. "Don't invest until you have word of mouth. Don't fool yourself into thinking that you'll solve other problems by just starting to ramp up a performance marketing program."
