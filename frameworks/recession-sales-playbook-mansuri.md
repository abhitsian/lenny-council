## One-liner

When the market contracts, shift your sales motion from top-line growth hunting to five interlocking moves: conservative milestone-based forecasting, retention-aligned comp, best-rep-on-CSM, research-driven warm prospecting, and rule-breaking product innovation.

## The framework

Mansuri names five pillars explicitly as the playbook for a downturn:

**1. Conservative milestone forecasting**
Plan assuming revenue goes down, not up. Set a floor number (e.g., 10% below current ARR), then define short-term quarterly or monthly checkpoints that pre-authorize you to revise up if you hit them — or revise down if you don't. Agree on the trigger thresholds with the board before the quarter starts, so there's no bias debate mid-quarter. Founders trend optimistic by default; this structure removes the temptation to keep forecasting your way out of reality.

**2. Retention-aligned comp plans**
Standard SaaS comp rewards new ARR only. Reps who close lots of deals that later churn are paid the same as (or more than) reps who close fewer, stickier deals. Mansuri's fix: tie a portion of comp and accelerators to renewal rates, reference-ability, and upsell on their book. The metric that matters in a downturn is net dollar retention, so the comp plan has to reflect that. The 50-50 base/variable split itself is also worth revisiting — it signals mercenary incentives rather than business alignment.

**3. Move your best AEs to CSM**
Cold outreach response rates hit historic lows in a downturn. Enterprise sales cycles stretch from ~62 days to ~115 days. New logo acquisition slows to a crawl. In this environment, the highest-leverage move is plugging the leaky bucket: take your best pre-sales reps and redeploy them to protect your existing customer base. The goal is zero churn long enough to survive until the market recovers. This is counterintuitive — most orgs save top talent for hunting — but Mansuri ran this move at Bravado himself.

**4. Research-driven warm prospecting**
Cold email is where deals go to die. Text intros from existing customers are where deals get done. The sequence: host a customers-only in-person event, collect warm referral names at the event, ask for a text intro (not email), keep the introducer on the thread for the first 10-20 messages to hold the prospect accountable. The pipeline engine is your current customer base. Use your unique cross-customer data to create research-grade content that prospects can't get elsewhere — the way Mansuri sent Sheryl Sandberg a custom Facebook employee sentiment report and got a same-day reply.

**5. Change the rules — don't optimize**
Optimizing the existing playbook (cutting price 10%, tweaking email sequences) won't work when the fundamental supply-demand equilibrium has shifted. Instead, look at what your buyers now need that the old model can't deliver. Bravado's answer was Bravado Flex — 100% commission-only or fractional sales roles — which went from 0 to one of their best months ever when full-time recruiting dried up. The move: stare at the whiteboard from the buyer's perspective, identify what constraint just changed, and build a product or motion that matches the new reality.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Market conditions have shifted toward capital efficiency and budget freezes (downturns, rate rises, funding contractions)
- New logo pipeline is drying up while existing customer churn risk rises
- Your comp plan rewards top-line volume with no retention accountability

**Doesn't apply when:**
- Market is in hyper-growth mode and top-line ARR is the primary investor signal
- You have no existing customer base to protect or leverage
- Your sales motion is primarily self-serve / PLG with no direct sales reps

Mansuri is explicit: "The way in which you sell has to be different based on market condition." These pillars are recession-specific, not universal.

## Signature questions

1. What percentage of our sales reps hit quota last quarter — and is the trend up or down month over month?
2. Does our comp plan reward a rep who closes 15 deals that mostly churn the same as one who closes 10 deals that all renew and upsell?
3. If cold outreach is failing, what research or insight can we create from our own data that would be worth a prospect's time regardless of whether they buy?
4. Are we deploying our best account executives where they can win new logos, or should we redeploy them to defend the customer base we already have?
5. What would our pricing, hiring, or go-to-market look like if we designed it from scratch for this market — not for the market of 18 months ago?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Mansuri explicitly warns against is optimizing your way out of a structural problem. Cutting price 10%, running more cold emails, or adjusting quotas slightly are all optimizations within the old model. When the supply-demand equilibrium has fundamentally shifted, those moves produce the same failed result with more effort. The right move is to step back and ask what the rules of the game are now — not how to play the old game slightly better. His Bravado Flex example is the proof: they didn't try harder at full-time recruiting; they built a different product for the new reality and had one of their best months ever.
