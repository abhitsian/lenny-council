## One-liner

Define what "good" looks like in terms of shipping frequency at every level of the org, then use gaps against that definition as the actual management conversation.

## The framework

Campbell calls this the Tempo Framework and describes it in three layers:

1. **Mission metric + guiding principles at the top.** Leadership sets: what do we do, how do we measure it, how are we going to get there. At ProfitWell this was "we automate subscription growth," measured by total ARR on the platform, achieved by being "the most helpful brand in SaaS."

2. **Org-level tempo definition.** Each function (marketing, product, sales) determines what good looks like in terms of shipping frequency. Examples: one product launch per month, one big launch per quarter, this many podcast episodes, this many content pieces. This is not aspirational — it is the agreed expectation.

3. **The gap conversation.** Leadership meetings become simple: "Here's what good looks like. Here's where we are. Why is there a gap?" The answers — resources, cross-team dependencies, unclear ownership — are all solvable. The framework makes the conversation concrete instead of evaluative.

The result is that "Tim isn't performing" problems get correctly diagnosed as org problems. If Tim was a proven performer elsewhere and is underperforming here, the question is what expectation Tim was operating against.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** Teams are smart but slow. Leadership is aligned but the signal doesn't reach layers below. You're having performance conversations without a shared definition of what good output looks like.

**Does not apply when:** You are pre-product-market fit and still figuring out what to ship. Cadence without direction compounds the wrong direction. Org design questions (who reports to whom) are also separate — Campbell is explicit that tempo framework comes first; org design is downstream.

## Signature questions

1. What does good look like for this team in terms of shipping frequency — and have we written that down anywhere?
2. If someone is underperforming, is it actually a Tim problem, or did we never set the expectation?
3. Are leadership and the teams below them aligned on tempo, or is alignment only happening at the top?
4. When we miss the target, what is the actual root cause — resources, cross-team friction, unclear ownership?
5. Do we have a shared mission metric that every org-level tempo definition feeds into?

## Common misuse

Treating tempo as a metric rather than a conversation. The framework fails if leadership sets frequency targets and then just measures whether they were hit. The point is the gap conversation — when someone misses the target, you find out why and you solve it. Skipping the "why" and going straight to performance management defeats the whole mechanism.

Campbell also warns against letting org design dominate the shipping problem. Teams often reach for restructuring when the actual fix is a shared definition of what good looks like and a regular conversation to close the gap.
