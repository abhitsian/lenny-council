## One-liner

Disruptive ideas generate two kinds of pushback — "why-not" objections that recite real-but-solvable obstacles, and "what-if" thinking that maps the upside if those obstacles fall — and the job is to lead with the what-if before you let the why-nots run the conversation.

## The framework

Sam names two moves that happen when a genuinely new idea enters the room:

**Why-not questions** — These are objections framed as blockers: the browser isn't ready, people won't trust cloud storage, what happens on an airplane without connectivity. They cite real constraints, but they're anchored to today's state. If you answer them before establishing the value, you kill the idea before it has a chance to prove itself. Sam heard the airplane objection 100 times pitching Writely. "Those are all just why-not questions."

**What-if thinking** — This is asking what the world looks like if the obstacle gets solved. What if payload to space costs a lot less? What if collaboration happens without sending attachments? What if the browser becomes a real app platform? The what-if has to be compelling enough that you're willing to spend the years it takes to answer the why-nots. With Writely, Sam and his co-founders stumbled into the what-if first (two people editing the same document) before they understood how hard the collaboration problem was. That ordering mattered — if they'd hit the hard problem first, they probably would have stopped.

**How the two work together** — The what-if sets the direction; the why-nots become the engineering backlog. As Sam puts it: once you have the what-if, the why-nots stop being reasons to quit and start being problems to solve.

**Supporting signals:**

- *Bifurcation test* — Truly disruptive products produce love-it-hate-it reactions, not mild indifference. If response is mostly in the middle, the product is incremental. Writely had early boosters who liked it more than the team did, and executives at Google telling them to stop. ChatGPT divided the world in two. Bell-curve products don't change categories.

- *User laziness as a design law* — Convenience always wins. The total energy a user must expend (learning, friction, habit-building) has to be less than the resulting ease — usually by a factor of around 10. Writely didn't ask for an email address on signup. Two minutes in, a gentle ask: just your email so we can send you a link if you close the tab.

- *North-star goal-setting* — Pure tech-play without a target rarely produces something useful. Pick an arbitrary but real goal that points somewhere interesting, then grind toward it. The goal makes surprising failures legible — you notice when something broke interestingly rather than just filing it under "doesn't work."

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when** you're evaluating a genuinely new technology or category-creating idea, deciding whether to start a project, or diagnosing why an organization keeps killing good ideas early. Also useful when you're on the receiving end of pushback on a nascent product.

**Doesn't apply** to incremental product work where the category exists, the problem is established, and the question is execution quality. Why-not/what-if is a framing tool for the zero-to-one phase, not an ongoing operating model.

**Edge**: The framework can become a rationalization for ignoring legitimate constraints. Sam's own test is whether the what-if produces real user value even in the best case. For crypto, his honest what-if answer was "then I have to run the SEC on my personal finances" — not compelling — so he concluded the pessimism was justified.

## Signature questions

1. If this obstacle were solved five years from now, what does the best case world look like? Is that world worth building toward?
2. Are the objections I'm hearing describing a permanent wall or a today-state that the world will move past on its own?
3. What reaction is the idea getting? Love-it-or-hate-it, or mild indifference? What does that tell me about whether it's actually disruptive?
4. Am I answering why-nots before I've established what the what-if is worth? (If yes: stop, articulate the value ceiling first.)
5. Is the user value clear in the best case, even if I don't believe execution will get there? No clear user value at ceiling = legitimate reason to stop.

## Common misuse

The failure mode Sam explicitly warns against: using why-not objections as a completeness check — gathering every blocker and treating the list as a go/no-go decision. This guarantees you kill the things worth building, because every genuinely disruptive idea has a long list of real obstacles. The discipline is to establish what the what-if is worth *before* letting the why-nots set the agenda.

A related failure: treating optimism as a stance rather than a tool. Sam is clear he is not naturally optimistic. He treats it as a conscious choice made because "there's not much of a prize for being pessimistic and right" — and because pessimistic framing makes you miss the surprising, useful failure modes that optimistic experiments surface.
