## One-liner

Treat the startup as an experiment: name what you believe, build the smallest thing that tests it, learn from real customers, and change strategy when the evidence says so while keeping the vision.

## The framework

1. **Start from the leap-of-faith assumption.** Before you scope anything, get the founder to say plainly what they actually believe will work. The hard part isn't the MVP — it's admitting you don't yet know if the belief is true. Ries' diagnosis: when people struggle to define an MVP, the problem is upstream — they aren't clear on what they want to learn.
2. **Build the MVP — the most efficient way to test that hypothesis.** MVP is not a stripped-down, low-quality product. It's whatever gets you the validation you need fastest. "Minimum" is fluid and set by your market; for a data-center battery buyer the bar is high, and the MVP still has to clear it. The practical heuristic: list the features you think are necessary, cut the list in half, cut it again, and build that.
3. **Run a series of experiments, not one.** You can't conclude anything from a single launch. While the product is half-built, put the half-built thing in front of customers — it costs nothing extra and tells you something. Find 10 customers, not 10 million, and let them tell you what's awful.
4. **Establish a baseline (innovation accounting).** Measure how bad things are now so you can prove you improved. Without a baseline, a high-quality launch that nobody uses leaves you unable to say why — wrong quality bar, or wrong value prop.
5. **Pivot or persevere.** A pivot is a change in strategy without a change in vision — one foot anchored in what you've learned, the other moving. When the experiments stop being productive (the metric is "up and to the right" but nowhere near what you need), give yourself a fixed window to test the new direction heads-down, then decide.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** any situation of genuine uncertainty where you don't yet know what the customer wants — new products, new features, corporate innovation (the *Startup Way* version is aimed at large companies). Ries is explicit that MVP scales across stakes and industries: he's used it on jet engines, robotics, cancer therapies, and an MVP that took 10 years to build (LTSE).
- **Doesn't / weaker fit:** when a team holds a core value or craft standard they'd rather go out of business than violate — that's not a leap-of-faith assumption to test, so don't pretend to test it, just build it that way. And the method only works if you can psychologically admit failure; if you can't accept "this didn't work," you can't learn, and the framework gives you nothing.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- What do you actually believe will work here? Tell me the leap-of-faith assumption, then we'll double-check it.
- What are we testing with this build? If the answer is "everything at full quality," you're not testing — you're just shipping.
- What's the smallest version that would still tell you something true? Now cut it in half.
- What's the baseline? If this launches and nobody uses it, will you know whether it's the value prop or the quality?
- If you're asking me whether to pivot — what does the evidence already tell you? (Working teams don't have time for this question.)

## Common misuse

Treating MVP as "build something cheap and low-quality." Ries warns against this directly: quality is defined by the customer, and an MVP that lacks the craft your thesis depends on doesn't actually test the thesis. The other failure he names is doing one experiment, getting a bad result, and declaring the value prop dead — "from one experiment you can never know." The deeper misuse is using the vocabulary to avoid the real work: people who can't admit they don't know what customers want, or can't admit something failed, will go through the motions of MVPs and pivots without ever learning anything.
