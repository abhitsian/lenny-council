## One-liner

Lead with an undeniable shift in the world (old game → new game), name the stakes so the
buyer sees winners and losers, point at the object of the new game they should want, then
position your product as the magic gifts that get them past the obstacles in the way.

## The framework

Raskin's structure has five pieces, in order. He draws it from movie structure, not from
the "arrogant doctor" pitch most people learn (problem → my solution → why it beats the
alternatives).

1. **Name the shift — old game to new game.** Every story opens with a shift in the world.
   Name it concisely: software → cloud (Salesforce), transactions → subscriptions (Zuora),
   opinions → reality (Gong). The naming is the hard part — being compact costs you
   completeness, and that's fine. This is what turns a pitch into a movement.
2. **Name the stakes.** Show winners are already playing the new game (Zuora pointed at
   Airbnb, Box) and that the future is split between a very negative and a very positive
   outcome — not just "sort of okay." His Star Wars frame: George Lucas kills Luke's aunt
   and uncle so the stakes become life and death. That's what "make it emotional" actually
   means in practice.
3. **Name the object of the new game** (he used to call this the *promised land*). Boil it
   down to a couple of words that read as the rallying cry / buyer mission: "turn customers
   into subscribers" (Zuora), "live there" / "belong anywhere" (Airbnb). Often best phrased
   as a question — "what would it take to turn every customer into a subscriber?" — so the
   buyer comes along as a co-author.
4. **Name the obstacles.** The things standing between the buyer and the object. These are
   the same things you'd normally call "the problems we solve," but because you set up the
   story first, they land as obstacles to a life-and-death goal, so they carry weight.
5. **Show the magic gifts.** Now — and only now — talk about your capabilities, features,
   and success stories. In movie terms these are the magic gifts the hero gets to win.
   This is where the product finally enters.

The buyer is the hero of this story. You're not the one being saved — by spelling out the
shift you change the buyer's world and invite them to come with you.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** aligning a leadership team and a sales motion around one story (sales decks,
  fundraising, marketing, recruiting); products selling a genuine change in how a market
  works, where there's a real shift you can name and winners you can point to.
- **Doesn't / be careful:** there's no template. Raskin actively refuses to license one —
  every team's narrative is a different number of slides, sometimes zero slides. Treat
  these as principles for building the story, not a fill-in-the-blanks formula. And if you
  can't name a real shift in the world, you don't have a strategic narrative yet — a list
  of "used to be / now it is" bullets isn't the same thing.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- What's the shift — the old game and the new game — in two or three words each? If you
  can't name it that tightly, you don't have it yet.
- Who are the winners already playing the new game, and what happens to the buyer if they
  don't move? Is the future genuinely split into bad and good, or just "sort of okay"?
- What's the object of the new game — the rallying cry — and can you phrase it as a question
  the buyer would want answered?
- Are you starting with the shift, or are you the arrogant doctor leading with your solution
  and why it beats the alternatives?
- When you say this shift out loud to a customer, do they say "yes, that's playing out for
  us" — or do you have to convince them it's real?

## Common misuse

The failure he names directly: people get hold of the Zuora deck, slap their logo on it, and
ship it — or they write a generic "the world is changing" slide with a long "used to be" /
"now it is" bullet list. Neither works. The work is in *naming* the old and new game
concisely, and in building a story specific to your team. He refuses to sell a template for
exactly this reason — "these are principles for building it, not any prescribed formula."
