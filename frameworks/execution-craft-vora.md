## One-liner

Spend the bulk of your time building the execution machine that gets a good-enough strategy
to market and improves it with customer feedback, because a perfect strategy that never ships
teaches you nothing — and that machine is what tells you whether your strategy or your
execution was the thing that was wrong.

## The framework

1. **Bias toward execution, not strategy.** Strategy is the glamorous part — whiteboarding,
   five-year plans, second and third-order effects. Customers don't see any of it. They see
   the product in their hands and whether someone took the time to fix the bug. The grind
   (the donuts, the dashboards, rewriting the spec) is what reaches the customer.
2. **Run the time split: ~20% strategy, the rest execution.** You can't have a *bad*
   strategy, so spend some time on it — but the bulk goes to confirming the strategy works
   for customers and building the machine to keep improving it. This holds even as you get
   senior; you execute on the *system* more, but you stay close to the customer.
3. **Good-enough strategy + perfect execution beats the reverse.** Perfect strategy with poor
   execution never makes it to market, so you win nothing and learn nothing — you can't tell
   if the strategy or the execution failed. Good-enough strategy with great execution may not
   win immediately, but you *know* the execution was solid, so you go back, update the
   strategy, relaunch, and repeat until it wins.
4. **Make strategy earn its keep by changing behavior.** A strategy exercise that doesn't
   change what you build, how you build it, or how you allocate people was just a feel-good
   session. Force the question: what changes for the customer as a result of this?
5. **Use feedback as a loop, not a verdict.** On receiving feedback: getting it doesn't mean
   you act on it as-is. There's a step in between — decide whether to take it in that exact
   way, look for more themes, or hold your position and give people better context on the
   trade-offs. On giving and using feedback: sublimate the ego, treat being right as less
   important than the outcome, and notice how much better things get when you stay open.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** teams stuck polishing strategy decks instead of shipping; PMs whose growth
  feedback is "be more strategic" but whose products aren't reaching customers; senior leaders
  drifting away from the build. Also any spec where the team can't say what the customer will
  actually feel differently after this ships.
- **Doesn't (the boundary she names herself):** she admits she retrenches *too far* into
  execution and worries too little about long-term strategy — that's her own stated bias.
  The 20% floor is there for a reason: you genuinely can't run on a bad strategy, and a
  year-long heads-down build can ship into a market that already moved. Execution-first is
  not execution-only.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- If we ship this, what specifically changes for the customer — what will they feel that they
  don't feel today?
- If this loses, will you be able to tell whether the strategy was wrong or the execution
  was? If not, you don't have an execution machine yet.
- What are you actually spending your time on this week — strategy, or getting the thing to
  market and unblocking the people building it?
- This came out of a strategy exercise — what behavior or portfolio allocation are we changing
  because of it? If nothing, what was the point?
- On this feedback you got: are you taking it exactly as given, looking for the deeper theme,
  or holding your position and explaining the trade-off?

## Common misuse

Reading "execution eats strategy" as license to skip strategy entirely. Vora keeps a 20% floor
on strategy and flags her own over-retrenchment into execution as a real bias, not a virtue.
The other misuse is the inverse: treating a strategy offsite as the deliverable. If the
five-year plan changes nothing about what you build or how you allocate people, it was a
feel-good exercise — the strategy only counts once it changes behavior and reaches the customer.
