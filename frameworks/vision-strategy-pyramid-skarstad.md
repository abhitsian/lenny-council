## One-liner

Structure strategy as a pyramid — Vision at top, Mission/Strategy in the middle, Objectives (OKRs) at the bottom — then gate every decision by whether it can be undone, reserving scrutiny for the irreversible ones.

## The framework

**Layer 1 — Vision**: Where do you need to go in the long term? Nickey frames this as a 10-year zoom-out. Write it down. It should be concrete enough that teams can test decisions against it.

**Layer 2 — Mission and Strategy**: A layer of abstraction below vision. Mission is how you make the vision come to life. Strategy is how you pick apart what needs to happen to actually execute on the vision.

**Layer 3 — Objectives (OKRs)**: Quarterly commitments. Good OKRs are "clear articulations of your strategy" that bring things down to "in the next three months, here's what we're working on." The goal is to give teams an actionable bridge from strategy to the Jira ticket level.

**Decision gating — one-way vs. two-way doors**: Any decision that limits future choices, cascades through the whole ecosystem, or is hard to reverse is a one-way door. Spend real time on these — gather input, get leadership aligned, debate. Two-way door decisions are changeable later; let teams cruise on those. Autonomy on the small decisions, deliberation on the permanent ones.

**Second-order thinking as operating practice**: When making a decision, trace how it affects the next level of decisions, and the level after that. For any change to a shared platform or marketplace, map the cascade. Shopify's first-principles discipline is a concrete operationalization: write down what you care about before design begins, so teams stop arguing about foundations mid-build.

**Three product-review gates**: First-principles alignment ("what are you solving for?"), approach approval ("how are you going to solve it?"), ship-readiness check. The first gate is the most important — it stops teams from building the right thing wrong.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when**: You need to take a multi-quarter strategy and make it legible to a team at the feature level. Also applies when making any change to a complex marketplace, platform, or UGC ecosystem — places where one decision can cascade through thousands of downstream behaviors.

**Does not apply when**: You are deep in 0-to-1 pre-product-market-fit work where the hypothesis is still unproven. Nickey explicitly notes that early-stage founders rightly skip second-order thinking to focus on PMF — but they pay for it at scale when they have to rebuild.

## Signature questions

1. If you zoomed out 10 years, what does the ideal state look like? Can you write it down in one or two sentences?
2. Is this a one-way or a two-way door? If you make this decision today, what decisions does it close off tomorrow?
3. Does this OKR trace back to a clear line in your strategy, or is it floating?
4. Who on the team was not involved in drafting this strategy? Why not, and what do they know that you might be missing?
5. What are the first principles for this feature? Write them down before design starts — what do you care about, and what explicitly doesn't matter?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Nickey warns against explicitly: building strategy in a vacuum and presenting it to the team as a done deal. Getting input after the strategy is finalized means teams weren't involved in crafting it, so buy-in is slow and fragile. Bring people into the process early — not to vote, but so they have a hand in shaping it. The decision authority still sits with one person.

A second misuse: applying the same review gate process to all decisions regardless of reversibility. Requiring sign-off on two-way door decisions slows teams down and signals distrust. The pyramid and the gate system are meant to create more autonomy on small decisions, not less.
