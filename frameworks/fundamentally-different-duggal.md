## One-liner

Insurgents cannot win on incumbents' ground — find the vector where your product is structurally different on quality, complexity, and price simultaneously, then refuse to scale until a 50%+ Sean Ellis score confirms customers actually love it.

## The framework

Duggal applies this principle at four connected levels:

**1. Strategic — pick the right fight**
Don't enter a market to be somewhat better. Find a dimension where you can redefine the category rather than compete within it. Nubank's bet was branchless digital credit in Latin America — not cheaper banking, but banking structured around the smartphone at a no-fee price point that incumbents couldn't match without dismantling their branch cost base.

**2. Product gate — the Sean Ellis threshold**
Before scaling any product, measure product-market fit using the Sean Ellis survey: ask users how disappointed they'd be if the product disappeared. Duggal's team requires 50% to answer "very disappointed" (raised from Sean Ellis's standard 40% to account for Brazilians skewing polite). Products below threshold stay in iteration, regardless of organizational pressure to scale.

**3. Customer discovery — hypothesis-driven, observe over ask**
Before any research, write a crisp hypothesis. Then test it by observing or asking indirectly rather than validating what you already believe. Direct questions ("would you love this?") produce social desirability bias. Observation and indirect questioning reveal the actual pain. When the team wants richer feedback quickly, the advice is to pick up the phone and call 10 customers yourself — by call five, you can predict what calls six through ten will say.

**4. Discipline against premature scale**
"We are not going to take a small problem and scale it." Scaling before PMF turns a small mess into a large one and burns the company's ability to build word-of-mouth growth. The corollary: once PMF is confirmed, scaling can largely take care of itself because customers tell their friends.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're an insurgent entering a market dominated by entrenched incumbents
- You're launching a new product line and need a go/no-go signal before committing resources to scale
- Customer acquisition depends on word of mouth rather than paid growth

**Doesn't apply cleanly when:**
- You're building sustaining features around a core product that already has PMF — those can be incrementally better and that's fine
- You're entering an adjacent market specifically to match competitive parity; Duggal acknowledges not every one of the hundred things you launch in a year can be fundamentally different

## Signature questions

1. In your product review today, how is this product fundamentally different — not slightly better — on quality, complexity, and price? If you can only answer on one of the three, is that enough?
2. What is the Sean Ellis score for this product, and what is the specific cohort that is already hitting 50%+? What does that tell you about which version of the product to double down on?
3. Before we scale this, are we solving a small problem cleanly, or are we scaling a large problem we haven't fixed yet?
4. What is your crisp hypothesis going into customer discovery — not what you hope to hear, but what you predict they will say that would validate building this at all?
5. If you are fighting on the incumbents' ground, what is your disruptive vector that changes the rules of the fight?

## Common misuse

The framework gets misapplied when teams treat it as a slogan rather than a gate. Duggal is explicit: "There are certain features where you're in the incrementally better business. Not every single thing of the hundred things you launch in a year can be fundamentally different." The failure mode is using the phrase to feel good about a product launch without actually running the Sean Ellis measurement, finding the bullseye cohort that already loves it, and iterating to expand that cohort before scaling. The other misuse is confusing "fundamentally different" with being novel or unconventional — it has to be different on dimensions customers care enough about to become very disappointed if the product went away.
