## One-liner

Run discovery and delivery simultaneously, and deliberately prioritize your riskiest bets first in discovery — because safe bets alone will never get you to true innovation.

## The framework

Camille attributes this to Marty Cagan and her experience at Hailo. The core structure has two tracks running at the same time:

**Discovery track** — continuously de-risk assumptions about ideas you haven't built yet. This is not a waterfall handoff ("designers tinker for months, then lob over the wall to coders"). It is a live, ongoing loop.

**Delivery track** — execute things that have already been de-risked into the predictable, low-risk column.

The critical wrinkle Camille adds: most teams instinctively pull high-risk bets *out* of the discovery queue in favor of more predictable, smaller swings. Her instruction is the opposite — **put the riskiest, highest-impact bets at the top of the discovery queue first**. The 2x2 (impact vs. effort or risk vs. reward) is a useful starting point, but the move is to take the top-right quadrant (biggest swing) and actively work to de-risk it rather than defer it indefinitely.

Once something is de-risked sufficiently, it moves into delivery. That is when you execute with speed and predictability.

The leader's role in this model: take the hot seat. Give the team permission to fail and take accountability when high-risk bets don't pan out. The team shouldn't be afraid to probe the dangerous assumptions; that's the whole point.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have a team capable of running parallel workstreams (discovery and delivery are separate concerns, not serialized)
- Your roadmap skews toward predictable delivery and keeps deferring the ambitious bets
- You are in a space where true innovation is required, not just incremental optimization

**Does not apply well when:**
- The team is small enough that context-switching between discovery and delivery is more expensive than serializing
- You are in pure execution mode — the strategic questions are already answered and the work is implementation
- The "risky" bets are actually just unresearched ideas with no way to run a meaningful discovery loop

## Signature questions

1. Look at your current roadmap: what is the highest-risk, highest-impact bet on there? When did it last get any discovery attention, or has it been sitting untouched for quarters?
2. Are you running discovery and delivery as parallel tracks, or are you still in a waterfall pattern where one feeds the other?
3. If a risky bet doesn't pan out, who is accountable — the leader or the team? What does your answer tell you about whether you've actually created permission to fail?
4. What assumptions are you making about your riskiest idea that you haven't tested yet? What is the cheapest way to find out if one of those assumptions is wrong?
5. When you do your planning, do you move risky ideas into the safe zone over time, or do they stay indefinitely in the "we'll get to it someday" pile?

## Common misuse

The most common failure is using the 2x2 to *justify deferring* the high-risk quadrant rather than to surface it for immediate discovery. Teams plot the opportunities, see something is risky, and respond by deprioritizing it. Camille's explicit warning: that pattern is exactly how you avoid genuine innovation. The framework only works if the risky bets actually get discovery investment, not just a position on a slide.

A second failure mode is treating "dual track" as permission to have designers disappear into a months-long discovery sprint. That is waterfall with a different label. Discovery must be continuous, lightweight, and tied to assumptions — not a phase.
