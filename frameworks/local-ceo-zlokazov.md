## One-liner

Product owners run fully cross-functional teams as autonomous local CEOs — with line management authority, end-to-end business ownership, and a non-negotiable bar for polished, wow products — while leadership dives deep on a rotating 7–10 priority projects to keep accountability without micromanagement.

## The framework

Zlokazov's model has five interlocking components:

**1. Local CEO ownership**
Product owners (not "mini-CEOs") are the line managers for everyone on their team — engineers, designers, data analysts, ops managers. Functional managers define *how* things are built; product owners define *what* to build and own the outcomes. The title "product owner" is not a scrum role — it signals genuine end-to-end accountability.

**2. Three product owner archetypes**
- *UX product owners* — consumer-facing, strong product taste, deep domain knowledge
- *Technical product owners* — typically former engineers; go deepest into technical details, read code with the team
- *Data science product owners* — former data scientists, very hands-on with metrics and experimentation

Zlokazov estimates 85–90% of the role is identical across all three. The specialization is a 5–15% variation on top of a common base: problem-solving, customer empathy, deep technical fluency, and business acumen.

**3. Selective deep-dives (the 7–10 rule)**
With ~100 projects in flight at any time, leadership doesn't track all of them. Instead, 7–10 highest-impact projects get genuine depth — sitting with engineers, reading code, reviewing every design decision. This is not micromanagement of those teams; it's signal to the entire org about what "good" looks like and what accountability feels like. Other teams self-regulate because they know their turn will come.

**4. Founder-level screen review**
Founders review 100% of screens shipped. Weekly product reviews give every product owner direct access to CEO and CTO. This is the "last line of control" — not a bottleneck, but a quality floor that makes radical autonomy safe to grant everywhere else.

**5. Wow before scale**
New products launch to a small first cohort, but even that cohort gets a fully polished product. No scrappy MVPs. The logic: if an early version fails to get traction, you need to know whether the *idea* is wrong, not the execution. Cutting features is allowed; cutting quality is not.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're building a high-volume, multi-product platform where shipping velocity matters as much as individual product quality
- The company has founder-level product taste at the top willing to review everything
- You're hiring smart generalists early in their career who will grow into full ownership roles
- Your product surface is complex enough that deep technical fluency is actually required (fintech, regulatory-heavy, multi-jurisdiction)

**Does not apply when:**
- Leadership is not willing or able to run the founder-review layer (the autonomy model breaks without the quality floor)
- Teams are too small to form cross-functional pods with genuine line-management authority
- The product domain doesn't reward depth — commodity products or pure marketing plays don't need engineers who read code with their PMs

## Signature questions

1. If this product shipped tomorrow and got no traction — would you know whether the idea was wrong or the product was just not good enough?
2. What does this product owner actually own? Can they hire, prioritize, and ship without asking permission?
3. Is this team fully cross-functional, or are they still waiting on a shared resource they don't control?
4. When did you last sit with an engineer and read the code for this feature? What did you learn?
5. Is this product 99% done — and if so, is marketing, customer support, and sales ready to use it fully?

## Common misuse

The framework is often mistaken for aggressive micromanagement dressed up as ownership. Zlokazov is explicit that the goal is *maximum autonomy* — the founder screen review and the 7–10 deep-dives exist to make autonomy safe to grant, not to override decisions. The failure mode is leaders who use this model to justify diving into everything, which kills the "local CEO" principle entirely. The other misuse is launching scrappy MVPs and calling it lean — the model explicitly forbids compromising on quality and UX even in a small first cohort, because it destroys your ability to read the signal from early traction data.
