## One-liner

Performance growth requires diversifying spend across four channels — paid, SEO, virality, and sales — managed like a fund portfolio, with creative assets (not tooling or bidding) as the primary optimization lever.

## The framework

Jonathan names four growth engines that any company can draw from: paid/performance marketing, SEO, virality, and sales. The core move is treating budget allocation across these channels the same way a fund manager treats a portfolio of assets.

**The four engines:**
1. **Paid/performance marketing** — Google, Meta, TikTok, Amazon; measurable ROI but volatile if overweighted
2. **SEO** — longer attribution cycles, harder to tie cause to effect, but compound returns when built correctly
3. **Virality** — referral mechanics, word of mouth, product-led loops
4. **Sales** — direct outreach, especially for B2B lead generation

**The fund manager model:**
- Each channel is like a stock position: individually volatile, collectively stable when diversified
- "We decrease the reliance of any individual project on a singular channel and its performance"
- Crash-and-burn happens when a brand finds a loophole or a single channel that scales fast, then the conditions change (algorithm update, iOS privacy shift, macro downturn) and the whole business collapses

**The creative leverage thesis:**
- Channels like Google and Meta have automated almost all bidding and targeting levers over the last 7–10 years
- The last remaining human lever is creative: what assets you show, to whom, at what funnel stage
- Creative testing requires isolating a single variable across two nearly identical ad sets, measuring on leveling metrics (click-through rate, impressions-to-conversion), and feeding learnings back to the creative team
- Authenticity beats polish: user-generated-style content consistently outperformed highly produced brand assets

**Attribution as ongoing investigation:**
- There is no single source of truth; anyone claiming otherwise is not being accurate
- Classic last-click cookie-based attribution broke when Apple removed IDFA in 2021
- Modern approach: triangulate cookie attribution + media mix modeling (regression analysis, e.g. Recast) + customer surveys + statistical modeling
- The goal is to find evidence that validates or invalidates campaign performance — including being willing to declare campaigns don't work

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A company has achieved product-market fit and is asking "to what scale can this work?" not "does this work?"
- Budget exists across multiple channels and there is organizational willingness to not over-optimize a single one
- Creative resources (team + budget) can produce and iterate assets, not just hand off a single brand file
- There is a marketing professional in-house who can interface with the agency or channel team, provide context, and act on attribution findings

**Doesn't apply when:**
- There is no product-market fit — paid acquisition will amplify a broken funnel, not fix it
- A company is early-stage and entirely looking to performance marketing to both discover and scale (the over-reliance risk)
- The business model requires very long payback periods without any modeling infrastructure (LTV/CAC modeling, lead scoring) to proxy for latent revenue
- Creative production capacity is zero — TikTok in particular fails without a machine producing net-new assets multiple times per week

## Signature questions

1. Are you over-indexed on a single channel? What happens to your business if that channel's conditions change tomorrow?
2. Who owns the feedback loop between your ad performance data and your creative team? Is it a formal process or anecdotal?
3. Is your attribution methodology measuring what you actually did, or what the last click was? How would you know if your campaigns weren't working?
4. At what point in your funnel does the customer journey become invisible to you — and are you treating that gap as a problem to solve or a known blind spot?
5. Are you optimizing toward cheap leads (CPL) or toward high-value customers? Are those two things pointing in the same direction for your business?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Jonathan warns against explicitly: finding a channel loophole or single high-performing tactic, scaling the whole business on it, then being blindsided when conditions change. He uses his own Uber referral arbitrage as the example — it worked until it didn't, and brands that built their entire growth on similar shortcuts "will not be very happy and the business will suffer dramatically."

A second failure mode: chasing low cost-per-lead as the efficiency metric for B2B lead gen. Cheaper leads often yield lower-value customers. The correct frame is cost per *high-value* customer, which requires building a lead scoring model that predicts conversion quality — not just volume.
