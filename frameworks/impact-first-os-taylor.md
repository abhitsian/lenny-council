## One-liner

Every morning, ask "What is the most impactful thing I could do today?" — and then be ruthlessly honest about whether the answer you give is actually true or just a reflection of your comfort zone.

## The framework

Taylor developed this operating system after a direct conversation with Sheryl Sandberg caught him conforming his job to the things he liked to do rather than to what the role actually required. The framework has four interlocking pieces:

**1. Flexible identity**
Stop organizing your identity around your superpower. Taylor describes himself as a builder, not an engineer — that framing lets him shift into management, partnerships, sales, or whatever the organization needs without experiencing it as a betrayal of who he is. "You really need to be able to not have such an ossified view of your identity that you can't transform into what the company needs you to be at that point."

**2. The daily impact question**
The central heuristic: "What is the most impactful thing I could do today?" Frame it as if an external board of advisors is telling you where you have maximum leverage. Sometimes that's recruiting. Sometimes it's product. Sometimes it's a partnership call you've been avoiding.

**3. Intellectual honesty about root cause**
The question is only useful if you answer it correctly, which most people don't. Taylor's specific failure mode is what he calls "single issue voting" — the tendency to reach for your discipline as the solution to every problem. Engineers engineer. Designers redesign. BD people find partnerships. The framework requires questioning whether the answer you instinctively reach for is true or merely comfortable. He estimates there's "at least 30% likely that you've chosen that because of comfort and familiarity not truth."

**4. Orthogonal advisors**
Build a board, co-founder set, or advisor network that is deliberately different from your own biases. The FriendFeed loss to Twitter wasn't a product problem — it was a distribution problem Bret didn't see because his team was 11 engineers building product while Twitter got celebrities onto the platform. The corrective isn't just to ask for advice; it's to ask advisors whose defaults are orthogonal to yours, and then ask "why" repeatedly to extract the underlying first principle rather than cargo-culting the rule.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are a founder or executive with genuine latitude over how you spend your time
- You are moving between roles or functions and need a portable decision heuristic
- You suspect you're working on something because it's comfortable, not because it's the highest leverage

**Does not apply (or needs modification) when:**
- You're an IC contributor whose role is scoped — the question becomes "what is the highest leverage thing within my scope today?"
- You're early-stage with no signal yet; you need enough data to know what "impactful" even means for your specific situation
- The organization has structural incentives that override individual impact judgment — in that case, fix the incentives first

## Signature questions

1. If you had an external board of advisors watching you work this week, what would they say you're spending time on that you shouldn't be?
2. When you diagnose the core problem in your business right now, which of your personal skills does the solution happen to require? Is that a coincidence?
3. You said customers didn't buy because of price — but what if they didn't see enough value? How would your roadmap be different if that were the real answer?
4. Who in your advisor or board network is most different from you in background and instinct? When did you last really listen to them about something that made you uncomfortable?
5. What job at your company would you never want to do? Is there any chance that job is the most impactful thing you could be doing right now?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Taylor explicitly names: treating the impact question as a rhetorical affirmation rather than a genuine diagnostic. You ask the question and immediately answer it with whatever you were already planning to do. The question only works if you hold a real pause before answering and actively cross-examine whether the answer is driven by evidence or by comfort. A second misuse: soliciting advice without asking "why" — which produces rules without underlying principles, so you cargo-cult someone's past experience rather than building a first-principles view.
