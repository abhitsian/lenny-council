## One-liner

Product operations is a dedicated function structured around three pillars — business data insights, customer and market insights, and process and practices — that builds the shared systems and infrastructure so product managers can concentrate on strategic work the majority of the time.

## The framework

Perri and Tilles lay out three discrete pillars. Each addresses a different category of operational drag on product managers.

**Pillar 1: Business and data insights (quantitative)**
Makes sure PMs have the engagement and revenue inputs to make smart decisions without having to fight for data access. This pillar focuses on instrumentation, dashboards, ad hoc reporting, and executive-level portfolio views. The product ops person here is not a database engineer pulling raw SQL — they interpret data through a product lens and put it in front of decision-makers in a usable form. High-growth companies typically start here.

**Pillar 2: Customer and market insights (qualitative)**
Aggregates research that already exists across the organization — customer interviews, support tickets, sales notes, Salesforce data — into a shared findings database that anyone can query. The product ops role here builds participant databases for opt-in research, creates toolkits for good interviewing, and helps surface qualitative signals from sales and support that would otherwise stay siloed. This is not taking user research away from PMs or researchers; it is enabling them to do it faster and sharing the results more widely.

**Pillar 3: Process and practices (governance)**
Standardizes how the product organization operates: roadmap templates and cadences, cross-functional review formats, OKR tracking, go-to-market coordination, and communication to executives at the right altitude. Enterprises and companies in transformation tend to start here. A key responsibility is making sure the CPO or VP of product can get portfolio-level visibility without digging in Jira.

**What product ops is not:**
- Not project management (no one is watching developers to keep them on schedule)
- Not decision-making (product ops informs; the PM decides)
- Not a replacement for PMs who lack skills (it only multiplies skilled PMs)

**Shared services over embedded:**
The preferred model is a lean shared-services team — not one product ops person per PM. The right ratio depends heavily on how well-instrumented the company's data is. Better instrumentation means fewer product ops people needed. Start with one person, pick the highest-leverage pillar, and demonstrate value before growing.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Product managers are spending 20–30% of their time on data extraction, interview scheduling, or template creation instead of strategic work
- The company has more than one product team and no shared systems for roadmaps, OKRs, or research
- Leadership lacks visibility into what product teams are working on and how it maps to strategy
- The company is scaling rapidly and PMs are burning out from operational overhead

**Does not apply when:**
- The team is a small startup where one PM doing everything off the side of their desk is still tractable
- The organization lacks basic product management skills — product ops will not fix skill gaps, it only helps skilled PMs go faster
- The team wants to outsource product decisions to a product ops function (this is a misuse that Perri and Tilles explicitly warn against)

## Signature questions

1. What percentage of the week are your product managers spending on strategic work versus operational busywork like pulling data, scheduling interviews, or updating templates?
2. Can your CPO or VP of product get a portfolio-level view of what every team is working on, and how it maps to strategy, without digging through Jira?
3. Which of the three pillars — data insights, customer insights, or process — is the single biggest drag on your team's ability to make fast, confident decisions?
4. When your PMs need customer interview participants or existing research, how long does it take them to find what's already known or schedule the right people?
5. Are product managers making decisions or are they spending most of their time getting ready to make decisions?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Perri and Tilles call out most explicitly: treating product ops as a project management function or as a decision-making proxy. If a product ops person is monitoring engineers to keep a sprint on schedule, or telling PMs what to build based on data they pulled, the function is being used incorrectly. The second failure mode is applying product ops to cover for PMs who lack core skills — it accelerates good teams, it does not substitute for training. A third failure mode is building a large embedded team (one product ops per PM) rather than a lean shared-services model; that approach defeats the efficiency rationale entirely.
