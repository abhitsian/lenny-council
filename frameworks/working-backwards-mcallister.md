## One-liner

Start with a specific, compelling customer problem — not a solution or technology — and let that problem guide everything you build, using a press release and FAQ as forcing mechanisms to test whether you're genuinely working backwards.

## The framework

McAllister describes two distinct layers:

**The principle — obsess over the problem first**
Before any solution discussion, you must have a real, articulated customer problem. If you can't state it without referencing the technology or feature you want to build, you're not working backwards. The problem should feel compelling on its own, before you mention any solution.

**The mechanism — internal press release + FAQ**
Amazon's enforcement tool for the principle:
1. **Problem paragraph** — What specific customer problem are you solving? If you skip this or can't write it, there may not be a real problem.
2. **Solution paragraph** — How do you solve it? Written as if the product exists.
3. **Customer quote** — What would a real customer say about this?
4. **FAQ (Frequently Asked Questions)** — Tests whether there's a legitimate plan to succeed: technical hurdles addressed, financial case made, key risks named.

**Jeff Bezos's three-filter test** (shared with McAllister over lunch):
- Is it a big idea?
- Is it something we should be doing? (strategic fit)
- Is there a legitimate plan to succeed?

All three must pass. The FAQ is the mechanism that proves the third.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies** when starting a new product or major initiative where the problem space isn't already locked in. Also useful as a diagnostic: if a team can't write the problem paragraph without referencing their preferred solution, they haven't done the work yet.

**Doesn't apply** (or is overkill) below a certain scale — small features or iterative improvements don't need the full press release apparatus. The spirit matters more than the format; many orgs can embed working backwards thinking into their existing PRD or doc culture without adopting the Amazon template literally.

**Culture constraint**: If your organization runs on slides, you likely can't mandate press releases upward. McAllister's advice: use the format with your own team first, build the muscle there, and find opportunities to offer it as an option elsewhere — don't force it across the org.

## Signature questions

1. What is the customer problem you're solving — stated without referencing the solution or technology?
2. If you skipped the problem paragraph in your press release, why? Does that tell you something?
3. Are you combining ingredients because they're available, or because there's a real problem that this combination solves?
4. Does the FAQ demonstrate a legitimate plan to succeed — not just aspiration, but actual hurdles addressed?
5. Would the customer problem feel compelling to a skeptic even before you describe the solution?

## Common misuse

The most common failure mode: teams go through the press release motions but start from a solution they've already decided to build. They write the problem paragraph last, reverse-engineering justification for the solution. McAllister calls this "retrofitting the customer." The tell is when the conversation leads with technology or capability ("we have these two things, we could combine them") rather than a customer pain. The press release format cannot fix this — it requires the discipline to hold the problem steady before any solution discussion begins.
