## One-liner

Having impact at any scale — on a team, a company, or the world — happens exclusively through artifacts and verbalizations that affect other humans; everything else is just having an idea in your head.

## The framework

Boz lays out three interlocking claims:

**1. Impact = communication output, nothing else**
You cannot have impact by thinking clearly, by being right, or by writing something once that nobody internalized. Impact is measured by what actually changed in other people's understanding or behavior. If the audience didn't get it, that's on the communicator, not the audience.

**2. Org structures, silence, and presence are all communication**
Org charts are communication devices — they convey who has authority and where to go for what. The absence of a check-in communicates trust or neglect depending on context. How you dress, how your camera looks on a video call, whether you smile — all of it is signal. "You cannot not communicate. You are always communicating something with your face, with your clothes, with your body."

**3. Extreme ownership over the communication gap**
When work comes back wrong six months later, the leader takes the L. "I will take the L on it. I did not communicate clearly what I wanted, what the expectations were." This is drawn from the US Marines and SEALs concept of extreme ownership: whenever something goes wrong, the first question is "What could I have done differently in terms of how I communicated for this to have gone better?"

**Supporting practices Boz names explicitly:**
- **HPMs** (Highlight / People / Me): weekly written updates managers send upward — what's highlighted in the work, how are the people on the team, how are you personally doing.
- **Multimodality**: say the same thing in an all-hands, then write it as a post. Different people absorb information at different rates on different channels. "Repetition never spoiled the prayer."
- **Lead with the fear**: acknowledge what the audience is worried about up front, before your conclusions. People will not process your answer if they don't trust your premise.
- **Leverage your manager**: ask for help early, in structured form — "here's where things stand, no response required unless you see something I should fix." Make it cheap for the manager to help.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are trying to change what a team, a peer, or an org does — any situation where your impact depends on other humans acting differently.
- You've delivered something (a spec, a strategy doc, a decision) and it hasn't moved anything. The diagnosis starts with communication quality, not audience quality.
- You are managing at any layer and things are going wrong. The first audit is: what did you communicate, when, and how clearly?

**Does not mean:**
- Ignore your audience's stated preferences entirely. The News Feed example cuts both ways: revealed preferences (doubled usage) diverged from stated ones, but Boz is explicit that the lesson is not "don't listen to customers." Some of what they said pointed to real execution mistakes (the "put it on Main" privacy problem).
- Fabricate a persona. Boz draws a clear line between tailoring your communication to where the audience is starting — which is empathy — and manufacturing a false persona, which is not what he is describing.

## Signature questions

1. If this work came back wrong, what did I communicate (or fail to communicate) that made that outcome possible?
2. What is my audience's starting mental model right now — and what do they fear? Have I addressed that fear before asking them to follow my reasoning?
3. Am I communicating in only one modality? Would writing this up after the all-hands reach the people the all-hands didn't land with?
4. Have I structured the ask so it's cheap for my manager to help me? Or am I presenting a wall of complexity they have to excavate?
5. What is the org structure, the silence, or the absence of a check-in communicating that I haven't thought about?

## Common misuse

Boz explicitly names this: people treat "communication is the job" as license to blame the audience. "People want to blame the audience. Well, the audience is just there." The framework runs in the opposite direction — the communicator owns the gap. If it didn't land, the question is what you did, not what they failed to hear. A second misuse: treating communication as a performance layer you add on top of doing the actual work, rather than recognizing it as the mechanism through which all work has impact in the first place.
