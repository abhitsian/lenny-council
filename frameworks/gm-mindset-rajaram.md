## One-liner

A healthy product team is one you can hand a problem to — "increase weekly active usage,"
not "build the iOS app" — and trust to come back with several options, a prioritization, and
a measured result, while the "how to build it" stays the domain of engineers and designers.

## The framework

1. **Hand over a problem, not a tactic.** The leader's job is to present a well-framed
   problem ("we want people using this five times a week instead of two"), not to dictate the
   solution ("build an iOS app"). The app is one tactic among many — improving the web
   experience is another. Naming the problem keeps the options open.
2. **Let the team generate and prioritize options.** A "well defined product team" can take
   that problem, propose a few different ways to solve it, pick the one with the best ROI,
   decide how to measure it, then execute and run a test to confirm it moved the metric.
3. **Keep the "how to build" with engineers and designers.** That domain belongs to them. A
   PM who keeps diving back into how the product gets built — usually because they were an
   engineer or designer two weeks ago — is in a failure mode, not adding PM value.
4. **Push the PM toward the customer.** The value a PM adds is being biased toward customers:
   talking to them, understanding their problems, facilitating the discussion. The traits
   that earn someone the seat are curiosity, customer centricity, communication, and
   problem-solving — not domain comfort.
5. **Don't abdicate.** Hiring a PM doesn't remove the leader's responsibility to know how the
   team is doing. Keep talking to the engineers and designers directly — they'll tell you
   bluntly whether the PM is adding value.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** when a team is shipping feature after feature and you can't tell whether any of
  it changed customer behavior (Gokul's "feature factory"); when deciding whether you even
  need a PM yet; when a newly promoted internal PM is struggling.
- **Doesn't:** Gokul is explicit that some teams don't need this kind of PM-led problem
  facilitation at all — infrastructure, dev-tools, and ML companies whose customers are
  engineers (his examples: Stripe, an infra team at scale) run with very few PMs because the
  engineers already understand the domain and the customer. The model assumes a gap between
  who builds and who the customer is.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- Is this a problem statement or a tactic in disguise? If I gave the team only the problem,
  would they still arrive at this solution — or did we hand them the answer?
- If this ships, what customer behavior changes, and how will we know? (If nothing changes,
  is it really a feature?)
- Did the team generate more than one option and pick on ROI, or did they execute the first idea?
- Are you, the PM, spending your time on the customer and the problem — or back in the "how to build"?
- Have you actually asked the engineers and designers whether this person is adding value?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Gokul names is the "feature factory": a team proud of shipping feature after
feature without knowing whether any of it moved customer behavior. It comes from handing teams
tactics instead of problems. The second misuse is the new PM who retreats into the build
details — their old comfort zone as an engineer or designer — instead of pushing toward
customers. The fix is coaching them out of that persona, not letting them stay in it.
