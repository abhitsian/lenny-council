## One-liner

Map your AI product onto a two-by-two of attribution (can you prove the value you create?) and autonomy (is a human still in the loop?), then build toward the top-right outcome-based quadrant — where pricing power is highest and value capture is 25–50% of value delivered.

## The framework

Two axes define your pricing archetype:

- **Attribution** (X-axis): Can you measure and prove the business impact your AI creates — tied to customer KPIs, revenue, churn, or cost savings?
- **Autonomy** (Y-axis): Does your product operate without human intervention, or is a human still in the loop as a copilot?

**Four quadrants:**

| | Low Autonomy (copilot) | High Autonomy (agentic) |
|---|---|---|
| **Low Attribution** | **Seed / Subscription** — pay for access; Slack-era SaaS where impact is real but unmeasurable | **Usage-based** — backend/infrastructure products that run autonomously but can't tie to business KPIs; usage as a proxy for value |
| **High Attribution** | **Hybrid** — seat fee + consumption (AI credits/tokens); Cursor is the canonical example: clear productivity attribution, human still approves code | **Outcome-based** (golden quadrant) — charge for work delivered by AI, autonomously; Intercom Fin charges $0.99 per AI-resolved support ticket; Chargeflow takes 25% of recovered chargebacks |

**The migration path:** Start by identifying your honest current quadrant. Then ask: how do I build attribution mechanisms (dashboards, value audits, KPI tracking) and how do I remove humans from the loop (agentic workflows)? That roadmap is your pricing roadmap.

**The value capture shift:** Legacy SaaS could capture 10–20% of value delivered. In the outcome-based quadrant, AI companies can capture 25–50% — because attribution is provable and the work is fully autonomous.

**The dual-engine principle:** Pricing strategy sits inside a larger frame — you must build both market share (acquisition) and wallet share (monetization + retention) simultaneously. Neither engine can be deferred. Single-engine strategies (grow now, monetize later) produce durable-looking but fragile businesses.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are an AI founder deciding on a monetization model at seed or Series A
- You're evaluating whether to stay on seat-based pricing or move to usage/outcome
- You need to make the case for pricing power to investors or co-founders who want to grow at all costs
- You're designing POCs and want to frame them as business-case co-creation, not technical trials

**Does not apply when:**
- Your product genuinely has no measurable outcome and no path to autonomy (pure infrastructure with no business-metric visibility)
- You're operating in a regulated industry where outcome-based pricing creates legal liability
- You're pre-product and need to validate demand first — get a customer willing to pay anything before optimizing the model

## Signature questions

1. Can your customer explain your pricing strategy back to you in a simple sentence? If not, you don't have beautifully simple pricing yet.
2. Which 20% of your product features drive 80% of willingness to pay — and have you already given those away for free?
3. Where are you today on the attribution-autonomy grid, and what would it take to move one step toward the outcome-based quadrant?
4. Are you co-creating your ROI model with the customer from day one of the POC, or are you showing up with one after the fact?
5. Are you running a single-engine strategy — consciously or unconsciously — and which engine are you neglecting?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Ramanujam explicitly warns against: **rushing to outcome-based pricing before you can prove attribution**. If you call yourself outcome-based but cannot demonstrate what business metric you moved, customers will challenge every assumption and you'll lose the commercial conversation.

The related trap: treating a POC as a technical functionality test. The POC's only job is to co-create a business case. Show up with an ROI model you built yourself after the POC and no one will believe it — because they didn't validate the inputs. The co-created ROI model, with customer-agreed assumptions, is the thing that earns pricing power.

A second misuse: anchoring on a low seat-based price in the early days because it accelerates acquisition, without a clear land-and-expand plan. Once customers are trained to expect $20/month, repricing is possible but painful. Price before product — or at minimum, price alongside product, not after.
