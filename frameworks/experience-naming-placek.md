## One-liner

Stop looking for a good name and start designing an experience — bold, polarizing names that create predisposition in the marketplace before launch deliver asymmetric and cumulative advantage that descriptive names never can.

## The framework

Placek breaks naming into three stages:

**1. Identify**
Start with behavior, not positioning. Ask: how are you behaving now, and how do you want to behave in the future? Behavior is bidirectional — the marketplace behaves toward you, and you toward it. Then map the competitive landscape for name distinctiveness. The output is a creative framework (Placek calls it a "window") that teams travel through — not a set of objectives.

Use the **Diamond Exercise** before generating a single name:
- Top: **Win** — how do you define winning?
- Right: **Have to Win** — what do you already have that makes you a winner?
- Bottom: **Need to Win** — what do you still need?
- Left: **Need to Say** — what must you communicate to win?

The "need to say" corner is where experience and behavior emerge. That is the raw material for naming.

**2. Invent**
Run three small teams (pairs, not big brainstorms) with different briefs simultaneously:
- Team 1 knows the real assignment
- Team 2 thinks they are naming a product for a competitor (e.g., Apple instead of Microsoft)
- Team 3 gets an entirely different category (a bicycle, a car, not technology at all)

Most winning names come from teams 2 and 3. Constraints dissolve, mistakes become acceptable, and oblique thinking surfaces what direct thinking cannot.

Layer in sound symbolism research. Each letter of the alphabet carries a vibration — V is the most alive and vibrant, B signals reliability, Z and S are noisy, X is fast and crisp. Names are engineered partly by choosing the right letters for the right experience before creative teams generate ideas.

Generate 1,000–3,000 ideas (not finished names — directions, fragments, concepts). Then clear for trademark, run through a global linguistic filter (cultural, political, sexual connotations across 76 countries), and present the survivors.

**3. Implement**
Help the client present the name internally. Build prototypes: the name on a cap, a mock Wall Street Journal ad, an announcement headline. Executives need to see the name in context of the marketplace, not floating in a vacuum. The goal is helping teams who believe in the name give their management permission to go bold.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are naming a company, product, or platform that will be spoken and searched for thousands of times over years
- You want to stand out from a category that has already converged on descriptive names (cloud-this, pro-that)
- You are willing to fight internally for a name that makes part of the team uncomfortable

**Does not apply when:**
- You need a descriptor for a feature, not a brand — internal tool names, versioning labels, and category tags don't need this rigor
- You are so early that you need a placeholder to close a round; a holding name is fine but recognize it as such
- The category is so new that no naming landscape exists to differentiate against

## Signature questions

1. What behavior do you want the marketplace to exhibit toward you — and what behavior do you want to exhibit toward the marketplace?
2. If your direct competitor launched tomorrow with the name you're considering, how would your team react? Relief or envy?
3. Is anyone on the team uncomfortable with this name? If not, is it actually bold?
4. What experience should this name fire in a customer's imagination — not what it describes, but what it makes possible to imagine?
5. Are you evaluating names or speculating on them? (Evaluation kills bold candidates early; speculation keeps them alive long enough to test.)

## Common misuse

The failure mode Placek warns against most directly is **evaluating too early**. Teams instinctively ask "Is this a good name?" instead of "What could we do with this name?" The first question kills bold candidates immediately because humans default to pattern-matching against what has already succeeded. The fix is to suspend judgment and speculate: show people mock-ups, put the name in a mock ad, ask "our competitor just launched as Windsurf — what do you think of that product?" instead of "what do you think of this name?"

A secondary failure: mistaking comfort for consensus. If the whole room agrees, you probably have a safe, descriptive name that will disappear into a crowded landscape. Placek looks for tension in the room as a positive signal, not a problem to resolve.
