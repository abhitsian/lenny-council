## One-liner

Apply product-market fit logic to job search: define yourself narrowly (3–4 attributes), validate with a listening tour, then negotiate from strength using a job mission with OKRs.

## The framework

Phyl Terry names four sequential tools that build on each other:

**1. Mnookin Two-Pager**
Write down what you want and what you don't want. Named after Allison Mnookin (former Intuit GM, now HBS professor), this is the starting draft — not a final decision. Share it with your Job Search Council so others can surface gaps or overlaps you missed.

**2. Listening Tour**
Take the two-pager to your network: former colleagues (reverse exit interviews), broader network, and recruiters. Ask the golden question: "If you were in my shoes, how would you approach this?" The goal is dual — get market feedback on your fit, and activate listening posts who become invested in your success. Do 10–15 conversations before calling the tour complete.

**3. Candidate Market Fit Statement**
A single sentence, 3–4 attributes: level, function, stage of company, industry, or a specific problem context. Narrow enough that your network can pattern-match to roles without being told explicitly. The narrower the statement, the more expansive your network's referrals will be — people expand outward from a specific anchor, not inward from a broad one. Post it on LinkedIn and share it back with everyone from the listening tour.

**4. Job Mission with OKRs + Four-Leg Negotiation**
Before final interviews, draft your own version of the job description — private at first. Show it to the hiring manager (never email; phone or in-person) as a collaborative check: "Here's how I see the role. Does this match what you're thinking?" This creates differentiation and surfaces misalignment before an offer is made. Once an offer comes, the OKRs anchor a four-leg negotiation: technical debt clearance, headcount, training budget, and mentorship/development — framed as "what I'll need to deliver the outcomes we agreed on," not as demands.

**Job Search Council (the infrastructure)**
A group of 6–8 job seekers who meet twice a week (fast seekers, out of work) or every two weeks (slow seekers, employed). Free, volunteer-run, moderated by a peer. The council functions as accountability, emotional balance sheet management, and a place to parse listening tour feedback with support.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are actively searching (laid off or slow seeker)
- You have enough career history to have a realistic candidate market fit to test
- The job market is competitive and "spray and pray" approaches are producing no signal

**Does not apply when:**
- You are very early in your career and genuinely do not know what you want — the two-pager will be thin, and that is okay; the council helps you build it iteratively
- You already have a specific inbound opportunity that bypasses the search process entirely
- The role and market are simple enough that fit is self-evident (rare for senior roles)

**Common situation to watch:** Candidate market fit shifts with market conditions. A director role that was achievable six months ago may now be competed down because laid-off VPs are applying for it. Update the statement as conditions change, not just once.

## Signature questions

1. What do you want, and what don't you want? Have you written it down and shared it with someone who will push back on it?
2. What does the market actually say you're a fit for — not what you want to be a fit for?
3. Is your candidate market fit statement narrow enough that someone could pattern-match to a specific role without you naming it?
4. If you were interviewing tomorrow, would you walk in with a job mission and OKRs drafted? If not, why not?
5. Are you managing your emotional balance sheet, or just your LinkedIn profile?

## Common misuse

**Spraying and praying, then calling it a search.** Terry is explicit: most people skip the Mnookin two-pager and the listening tour entirely, go directly to applications, and wonder why nothing converts. The two-pager and listening tour feel slow, but the council data shows average search time is three months — at the low end of the national three-to-six-month average.

**Keeping the candidate market fit broad "to keep options open."** This is the primary failure mode. A broad statement produces no referrals. A narrow statement produces unexpected lateral expansions from your network that a broad one never would. The resistance to narrowing is universal — Terry names it explicitly as the thing every bone in your body will fight — and overcoming it is the lever.

**Treating the job mission with OKRs as a power play.** The frame is collaborative, not aggressive. The point is not to strong-arm a hiring manager but to show accountability and surface misalignment before you start. Companies that react badly to this are a red flag — they are telling you they do not actually know what they want from the role.
