## One-liner

A three-axis model for PM career growth: what you produce (outputs before outcomes before direction), what you bring to the table (impact through high-quality artifacts), and your operating model (getting decisions made without making all of them yourself).

## The framework

Vikrama names this the **Three W's**:

**1. What you produce — the output → outcome → direction progression**
- Early career: focus on *outputs* — tangible, scoped deliverables (shipping features, writing the first draft of a brief, ranking content sources, contributing to a GTM). Don't skip to strategy before you've demonstrated output.
- Mid-career: add *outcomes* — owning product areas and goals that you can measure and influence.
- Senior: operate at *direction* — shaping where the product goes.
- The watch-out: as you ascend, you still have to maintain output discipline. Don't shed IC craft when you move up. "Don't forget your IC roots, don't forget the IC component."

**2. What you bring to the table — impact on impact**
- The question is not just whether your product did well — it's whether you can demonstrate *you* drove it. That demonstration happens through artifacts: PRDs, product notes, strategy docs, experiment plans, design briefs, pre-sprint planning documents.
- Four artifact-quality pillars: data/metrics, design/research, technology skills, strategy.
- If you can't point to these documents, your contribution is invisible regardless of outcomes.

**3. What your operating model is — three tenets**
Vikrama states these as a mantra, verbatim:
1. Raise difficult issues without being difficult to work with.
2. Bring out important topics without drawing importance to yourself.
3. Be in charge of getting decisions made without having to make all the decisions yourself.

The underlying skill: bringing emotional conversations back to a logical register. PMs who can do this get more done and grow faster.

**Eight PM skill axes** (secondary framework for self-assessment and skill targeting):
Data, design/research, technology, strategy, communication, collaboration, organizational skills, community.
When calibrating, benchmark against the best in the industry — not just your team — so your self-assessment doesn't plateau artificially.

**Career stall diagnosis** (three mindset blockers):
1. Focusing on things outside your control rather than within it.
2. Slowing rate of change — stopping the deliberate skill acquisition you had early in your career.
3. Limiting self-stories — labels like "high-agency PM" or "hyper-collaborative PM" that quietly excuse weaknesses.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Coaching PMs who are stuck or unsure why their growth has slowed.
- Building a PM career ladder for a team without one.
- Onboarding a new PM (even a senior one) who needs to re-establish credibility in a new context.
- Running performance reviews where impact is hard to separate from product luck.

**Doesn't apply cleanly when:**
- You're in a role where artifact production has genuinely been delegated (staff-plus, CPO level) — Vikrama acknowledges this exists but argues the PM should still retain hands-on craft instincts.
- The role is more about stakeholder management or GTM than product building — the skill axes need reweighting.

## Signature questions

1. "Can you show me your last PRD? Can you show me the last product note that you sent? Can you show me the product strategy doc?"
2. Where on the output → outcome → direction axis are you actually operating, versus where you think you are?
3. What are the stories you're telling yourself about the kind of PM you are — and what behavior are those stories quietly excusing?
4. Are you focusing on things you control, or are you spending energy on why the organization isn't changing for you?
5. Of the eight skill axes, which one gives you maximum leverage to improve right now — and have you benchmarked yourself against the best in the industry, not just your team?

## Common misuse

Vikrama's explicit warning: PMs conflate "working on an impactful product area" with career growth. They assume if the product does well, their growth follows automatically. It doesn't. You can work on a high-priority area and still stagnate if your artifact quality is invisible, your operating model creates friction, or your self-story is creating blind spots. The Three W's exist precisely because product outcomes and PM growth are separable — and PMs who rely only on product luck get passed over.

A secondary misuse: treating the framework as an excuse to stay in execution mode indefinitely. The output axis is not about staying an IC forever. It's about never abandoning IC craft *while* expanding to outcomes and direction.
