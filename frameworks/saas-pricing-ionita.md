## One-liner

Most companies leave money on the table by monetizing too late, underpricing, and treating pricing as a one-time decision — fix all three by matching price to value across customer segments and revisiting it on the same cadence as your roadmap.

## The framework

Ionita names three compounding mistakes founders make with pricing:

**1. Waiting too long to monetize**
Free users are an R&D cost, not a revenue strategy. The real signal of product-market fit is people opening their wallets. Delaying cheapens the product — users assign a $0 value to what you've built — and creates a harder backlash when you eventually charge.

**2. Underpricing (and under-segmenting)**
A single premium tier is almost always wrong. Different users derive wildly different value. If "guilt" shows up as a reason users convert, your free tier is too generous and you're capping revenue from your most engaged segment. The fix is a bifurcated strategy: plans segmented by how much value users actually extract, not by arbitrary feature gates.

Sub-framework — **Day One vs. Day 100 Features**: 
- Day One features = things that get users to the aha moment in their first session. These should be free.
- Day 100 features = advanced functionality whose value requires scale or usage history in the platform. These belong in a higher-priced tier and should be invisible to new users at first.

At Invoice2go, applying this doubled the upgrade rate from starter to pro while simultaneously raising the pro price by ~30%.

**3. Setting it and forgetting it**
Pricing is a product decision, not a one-time launch artifact. Every 6–12 months you ship something meaningful. Treat each release as a trigger to ask: are we being compensated for the new value we've created?

**Match Price to Value**
The right value metric (the unit customers pay for) creates a natural escalator — as usage grows, revenue grows without a new sales motion. Seat-based models are the historical default; usage-based models better match price to value over time. In practice, ~95% of healthy SaaS businesses use a hybrid: tiered plans each with a usage quota, with overages or upgrade triggers when limits are hit.

**The Pricing Committee**
Pricing is cross-functional. PLG companies: PMs + data scientists. Enterprise: sales + finance + rev ops. This team runs:
- Feature prioritization surveys (must-have / nice-to-have / not necessary, or a 100-point allocation exercise)
- Van Westendorp willingness-to-pay questions (too cheap → good value → expensive but acceptable → prohibitively expensive)
- Geo-segmented pricing experiments before US rollout

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have a PLG or bottom-up SaaS product with a freemium or free-trial motion
- You have meaningful usage data on who converts and why
- You're at the stage where monetization is being treated as separate from the product roadmap

**Does not apply (or needs adaptation) when:**
- You're in a pure enterprise top-down sales motion where pricing is negotiated deal by deal
- You have no usage data yet and are still in early design-partner mode — in that case, the "pricing committee" exercise is premature; start by losing some deals on price to find the ceiling

## Signature questions

1. If you surveyed your paying users today, what would they say is the main reason they converted? If "guilt" appears anywhere, how do you close that gap?
2. What's the Day One aha moment for a new user? Are any features that deliver that moment currently behind a paywall?
3. When did you last revisit your pricing? What shipped since then that you haven't been compensated for?
4. Who are your highest-value segments and what are they willing to pay? Do you have a plan designed for each of them, or are you forcing all of them into one tier?
5. What's your value metric — the unit of value that scales with usage? Is your pricing structure tied to it, or are you still using a flat seat model by default?

## Common misuse

Ionita explicitly warns against applying pricing changes in isolation. The failure mode is picking a new price point or launching a new plan without doing the full rebalancing exercise — understanding what users actually want, segmenting by willingness to pay, and mapping pricing to the user lifecycle from single-player to team to enterprise. A price bump without that work is noise; the revenue impact comes from doing it holistically.

The second failure mode she names is using pure usage-based pricing without a fixed floor. CFOs want predictability. A model that surprises buyers with runaway costs erodes trust and creates churn at contract renewal, even if the product is delivering value.
