## One-liner

Dream the future you actually want first, then work backward step-by-step to will it into existence — rather than stacking today's available resources to see how high you can go.

## The framework

Perkins describes two modes of planning:

**Column A (brick-stacking):** Look at what resources, skills, and constraints you have today, then figure out what you can build with them. Most planning defaults here. The ceiling is determined by what's already in front of you.

**Column B (vision-first):** Start by imagining the future you actually want to exist — regardless of whether it seems achievable. Then work backward to find the first small step toward it. The ceiling is set by imagination, not current resources.

The operating sequence:

1. **Set the long-range vision.** Ask: "What is wild success in 10 years? What is terrible failure?" Make it concrete enough to write down. Perkins maintains a literal wall in her home office: her vision of the world in 2050.

2. **Break vision into mission pillars.** For Canva: "empower everyone to design anything / with every ingredient / in every language / on every device." Each pillar becomes a long-running investment track.

3. **Set Crazy Big Goals within each pillar.** These are specific, time-bound targets that feel inadequate before you — because feeling inadequate is the point. The inadequacy drives the work. Couple each goal with a concrete celebration ritual so the team marks the rung before climbing to the next.

4. **Run chaos-to-clarity on every project.** Each idea starts in chaos (a belief, a problem, an instinct). The first step is simply writing it down. Then building a pitch deck. Then adding designs. Then a prototype. Each step adds clarity and makes the idea visible to others — which is what allows it to become real.

5. **Use rejection as deck refinement.** When stakeholders (investors, customers, teammates) push back, treat each objection as a missing slide. Perkins responded to "market not big enough" by adding a market-size page; "same as competitors" by adding a competitive gap page. The pitch gets stronger with each rejection, not weaker.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're founding or reorienting a company and need to set a durable direction
- You're starting a major new product area and want to avoid the local-maximum trap
- The team is getting pulled entirely into short-term fires and losing orientation to the larger goal

**Does not apply when:**
- You need a tight 90-day execution plan — column B thinking sets direction, it does not replace near-term roadmapping
- The vision is already set and agreed; the work at that point is the ladder, not the wall
- The organization lacks the stability to hold a long-range goal — in genuine survival mode, column A is necessary

One explicit boundary Perkins names: timing will be wrong. Column B goals almost always take longer than expected. The framework does not require accurate timelines; it requires commitment to direction. "We might think things are going to take six months and they take two years."

## Signature questions

1. If you zoom out to 10 years — what does wild success look like? What does terrible failure look like?
2. Are you building toward the future you want, or stacking the bricks in front of you and hoping something good emerges?
3. What is the very first tiny step you could take — even an embarrassingly small one — that moves toward that vision?
4. When you get rejected or pushed back, what is the slide you're missing that would pre-answer this objection?
5. Does your day-to-day work — where your team's time actually goes — ladder up to the mission you say you have?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Perkins names explicitly: having a mission "on the wall somewhere" while actual team time and resources flow in a completely different direction. Column B thinking is not a vision statement exercise — it requires that the mission pillars, the Crazy Big Goals, and the day-to-day work all ladder up to each other. The gap between stated mission and real resource allocation is what she calls the inauthenticity that many companies fall into. The test is not whether the vision exists; it is whether what you are actually building closes the gap.

A second failure mode she implies: borrowing another company's operating practices (their "bricks") and trying to insert them into your own house. Each scale stage requires reimagining processes authentically for that stage — not grabbing something off the shelf from a larger company and sticking it in.
