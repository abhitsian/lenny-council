## One-liner

Large companies that stay ahead proactively challenge and disrupt their own product capabilities before a competitor does it for them — waiting until the threat appears is already too late.

## The framework

Shrivastava grounds this directly in Clayton Christensen's Innovator's Dilemma and names the failure mode precisely: market-share leaders get incremental in product strategy, then an upstart disrupts them. The framework has three working parts:

1. **Recognize the complacency trap.** Large companies default to protecting existing market position. Product leaders must actively ask whether the team is optimizing for the current product or anticipating what could replace it.

2. **Fund the cannibal.** The disruption effort has to be real — a new service, capability, or platform that could threaten the current model. Her example: at Amazon she was the first PM on HoneyCode, a no-code app development platform that sat outside AWS's infrastructure-first identity and represented a deliberate move into territory Amazon had never entered.

3. **Challenge assumptions as a listening discipline.** Self-disruption starts at the individual PM level. Proactively questioning your own assumptions — not just externally listening to customers — keeps the organization from drifting into false consensus.

Secondary framework in this episode: **Rule of Seven (async-to-sync escalation).** If an email thread reaches seven exchanges without resolution, stop threading and call a live conversation. At larger companies, the threshold shifts to around ten, but the principle holds: async has a ceiling, and crossing it wastes more time than a synchronous conversation would.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

Applies when: you're at a company with a defensible market position and a maturing product — the exact conditions that make incremental thinking feel safe and rational. Also applies when competitors are visibly moving into adjacent areas.

Does not apply cleanly when: you're an early-stage startup still finding product-market fit. The self-disruption mindset presupposes there's something worth disrupting. Also: this framework requires executive air cover — a PM who tries to self-disrupt without organizational support will hit the prioritization filter every time.

## Signature questions

1. Are we getting too complacent? What would an outside attacker build to make our current product obsolete?
2. Is our product strategy genuinely forward-looking, or are we iterating on what we already have?
3. Are we proactively trying to challenge our own assumptions, or are we just listening for confirmation?
4. If a well-funded startup targeted our exact users, what would they build that we haven't?
5. When did we last fund something that could cannibalize our existing revenue or user base?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Shrivastava warns against explicitly is misapplying the MVP concept when context demands a higher bar. In SaaS you can ship rough and iterate; in a safety-critical or hardware product, cutting corners on the MVP to move fast can be catastrophic. The principle of "get something out and learn" still applies — but the bar for what counts as shippable shifts dramatically with the stakes involved. Teams that copy the SaaS MVP playbook into hardware, autonomous systems, or regulated environments without adjusting the bar are misusing the framework, not applying it.
