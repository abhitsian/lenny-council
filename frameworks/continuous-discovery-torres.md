## One-liner

Start every product decision from a business outcome, map the customer's experience into a tree of unmet needs (the opportunity space) before you touch solutions, and keep that tree honest by talking to a customer every week.

## The framework

1. **Put an outcome at the root.** The tree starts with a single business outcome, then
   branches into the opportunity space, then into solutions, and maybe assumption tests below
   that. The whole point is to give teams structure for the hard, open-ended job of going from
   an outcome to "what do I build."
2. **Define an opportunity as an unmet need, pain point, or desire — not a solution.** Torres
   says 98% of people write opportunities that are actually solutions. The opportunity space is
   the problem space; staying in it is the part teams find uncomfortable and skip.
3. **Structure the opportunity space as an experience map.** Lay out the distinct moments in
   the customer's journey (for Netflix: deciding to watch, deciding what to watch, the viewing
   experience, post-viewing), then hang the needs and pain points under each moment. Top level
   maps to steps in that experience map — roughly three to seven branches so you can hold the
   tree in your head.
4. **Work down the tree to smaller opportunities.** As you go vertically, opportunities get
   smaller and more solvable while still laddering up to the big evergreen problem. This is what
   makes a continuous cadence possible — you're addressing something you can actually ship
   against this week, not the unsolvable parent.
5. **Feed the tree with weekly interviews that collect stories.** Opportunities emerge from
   customers' stories, not from direct questions like "what do you like to watch." You ask
   about a specific past instance and follow the timeline. Interviewing well is the skill the
   whole thing rests on.
6. **Evaluate solutions with assumption tests, not one big experiment.** Run several small
   assumption tests across three ideas at once in a single week, then compare. Assumption
   testing and delivery are the same work — the start of delivery, not a separate phase.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** digital products that are never "done" and a team that owns an outcome and is
  empowered to pick its own solutions — the trio model. Torres says this works the same in a
  three-person company or a hundred-thousand-person one; the only difference at scale is more
  adjacent teams and dependencies to manage.
- **Doesn't (or strains):** when leadership hands the team prescribed features instead of an
  outcome (a feature factory). You can still do the work to understand which outcomes matter
  and make better local decisions, but the full loop assumes the team is empowered. It also
  assumes you can sustain weekly contact — the method is built around interviewing precisely
  because most teams can't observe customers every week.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- What outcome is at the root of this? If you can't name a business outcome, what are we
  actually optimizing?
- Read me your opportunities. Are any of these solutions in disguise? An opportunity is an
  unmet need, pain point, or desire.
- When did you last talk to a customer about this — and did you collect a story, or did you
  ask them what they think?
- What's the smallest version of this problem you could solve this week, and which bigger
  opportunity does it ladder up to?
- You have one idea. What are the other two you're testing against it, and what assumption
  would kill each one?

## Common misuse

Filling the opportunity space with solutions. Torres says she reads blog posts about the tree
and "cry[s] a little bit because their opportunity space is all solutions" — the team jumped
to features and skipped the problem framing the tree exists to force. The related misuse is
treating discovery as a phase you do before delivery; her stance is you're always doing both,
and the weekly habit is what makes the bets get better over time.
