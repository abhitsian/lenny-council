# A-Side / B-Side Framework

## One-liner

Every life and career has an A-side (the highlights you share) and a B-side (the failures, setbacks, and mundane struggles you hide) — and the B-side is equally important for understanding trajectory and building resilience.

## The framework

The framework borrows from the old physical record format: an A-side (the hit, the impressive stuff) and a B-side (the filler, the rough cuts). Applied to careers and companies:

**A-side** — the wins you lead with. Impressive roles, growth numbers, exits, visible accomplishments. What shows up on stage and in podcasts. The "Instagramable version" of someone's trajectory.

**B-side** — the failures, layoffs, visa losses, dead ends, and months of confusion between the highlights. The stuff that gets shoved under the rug because it's harder to tell and easier to skip.

The framework is not about false modesty. Gina actively shares her A-side when it opens doors. The point is recognizing that:

1. Most people are living in a B-moment most of the time. Not recognizing B-moments as moments — temporary, not permanent — is what makes people give up.
2. B-sides are survivorship-biased out of public discourse. What you hear on stage is already filtered to the hits.
3. The same A/B structure applies to companies, regions, and markets — not just individuals. Latin America has a B-side (instability, bureaucracy) and an A-side (massive untapped tech opportunity) that people rarely hold together.

**Resilience mechanism:** Framing a bad period as a "B-moment" rather than a "failed life" is what allows re-entry. You're not broken — you're on the B-side of the record.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Advising founders or early-career people who are discouraged by a losing streak
- Building a talk, pitch, or narrative where you want to signal real experience rather than a curated highlight reel
- Evaluating a company or market that only gets described in extremes (all promise or all risk)
- Mentoring someone who is comparing their B-side to everyone else's A-side

**Does not apply when:**
- The goal is explicitly to present credentials to open doors — the A-side is the right tool there, and Gina is explicit about using it that way
- The failure pattern is systemic and needs diagnosis, not normalization — "B-side" framing can accidentally let structural problems slide as "just part of the journey"

## Signature questions

1. What's the B-side of this story — what are you not saying because it's embarrassing or hard to explain?
2. Is this person in a B-moment right now, and are they treating it like a permanent verdict on themselves?
3. When you look at this market / company / person, are you seeing the A-side because that's all that gets published?
4. What would the non-Instagramable version of this trajectory look like if you told it honestly?
5. How long is a career, really? Are you panicking because you're 26 and in a bad stretch?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Gina warns against is using the A-side framing as an excuse to hide the B-side entirely — even from yourself. She notes that she is "really good at forgetting the B stuff" and that this is a problem: you can't learn from failures you've memory-holed. 

The second failure mode is treating B-moments as defining rather than temporary. The framework only works as a resilience tool if you genuinely believe the B-moment will pass. "This too shall pass" is how she operationalizes it personally.
