## One-liner

Your positioning is whatever lets a customer answer "why pick you over the alternatives,"
and you find it by listing what you'd lose to if you didn't exist, then laddering each
differentiated capability through "so what?" until you hit value the others can't deliver.

## The framework

1. **Name the competitive alternatives.** Not just named competitors — what the customer
   would actually do if you didn't exist. Often the status quo: a spreadsheet, an intern,
   manual work, or indecision. Sales already knows this answer.
2. **List your differentiated capabilities.** Features, pricing model, support model,
   portfolio breadth — anything you have that the alternatives don't. Fill the whiteboard.
3. **Ladder each one through "so what?"** Keep asking until you reach the value a customer
   cares about. Because the value descends from a capability the others lack, it's value
   they structurally can't match.
4. **Derive the insight by reversing the value.** The insight is the context that makes your
   differentiated value matter. You can reverse-engineer it from the value — even if the
   product wasn't built with that insight in mind.
5. **Pick the market category that frames it.** The category's only job is to answer "what
   is this thing." Anchor to an existing category when you can ("CRM for very small
   businesses") so you don't spend the pitch explaining a new word.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** B2B products with real alternatives and a sales motion (champion-driven
  deals), repositioning a "me-too" product in a crowded category, or any spec where the
  team can't crisply say why a user picks this over the workaround.
- **Doesn't:** if you have happy paying customers the differentiated value already exists —
  you may just need to surface it, not invent it. And Dunford is skeptical of *category
  creation* as the default path; starting as a niche in an existing category is usually the
  easier way to get traction.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- If you vanished tomorrow, what would this customer actually do instead? (If the answer is
  "a spreadsheet," that's your real competitor.)
- You said the feature is X — so what? And so what about *that*? Keep going until I hear value.
- Can the alternatives deliver this value? If yes, it isn't differentiated.
- Who is this *not* for? Who should you disqualify so you can serve the right buyer better?
- Did product, marketing, and sales define this value in one room — or did sales never get
  the value story and default to pitching features?

## Common misuse

Pitching features without the value ladder ("here's the dropdown menus, here's what they
do"). The fix she names: the product team usually *did* the value thinking while building,
but it never made the jump to the pitch. Run the cross-functional pass so the value story
actually reaches the customer.
