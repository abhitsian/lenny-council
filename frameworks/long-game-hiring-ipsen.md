## One-liner

Sustainable hiring and career growth come from continuous relationship-building and market awareness maintained long before you actually need to hire or switch jobs — not from reactive sprints when a seat opens up.

## The framework

Ipsen's framework operates across three personas — founders/hiring managers, PMs and product leaders, and recruiters — and gives each a parallel prescription. The core architecture:

**1. Keep a continuous pulse on the market**
Both sides of the hiring equation (companies and candidates) should always know what "good" looks like — which companies are thriving, which individuals are well-regarded commodities. This intelligence is built before a search opens, not during it.

**2. Build relationships before you have a need**
The best placements come from relationships cultivated months or years in advance. Founders should meet benchmark candidate profiles with no agenda other than learning. A seven-month courtship — advisory involvement, org design conversations, warm trust-building — produces a hire in two weeks. A cold outreach produces a transactional exchange.

**3. Define the mandate first, not the title**
Hiring is doomed from inception when you can't specify what this person will actually do. Start by working backwards: what's the concrete 90-day outcome? What does "crushing it" look like in year one versus year two? The title (head of product, VP, CPO) is downstream of the mandate and company stage, not upstream.

**4. Hire for this role at this time — not the best talent in the abstract**
Chasing big-name executives who have drifted far from the work ("shiny objects") is the most common early-stage mistake. The right question is who is best for this specific role at this specific stage, which is a different question than who is the most impressive person available.

**5. Play the long game as a candidate**
Build breadth of experience across product types, not just depth in one specialty. Leave impact that others can describe — cross-functional fingerprints that show up in back-channel references, not just self-reported wins. Never omit short stints; instead, own the story behind them. Logo-collecting and title-chasing are both visible patterns that erode credibility.

**6. Recruiters: lead with relationship, not role**
The most common recruiter failure is treating candidates as commodities — ignoring what they've said, chasing placements under quota pressure. Trust, once lost, is impossible to recover. The durable approach: listen, remember, find non-transactional reasons to stay in touch, and let that accumulated trust create the condition where a candidate will actually hear you out.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** hiring for senior or executive product roles where quality matters more than speed; a founder is preparing a search 6-18 months out; a PM is thinking about career trajectory and how to position for future moves; a recruiter is building a sustainable book.

**Does not apply when:** the hire is truly urgent (a critical engineering patch, a time-sensitive interim), the role is entry-level where market knowledge and relationship networks matter less, or you are hiring for a narrow specialist function where back-channel referrals from trusted networks do not exist yet.

## Signature questions

1. Who is best for this specific role at this specific stage — and is that actually the same as the most impressive person on the market right now?
2. What is the concrete mandate for this hire? What does "crushing it" look like at 90 days, 12 months, and two years?
3. Are you in the room with great people before you have a need, or only when the seat is open?
4. As a candidate: can others speak to your impact — do cross-functional peers describe what you built, or do they struggle to name it?
5. Are you chasing logos and titles, or chasing learning and impact? Is the thing you're excited about the role itself or what it signals to the market?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Ipsen explicitly warns against is treating the long game as a time-of-need checklist — calling your network only when a seat opens, doing back-channel references only for provided references, or building rapport with a candidate only when you want to place them. All of that reads as transactional, and transactional behavior collapses the trust that makes the whole system work. The relationship infrastructure has to be built and maintained continuously, with no immediate agenda, or it does not exist when you need it.
