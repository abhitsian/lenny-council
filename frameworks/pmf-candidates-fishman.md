## One-liner

Evaluate job opportunities against three non-negotiable criteria — people you'll work alongside, a mission that makes lives better, and financial discipline — and walk away any time you settle for two out of three.

## The framework

Fishman borrows the product-market fit acronym intentionally: just as a company needs fit with its market, a candidate needs fit with the company before investing their most finite resource — time.

**P — People**
The people you will work with every day. Can you have hard conversations with them? Can you disagree and reach a resolution that feels productive? This includes your peer set, the leaders above you, and the team below you. The hardest dimension to evaluate and the one Fishman has gotten wrong most visibly.

**M — Mission**
If you do your job well and the company grows, do a lot of people end up better off? Not just founders and employees with a good financial outcome, but customers whose lives improve. At Patreon the test was: creators could stop being starving artists. The mission dimension is non-negotiable; without it, growth work feels hollow.

**F — Financials**
Does the company have fiscal discipline? Can they look into the future honestly and manage capital responsibly? Companies that raised during peak-pandemic assumptions and spent accordingly are the ones laying people off; the quiet, conservative ones are not in the news. Ask about runway, burn rate, and the CFO's philosophy — not just the fundraising headline.

**The "two out of three" trap**
Every time Fishman shortcut one criterion and settled for two out of three — Wyzant (people miss: founders wanted a silver bullet, not a system), Imperfect Foods (people miss: C-suite infighting that paralyzed planning) — it ended with him out of a job or burned out. Every time he held all three — Lyft, Patreon — the run was long and the outcomes were real.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies:** Any job change where you have optionality and enough runway to be selective. Most valuable when joining a company at the leadership level, where the leverage of a bad match is highest.

**Does not apply as written:** When you are desperate — early career, no savings, or in a market with few openings. In those cases, Fishman's framework is still a useful diagnostic for what you are accepting and why, but it should not be used to reject the only offer on the table.

## Signature questions

1. Tell me about the last strategic offsite you had as a leadership team. Where did people disagree, and how did you actually get to a resolution?
2. Can I observe an executive team meeting or a product review before I decide? (If the answer is no, what does that tell you?)
3. What is your runway and what assumptions built your current operating plan? Have any of those assumptions changed since you last raised?
4. Can you give me a list of people you have managed — including someone you had to let go — so I can speak with them directly?
5. If the company does well and you grow significantly, who specifically has a better life because of it, and how?

## Common misuse

Fishman's explicit warning: using the framework as a pattern-match rather than a first-principles evaluation. Founders ask him "how do we find another you, but 10 years earlier?" — which skips the framework entirely and just tries to replicate a person. The right move is to identify which competency gaps exist, then hire to fill those gaps. The same trap exists in reverse: candidates who evaluate a company by its brand name or funding round are pattern-matching, not evaluating. PMF for candidates forces you to do the slower, harder diligence — back-channel references, observing team dynamics, asking about financials — before you sign.
