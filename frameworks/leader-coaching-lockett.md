## One-liner

Technical leaders over-rotate on advising when they should coach; shifting to curiosity-led coaching using three interlocking frameworks (GROW, three-level listening, Nonviolent Communication) unlocks team ownership, reduces burnout, and surfaces the interpersonal dynamics that quietly drive or kill a business.

## The framework

Rachel Lockett organizes her coaching approach into five named components that stack on each other:

### 1. Coach vs. Advise Decision

Advise when: the person lacks the skill, the answer is urgent, or you have a specific outcome you need them to execute. Coach when: the person has the context and can solve their own problem. Most leaders over-index on advising with people they hired as experts.

### 2. Three Levels of Listening

- **Level 1 — Internal**: You're in your own head, thinking about the implications for you. This is where most people operate.
- **Level 2 — Focused**: You can repeat back what the person said. Standard one-on-one quality.
- **Level 3 — Global**: You hear beneath the words — body language, tone, emotional subtext, the gap between what's said and what's communicated. This is what coaching requires.

### 3. GROW Model (Powerful Questions)

Four categories of questions to run a coaching conversation:
- **G — Goal**: What does success look like? What outcome do you want?
- **R — Reality**: Where are you stuck? What have you tried?
- **O — Options**: What are the paths forward? What could you do next?
- **W — Way Forward**: What are you going to do? What's one thing in the next two weeks?

The four categories are a toolkit, not a script. You don't run them in order every time.

### 4. Nonviolent Communication (NVC) for Conflict

From Marshall Rosenberg. Four steps for difficult conversations:
- **Observations**: State what happened factually. Only what a camera could record. ("In the last three sprint planning meetings, you didn't include me.")
- **Feelings**: Name your emotion without blame. ("I felt anxious not knowing what was on the roadmap.")
- **Needs**: State the universal human need not being met — clarity, collaboration, connection. No requirement on the other person yet.
- **Request**: A small, achievable ask that gives the other person a way to meet you. They can counter-propose, but the goal is mutual understanding, not convincing.

Common trap: "I feel like you're being a jerk" is not a feeling. A feeling is an emotion word after "I feel" — not a judgment dressed as a feeling.

### 5. Zone of Genius (80/20 Energy Audit)

The goal is 80% of work time spent in your natural gifts — the things that give you energy rather than drain it. The remaining 20% is logistics and necessary overhead. To find your gifts:
- Track for two weeks: each night, list five things that gave you energy and five that depleted you.
- Ask five to ten people: "When I walk in the room, what shows up? What are my strengths?"
- Scan your calendar: what do you dread vs. look forward to?

Corollary: navigating your career toward your gifts is your job, not your manager's. Once you know your gifts, name them to the people around you so they can route work accordingly.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A leader is becoming the bottleneck because they're solving every problem themselves
- There's a co-founder or team conflict that keeps recurring without resolution
- Someone is depleted or burning out, and the cause is chronic mismatch between role and strengths
- You need to surface a hard interpersonal truth without triggering defensiveness

**Does not apply when:**
- The person genuinely lacks the skill or context to solve the problem — that calls for advising, not coaching
- There's an urgent issue with a known right answer you need them to execute
- You're in coach mode but actually want to guide them to a specific conclusion (that's "leading the witness," not coaching)

## Signature questions

1. When someone brings you a hard problem, are you asking what they need from you — or just reaching for an answer?
2. Would you enthusiastically rehire this person for the same role today? (Clarifying question for talent decisions — binary, immediate, honest.)
3. What are the five things from today that gave you the most energy? What drained you most?
4. How are you complicit in creating the conditions that you say you don't want? (Jerry Colonna's question, which Rachel explicitly endorses.)
5. What is happening here factually — what could a camera record? (NVC entry point before any conflict conversation.)

## Common misuse

The most explicit warning in the episode: don't coach someone into guessing what's already in your head. If you have a specific answer you want them to reach, that's a game, not coaching — and it destroys trust. Coaching works only when you're genuinely open to wherever the person lands. If you're not, advise them directly instead.

Second misuse: conflating "lean into your strengths" with avoiding difficult interpersonal work. Rachel draws a hard line here — the 80% gift-work principle applies to role design and career navigation, not to ducking hard conversations. Conflict avoidance is different from protecting your energy; the former keeps you stuck, the latter is sustainable.
