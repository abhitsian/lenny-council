## One-liner

A five-step repeatable cycle that replaces passive career waiting with active partnership: help your manager first, then negotiate for what you want.

## The framework

Ethan names five steps, meant to be repeated continuously:

1. **Do your current job well.** Before asking for anything, make sure your manager isn't privately wishing you were different. "Step one is do your job well" — and "well" is measured by your manager's view, not yours.

2. **Ask your boss how you can help.** Go directly and ask: "What can I do to help you? What do you need?" Ethan notes that very few people do this. The act of asking puts you on the same team and begins a real partnership.

3. **Do what they ask.** Whatever the manager says, do it — even if it isn't exciting work. This step builds the credibility and trust that the next step depends on.

4. **Tie their need to your goal.** Once you've delivered, go back and say: "I'm really enjoying working with you. Is there something you need that would also help me reach my goal?" Name the goal explicitly — a promotion, a new skill, a different role. "The reason this works is managers help those who help them. It's just human nature."

5. **Repeat.** The loop closes and restarts. The value compounds as trust builds.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

Applies when you want to change something about your role — promotion, pay, different work, new skill — and your manager isn't actively driving that change. Also works when managers are well-intentioned but too busy to focus on your career.

Starts to taper at senior executive levels, where the explicit conversation becomes implicit expectation: "At my level... they're expecting you to anticipate what's needed" rather than asking step by step.

Doesn't help much if your manager is genuinely exploitative ("Oh, you're working harder — keep doing that") or if you truly have nothing you want to change. Both are rare.

## Signature questions

1. Are you doing your job well enough that if you went to your manager and asked how you could help, their first thought wouldn't be "do your actual job"?
2. Do you know your specific goal? A raise, a promotion, a different kind of project — can you name it to your manager?
3. Have you asked your manager what they need, or have you been waiting for them to tell you what to work on next?
4. If your manager is not great at investing in your career, what's in your control that doesn't depend on them being better?
5. As you become more senior, have you shifted from asking "how can I help?" to proactively identifying what needs to be done and keeping leadership in the loop?

## Common misuse

Jumping to step four before completing steps two and three. Asking your manager "what can we do together to help me grow?" without first delivering real help to them reads as entitlement rather than partnership, which is the exact dynamic the loop is designed to replace. Ethan is explicit that step four's effectiveness depends entirely on the goodwill and rapport built in the earlier steps.

A secondary failure mode: not knowing your goal before entering step four. The negotiation in step four requires you to name something specific. Vague aspiration ("I want to grow") gives the manager nowhere to work with.
