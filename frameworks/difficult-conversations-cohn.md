## One-liner

Give leaders templated language for uncomfortable feedback conversations, and give co-founders a structured pre-alignment questionnaire, so the discomfort of avoiding these conversations is greater than the discomfort of having them.

## The framework

### Part 1: Conversation Scripts

Cohn structures every difficult conversation around four moves:

1. **Observable facts, not judgments.** Open with what you've seen or heard — not what you feel or infer. "What I've observed is…" or "What I'm hearing from your peers is…" This makes the feedback neutral enough to land without triggering a defense response.

2. **Connect to shared expectations.** Tie the observation to an agreed standard or goal, not to your personal disappointment. "We both know the most important way you can succeed here is…" — this shifts the framing from "you're wrong" to "here's what matters for us both."

3. **Clear ask with a timeline.** Close with a specific behavior change and a deadline, not a vague aspiration. "I need you to fix this within the next 30 days. Otherwise, we're going to have to find a way to part ways."

4. **Hope for the future.** End by signaling you care about the person's career and see a path forward. "I want to continue to help you find opportunities to build your skills and to advance." This keeps people engaged even after hard news.

**Script for defensiveness:** If the person escalates, pause the conversation rather than fight back. "Let's just pause for a second because I'm feeling the energy has changed and I can see that you're getting a little bit heated by what I'm saying and I want you to know that I have no intention of upsetting you. I just want to be able to talk to you about the things that are going to help you in your career."

**Firing sequence:** Cohn treats the firing conversation as the last step, not the hard one. The hard step is the crystal-clear warning before it: name the behavior, state it has not changed after multiple conversations, set a 30-day deadline, and be explicit that parting ways is the consequence. If you do this well, the actual firing conversation is short and unsurprising.

### Part 2: Three Questions to End Every Meeting

At the close of any meeting, surface three things before people leave:
1. What did we decide here?
2. Who needs to do what by when?
3. Who else needs to know?

Cohn's observation: ask six people in the same meeting "what did we decide?" and you will get six different answers. Making this a ritual — not a template you post afterward — forces real-time alignment while the room is still together.

### Part 3: Founder Prenup

Before co-founding, work through these questions explicitly:

- **Values.** Each founder selects their top 3–5 core values. Adjacent values (excellence + learning) can coexist; values in tension (excellence + work-life balance) need a conversation.
- **Vision of success.** What does the company look like at its full potential? IPO-scale venture, profitable independence, or something else? If co-founders have never spoken this aloud, assumptions diverge in year 5.
- **How do you handle conflict?** Especially: ask someone close to you how *they* see you handle conflict — your self-assessment is likely wrong.
- **How do we decide when we disagree?** Pre-agree on a method: the person who cares most wins, the domain expert wins, you alternate, or you require consensus. Any method works; having no method doesn't.
- **What company culture matters to you?** Family-feel vs. results-driven vs. something else. Two founders who never compare notes on this will feel like they're running two different companies.

Cohn cites Noam Wasserstein's data: 65% of startups fail because of co-founder conflict, not market or product.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are a manager or executive with a direct report who is underperforming, not meeting expectations, or heading toward termination.
- You are a founder about to take on a co-founder.
- You are in any meeting that is producing re-decisions because no one captured the actual decision the first time.
- You are a leader who is avoiding a conversation because you're afraid of the emotional reaction.

**Does not apply when:**
- The feedback is genuinely ambiguous or you have not yet done the work to identify observable evidence. Cohn explicitly says: "What's my evidence that this is happening?" — if you can't answer that, you're not ready to use the script.
- The relationship has already broken down past the point of a single conversation. The prenup works pre-founding; it has limited use five years in when the damage is already structural.

## Signature questions

1. What is the observable data that tells you this is a real problem — not a feeling, but a fact someone else in the room could confirm?
2. Have you been crystal clear with this person about what you need from them, or have you been hoping they'd figure it out?
3. What are you putting on top of this conversation that's making it feel bigger than it is? What's the actual worst case if they get upset?
4. If you ask everyone in the room "what did we decide?", would they all say the same thing?
5. Do you and your co-founder know each other's answer to "what does winning look like for this company in ten years?"

## Common misuse

The failure mode Cohn names most explicitly is **avoidance disguised as kindness**. Leaders tell themselves they're protecting the employee by not having the hard conversation. Cohn's position: you are not protecting them. You are delaying a conversation they need to have, often for months or years, and in the process denying them the chance to change. The employee who finally got the feedback after being let go said, "I wish someone had told me that 15 years ago. I think I could have had a different career."

The second failure mode is **leading with judgment instead of observation**. Scripts fail when you open with how you feel or what you think it means rather than what you've actually seen. The moment a feedback conversation becomes evaluative before it becomes factual, the other person is defending their identity rather than hearing the data.
