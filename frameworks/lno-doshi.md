## One-liner

In a high-leverage role your tasks aren't equal — sort each one into Leverage (effort
returns 10–100x), Neutral (returns about what you put in), or Overhead (returns far less),
then pour your perfectionism into the L tasks and deliberately under-invest in the rest.

## The framework

1. **Sort every task into L, N, or O by its return on effort.** L tasks ("leverage") give
   you 10x or 100x back in impact for the effort you spend. N tasks ("neutral") give you
   roughly what you put in — 1x, maybe 1.1x. O tasks ("overhead") give back a lot less than
   you put in.
2. **The same activity can be any of the three — it depends on the situation.** Filing a bug
   report, taking meeting notes, writing a doc: the activity type doesn't tell you the
   bucket. A bug on a critical path is an L task and deserves a detailed report; a minor one
   is an O task where you attach one screenshot and hit submit. Notes from a routine meeting
   are O (just list the action items and owners); notes from a contentious CEO product review
   are L (spend an hour or two getting the language exact so nobody backtracks later).
3. **Let your inner perfectionist shine only on L tasks.** The extra time for L tasks doesn't
   come from working more hours — it comes from spending *less* time on N and O tasks. The
   default failure of ambitious people and perfectionists is treating all three buckets the
   same.
4. **Expect to procrastinate on L tasks, and plan for it.** L tasks bother you precisely
   because you know their impact and you're a little scared of them (strategy, the hard PRD,
   the cross-team alignment that needs an awkward conversation). Time becomes the convenient
   excuse. Two tactics he uses: **placebo productivity** (intentionally clear N and O tasks
   for a day or two so you feel "earned" into the L task) and **change of location** (do the
   L task from a coffee shop or somewhere other than your usual desk to force the focus
   shift).

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** to "creative, high-impact, high-leverage roles" as he puts it — PM, founder,
  engineering/design leadership — where there are far more positive-ROI things you *could*
  do than you should. The whole point is choosing among an overflowing plate.
- **Doesn't (or matters less):** in roles where the work is genuinely uniform, or where the
  task list is short enough that triage buys you nothing. The framework earns its keep when
  you're overwhelmed and everything feels equally urgent — which is the exact state he was in
  at Google before he adopted it.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- If you did this task at 100x the care, would the outcome actually change 100x — or are you
  polishing an O task? What return does the *next hour* on this buy?
- What's the L task you already know you're avoiding? (It's usually the one that bothers you
  most because you're not doing it, or not doing it well enough.)
- Where is your perfectionism landing right now — on the PRD that moves revenue, or on the
  expense report?
- You called this a quick win. Is it leverage, or is it just low-denominator work you're
  reaching for because it's fast? (This is his opportunity-cost critique of the ROI mindset.)
- For this specific instance of the task, is it L, N, or O — not "what kind of task is this in
  general"?

## Common misuse

Treating every instance of an activity the same way — making *all* your notes great, filing
*every* bug with 15 annotated screenshots, perfecting the expense report. That's the
perfectionist default he warns against; it spends L-task energy on O-task work and leaves no
time for the few tasks that actually return 10–100x. The related trap (from the same episode)
is the **ROI mindset**: optimizing for positive return makes you shrink the time denominator,
so you pile on quick wins and low-hanging fruit — which feels productive but maximizes
opportunity cost. In a high-leverage role the goal is to minimize opportunity cost (work on
the optimal thing), not to rack up cheap positive-ROI wins.

Two adjacent frameworks from the same conversation fold in here:

- **Pre-mortem.** Before an important launch, gather the cross-functional team and open with
  the prompt "imagine this has already failed six months from now — what went wrong?" Working
  backward from a hypothetical failure gives people psychological safety to raise concerns and
  a shared vocabulary (he has the team name **tigers** — threats that will actually kill the
  project — plus paper tigers and elephants, then vote on the scariest ones from *others*).
  "If you do a pre-mortem right, you will not have to do an ugly post-mortem." (~00:24:50;
  tigers ~00:27:14) Low downside, high upside — one meeting plus prioritization by the leader.
- **Three levels of product work: impact, execution, optics.** People default to one level,
  and conflict on teams is often a level mismatch in disguise — e.g. a PM litigating
  *execution* detail while the CEO is reasoning at the *impact* level (brand, customer
  experience). Optics is creating awareness of the impact/execution you're doing; it's
  legitimate and even necessary, but turns harmful when an org's culture quietly makes optics
  *the goal*. His takeaway isn't "always optimize for impact" — it's that the shared
  vocabulary lets a team decide which level matters given their situation (early-stage teams
  often should optimize for execution; a platform team with availability issues may need more
  optics/communication). (~00:46:12; level mismatch ~00:47:58; situational call ~00:52:23)

These two are well-articulated in the transcript but LNO is the only one Shreyas explicitly
calls a "framework," and it's the most self-contained, so it's the primary card here.
