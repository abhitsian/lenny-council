## One-liner

Distraction is not something that happens to you — it is an action you take away from what you intended, and four sequential steps (master internal triggers, make time for traction, hack back external triggers, prevent distraction with pacts) give you a framework to reclaim agency over your attention.

## The framework

Eyal builds the model on a single definitional move: the opposite of distraction is not focus — it is **traction**. Both words share the Latin root *trahare* (to pull), and both end in -action. Traction is any action that pulls you toward what you said you were going to do. Distraction is any action that pulls you away. What separates them is one word: **intent**.

From that foundation, four steps in order:

**Step 1 — Master internal triggers**
90% of distractions come from inside, not from technology. Boredom, anxiety, loneliness, uncertainty, the discomfort of hard work — these are the actual triggers. The move is to identify the internal trigger firing when you get distracted, then use tools to sit with it rather than escape it.

Key tools:
- **10-minute rule**: Set a timer for 10 minutes when you feel the urge to defect. Don't say no to the distraction; say "not yet." Surf the urge — emotions are waves that crest and subside.
- **Reimagine the task**: Change how you relate to the difficult work, not just when you do it.
- **Identity mantra**: "This is what it feels like to get better." Repeating this while uncomfortable acknowledges the feeling without surrendering to it.

**Step 2 — Make time for traction**
You cannot call something a distraction unless you know what it distracted you from. A to-do list has no constraints and no feedback loop. A time-boxed calendar does. Turn values into time: first the *you* domain (sleep, exercise, health), then *relationships* (partner, kids, friends), then *work* (reactive work and reflective work — both need explicit slots).

The metric to track: not "did I finish?" but "did I do what I said I would for as long as I said I would, without distraction?"

**Step 3 — Hack back external triggers**
External triggers — notifications, pings, colleagues tapping your shoulder — account for about 10% of distractions but they compound the internal ones. "Hack" signals gaining unauthorized access: these triggers hijack attention you didn't consent to give. Countermoves include: do-not-disturb on devices during focus blocks, visual signals for family or colleagues (the "concentration crown"), printed calendar blocks that communicate to managers what you are working on.

**Step 4 — Prevent distraction with pacts**
Pacts are pre-commitments — the last line of defense. Three types:
- **Price pact**: A financial disincentive for going off track (a bet with someone else).
- **Identity pact**: Adopting "indistractable" as a self-description, the same way a nonsmoker doesn't debate whether to light a cigarette.
- **Effort pact**: Adding friction to the distraction path. The Wi-Fi router on an outlet timer that shuts off at 10pm, or the Forest app that kills a virtual tree if you pick up your phone.

These four steps must be worked in order. Using pacts before fixing internal triggers and making time for traction is treating the symptom, not the cause.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

Applies when:
- You know what you should be doing but find yourself drifting toward easier or more urgent tasks.
- Procrastination is your consistent pattern, not a one-off.
- You want to build sustainable focus habits, not just push through one sprint.
- You're a manager trying to build an indistractable team culture — the same logic (psychological safety, forum to discuss the problem, management exemplifying the behavior) applies at org level.

Does not apply when:
- The problem is genuine task ambiguity — you don't know what to do, not that you're avoiding doing it.
- Someone is in the 3–5% who are pathologically addicted, for whom Eyal explicitly says different protections apply.
- You already have a working system that produces the outputs you want. Eyal is explicit: if your life is working, this is not the book for you.

## Signature questions

1. When you went to check email / Twitter / Slack instead of the hard thing — what were you feeling in the moment right before you did it?
2. Is there a specific time block in your calendar for this task, or is it just on your to-do list?
3. What does your calendar say about your values — does the time allocated actually reflect what you say matters to you?
4. Are you measuring whether you finished, or whether you did what you said you would do for as long as you said you would?
5. Is distraction at your company a symptom of something deeper — a dysfunction that people can't raise safely?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Eyal returns to repeatedly is skipping to step four — using pacts, app blockers, or environment tricks — without first addressing internal triggers. Hacking back external triggers feels concrete and actionable, so people start there. But if you haven't identified what emotional discomfort you're escaping and haven't built the tolerance to sit with it, you'll route around any blocker you install.

The second failure mode is using a to-do list as a productivity system. A to-do list is a register of outputs with no constraints, no feedback on how long things actually take, and no mechanism for trade-offs. It reliably produces the planning fallacy (tasks take three times as long as estimated) and reinforces the self-image of someone who can't manage their time. The fix is a time-boxed calendar, not a better to-do list app.
