## One-liner

You're in the build trap when you measure success by how much you ship, not by what
that shipping changes for customers and the business — and you climb out by tying every
piece of work back to a goal you can name before you build it.

## The framework

1. **Judge work by outcomes, not output.** The trap is rewarding teams for shipping as
   many things as possible. The escape is asking, before you release, what you hope will
   happen, which metric should move, and how you'll instrument it so you can check
   afterward whether it actually did.
2. **Separate the development operating model from the product operating model.** Agile,
   Scrum, and SAFe are a *development* operating model — they help you get things out the
   door. They don't tell you whether you're building the right thing, how to go to market,
   or how to do product management. Don't mistake one for the other.
3. **Stand up the product operating model across four pieces:**
   - *Product strategy* — do you have one, and does the work ladder up to company goals?
   - *Organizational design* — are you organized around your products, with skilled PMs up
     and down the org?
   - *Product operations* — the infrastructure: can teams get the data and reach customers
     to make decisions?
   - *Culture and incentives* — are you rewarding shipping volume, or value tied back to
     the business?
4. **Make the work trace to business goals (the strategy cascade).** If the company goal is
   "expand geographically," you should be able to see what in the portfolio actually enables
   that. Most large orgs lack the transparency to even see whether their teams' work connects
   to goals — fixing that transparency is part of the job, not bureaucracy to avoid.
5. **Describe yourself and your work in value terms.** When you make the case to leaders,
   frame it as the problem solved and the metrics moved, not the agile cadence you ran.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** large, traditionally non-software organizations (banks, telecoms, pharma)
  going through an agile or digital transformation, where teams sprint back-to-back and
  measure themselves on throughput. Also any team or spec that can't say what should change
  when the thing ships.
- **Doesn't:** Perri is explicit that process itself isn't the enemy. A 4,000-team org
  *needs* roadmapping tools and enough process to be efficient and to see what everyone is
  doing — the point is to connect that process back to value, not to strip it out. The
  framework also assumes a real product role exists; where the org only has order-taking
  product owners with no path to strategy, the first move is creating that role and path.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- When we release this, what do we hope will happen — which metric moves, and how will we
  instrument it to know?
- Is this development-operating-model work (getting it out the door) or product-operating-model
  work (knowing it's the right thing to build)? You're showing me the first; where's the second?
- The company goal is X — what in this portfolio actually enables X? Can you even see it?
- Are we rewarding people for shipping as much as possible, or for value tied back to the business?
- Describe this from a value perspective, not the agile cadence: what problem did it solve and
  what number did it move?

## Common misuse

Adopting Scrum or SAFe and believing that, by itself, makes you good at building products.
Perri's warning: those frameworks are a development operating model. They get work out the
door but say nothing about whether it's the right work, where discovery fits, or how value
connects back to company goals. Teams end up sprinting back-to-back, filling developers'
backlogs, and measuring user-story throughput as a number that just goes up — which is the
build trap, dressed up as agile maturity.
