## One-liner

A product that retains is the thing growth runs on: you build growth loops into the
product so it grows itself, but if users don't stick and you can't keep finding more of
them, you don't have product market fit to grow in the first place.

## The framework

1. **Retention is the precondition, not the payoff.** Winters' test for product market fit
   has two halves and you need both: the product retains *and* you have a scalable way to
   keep finding users. A product that retains well but can't find more users isn't PMF — and
   the implied inverse, acquisition without retention, isn't PMF either.
2. **Build growth loops into the product, early.** Instead of bolting acquisition on after
   you've "won," you design loops — content loops, sales loops, viral loops — into the
   product so it grows itself. He's seen founders do this *before* PMF, not to scale
   prematurely but so the distribution advantage is ready the moment the product works.
3. **Sequence kindle → fire.** Early growth is "kindle strategies": non-scalable hacks the
   founders run to get the first users. Those exist only to unlock "fire strategies" — the
   scalable loops (content, sales, viral, paid) that take you to millions. You hire a
   full-time growth person to harness a fire strategy *after* it's proven, not before.
4. **Defend what you've built — gains erode.** At scale the job shifts from only adding
   growth on top to protecting the high conversion rate and engagement you already have.
   Those can go away (a Facebook change, an SEO algorithm shift), so protecting them gets
   *more* important the more valuable the thing you've built is.
5. **Treat PMF as something you can fall out of.** User expectations and the competitive bar
   rise every day; if you stop pushing the product, user experience, and latency forward you
   can drift out of PMF entirely — and clawing back is slow and expensive.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** deciding what to invest in once you have early traction; resisting the pull to
  pour everything into new acquisition; justifying "un-sexy" retention/performance work at
  scale; pressure-testing a PMF claim that rests on signup numbers alone.
- **Doesn't / weaker fit:** the very earliest pre-traction moment is still founder-run kindle
  work — you're not yet hiring growth or optimizing loops. And this clip is about consumer
  and marketplace products (Pinterest, Eventbrite); Winters notes B2B is now blending
  self-service and sales loops ("product led sales"), which changes the loop shape.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- If this product retains beautifully but you can't find more users, is it actually product
  market fit — or just a good product nobody new is reaching?
- Where's the loop? How does this product grow itself, versus you having to buy every next
  user?
- Are you adding growth on top, or are you protecting the conversion and engagement you've
  already built — what happens to the business if those erode?
- Is this a kindle hack or a fire strategy? If it's a hack, what scalable loop is it meant to
  unlock?
- User expectations keep rising — what are you doing so you don't quietly fall out of PMF in
  a year?

## Common misuse

Treating acquisition as the goal and retention as something to fix later — piling more
top-of-funnel growth on a base that doesn't hold, or calling a pile of signups "product
market fit." Winters' warning runs both ways: acquisition without retention isn't PMF, and at
scale, neglecting the retention and engagement you've already earned means you slowly lose
the most valuable thing you have.
