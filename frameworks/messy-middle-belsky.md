## One-liner

The years between starting something and finishing it are mostly lows, so the job is to
endure those lows long enough to figure it out — by manufacturing small wins, keeping the
team told they're making progress, and gut-checking whether you still believe in what
you're building.

## The framework

1. **Accept that the middle is lows, not a steady climb.** A venture, a new startup, or a
   turnaround inside a big company is messy because it's full of lows — anonymity,
   uncertainty, and anxiety. Belsky's point is that this is "par for the course," not a sign
   you're doing it wrong. Our biology wants constant reward and affirmation, but the things
   worth building take years to defy the odds, so you have to overcome that wiring.
2. **Short-circuit the reward system with micro-goals.** Because the big payoff is years
   out, you set small milestones the team mutually agrees to celebrate "even though in the
   greater scheme of things, they don't matter much." This keeps the dream alive when the
   real reward is too far off to motivate anyone.
3. **Merchandise progress.** Belsky's analogy: you're driving the team cross-country with the
   windows blacked out and everyone in the backseat. If they don't get the narrative — we're
   making progress, traffic's clearing, we just crossed a state line — they go stir-crazy.
   The product leader is the steward of that progress narrative.
4. **Use the conviction test to decide whether to keep going.** When a founder asks whether
   to continue, his one question is: knowing everything you now know, do you have *more or
   less* conviction in the problem and the solution than when you started? More conviction →
   you're just in the messy middle, stick with it. Less → quit or pivot, because without
   conviction you don't have the fuel.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** long-horizon efforts where the payoff is years out and there's no steady drip
  of external validation — early startups, a new bet inside a big company, a turnaround. The
  defining condition is volatility and a long stretch where "no one knows or cares about"
  what you're doing.
- **Doesn't:** the framework is about *enduring*, not staying forever. Belsky is explicit
  that some things should be quit — if conviction has genuinely dissipated, "you should not
  be doing what you're doing." So it doesn't apply once the honest answer to the conviction
  question is "less." The one guardrail he adds: don't make that call on a bad day, since
  everyone has ups and downs.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- Knowing everything you know now, do you have more or less conviction in this than when you
  started? (That single answer decides endure-vs-quit.)
- What are the micro-milestones the team has agreed to celebrate this quarter — even the ones
  that don't really matter in the big picture?
- Does the team actually hear the progress narrative, or are they in the blacked-out backseat
  with no idea we're moving?
- Are you being honest about the lows — the things that aren't working, the data you don't
  have — or are you sugarcoating to keep morale up?
- Is the team here for the mission, or for a reward that's still years away? If it's the
  reward, what's carrying them through the lows?

## Common misuse

Treating the framework as a license to never quit. Belsky warns specifically against the
founder who keeps going on momentum and money rather than belief — "No, founders never quit."
The conviction test exists to catch exactly that: if the honest answer is "less conviction,"
the move is to pivot or stop, not to grind out more lows. The matching anti-pattern on the
leadership side is the promoter who "sugarcoats the truth" and "glosses over the hard parts"
— hiding the lows instead of merchandising real progress through them.
