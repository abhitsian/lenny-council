## One-liner

Split the product roadmap into a public roadmap (user-requested features) and a secret roadmap (internally-brainstormed ideas nobody asked for) so competitive differentiation comes from the secret list, not from who executes the public list fastest.

## The framework

**Public Roadmap** — everything users have directly asked for. Gathered from support channels, reviews, feedback forms. Prioritized by impact and market size. The catch: every competitor has the same list. Winning here is incremental at best.

**Secret Roadmap** — ideas generated entirely internally, through company-wide quarterly brainstorms. Nobody asked for anything on it. Users shown these items might say they don't need them. The ideas come from the team's "unique vantage point" — deep knowledge of the user, the problem space, and the underlying technology. Goal: change user behavior so completely that once they try it, they never go back.

**How the secret roadmap gets populated:**
1. Company-wide quarterly brainstorm — engineering, recruiting, marketing, everyone included.
2. Team votes and ranks ideas.
3. Product team takes the ranked list and evaluates feasibility and technology fit.
4. High-potential ideas go on the secret roadmap without public announcement.
5. Features are launched by giving them directly to users and measuring behavioral effect.

**Signal for success:** not just adoption, but behavioral change. "People are doing things one way — if you're able to show them another way, and once they try it, they never go back. That's what a product is."

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're in a competitive market where all players are receiving the same user feedback.
- Your team has accumulated genuine proprietary insight about users and the problem space — not just feature requests, but behavioral understanding.
- You can ship fast enough to test ideas cheaply (Captions targets one marketable feature per engineer per week).
- You're building consumer or prosumer products where behavior change = retention.

**Doesn't apply when:**
- Your team lacks real domain depth. The secret roadmap requires earned insight, not guesses.
- You're in a compliance-heavy or B2B context where buyers specify requirements in RFPs — there's no "secret" path in a structured procurement process.
- The product is very early and hasn't yet confirmed that the public roadmap basics work. The secret roadmap is a layer on top of a working product, not a substitute for it.

## Signature questions

1. Is this feature on our public or secret roadmap? If it's public, who else is already building it?
2. If a user saw this idea today, would they say they need it — or would they be confused? (Confusion is a green flag for the secret list.)
3. What would a user have to stop doing or start doing differently for this feature to succeed? Is that behavioral change we're trying to cause?
4. Does this idea come from something we understand about our users that our competitors probably don't? What's the source of that insight?
5. Are we winning by executing the public roadmap better, or by putting things on the field that competitors haven't thought of yet?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Gaurav warns against is treating the secret roadmap as a playground for disconnected innovation. The ideas still have to come from a real, earned understanding of the user — not random brainstorming divorced from the product mission. The eye contact feature (shifting recorded eyes to face the camera) succeeded because it came from a deep understanding of creator anxiety when recording, not from general AI capability exploration. If the secret roadmap becomes "things we think are cool," it stops being a competitive advantage and becomes a distraction layered on top of an already-crowded public roadmap.
