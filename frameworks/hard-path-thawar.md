## One-liner

When choosing between options, pick the harder path — because even if it fails, you've gained learning, worked with smarter people, and built something real; the easy failed path leaves you with nothing.

## The framework

Farhan maps out a 2x2 of outcomes across path difficulty:

**Easy path + succeeds:** you get the result, but you've done less work and probably worked with less capable people.

**Easy path + fails:** you've lost, and gained nothing — no skills, no relationships, no proof of work.

**Hard path + succeeds:** you get the result AND you've done harder work, likely alongside sharper people.

**Hard path + fails:** you still win — you've done something difficult, worked with smart people, and built transferable skills and work product.

The asymmetry is the point. The hard path is the only one where failure is still a net positive. The easy path has no such insurance.

Farhan adds three conditions that keep "hard" meaningful rather than just painful:

1. **It has to be on a path you can actually learn from.** Not arbitrary difficulty — hard *because* the domain or the people stretch you.
2. **The people around you matter.** Smart people cluster on hard problems. That's by design, not accident.
3. **You should be building something, not just surviving.** The job seeker who builds a Shopify app learns more than the one who sends 10 resumes a day — and has proof either way.

He applies the same logic to career choices (taking harder courses at Waterloo to meet sharper classmates), hiring (pairing with someone over 90 days beats 8 hours of interviews), and technical decisions (building platform infrastructure instead of the point feature Shopify merchants asked for).

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when** you have a genuine choice between options of similar plausibility and the harder one offers better learning, better people, or better long-term leverage.

**Does not apply when** the harder path is just inefficiency or poor design dressed up as ambition. Farhan is explicit: "I mean, let's try to do the hard thing that we can learn from... it's just it might take more manual work or it might not be the way most people do it, but we think we can learn more from that path." Dumb hard is not the same as meaningful hard.

**Also does not apply** when you're being asked to take a risk with zero signal or in a domain where failure has irreversible consequences. Toby's lesson to Farhan after the React Native mistake wasn't "you took a risk" — it was "you *didn't* take the risk you should have taken given what you already knew."

## Signature questions

1. If this path fails, what will you actually have — skills, relationships, work product, or nothing?
2. Who are the smartest people working on this problem, and does your path put you in the same room as them?
3. Are you hedging because the data says to, or because you're afraid of looking wrong?
4. If you had to do this the harder way — build the platform instead of the feature, pair program instead of solo, run the trial instead of the interview — what would you actually learn?
5. Is this hard because it's genuinely more valuable, or just because no one has thought it through yet?

## Common misuse

The most common mistake is treating any friction as evidence you're on the hard path. Farhan is clear that difficulty has to be purposeful — you're choosing harder *because* it produces better learning, better people, or better leverage, not because it's slower or more painful by default.

The second failure mode is hedging. When Farhan had to decide between Swift (iOS) and React Native (Android) in his first week at Shopify, he split the difference — and burned 18 months of work for a hundred engineers as a result. Toby's diagnosis: he didn't take the risk. The hard path framework breaks down when you water it down into a compromise that captures neither the safety of the easy path nor the upside of the hard one.
