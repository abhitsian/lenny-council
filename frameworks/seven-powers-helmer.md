## One-liner

Seven durable structures — Counter-Positioning, Scale Economies, Switching Costs, Network Economies, Branding, Process Power, Resource — each defined by a Benefit plus a Barrier, sequenced by business lifecycle phase; operational excellence is not on the list.

## The framework

Power requires two things simultaneously: a **Benefit** (a cost or price advantage over competitors) and a **Barrier** (something that prevents competitors from copying that advantage). Helmer calls this "the to be or not to be test." If you pass both, you have power. If you only have the benefit, you're on a treadmill.

The seven powers, grouped by when they become available:

**Early stage (origination / counter-positioning)**
- **Counter-Positioning** — a novel business model incumbents can't replicate without cannibalizing themselves. Almost every startup begins here; product-market fit is essentially a substitution against an existing player.

**Takeoff and growth**
- **Scale Economies** — fixed costs spread over more customers than competitors, producing a per-unit cost advantage. Netflix content cost is the canonical example.
- **Switching Costs** — accumulated investment by customers in your product that makes leaving more costly than staying.
- **Network Economies** — value per user increases with total users, but only when the effect is *material* enough to produce a pricing or margin advantage (not merely present).

**Stability phase (rarely achievable earlier)**
- **Branding** — durable price premium from customer affinity. Brand recognition bought via advertising is not this.
- **Process Power** — proprietary, opaque operational complexity that competitors cannot reverse-engineer or hire away. Toyota and TSMC are the reference cases; most companies don't qualify.
- **Resource** — legally protected ownership of something valuable (patents, licenses). Obvious to everyone; the only question is whether you can create it.

**Power Progression**: early-stage founders should focus on Counter-Positioning first, then build toward Scale Economies, Switching Costs, and Network Economies as they scale. Branding, Process Power, and Resource are either unavailable early or not where scarce founder attention should go.

**The three value drivers**: only three things create business value — Power, Market Size, and Operational Excellence. Everything else is a sub-component of one of these.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Evaluating whether a competitive advantage is durable or mimicable.
- Deciding where to allocate attention during a specific phase of company growth.
- Stress-testing a "moat slide" in a pitch deck or strategy review.
- Assessing second and third acts for a business (AWS, iPhone, AWS from Amazon — all new Power cycles).

**Does not apply when:**
- Choosing what to build next week — this is a long time-horizon tool, not a sprint prioritization tool.
- Diagnosing day-to-day operational execution — operational excellence is necessary but is explicitly not power.
- The business hasn't found product-market fit yet — but Helmer now argues founders should still be *tilting probabilities* toward power-enabling business characteristics even pre-PMF.

## Signature questions

1. Do we have a Benefit AND a Barrier, or just one of the two?
2. If a well-funded incumbent copied exactly what we do, what stops them from winning?
3. Are we calling something a flywheel — and if so, is the network effect actually material enough to shift our margins or pricing, or is it just present?
4. What phase of the business lifecycle are we in — origination, takeoff, or stability — and which powers are actually available to us right now?
5. Is this an operational excellence advantage, or is it genuinely inimitable? Could a consulting firm teach a competitor to do this?

## Common misuse

Helmer explicitly warns against three failure modes:

**Confusing operational excellence for power.** The most common slide error: "our team is the best, we execute fastest." These advantages can be copied — by hiring, by consultants, by competitors studying you. They matter enormously for winning market share during takeoff, but they don't create durable margin.

**Claiming network economies when you only have network effects.** Network effects that don't produce a material pricing or cost advantage are not a power. Uber and Lyft both have network effects; neither has network economies — the advantage is real but too small to drive materially different returns.

**Misapplying branding.** Brand recognition from ad spend is not branding power. Branding power is a durable price premium customers grant because of what the brand means to them — built over years, not campaigns.
