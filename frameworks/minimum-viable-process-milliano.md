## One-liner

Process is variance-reducing by design — introduce the minimum that brings weak performers up without capping your best ones, and always build in explicit escape hatches so high performers can break the template when the work demands it.

## The framework

Eeke names this "Minimum Viable Process" (MVP, her deliberate reuse of the acronym). It has four working parts:

1. **Name the cost explicitly.** Before introducing any process, acknowledge what it trades away: variance reduction pulls the median up but also pulls the ceiling down. The people you're accidentally limiting are usually your highest performers.

2. **Set the floor, not the ceiling.** Templates and checklists define the minimum standard. They're not the target — they're the bar below which the team shouldn't fall. The template header should literally say: "If this doesn't work for what you're trying to explain, don't use it."

3. **Build in the escape hatch.** High performers need organizational air cover to deviate. Managers must detect who doesn't need the process and give them explicit permission to work around it — what Stripe COO Claire Hughes Johnson called "breaking the org for this person."

4. **Pair process with structured creative space.** Counter the variance-reduction of process with dedicated variance-expanding rituals: a "Think Bigger" section in every team charter ("with 20% more time, what would you do?"), and an annual "Crazy Ideas" doc seeded by the CEO, where the prompt is explicitly "90% of these make no sense, but 10% will make a 10x–100x difference."

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** A team is scaling past the point where everyone already performs at a high standard — when you're genuinely worried the variance is too high and you need a floor. Also applies when you're setting up planning docs, spec templates, or review rituals at any stage.

**Doesn't apply when:** The team is still small enough that everyone is operating in founder/high-performer mode. Adding process before you need the floor just removes ceiling. Also doesn't substitute for the underlying problem: if a team is drowning in bugs and tech debt, no process framework rescues them — you have to diagnose and fix the hierarchy-of-needs issue first.

## Signature questions

1. Before you add this process, what variance are you actually trying to reduce? Name it specifically.
2. Does this template set a floor, or is it accidentally defining the ceiling too?
3. Who on your team genuinely doesn't need this process to do their best work? Are you willing to break the org for them?
4. Where is the escape hatch in this template — the explicit permission to deviate if the work demands it?
5. What's your "Crazy Ideas" equivalent — a recurring ritual that gives people explicit permission to think bigger than the current roadmap?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Eeke explicitly warns against: treating process as a target rather than a floor. When teams see a template, they often optimize toward it — stopping at "good enough for the template" rather than going further. The MVP framing is meant to prevent this: the template defines minimum viable, not maximum acceptable. A second failure mode is introducing process before diagnosing whether the real problem is tech/product debt overwhelming the team — in that case, process adds overhead without removing the hierarchy-of-needs block that's killing creative capacity.
