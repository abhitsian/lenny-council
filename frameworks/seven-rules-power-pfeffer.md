## One-liner

Power is a learnable skill, not a personality trait — and acquiring it requires deliberately practicing seven behaviors most people avoid because they confuse power with how they've seen it misused.

## The framework

Pfeffer names seven rules, presented as a progression from internal mindset to external action:

1. **Get out of your own way** — Stop pre-emptive apologies, imposter syndrome narratives, and avoiding discomfort from "power is dirty" thinking. If you believe power is wrong, you won't do what it takes to succeed.

2. **Break the rules** — The rules were made by people the rules favor. Standing out requires doing something unexpected — in how you work, what you take on, or what you ask for. The basic move: ask. Most people don't ask. Asking costs almost nothing.

3. **Appear powerful** — Body language, eye contact, voice volume, command of the room, not reading from notes. These signals are read before content. They are skills, not traits — Robert De Niro learned to act; Steve Jobs learned to present.

4. **Build a powerful brand** — If decision-makers don't know who you are, you cannot be chosen. Do something that makes you identifiable and associated with specific value. Visibility without substance is hollow, but substance without visibility goes nowhere.

5. **Network relentlessly** — The core move is generosity (who can I connect and to whom?), not transactional collection. The goal is to become a broker — someone who sits between groups that benefit from being connected. Weak ties (people different from you) surface non-redundant information and opportunities.

6. **Use your power** — Power used creates more power. Getting things done earns more resources and more opportunity. Ambivalence about the role you hold, or declining to use authority you have, signals to others that you don't deserve it.

7. **Success excuses almost everything** — The world is not homeostatic. It is self-fulfilling. If people perceive you as powerful, you become more powerful. What you did to acquire power tends to be forgotten or forgiven once you have it. Pfeffer includes this rule specifically to counter the Icarus myth — the belief that overreach will always be punished.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** You are operating in a hierarchical organization where advancement, resources, and influence depend on visibility and political skill — which is most organizations. Also applies when you come from a background that structurally disadvantages you (the framework is especially pitched at people for whom the default system is stacked against them).

**Doesn't apply when:** You have chosen autonomy over power consciously (Pfeffer explicitly notes: "You can have power or you can have autonomy, but you cannot have both" — and says he chose autonomy). The framework doesn't address whether to pursue power; it addresses how, once you've decided you want it.

**The trade-off to name:** Power comes with scrutiny, loss of schedule control, loss of privacy, and the "trust dilemma" — you can't know if people are close to you or close to your position. Pfeffer doesn't hide this.

## Signature questions

1. Where are you getting in your own way — apologizing, underplaying your qualifications, or avoiding things because they make you uncomfortable?
2. What are you known for specifically? If the decision-maker has to say your name to a room, what word follows it?
3. Who are the 10 people who, if they knew you and you knew them, would materially change what you can accomplish? How are you going to meet each one?
4. Are you actually using the authority and resources you already have, or are you waiting for more before you act?
5. What rule does everyone in your context follow that, if you broke it, would make you stand out in a useful way?

## Common misuse

Pfeffer's explicit warning: people confuse the tool with how it has been used. They see bad actors wielding power and conclude power itself is the problem — then opt out of acquiring it. His framing: a hammer is not evil because someone used it to harm someone. A surgeon's knife and a mugger's knife are the same object. Opting out of power because of how others have used it doesn't make the world better; it just means fewer people with good intentions hold influence.

The second failure mode: treating the rules as a checklist to perform once, rather than skills to build through practice. Knowing the rules and doing them are different things — Pfeffer's class is built around the Knowing-Doing Gap principle: if you learn it but don't practice it, it disappears.
