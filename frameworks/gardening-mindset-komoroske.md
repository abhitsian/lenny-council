## One-liner

Cultivate conditions where value grows on its own — plant cheap seeds in things with compounding potential, respond to the ones that show signal, and navigate organizational constraints by operating within the adjacent possible rather than jumping to distant end-states.

## The framework

Komoroske names three interlocking ideas, all aimed at the same problem: how do you create disproportionate impact in an uncertain system?

**Gardening vs. Building**
The builder mindset starts with a plan and manipulates reality to match it. The output is bounded by the effort put in. The gardening mindset starts by looking for things that can grow on their own — with network effects, compounding loops, or ecosystem dynamics — and then seeds those, directs them lightly, and invests more only when signal appears. Key moves:
- Find things shaped like ecosystems: if it works, it accelerates. "Truffle hunting" for compounding loops.
- Make seeds cheap to plant. If the cost of a failed seed is low and the planter enjoys it, the opportunity cost is near zero.
- Respond to signal, not to plans. Stop watering if the signal stops. Keep watering if it doesn't.
- Protect the acorn. Use 70% of team effort on clearly legible value ("they should obviously exist") to buy space and credibility for the 30% of seed-planting work.

**Adjacent Possible**
The adjacent possible is the set of actions directly within your reach that would almost certainly work if taken. Most strategies overreach — they jump to an imagined end state rather than taking the next safe step. The practical method:
- Set a low-resolution North Star 3-5 years out that every informed person would find plausible and would celebrate if reached.
- At each moment, look within the adjacent possible for the action with the steepest gradient toward that North Star.
- Take that step. The world reconfigures. Repeat.
- Don't seek false precision on distant outcomes. "Zero or 1,000" decisions don't require knowing if it's 1,000 or 1,001.

**Organizational Kayfabe**
Kayfabe (originally from professional wrestling) is a thing everyone knows is false and yet everyone acts as if it's true. In organizations, status updates get filtered upward through multiple layers of optimism, each layer adding a little whitewash until leadership is many orders of magnitude away from ground truth. This is emergent — it doesn't require bad actors. The underlying asymmetry is simple: you can't make your boss look bad. Recognizing kayfabe:
- Disconfirming evidence won't flow to you automatically. Seek it actively.
- If you try to "hit the ground truth button" publicly, you'll be stopped — because it threatens the system that people depend on.
- Work around it: small seeds, low stakes, pilot environments where disconfirming evidence can arrive incrementally rather than catastrophically.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- The problem domain has compounding potential — network effects, ecosystem dynamics, or anything where success begets more success.
- You're operating in a large organization where coordination costs are high and kayfabe is active.
- You're navigating genuine uncertainty where false precision feels tempting.
- You're trying to grow talent or ideas that need protection before they're legible.

**Doesn't apply when:**
- The problem genuinely requires a plan executed at scale with tight dependencies — launching a physical product line, for example. Some things are buildings, not gardens.
- Speed to a known destination matters more than anti-fragility. If the North Star is well-established and the path is known, you might just want to build.
- The seed is so small that even with compounding it stays small. Not everything has a compounding loop.

## Signature questions

1. If this thing worked, would it work at an accelerating rate — or would it stay linear? (Is this a garden or a building?)
2. What's the cheapest seed I could plant that would give me a real signal within a short time horizon?
3. What is my adjacent possible right now — not my ideal end state, but the actions within arm's reach that would almost certainly work?
4. What disconfirming evidence am I not hearing, and why isn't it reaching me?
5. Who on my team has a seed of an idea that I'm tempted to redirect toward a "safer" task? What would happen if I said "go for it"?

## Common misuse

Komoroske explicitly warns against two failure modes:

**Treating gardening as an excuse for vagueness.** The gardening mindset is not "let's see what happens." It requires active truffle-hunting for compounding loops, deliberate seed selection, and disciplined attention to signal. If nothing is growing, you stop. It's responsive, not passive.

**Using the adjacent possible to justify incrementalism without direction.** "Don't be scared to go a little incremental" is not the full advice. Without a North Star, incremental steps random-walk you into a corner. The framework requires both: a far-off, low-resolution direction and a disciplined insistence on only taking steps within your actual reach. Jumping to the end state is as wrong as wandering with no destination.
