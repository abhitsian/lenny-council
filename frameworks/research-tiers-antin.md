## One-liner

Research teams are trapped doing middle-range work that feels meaningful but drives no business impact — the fix is ruthlessly shifting to macro strategic research and fast micro usability work, with researchers integrated from day one.

## The framework

Antin divides all user research into three tiers:

**Macro research** — big-picture, strategic, business-focused work. Competitive landscape studies, concept-car projects that look 3–5 years out, TAM studies, synthesis of a full year's insights into a directional thesis. Tied to annual planning and company OKRs. This is underinvested.

**Middle-range research** — the "blobular" middle. Questions like "How do Airbnb hosts feel about their payment options?" These are interesting, often well-intentioned, and almost never impactful enough. They yield insights that are hard to operationalize, invite post-hoc bias ("we knew this already"), and fulfill a need to feel user-centered without changing any decision. This is dramatically overinvested.

**Micro research** — laser-focused, often fast. Usability testing, A/B result diagnostics, conversion funnel debugging, copy and CTA testing. Can yield results in 48 hours. Directly tied to metrics. This is underinvested and often wrongly labeled "junior work."

The structural diagnosis underneath the tiers: research is deployed as a reactive service function, called in at the end of a product cycle to validate decisions already made. That structural position forces teams into middle-range work. The fix is integrating researchers from the beginning of product processes, with consistent relationships to PMs and designers, so they shape the question rather than just answer it.

The five tools a strong researcher carries: (1) formative/generative research, (2) evaluative/usability research, (3) rigorous survey design, (4) applied statistics, (5) technical skills (SQL or prompt engineering for data access).

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A research team is busy but leadership can't point to concrete business impact from the work.
- Researchers are being called in after product decisions are already locked.
- A team wants to right-size research investment — what to do more of vs. cut.
- Hiring or evaluating a researcher and need a capability bar.

**Does not apply when:**
- A product is pre-PMF and the core user problem is genuinely unknown — macro research is the whole job, the tiers don't help you sequence within it.
- The team has no researchers at all; the framework is about rebalancing an existing research function, not justifying the first hire (though Antin does argue for early hires).

## Signature questions

1. When was the last time a piece of research changed a product decision that had already been made? If the answer is never, the research is probably all middle-range.
2. Is the researcher in the room when the product question is being framed, or only after it's already been framed?
3. Does the researcher know the company's OKRs, quarterly metrics, and conversion funnel by heart? If not, why not?
4. Is the research being asked to validate a direction, or to falsify it? Which does the team actually want?
5. Could this research question be answered in 48 hours? If yes and it's taking weeks, something structural is wrong.

## Common misuse

Antin's explicit warning: teams hear "do less middle-range research" and conclude they need fewer researchers or that research generally is less valuable. The opposite is his point. The discipline needs to survive by doing *different* work — more macro, more micro — not less work overall. The reckoning he describes is about structural integration, not about cutting headcount.

A second misuse: researchers going too far with the "business focus" advice and claiming their research findings directly prescribe what to build. The "what, so what, then what" structure is correct; jumping from insights to specific design mandates without the rest of the team is overcorrection. Researchers inform decisions — they do not make them unilaterally.
