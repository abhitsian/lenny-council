## One-liner

Pick one intentional highlight per day, then design your environment — not your willpower — to protect the attention and energy required to do it well.

## The framework

Four steps, run as a daily cycle:

**1. Highlight**
Before the day starts (or the night before), ask: "If someone asks me at the end of today what the highlight was, what do I want to say?" That answer becomes the anchor for everything else. The highlight can come from three sources:
- *Urgency* — something that has a real deadline today
- *Satisfaction* — a meaningful project you want to make progress on
- *Joy* — something personal, relational, or restorative that would make the day feel good

Write it down. A sticky note on your monitor is enough. The act of writing it matters even if you don't look at it again. Block 60–90 minutes of your calendar around it.

**2. Laser**
Create physical and structural barriers between you and distractions so you aren't relying on willpower to stay focused. Willpower always loses. Tactics include: delete infinity-pool apps from your phone, stay logged out of social platforms, use separate devices for work and distraction apps, cancel internet access during focus time, add an email signature resetting response-time expectations, and slow the email reply loop so the inbox cycle decelerates on its own.

**Infinity pools** (endlessly replenishing feeds — email, Twitter/X, TikTok, LinkedIn, news) and the **busy bandwagon** (the cultural expectation that you should be maximally reactive) are the two systemic forces that defeat focus without any tactics in place.

**3. Energize**
Attention runs on a body. Sleep is the highest-leverage variable. Regular exercise and managed food/caffeine timing support focus. The apply-your-own-curiosity principle extends here: run experiments on sleep, exercise, and nutrition the same way you run experiments on your highlight.

**4. Reflect**
At end of day, treat the day as an experiment, not a verdict. Did your highlight happen? What helped or blocked it? Write down one to three things you're grateful for. The goal is curiosity about the day, not self-judgment. This loop — intention in the morning, reflection at night — gradually trains your brain to notice what's working.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** You have meaningful "Project A" work — creative, strategic, or personal — that doesn't appear on anyone else's urgent list and keeps getting squeezed out by reactive demands. Works for employees inside organizations, not just founders.

**Doesn't apply (or applies differently) when:** Your job is inherently reactive and on-your-feet — clinical roles, operational on-call, support — where the highlight shifts from focused work to end-of-day recovery or personal restoration instead.

## Signature questions

1. If someone asks you tonight what the highlight of your day was, what do you want to say?
2. What is your Project A right now — the thing that wouldn't otherwise get attention — and when did you last make progress on it?
3. Which infinity pool is your personal kryptonite, and what's the structural barrier (not the willpower promise) you've put between yourself and it?
4. Are you designing your calendar as a canvas you control, or treating it as a thing that happens to you?
5. When something didn't work last week, did you get curious about why, or did you just judge yourself for failing?

## Common misuse

The framework warns explicitly against treating these four steps as a willpower discipline — as in, "I'll just try harder to focus." That framing guarantees failure because it leaves the environmental defaults untouched. Knapp and Zeratsky's core point is that environmental design is the mechanism, not resolve. Deleting the app matters more than deciding not to open it. Similarly, the framework is not a productivity maximization system — it does not promise an empty inbox or complete task lists. The failure mode they name is chasing efficiency at the expense of intention: getting faster at the wrong things.
