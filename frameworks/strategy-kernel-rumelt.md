## One-liner

A strategy is a design for overcoming a high-stakes challenge, composed of three inseparable parts: a diagnosis of what's actually hard, a guiding policy for how you'll deal with it, and coherent actions that implement that policy — and if any one is missing, you don't have a strategy.

## The framework

Rumelt calls these three parts the **kernel**. They must all be present.

**1. Diagnosis**
Understanding what's actually going on — not just stating a problem, but explaining *why* it is a problem and *what makes it hard*. A diagnosis is also a decision about what to pay attention to. It isn't a goal or a complaint ("we're not growing fast enough"); it's a structured hypothesis about the situation. The question to ask: *What makes this hard?*

**2. Guiding policy**
The direction you've chosen in response to the diagnosis. It answers: *how are we dealing with the situation?* It defines what you will and won't do. A guiding policy is fundamentally exclusive — it forecloses some options to concentrate on others. A list of 17 priorities is not a guiding policy.

**3. Coherent actions**
The things you actually do. They must be coherent in two ways: (a) they must implement the guiding policy, and (b) they must not contradict each other. Actions that fight each other (grow revenue AND cut investment) are not coherent. This is the part most often omitted because people confuse strategy with high-level thinking.

**The crux (refinement tool)**
Borrowed from rock climbing: the crux is the hardest move on the climb. In strategy, it's the hardest part of the challenge — the thing that, if you can't address it, makes the whole strategy fail. Identifying the crux helps you focus the diagnosis and choose the right guiding policy. Insight comes from immersing yourself in the crux, not from picking a strategy off a menu.

**Power (why the strategy is believable)**
A strategy needs a source of asymmetry — something you have or know that competitors don't. Power can come from network effects, relationships, reputation, knowledge, customer base, speed, or scale. Without a source of power, a strategy is wish-thinking. Power should inform where you direct your coherent actions.

**Action agenda (the naming move)**
Rumelt's practical advice: don't call it a strategy. Call it an *action agenda* — what are we going to do about this problem? The word "strategy" carries so much baggage (vision, mission, values frameworks) that it distracts from the actual work of defining a challenge and choosing concrete actions.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have a meaningful challenge to address — not just aspirations or targets
- You're in a competitive or resource-constrained situation where focus matters
- An organization needs to align around what it will actually do
- You're evaluating whether a so-called strategy is real or performative

**Doesn't apply when:**
- You're in pure operational mode executing an already-validated strategy
- The situation is genuinely so uncertain (e.g., very early startup pre-market-signal) that diagnosis isn't yet possible — there you're running search, not strategy
- What you need is goal-setting or OKRs, not strategic direction (these serve different purposes)

## Signature questions

1. What makes this hard? (The diagnostic question — don't accept goal-statements as answers)
2. Of all the challenges on the table, which ones are both important *and* actually addressable?
3. What is the source of asymmetry — the thing we can do that others can't, or know that others don't?
4. Do the actions we're planning implement the guiding policy, or are they a separate wish list?
5. Are any of our actions fighting each other? What do we give up by saying yes to this?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Rumelt returns to repeatedly: **confusing ambitions or goals with strategy**. Companies produce documents full of profit targets, growth aspirations, and values statements and call it strategy. It isn't. Goals describe what you want to happen. Strategy describes what you're going to do about a specific challenge, and why doing it will work given the situation you're actually in.

A secondary failure mode: **a diagnosis that is just a restatement of the ambition**. Saying "our diagnosis is that we're not growing fast enough" is not a diagnosis — it's a complaint. A real diagnosis explains why growth is blocked and what specifically makes it hard to fix.
