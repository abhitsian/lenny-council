# Pattern Breakers: The Three-Element Framework for Breakthrough Startups

## One-liner

Breakthrough startup ideas require three converging powers — an inflection (external event enabling radically new behavior), an insight (non-obvious truth about how to harness it), and founder-future fit (the founder's unique claim on that specific future) — because startups win by changing the subject entirely, not by executing better than incumbents.

## The framework

### Idea elements (how to find the right idea)

**1. Inflections**
An inflection is an external event that creates the potential for radical change in how people think, feel, and act. It must be specific: a turning point, not a trend. The test has three parts: (a) what is the specific new thing that was just introduced; (b) how does it empower specific people in specific ways; (c) under what conditions will that empowerment be realized, and what could prevent it.

Three types of inflections:
- Technological (iPhone 4s GPS chip → ride-sharing; camera quality → Instagram)
- Regulatory (COVID telemedicine law change → cross-state virtual care)
- Belief (people's permanent shift in willingness to do something, e.g. work from home)

Moore's Law is not an inflection. A continuous improvement curve is not an inflection. An inflection is a turning point.

**2. Insights**
An insight is a non-obvious truth about how one or more inflections can be harnessed to change people's behavior. It comes from the founder, not from the market. The critical qualifier: an insight must be non-consensus and right — not just right. If your idea is right and consensus, it is probably too similar to what incumbents already offer, meaning the startup is buying into the current definition of the market. Great startup ideas feel slightly crazy to most people, but trigger an "I need this" response in a specific subset.

Insights are earned, not intuited. Founders earn secrets by getting their hands dirty with new technology, savoring surprises, and noticing things others pass over. Being non-consensus is not the same as being contrarian — contrarianism is still conformity defined relative to someone else.

**3. Founder-future fit**
The question is: who knows the most about this future, has the most intrinsic motivation to pursue it, and has the best network in the ecosystem of that future? There is no canonical great founder. Marc Andreessen at 22 tinkering with the web was the right person for Netscape. Qasar and Peter from Applied Intuition — Michigan natives who had worked at the Big Three and Google Waymo — were the right people to sell autonomous vehicle simulation to automotive CEOs. The fit is about who is most authentically matched to a specific radical future, not about generic founder traits.

### Action elements (how to move the idea forward)

**4. Movements**
A movement is a different way of developing a market. It leverages a grievance of a minority against the tyranny of a majority. Early customers, employees, and investors join for aesthetic reasons — they believe what the founder believes — not for practical reasons. A startup creates a movement by showing the weakness in the strength of the incumbent, not by attacking the incumbent directly. Airbnb did not say "hotels suck." It said "live in Paris like a Parisian" — which turned the Four Seasons' consistency into something you had to apologize for.

**5. Storytelling**
The hero's journey structure maps directly onto startup communication. The founder is Obi-Wan, not Luke — the early believer is the hero. The story alternates between the world that is (the problem, the grievance) and the world that could be (the different future). The same structural story is told differently to different audiences: investor, customer, employee. A common failure is pitching everyone the same deck built to address objections from non-believers, which produces a "Franken deck" that obscures the message for the people who were already prepared to believe.

**6. Disagreeableness**
Startups are fundamentally disagreeable acts. The pattern-breaking founder is willing to sacrifice status in the dominance hierarchy because fulfilling the mission matters more than fitting in. Healthy disagreeableness is not stonewalling — it is listening to advice, understanding the truth the other person holds, and choosing a different truth with full awareness. This also applies internally: startups succeed chaotically. A startup does not beat an incumbent by executing better; it wins by proposing a radically different future that disorients the incumbent.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Evaluating whether a startup idea has genuine breakthrough potential vs. incremental extension of a market
- Deciding which early customers to prioritize (lighthouse customers living in the future, not mainstream adopters)
- Identifying why a pivot was the right move (insight was correct, implementation was wrong)
- Building the pitch narrative for seed-stage or pre-seed fundraising

**Does not apply when:**
- You are extending an existing product into an adjacent market with proven demand
- You are doing sustaining innovation inside a large organization (different risk profile, different go-to-market)
- The goal is operational efficiency or better execution in a defined market — this framework is about escaping the comparison trap entirely, not winning comparisons

## Signature questions

1. Is this idea from the future? What part of the future have you been living in that gives you the right to have an opinion about it?
2. What is the specific inflection, the specific empowerment it offers, and to whom — and under what conditions might that empowerment fail to materialize?
3. What are the three biggest surprises you encountered while developing this idea? (If you can't name any, you may be seeking validation rather than truth.)
4. Is your insight non-consensus and right, or just right? Who hates this idea — and why does that subset of people hate it?
5. Why are you the founder for this specific future — not as a general founder, but for this particular radical future?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Maples warns against most explicitly is confusing a trend or an improvement curve for an inflection. Moore's Law is not an inflection. Neither is "AI is getting better." The inflection has to be a specific turning point — a new thing introduced at a specific moment — that empowers specific people in specific ways, and whose empowerment conditions can be stress-tested. The second common misuse is building an insight that is right but consensus: it sounds reasonable to everyone, which means it is probably not radical enough to let the startup avoid the comparison trap. An idea that most people find reasonable is likely an extension of the present, not a proposal for a different future.
