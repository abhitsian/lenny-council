## One-liner

Before you can win, you have to stay in the game — prioritize asymmetrically positive decisions over high-variance bets, because survival is the precondition for eventual success.

## The framework

Jason calls this principle "not losing is a precursor to winning." It has three interlocking parts:

**1. Asymmetric risk filtering**
Evaluate every major decision by its downside, not just its upside. The question is: could this compromise the enterprise? If yes and the upside isn't proportionally outsized, don't take it. Investor culture celebrates going for it; Jason's frame is about preserving the option to keep going. He looks for decisions where the downside is bounded and the upside is large — asymmetrically positive.

**2. Survival as the active strategy**
Most companies lose because they run out of time, not because they lack the right idea. Founders give up before timing aligns, before the right product insight emerges. The right move in a hype cycle is not to chase growth at all costs but to ask: will we still be around when conditions improve? "The end is never the end" is his tactical version of this — when a decision feels impassable, that's rarely actually the end.

**3. Gross margin as a survival signal**
Gross margin is a coarse but fast instrument for asking: am I adding enough value to survive compression? A new business with a 40% margin should be stress-tested at 60% first — that forces the real question of who else can do this and at what margin. Thin margins compound vulnerability; high margins buy time and optionality.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're in a hype cycle and pressure to "go for it" is high
- You're making a financing or partnership decision with irreversible downside
- You're starting a new business line and evaluating market viability
- The stakes are personal — this is your one shot, not a portfolio bet

**Does not apply when:**
- You're an investor building a portfolio (different incentives — you need some shots to be big swings)
- You're in a commodity market where survival means aggressive price competition
- You're at a company large enough that a failed bet won't compromise the whole enterprise

## Signature questions

1. Could this decision compromise the entire enterprise? If yes — is the upside truly asymmetric, or are we just calling it that?
2. Are we surviving long enough to get timing right — or are we burning through optionality chasing the hype cycle?
3. If this business has a 40% gross margin today, why can't it sustain 60%? What does that answer tell us about durability?
4. What is the actual risk profile of this decision — not the upside narrative, but what happens if it goes wrong?
5. Are we in this for 5 to 10 years, or are we making a bet that only makes sense if it pays off fast?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Jason explicitly names: confusing investor logic with founder logic. Investors build portfolios — they can absorb losses. Founders have one enterprise, one shot. Applying portfolio-style "just go for it" thinking to a single company leads to unrecoverable downside. The second misuse: treating a high-risk bet that happened to pay off as a replicable model for the team — it creates culture that takes high-variance decisions without the same luck, compounding volatility. The goal is not risk avoidance but asymmetry: bounded downside, large upside.
