## One-liner

Two parallel three-level pyramids — one for consumer products, one for marketplaces — that tell founders what to obsess over at each stage of growth, and why MAUs and GMV are the wrong races to run.

## The framework

### Hierarchy of Engagement (consumer products)

**Level 1 — Core Action**
Every consumer product has one action that, when completed, confirms the user understands the product and is likely to return. For Pinterest it's pinning. For YouTube it's subscribing (not watching). For Snapchat it's sending a snap. Measure users completing the core action — not MAUs. NUX must lead to this action. If an experiment doesn't move core-action completion, it failed.

**Level 2 — Retention**
The product must get better the more you use it, and give users more to lose by leaving. Pinterest's algorithmic home feed personalized around pins is the model: each core action enriches the experience and accumulates switching cost. Evernote's note library is another — can't leave because everything is there. Test: do weekly cohorts of core-action completers flatten out, or do they keep draining?

**Level 3 — Self-perpetuating loops**
User activity converts back into product value for all users (network effects), and pulls new users in (growth loops) or re-engages dormant ones (re-engagement loops). Houseparty and Clubhouse had the right ingredients but push-notification overload broke the flywheel. Evernote never had a loop — had to pay for every user, ran out of steam.

### Hierarchy of Marketplaces (two-sided markets)

**Level 1 — Focus on a constrained segment (the thimble)**
Don't chase GMV breadth. Pick the smallest viable market and make both sides genuinely happy — "happy GMV" not raw transaction volume. DoorDash won by going after the suburbs, where there was no competition and desperate customers. Postmates spread across cities and categories, always competing against someone. Achieve minimum viable happiness before expanding.

**Level 2 — Tip the market**
Scale in service of a tipping point: the moment you've saturated a constrained market enough that the flywheel starts pulling participants to you instead of you chasing them. Two loops drive this: (a) growth loops — supply refers demand or demand refers supply (REKKI suppliers sending customer lists; Etsy sellers handing out business cards linking to their store); (b) happiness loops — ranking and reputation mechanisms that reward high-quality supply and churn bad supply, so the marketplace gets better as it grows.

**Level 3 — Dominate the market (blitz scale)**
Once one market is tipping, run three vectors at once: (a) deepen in the current market, (b) stretch into adjacent use cases, (c) plant flags in new markets with the same playbook. But always put more wood behind fewer arrows — spreading a million dollars thin across ten cities loses to a competitor putting all of it in one city.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies to:**
- Consumer social and consumer tool products at any stage (the engagement hierarchy)
- Two-sided marketplaces, B2C or B2B, working through early growth (the marketplace hierarchy)
- Any product leader trying to prioritize roadmap and identify which level they're under-investing in

**Does not apply well to:**
- Pure anonymity products — no accruing benefits or mounting loss means Level 2 of the engagement hierarchy can't be built
- Homogeneous supply marketplaces (Mechanical Turk-style) — adding more supply doesn't compound happiness, so tipping is hard
- Marketplaces with very low repeat use (e.g., plumber-finding) where cornering the demand side is structurally hard
- Enterprise SaaS with long sales cycles and centralized buying (though "happy user" and "focus" principles still rhyme)

## Signature questions

1. What is the single core action in your product — the one where if a user does it, you know they understand what the product is and will probably come back next week?
2. How does your product get better the more a user uses it? What do they stand to lose by leaving?
3. Where is your flywheel — what happens when one user uses the product that makes it better for another user, or pulls in a new user without you having to do anything?
4. Are you chasing GMV or happy GMV? What does a buyer or seller who had a genuinely great experience look like — and are you tracking that?
5. What is your thimble? What is the most constrained market where you could achieve minimum viable happiness before you worry about growth?

## Common misuse

Tavel's explicit warning: founders see the full, mature expression of a product — TikTok, Instagram, ROBLOX — and try to build all of it at once. The NUX gets cluttered, the core action gets buried, and activation fails. The biggest mistake she sees is not nailing Level 1 before worrying about Level 2 or 3. On the marketplace side, the parallel error is chasing aggregate GMV (or spreading across geographies and categories too early) instead of achieving minimum viable happiness in a single constrained market first. Etsy is the cautionary tale: chasing GMV growth pulled in mass-produced goods, eroded trust with handmade sellers and buyers, and precipitated a CEO change.
