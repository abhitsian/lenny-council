## One-liner

When opposing stakeholders are stuck in a loop, ask each side to make the other's case, name the tiebreaker, and put a deadline on the decision.

## The framework

Keith names three moves, in order:

**1. Generate empathy through steel-manning**
Ask each side to articulate the strongest version of the opposing argument. The goal is not to trick them into agreeing — it is to get them to genuinely engage with the other side's constraints. Sometimes a person talks themselves around mid-argument: "I think you're right that we should focus on growth here."

**2. Name the decision-maker**
Debates persist when accountability is diffuse. Before closing the room, make explicit who breaks the tie if consensus fails. That person is often the CEO, but can be a GM, head of product, or head of engineering depending on the domain.

**3. Set a time horizon**
State when the decision will be made and that once made, the group moves on. The combination of "everyone's heard" + "here's the tiebreaker" + "here's the deadline" ends most loops.

Keith pairs this with a feedback structure borrowed from Travis Kalanick at Uber — **T3B3**: three positives, three constructive pieces of feedback. He builds T3B3 into his one-on-ones and demands constructive feedback from his reports before giving any. Creating the expectation of receiving feedback is what makes giving it safe.

He also operationalizes transparency through a written **"How to Work with Keith"** document covering: his expectations of what success looks like, areas he is actively trying to improve (with examples), and explicit commitments to the team including helping people find their next job outside the company if needed.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Two or more parties have legitimate, role-based reasons to disagree (different OKRs, different time horizons)
- Debate has gone circular — the same arguments are being replayed
- There is a real decision to make with a real deadline

**Does not apply when:**
- The decision has already been made by the founder/CEO and the "debate" is performative
- One side lacks context to steel-man the other (works best with informed, senior participants)
- The issue is a values question rather than a trade-off question — Keith notes Tony Xu short-circuited debate on cutting restaurant commissions during COVID: "Founder-led decision. This is the right thing to do."

## Signature questions

1. What are the three best reasons the other side is correct?
2. Who is the actual decision-maker here — and does everyone in this room agree on that?
3. When does this decision need to be made, and what happens if we miss that date?
4. Have you thanked them for the feedback, and what have you done with it?
5. What does this person need to feel heard — not convinced, just heard?

## Common misuse

Keith is explicit that empathy is the prerequisite, not the technique. The steel-man move fails if the person asking it has already decided the outcome and is using it to manufacture buy-in. The framework works because Keith's credibility comes from not building empires — "everyone knows I'm not trying to build an empire" — which gives him the standing to call the question. A facilitator who is perceived as having a stake in the outcome loses that standing immediately.

The second failure mode is acting on feedback without reporting back. Keith treats this exactly like a customer issue: "We'd say, 'Heard it. We're going to come back and report out on what we're going to do differently, and then we're going to track the progress.'" Pulse surveys that collect feedback and display results with no follow-up action kill psychological safety faster than not asking at all.
