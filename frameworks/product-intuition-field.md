## One-liner

Intuition is not a source of truth — it is a hypothesis generator that must be debated, tested against data, and refined until a working hypothesis emerges that a team can move on.

## The framework

Dylan Field names three stages to how product intuition actually works:

**1. Generate hypotheses**
Intuition surfaces a hypothesis. So does everyone else on the team. The starting state is multiple competing hypotheses, not a single correct view.

**2. Debate and test**
Put hypotheses forward. Debate them explicitly. Seek data that supports or negates each one. Ask follow-up questions until you get to root problems — not stated preferences, but what people are actually trying to solve.

**3. Winnow to a working hypothesis**
Through debate and data, reduce the field of hypotheses to one working hypothesis. That working hypothesis becomes the basis for moving forward. It is not final truth — it is the best current bet.

The operationalization of this framework at Figma is feedback-first: reading every online mention of Figma across platforms, maintaining Slack channels for signal ingestion, conducting deep user research sessions, and asking follow-up questions to get beneath surface-level requests. Early in Figma's history, the go-to-market strategy was built on this too — finding the most influential designers, reaching out personally to learn from them and get feedback, rather than deploying growth hacks.

A companion principle governs complexity: **irreducible complexity**. One plus one does not equal three — adding features together can produce something worth less than either part. The design implication is the rule Dylan cites: "Keep the simple things simple. Make the complex things possible."

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A team has competing product opinions and no clear way to adjudicate them
- Leadership wants to move fast but needs buy-in and shared direction
- The decision space is uncertain enough that a single person's taste is insufficient to act on

**Doesn't apply when:**
- Speed matters more than rigorous hypothesis refinement (use a bias-to-ship heuristic instead)
- The working hypothesis is well-established and the team just needs to execute
- Data is not accessible and debate cannot be resolved — in that case, default to shipping and learning

## Signature questions

1. What is the actual root problem this person is trying to solve — not the feature they're asking for?
2. What are the competing hypotheses here, and what data could confirm or negate each?
3. Have we winnowed this down to a working hypothesis the whole team shares, or are we still carrying multiple?
4. If we add this, does the product stay coherent — or does one plus one become one and a half?
5. Are we keeping the simple things simple while making the complex things possible, or have we made the simple things complex?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Dylan warns against is treating intuition as settled judgment rather than as the beginning of a process. If someone mistakes their hypothesis for the working hypothesis before debate and data have had a chance to operate, the team gets pulled toward one person's taste without the winnowing that makes it defensible.

A secondary failure mode: using process to substitute for the framework. Dylan explicitly names PMs who "treat it too much like process" — running the mechanics of product management without generating a real point of view or strategy. The framework requires having a hypothesis worth debating, not just a backlog to manage.
