## One-liner

Deliberately plan to make your own product look silly every 6-12 months — because if you don't, the market will do it for you.

## The framework

Varun calls this the **self-cannibalization rhythm**: while you ship incremental improvements in response to user feedback (table stakes), you simultaneously run longer-horizon bets — 3, 6, 9 months out — that re-invent the form factor of your existing product entirely.

The structure has two tracks:

**Track 1 — Incremental (listen to users)**
User-requested features, bug fixes, quality improvements. These keep the product competitive today. Varun calls them "table stakes." You do them, but they're not the reason you win.

**Track 2 — Disruptive (don't listen to users)**
Bets that users are not asking for, because users don't know what's possible. These target a future where your current product looks dumb. If you get Track 2 right, Track 1 becomes irrelevant to your competitors.

The forcing question: *Is the next version of this product going to embarrass the version we're shipping today?* If the answer is no, you're only on Track 1 — and your moat is depreciating.

A secondary principle Varun pairs with this is the **dehydrated company** hiring model: only hire when the team is genuinely underwater for a function. Overstaffing kills ruthless prioritization — people manufacture work to fill their role, and that manufactured work crowds out the disruptive bets.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're in a space where the underlying technology (model capability, infrastructure) is improving at exponential rates, making last year's product architecture obsolete
- You have a distinct technical moat that can be extended into future form factors (Windsurf's code-base understanding being an example)
- You've already validated a market and are now defending and extending it

**Doesn't apply when:**
- You're still searching for product-market fit on Track 1 — you need to know what users value before you can disrupt it
- Your space moves slowly enough that 6-12 month bets carry too much opportunity cost vs. compounding small improvements
- Your team doesn't have the technical depth to run disruptive bets in parallel with maintenance — a dehydrated team can't run both tracks simultaneously without burning out

## Signature questions

1. Does what we're building right now have a roadmap item — 6-12 months out — that would make today's product look dumb? If not, why not?
2. What are your users explicitly asking for, and what are the things that would change their lives that they're not asking for? Are you resourcing both?
3. Where is your core moat — what do you understand about your domain that a frontier model or a zero-to-one builder doesn't? Are your disruptive bets compounding that moat?
4. Are you drinking your own Kool-Aid on current hypotheses? What would have to be true for your current product architecture to be wrong in 12 months?
5. If incremental requests are "table stakes," what's the one thing you'd need to get an A+ on — even if you fail everything else?

## Common misuse

Varun explicitly warns against using the disruptive track as an excuse to ignore users entirely. The rhythm requires both tracks running in parallel — not choosing one over the other. Teams that hear "6-12 month bets" and deprioritize listening to users entirely end up building the wrong next thing. The point is that user feedback defines table stakes; the disruptive bets have to come from the team's own judgment about where technology and the problem space are heading, not from silence on user requests.

The second failure mode: confusing "lean team" with "small team forever." Varun is explicit that the dehydrated model exists to enforce prioritization and prevent manufactured work — not to avoid hiring. The goal is the minimum team to satisfy the ambition. If the ambition grows, the team should grow.
