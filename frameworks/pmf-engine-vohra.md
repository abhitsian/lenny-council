## One-liner

Product market fit is measurable and improvable: survey users with the Sean Ellis question, focus exclusively on "somewhat disappointed" users who already resonate with your core benefit, and let their feedback algorithmically generate your roadmap.

## The framework

The engine has four claims Rahul treats as the foundation:

1. **You can measure PMF.** Ask every user: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" — with three responses: very disappointed, somewhat disappointed, not disappointed. The benchmark (from Sean Ellis, who coined "growth hacker") is 40% very disappointed. Below that, companies struggle to grow. Above it, they tend to accelerate.

2. **You can optimize PMF.** The lever is not the "very disappointed" users (they already love you) or the "not disappointed" users (too far gone). It is the **somewhat disappointed** users — specifically, the subset for whom the core benefit of your product already resonates. Disregard the somewhat-disappointed users who like you for a different reason than your core fans do, because building for them pulls the product in the wrong direction.

3. **You can systematically increase the score.** For the target segment (somewhat disappointed, core benefit resonates): identify what they love and what they don't. Spend half your planning cycle doubling down on what fans love, and half overcoming the objections of this target segment.

4. **An algorithm can write your roadmap.** Working down the ranked list of objections and strengths, applied to the target segment, produces a prioritized roadmap that is "guaranteed to increase product market fit."

**Segmentation logic in practice (Superhuman example):**
- Core benefit: speed and keyboard shortcuts
- Somewhat disappointed users asked if they use Superhuman *for speed* — if yes, their objections are on-roadmap; if no, they're politely disregarded
- Result: a tight feedback loop where only signal from the right people drives the build

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are pre- or early-PMF and need to decide what to build next
- You have enough active users to survey (even a few hundred is enough — Rahul ran early surveys on ~100 users)
- You are launching a new product or a significant new subcomponent (Rahul still applies it to individual sub-products like Superhuman for Sales)

**Does not apply when:**
- You are in a marketplace or network-effect business where speed to scale overrides product quality optimization — the engine is for finding deep product love, not for winning a land-grab race
- Your user base is so large and segmented that a single PMF score obscures important sub-products; at that point, run the engine per subcomponent rather than on the whole

## Signature questions

1. What percentage of your users would be very disappointed if your product disappeared tomorrow — and is it above 40%?
2. Of the users who are only somewhat disappointed, which ones share the same core benefit as your most devoted fans?
3. For that target subsegment, what do they love most — and what, specifically, is holding them back from loving it more?
4. If you only did one thing this planning cycle, what single change to the product would move the most somewhat-disappointed users into the very-disappointed bucket?
5. Are you acting on feedback from users whose primary draw to your product is different from your core value proposition — and if so, why?

## Common misuse

Rahul explicitly flags two failure modes:

**Acting on very-disappointed feedback:** They already love the product — their requests are incremental, not directional. Over-indexing on your biggest fans optimizes for the satisfied rather than expanding the base.

**Acting on not-disappointed feedback:** They are too far from product love that no amount of building will bring them in. Their objections describe a different product, not a better version of yours.

**The subtler trap:** Within the somewhat-disappointed segment, acting on users whose core draw is different from your core value proposition. Rahul is explicit: even if you built everything they asked for, they're pulling the product in a different direction. Disregarding them feels uncomfortable — it goes against the instinct to listen to all users — but it is load-bearing to the engine working.
