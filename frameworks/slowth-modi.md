## One-liner

When a crisis forces you to stop growing, treat it as the moment to deepen customer trust — because the businesses that protect existing commitments over new acquisition end up with a brand competitors can't replicate.

## The framework

Laura Modi named the practice "slowth" — slow growth as a deliberate strategic posture, not a failure mode. It has two phases:

**1. The decision to pause**
When inventory (or capacity, or supply) cannot keep pace with acquisition, the correct move is to close acquisition entirely rather than risk breaking promises to existing customers. At Bobbie, this meant shutting down the website for six months during the 2022 formula shortage — when demand was highest and every growth metric was screaming to push harder.

The logic: infant formula is not a product you can be out of. Breaking a commitment to a parent on formula is a permanent brand failure. Better to serve the 70,000 you have, reliably, than to acquire 200,000 and fail half of them.

**2. Reframing the team internally**
Slowth isn't just a strategic decision — it's a culture problem. Laura's growth team had been hired to grow. Telling them "your job now is to not grow" is demoralizing without a reframe. She renamed the entire growth team the **Slowth team**, giving the deceleration a name, an identity, and eventually creative direction (e.g., emailing subscribers to nudge cancellations to reduce inventory pressure).

**Supporting moves that reinforce the framework:**
- **Branding the mundane**: Name internal programs so people remember them and feel pride in them. The Slowth team, Project Shamrock, the Secret Shopping Program — names manufacture momentum and memory.
- **Manufactured momentum**: When business is slow, create arbitrary external deadlines to force forward motion. "We're launching May 1st." Why? "Because we said it."
- **60/40 product-brand vs. marketing**: 60% of growth comes from the product and brand doing the work. The 40% (acquisition, paid, word-of-mouth) only works if the 60% is strong. Protecting existing customers is how you strengthen the 60%.
- **Content, Community, Commerce**: D2C businesses default to Commerce first. Flipping the order — lead with content and community, let commerce follow — builds a durable acquisition flywheel that doesn't depend on paid marketing.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have a trust-sensitive product where a single failure (out-of-stock, broken promise) permanently damages the relationship
- Supply or capacity is the constraint, not demand — more customers will break you
- Your brand equity with existing customers is worth more than the short-term revenue from new ones
- You're at an early stage where your reputation with a small, loyal base is the only real asset you have

**Does not apply when:**
- Your product is not subscription or repeat-purchase — churn doesn't create cascading trust failures
- You have genuine capacity to serve new customers without degrading existing ones
- The competitor threat from inaction outweighs the customer retention risk (commodity markets)

## Signature questions

1. If we run out of product for the customers who've already committed to us, what happens to the brand?
2. Are we telling our growth team to do something that contradicts why we hired them — and if so, have we given it a name they can be proud of?
3. What percentage of our growth is actually coming from the product and brand doing the work (the 60%), versus acquisition machinery (the 40%)? Are we confusing the 40% for the 60%?
4. Is our content and community work strong enough that if we turned off paid marketing tomorrow, we'd still grow in 12 months?
5. Where is there a manufactured deadline we could set right now to force momentum — not because it's natural, but because if we don't do it now, we may never do it?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Laura explicitly warns against is treating slowth as strategic retreat rather than a trust-building offensive. The risk is that leaders use it to justify inaction or coast — "we're in slowth mode" becomes an excuse to not manufacture momentum internally.

The second misuse is the inverse: companies that should pause don't, because they're addicted to growth metrics. Laura's warning about paid marketing applies here: "We should not be paying for every customer and we should be very careful that people aren't getting hooked on the drug that is paid marketing." Once you're on that drug, slowth feels impossible even when it's obviously correct.
