## One-liner

When you are the leader in a core product, fund new ventures with the profits from that core — not by pulling engineers away from it — or you leave yourself open to being out-invested by competitors in the thing you already own.

## The framework

Vijay draws the lesson directly from Mixpanel's 2018 expansion into messaging and data infrastructure: the company moved engineers from the core analytics product to build those adjacent categories. The result was 40% revenue churn, not because customers stopped needing analytics, but because Mixpanel fell behind competitors who kept investing in that core.

The principle has two parts:

**1. Protect the core relentlessly.** If you hold a leadership position in a product category, continue to out-invest every competitor in that core. The moment you starve it of headcount, a focused rival can close the gap. Leadership in a market is not durable if you stop earning it.

**2. Fund expansion with profits, not people.** New ventures should be seeded from the profit the core generates — or from external venture capital — not by redeploying engineers who are currently building and maintaining the thing that generates that profit. Pulling people creates two problems at once: the core weakens and the new bet is under-resourced.

Adjacent to this, Vijay identifies a second trap: accidentally entering a whole new category. Products bolted onto a core tend to become the fifth or sixth best option in their category. The fifth-best CDP or the eighth-best feature-flagging tool will contribute 5–10% of revenue at best, will not accelerate growth, and will still consume engineering capacity.

The two-phase recovery Mixpanel ran after refocusing illustrates the sequencing: Phase 1 was firefighting — sort churn reasons by ARR impact, make them into problems, give every engineer direct customer access, ship fast. Phase 2 was design-led architecture — dedicate designers to the system structure, decouple them from the tactical sprint, then re-integrate with a focus on reach and consistency across the product.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have a clear market-leadership position in a core product and are considering adding new product lines.
- You're seeing churn on the core driven by missing features, not by lack of adjacent products.
- You're tempted to move a meaningful portion of your engineering team to a new bet.

**Does not apply when:**
- The core is genuinely mature and over-invested — real expansion may be the right move and profits do exist to fund it.
- You are building the initial product and have no core to protect yet.
- The adjacent category is deeply synergistic (it multiplies the value of the core, not just coexists with it), and you can staff it without weakening the core.

## Signature questions

1. Are we considering pulling engineers from the core to staff this new bet — and if so, what happens to the core's competitive position while we're distracted?
2. What is our actual leadership position in the core right now? Can a focused competitor out-invest us if we hold headcount flat?
3. Is this adjacent product solving a problem that would make our core dramatically more valuable, or is it entering a separate category with established leaders?
4. Do we have profits — or a clear path to profits — that could fund this expansion without reallocating people?
5. Are we in Phase 1 (bleeding on table stakes, need speed) or Phase 2 (stable retention, time to improve architecture and reach)? Are we treating them as the same?

## Common misuse

The framework is not an argument against ever expanding beyond the core. Vijay is explicit that the failure mode was the mechanism — moving people — not the ambition. Teams misapply this by treating it as "never build anything new," when the actual warning is about the funding source for new bets. If expansion is staffed from new hires or from profits, the core remains protected. The mistake is using the core as a talent reservoir.

A secondary misuse: applying Phase 1 firefighting (ship fast, sort by ARR, skip holistic design) past the moment it's useful. Vijay is clear that the speed-first approach "outlives its usefulness pretty fast" — it's the right response to a burning house, not a permanent operating model.
