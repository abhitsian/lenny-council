## One-liner

Leadership is measured by whether you moved the business forward — not whether you completed your tasks — and you build that capacity by understanding people's motivations deeply and following the best practitioners, not a predetermined career plan.

## The framework

Yehoshua's framework has three interlocking pillars:

**1. Impact over task completion**
The question is not "did I achieve what I was asked to do?" but "did you build something people actually used? Did you do the right thing for the company?" At every level — engineer, PM, or executive — the unit of success is enabling the organization to move forward, not hitting personal deliverables. This sometimes means recommending your own job not exist. Early in Yehoshua's career, she was offered her first management role, took a vacation, came back, and told her manager the team shouldn't exist. That willingness to subordinate personal ambition to organizational impact is what she means by doing a great job.

**2. Follow great practitioners, not career plans**
Career progression comes from following people who are the best at what they do — not from choosing a domain or building a five-year plan. The reason: skills can't be taken away. A company can fail, but the skills and networks you build working with excellent people persist. This is also how she evaluates leadership jobs: "Take a job where if you hire people, it's going to make their careers." If you can't honestly say that, it's the wrong job.

**3. Understand motivations, in products and in people**
The same skill that makes a great PM — asking why does someone want to click that button, what's going to give them delight — is what makes a great leader. You have to understand why someone wants to join your team, what they're trying to accomplish, what's their career goal. Read the room: when someone says something and another person is in the room, watch the other person's face. You can understand so much if you're paying attention.

**On AI specifically:** Yehoshua adds a fourth pillar for product builders in the AI era. The failure mode is building compensations for current LLM limitations that will become dead weight as models improve. The right frame: ensure your product gets better as the LLMs get better. Your differentiator has to be something durable, not a workaround for today's model gaps.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Evaluating whether you're actually driving impact or just shipping features that don't get used.
- Deciding where to take your next career step and feeling lost without a plan.
- Assessing a new leadership job and whether it's worth joining.
- Building alignment between product and engineering at the exec level.
- Deciding how to integrate LLMs into a product and what to invest in.

**Does not apply when:**
- You're in a phase of hyper-growth where operational execution matters as much as impact direction — at 5,000-10,000 people, organizational rigor becomes a real constraint, not just noise.
- Your chaos is strategy-changing chaos (changing direction and projects constantly), not scaling chaos. Yehoshua is explicit that some chaos is fine and some is not — the kind that stops people from achieving anything is the kind that isn't okay.

## Signature questions

1. Are you helping the business move forward — or are you hitting the targets you were asked to hit? What's the difference in this specific situation?
2. Who are the best practitioners in the skill you most want to develop right now? Are you positioned to work with them or learn from them?
3. When you make hiring decisions, can you honestly tell candidates that coming here will make their careers? What would have to be true for that to be accurate?
4. In your last product launch, did you design for the users you have today or the larger number of users you'll have tomorrow? What's the difference in what you built?
5. Are the AI features you're building compensations for current LLM limitations — or would they become more valuable as the models improve?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Yehoshua warns against most directly: confusing task completion with impact. PMs and leaders at every level optimize for their personal deliverable — launching the feature, hitting the OKR, getting the promotion — rather than asking whether the thing they built actually moved the business. The related trap is career planning as a substitute for skill-building: spending energy building a five-year plan instead of putting yourself in position to work with the best people in the skills you want. Plans become outdated; skills and networks compound.
