## One-liner

Default to understaffing every project on purpose, then fight entropy relentlessly by injecting intensity at every level — because in a power law world, the cost of slipping even slightly from peak effort collapses your outcome, not just dents it.

## The framework

MacInnis describes three interlocking ideas that run together as one operating model:

**1. Deliberate Understaffing**
When you cannot know the right staffing level for a project, under-staffing is the less harmful default. Overstaffed projects generate politics, pull people onto lower-priority work, and produce cruft. The discipline is to under-staff intentionally and then distinguish "under-staffed" from "under-under-staffed" — the latter being the line you don't cross.

**2. Power Law + Entropy**
Outcomes don't distribute linearly. Being at the 80th percentile of effort barely moves the reward needle; the inflection point only appears at the extreme right. Meanwhile, entropy — the second law of thermodynamics — constantly pulls every team, codebase, and process toward disorder. Left unattended, teams optimize for local comfort over company outcomes. The only antidote is energy, injected continuously.

**3. Intensity Preservation Across Management Circles**
The CEO is the purest source of ambition and energy. Every concentric management circle beyond the CEO has the potential to be an order-of-magnitude drop-off in intensity. MacInnis's model: executives must mirror that intensity, not buffer it. Buffering is already happening everywhere below them; their job is to prevent signal loss at their layer.

The PQL (Product Quality List) is the operational artifact of this model: a lightweight, iterable checklist that lowers beta (output volatility) in specific places without suppressing alpha (creative upside) everywhere else.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are in a competitive market where leaving a crack for a competitor is a real risk
- The company is winning — the extraordinary effort narrative only lands when people can see the extraordinary outcome it's contributing to
- You have a large enough organization that intensity drop-off across management layers is already a structural risk

**Does not apply when:**
- The team is already burning out on a trajectory that isn't working; intensity without a credible win thesis is just grind
- The work is mature and reliability (low beta) matters more than creative risk-taking; for those domains, more process is correct
- You are genuinely understaffed to the point of producing poor-quality output — there is a line between deliberate under-staffing and dysfunction

## Signature questions

1. Is every project at this company deliberately understaffed, or did we let some of them drift into overstaffing without noticing?
2. Where is entropy creeping in right now — and what energy injection are we using to fight it?
3. As an executive, are you mirroring the founder's intensity or buffering it from the team below you?
4. For this specific product area: do we want high alpha or low beta? Have we applied the right amount of process for that answer?
5. When you last got an escalation on a Friday night, what did you do — and was that the behavior you want modeled throughout the org?

## Common misuse

MacInnis explicitly warns against two failure modes:

**Buffering intensity** — the most common executive mistake. Many managers think protecting their team from the CEO's pressure is good leadership. MacInnis says the opposite: enough buffering already happens below you. Your job is to preserve signal, not absorb it.

**Conflating understaffing with quality sacrifice** — deliberate understaffing is about forcing prioritization, not accepting shoddy output. The PQL (factory inspection checklist) exists precisely to ensure that under-resourced teams still ship to a defined quality bar. Understaffing without a quality floor just produces cutting corners, not cutting scope.
