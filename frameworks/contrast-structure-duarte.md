## One-liner

Structure any persuasive communication as a pulse between the current state and a desired future, ending on "new bliss" — the world with your idea adopted.

## The framework

Duarte derived this by mapping landmark speeches (Steve Jobs's iPhone launch, MLK's "I Have a Dream," Lincoln's Gettysburg Address) on quarter-inch graph paper, looking for a shared pattern. She found it: every high-performing speech alternates between two states.

**Three named beats:**
1. **What Is** — the current reality, stated plainly. Sets the baseline the audience already lives in.
2. **What Could Be** — a glimpse of the alternate future if the idea is adopted. Creates longing.
3. **New Bliss** — the final, horizontal "arrival" line. A concrete picture of the world after transformation.

**The motion:** The talk oscillates — What Is → What Could Be → What Is → What Could Be — repeatedly, building tension and releasing it. The last beat lands on New Bliss, not on another problem.

**Scale:** Duarte explicitly treats this as a mental model, not just a keynote scaffold. The same three beats work in a board presentation, a team meeting, a funding pitch, or a conversation with a spouse.

**Companion principle — Audience as Hero:** The presenter is the mentor (Obi-Wan, not Luke). The audience holds the real power — they decide to accept or reject the idea. The What Is / What Could Be structure works because it shows the audience *their* journey, not the presenter's achievement.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You need to move an audience from the status quo to a new state — any size room, any format.
- The goal is persuasion or change, not just information transfer.
- You have a "What Could Be" that is genuinely better and specific, not vague aspiration.

**Does not apply when:**
- The audience already agrees and just needs execution instructions (here, dense alignment slides or a slide-doc format work better).
- The audience's communication culture demands conclusion-first density (e.g., internal consulting decks, McKinsey-style memos) — in those contexts, the Minto Pyramid is more appropriate and Duarte acknowledges both can coexist.
- The stakes are low enough that any contrast structure would feel over-engineered for the room.

## Signature questions

1. Who is the hero here — and have I written my talk *for* them, or for myself?
2. What is the current state my audience is actually living in right now, before I walked in?
3. What specifically does the world look like once my idea is adopted — can I paint that picture with enough detail that they can feel it?
4. Am I ending on "New Bliss," or am I ending on another problem?
5. Could I deliver the core of this in three sentences (What Is, What Could Be, New Bliss) if I had thirty seconds in a hallway?

## Common misuse

Duarte's explicit warning: people treat this as a tool only for high-stakes keynotes, then revert to unstructured slides for everything else. She is direct that the three-beat structure is most valuable when it lives as a *mental model* — a reflex you can deploy in any room, any length, any format. Investing in the structure only for the big stage and winging everything else defeats most of the leverage. The other failure mode: spending all the preparation time on slide production and skipping the narrative architecture — going to a slide repository and assembling old slides rather than designing the contrast structure first.
