## One-liner

Build your brand strategy in three sequential layers — purpose (why you exist, 10-year north star), positioning (how customers understand your product, 18-month frame), and personality (how you show up, grounded in brand tension) — so your marketing work compounds instead of scatters.

## The framework

**Purpose** — Why do you do what you do, independent of financial gain? Stated as "we exist to ___." Should be speakable at a conference introduction without sounding awkward, and should work as the header for your About page. Lives on a 10-year frame. Does not change with product pivots. Helps make company decisions and helps the public want you to win.

**Positioning** — The space you occupy in your target customer's mind. Built by answering: Who is it for? What problem do they have? How do they address it today? What do you make and how does it work? What would a happy user tell someone else? Those answers collapse into the classic four-statement formula: *For [target audience] who [need/opportunity], [product name] is a [category] that [benefit], unlike [alternative], our product [differentiator].* Positioning dictates all downstream marketing — naming, copy, website, campaigns. Exists on an 18-month frame and can evolve.

**Personality** — How the brand shows up in the world, especially in places where people show up (TikTok, Instagram, customer conversations). Built in two steps:
1. Pick two of Jennifer Aaker's five brand personality dimensions where you spike: Sincerity, Excitement, Competence, Sophistication, Ruggedness.
2. Write five attributes as tension statements: "We are X, but not Y" — where Y takes X too far. (Example: "We are playful, but not silly.") Tension makes a brand interesting; listing five synonyms does not.

**Supporting tools inside the framework:**
- *Naming brief* — what to communicate, what to avoid, competitive names, seven always-apply criteria (trademark, domain, distinctiveness, timelessness, message reflection, pronunciation, appearance).
- *Concentric circles audience model* — TAM down to target audience (who you outwardly try to acquire for the next 18 months) down to the model persona (a specific, named, fully described person).
- *Bar test* — read your positioning aloud as if you're at a bar telling someone in your target audience. If you can't say it out loud naturally, the copy is not done.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** you're a seed or early-stage startup defining your brand from scratch, a later-stage team noticing that 10 different employees describe the product 10 different ways, or anyone who needs to hand brand guidance to an agency, writer, or new hire.

**Does not apply when:** you already have strong brand equity in a master brand name — in that case, product names should be boring and descriptive (Square Register, Google Maps) because you want the equity to flow from the company name, not fragment.

## Signature questions

1. If I asked 10 of your customers and 10 of your employees what the company does, would I get roughly the same answer?
2. Can you describe your product in one sentence that you'd actually say out loud at a bar?
3. Why does your company exist in a way that has nothing to do with making money?
4. Who are you specifically trying to acquire for the next 18 months — and can you describe one real person who represents that target?
5. If your brand were a person, would I want to hang out with them — and what would make them interesting rather than generic?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Jackson explicitly warns against: treating the style guide as the brand. Most companies have a style guide that covers logo, fonts, and colors — but not who they are, why they exist, or what their personality attributes are. That visual-only brand guide will not tell a copywriter, agency, or new hire how to write a headline, what to say on the About page, or why ADP's "summer fun" email felt so wrong. The brand strategy document — purpose, positioning, personality — is the input that makes the visual style guide meaningful. Build the strategy first, then the visuals follow.
