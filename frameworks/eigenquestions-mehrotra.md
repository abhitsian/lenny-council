## One-liner

When you're staring at a pile of hard decisions, find the one question that — once
answered — makes most of the others answer themselves, and debate that one first.

## The framework

1. **List the questions you're stuck on.** The decisions that keep coming back meeting
   after meeting without resolving.
2. **Re-rank them by leverage, not by importance or order.** Don't sort by which decision
   is biggest or which came up first. Sort by which one, if answered, would knock the most
   other questions off the list. That highest-leverage one is the eigenquestion.
3. **Reframe the fight as that abstract question.** At YouTube the surface fight was "do we
   link Modern Family searches out to ABC.com?" Mehrotra reframed it as "ten years out, will
   the online video market reward consistency or comprehensiveness?" — a question people
   could actually reason about instead of a good-vs-evil shouting match. Once they answered
   "consistency," link-out, embedded players, and even taking back the iPhone app all
   resolved the same way.
4. **Cement the answer so you stop relitigating it.** He named the company Coda — "a doc
   backwards" — specifically so the doc-vs-app eigenquestion couldn't get reopened.

The name comes from eigenvectors in linear algebra — the most discriminating directions in
a space — but he's explicit the math doesn't matter; it's just a vivid label.

**Companion tests he describes:**
- **The teleportation test (low-stakes practice).** Pretend scientists invented a
  teleportation device and hired you to bring it to market — but they'll only answer *two*
  of your questions before they want a plan. Which two? Good answers land on safety
  ("is it safe enough for humans or not?") and cost structure ("more expensive CapEx or
  OpEx — to buy or to run?"), because those two questions form quadrants that imply
  completely different go-to-market plans. He uses it to *teach* the skill in a no-pressure
  setting, the way you'd practice an instrument outside a recital.
- **The doc-vs-app framing (the recurring product eigenquestion).** "Anyone can make a doc
  as powerful as an app" vs. "anyone can make an app as easily as a doc" sound similar but
  point in opposite directions. The standing eigenquestion is: if people are going to
  misunderstand Coda, would we rather they read it as a doc or an app? They chose doc.
- **Eigenquestions are everywhere.** Any product has one — "how do you place a gas station?"
  is the question that explains why three of them cluster on one corner. The habit is to
  keep asking: what's the question that really drives this answer?

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** a knot of interdependent decisions that keeps recurring without resolving,
  where the team is debating tactics ("do we link out?") instead of the principle underneath
  them. Best when the underlying question is genuinely *debatable* with reasoning and data,
  not just a values clash.
- **Doesn't:** he's blunt that the technique is a skill, not a formula — "it's not always
  easy to know how to do it." It also doesn't transfer as a *teaching* tool when the example
  is a once-in-history call: nobody can re-run the YouTube decision, which is why he reaches
  for invented, low-stakes prompts to actually practice it.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- Of all these open questions, which one — if we just answered it — would make most of the
  others obvious? Rank by that, not by importance.
- What's the abstract version of this fight? Can we turn "should we do X" into a principle
  we can reason about for the next decade?
- If we had to ask only two questions before committing to a plan, which two? (The
  teleportation cut.)
- What decision does knowing that actually let you make? (His tell for a useless question —
  "why would you ask what size it is?")
- Once we answer this, how do we cement it so we stop relitigating it next month?

## Common misuse

Asking questions that don't actually unlock a decision — his "what size is it?" example. The
test he applies: "what decision is that going to allow you to make?" If knowing the answer
doesn't collapse other questions, it's not an eigenquestion, it's just curiosity. The other
trap he names is only ever practicing this on high-stakes, in-the-moment calls — like only
playing basketball in real games — so you never actually build the skill.
