## One-liner

In any exchange there are three distinct realities — your intent, the observable behavior, and the other person's impact — and you are only ever privy to two of them, which means staying on your side of the net is the foundation of every effective relationship and every piece of useful feedback.

## The framework

Carole Robin's model, drawn from 20+ years teaching Interpersonal Dynamics ("Touchy Feely") at Stanford GSB, has several named components:

**Three Realities**
Every interaction contains three separate realities:
1. Reality 1 — Your intent: what you meant, your internal state, your history and background.
2. Reality 2 — The behavior: what was actually said or done (verbal or nonverbal). This is the only reality both parties share.
3. Reality 3 — The impact: how the other person received it, what it triggered in them, their background.

You know Realities 1 and 2. The other person knows Realities 2 and 3. Neither of you knows the other's private reality.

**Stay on your side of the net**
A metaphorical net sits between Reality 1 and Reality 2. Crossing it — claiming to know the other person's intent or impact — is the source of most interpersonal breakdown. All feedback should be anchored in observable behavior (Reality 2) and your own felt experience (Reality 3, stated as a clean feeling word, not an attribution).

**The Feedback Formula**
"When you do [specific observable behavior], I feel [genuine feeling word from the vocabulary], and I'm telling you this because [desired outcome]." The formula fails if "feel" is followed by "that" or "like" — those constructions are attributions masquerading as feelings and land as accusations.

**The 15% Rule**
Relationship depth grows by stepping 15% outside your current comfort zone — disclosing a little more than feels natural, then watching what happens and resettling into a new shared comfort zone. The same rule applies to feedback: raise it while it's still a pinch, not after it becomes a crunch.

**Anger as a secondary emotion**
Anger is a distancing emotion that masks fear or hurt underneath. Fear and hurt are connecting emotions. Leaders who lead with anger cut off the very response they need. Leaders who name the fear or hurt create alignment.

**Two antennae**
One antenna tracks your own internal state; the other picks up signals from the other person. Interpersonal competence means honing both until you can catch subtler and subtler signals.

**Relationship Continuum**
Relationships exist on a spectrum from contact/no-connection (or dysfunction) to exceptional. Exceptional relationships have six characteristics: mutual knowing, mutual being known, trust that disclosures won't be weaponized, the ability to be honest, productive conflict resolution, and commitment to each other's growth.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

Applies when: giving or receiving feedback on behavior; diagnosing why a conversation went sideways; moving a relationship from functional to deeper; recovering after something went wrong (repair).

Less directly applicable when: the situation is purely task-mechanical with no ongoing relationship at stake; the person you need to give feedback to is genuinely inaccessible; the issue is a business strategy disagreement rather than an interpersonal one.

The model is explicitly about interpersonal feedback, not performance reviews or product critique — Robin distinguishes these clearly and says unresolved interpersonal issues poison the other kind.

## Signature questions

1. Which of the three realities are you actually in right now — and are you crossing the net into the other person's?
2. What is the specific observable behavior (Reality 2) that you can point to, and can someone watching a video confirm it happened?
3. What is the clean feeling word here — not a judgment, not an attribution, an actual feeling?
4. Is this a pinch or a crunch? If it has been building, why did you wait, and what mental model made waiting feel safer than speaking?
5. Under the anger — what is the fear or the hurt?

## Common misuse

The most explicit warning Robin gives: people say "I feel that..." or "I feel like..." and think they have given a feeling. They haven't. Both constructions produce attributions — claims about the other person's intent or character — which immediately put the listener on the defensive. The formula only works with a genuine feeling word (scared, hurt, dismissed, proud, confused). A vocabulary of feelings is required infrastructure, not a nice-to-have.

A second misuse Robin flags: crossing the net while believing you have stayed on it. Calling someone "insensitive," "disrespectful," or "not committed" feels descriptive but is a label, not a behavior. Behaviorally specific means something a camera could capture: "you looked at your watch while I was mid-sentence" is specific; "you don't respect students" is not.
