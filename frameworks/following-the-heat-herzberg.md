## One-liner

At each growth stage, the critical bottleneck shifts — and so should your best people and organizational focus, the way heat moves through a system.

## The framework

Raaz describes organizational energy as "heat" that concentrates wherever the constraint is. The stages she observed at Wiz:

1. **Product kitchen** — Early days: everyone is waiting for something to exist. The heat sits in product discovery. What are we building? Does anyone want it?
2. **Engineering** — Once there's signal of demand, heat moves to building. Someone wants it; now make it work.
3. **Sales** — First clients are in. The product is buildable and sellable. Heat moves to sales: "We have this thing, now go sell it."
4. **Marketing** — Sales is functioning but demand is constrained by awareness. Heat moves to marketing: "Give me more pipeline. Nobody has heard of Wiz."

The key behavior: the people and decisions that matter most at one stage do not stay the most important forever. When the bottleneck moves, leadership — and the best talent — must move with it. Raaz herself followed the heat, going from VP of Product to CMO when awareness became the company's binding constraint.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** you are at a B2B company that has already found or is approaching product-market fit and is scaling. The framework helps leaders decide where to concentrate executive attention and where to deploy high-leverage people.

**Does not apply when:** you are pre-PMF and still in the product discovery loop — in that stage, the heat is almost entirely in the product kitchen and does not move until real pull exists. Also less useful for consumer businesses where the product-to-market funnel is structured differently.

## Signature questions

1. Where is the heat right now — which function is the actual bottleneck to our next stage of growth?
2. Are our best people deployed to the current constraint, or are they still optimizing the last one?
3. What is the signal that heat is starting to shift to the next stage?
4. Are you personally following the heat, or holding onto a role because it is comfortable?
5. Are you hearing from sales, "Give me more pipeline" — and if so, has the heat moved to marketing yet?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Raaz implicitly warns against: keeping the best people anchored to a function that was the bottleneck at a previous stage. If your top talent is still optimizing product when sales is the constraint, or optimizing sales when awareness is the constraint, the heat has moved but the org hasn't followed it. She also warns against hiring an outside leader to solve a problem the founders haven't personally understood yet — if the founding team can't do something end-to-end even once, hiring someone from the outside to solve it rarely works.
