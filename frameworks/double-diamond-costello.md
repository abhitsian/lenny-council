## One-liner

Alternate between broad exploration and narrow selection across customer, problem, and solution — and earn the right to be heard by building credibility, reliability, and authenticity while keeping self-interest out of the room.

## The framework

**Double Diamond** maps the product development journey as three nested expansions and contractions:

1. **Customer selection** — go broad ("Who might this be for?"), then narrow to one target customer
2. **Problem selection** — go broad ("What problems does this customer have?"), then narrow to the one worth solving
3. **Solution selection** — go broad ("What could we build?"), then narrow to the first version

Each inflection point maps to a review artifact: kickoff, customer and direction selection, design concept review, product spec, full experience review / design crit, and launch review.

**Trust Equation** (attributed to Anne Raimondi, former Asana board member):

> Trust = (Credibility + Reliability + Authenticity) / Perception of Self-Interest

- **Credibility** — bring the insight: customer observation, competitor knowledge, product numbers
- **Reliability** — say-do ratio; follow through on what you commit to
- **Authenticity** — show up as yourself, be vulnerable, admit what you don't know
- **Self-interest (denominator)** — the more people think you have an agenda, the less they trust you

Both frameworks share the same operating principle: structured inquiry over rushed conviction.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A team is operating from opinion rather than customer evidence
- A PM needs to build credibility with more-experienced stakeholders or cross-functional skeptics
- Planning is stalling because scope, customer, or problem aren't narrowed yet

**Does not apply when:**
- Moving at prototype speed to test a new technology (Paige explicitly bypassed Double Diamond when Asana staffed an LLM rapid-prototype team: "We went straight to prototyping instead of going through that Double Diamond")
- The customer and problem are already locked and well-evidenced — in that case, start at solution exploration

## Signature questions

1. What customer are we solving for — and have we actually narrowed to one?
2. What evidence do we have that this is the problem, rather than our best guess about the problem?
3. Are we in an inquiry posture or an advocacy posture right now?
4. "How might the opposite be true?" — used to challenge scarcity thinking and surface options that feel impossible
5. On trust: what's my credibility with this person, and is my self-interest visible to them?

## Common misuse

Paige warns against **performative collaboration** — going through the motions of reviews and kickoffs without genuinely wanting the feedback: "Performative collaboration where they're not in a room or want to do a review, but ultimately, they don't really want the questions or the feedback." The Double Diamond only works if you actually use the broad phases to get surprised by what you find. Teams that treat the reviews as checkboxes will still ship from their original opinions.

On the Trust Equation: the most common failure is optimizing for credibility (being the smartest person in the room) while letting self-interest creep up. Credibility alone doesn't carry trust; if people sense you have an agenda, it divides everything else.
