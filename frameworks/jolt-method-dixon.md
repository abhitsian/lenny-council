## One-liner

Buyers who go silent late in the deal aren't afraid of missing out — they're afraid of being blamed if something goes wrong; diagnose that fear first, then de-risk the decision to unlock action.

## The framework

Based on analysis of 2.5 million sales calls, Dixon found that 40–60% of qualified pipeline ends in no decision, and 56% of those no-decision losses come from buyers who *want* to buy but can't because of fear of failure — not status quo bias. The JOLT Method is the response.

**J — Judge their level of indecision**
Before doing anything else, surface what's actually holding the buyer back. Dixon calls the technique "pings and echoes": articulate a hypothesis about the buyer's specific anxiety ("a lot of customers at this stage get overwhelmed by the number of options — are you and your team clear on what would be in or out of the proposal?") and listen for confirmation or refutation. The ping tells you whether you're dealing with a choice problem, an information-overload problem, or an ROI-believability problem — and that tells you where to go next.

**O — Offer a recommendation**
Options are useful early; they become a liability at decision time. High performers stop asking what the buyer wants and start recommending what they should do. This triggers the "delegation effect": when the buyer follows your recommendation, the risk is now shared. Dixon's analogy — the waiter who says "I'd get the sea bass, it's our best" — is exactly the posture.

**L — Limit the exploration**
Buyers stuck in endless research aren't doing it because they love reading whitepapers. They're doing it because they don't trust the salesperson and don't want to be surprised after signing. The fix has two parts: (1) build trust early by being transparent about your product's weak spots — "we get mixed reviews on that capability" — and (2) demonstrate enough domain expertise that the buyer stops trying to become the expert themselves. The goal is to get the buyer to delegate their confidence to you, not to Google.

**T — Take risk off the table**
Two moves: first, reset expectations downward ("let's build the business case around 5X, not 10X, so you're set up to over-deliver"); second, add explicit safety nets — roadmapping the implementation before the contract is signed, including professional services as an "insurance policy," and offering contract carve-outs where possible. The buyer needs to feel like they're skydiving with a tandem instructor, not alone.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** the buyer has already agreed the status quo is unacceptable and signaled intent to move forward, but then starts relitigating concerns or going quiet. The deal is stuck between intent and action.

**Does not apply when:** the buyer is still genuinely indifferent to their status quo (status quo bias, not omission bias). In that case, Challenger-style teaching and FOMO pressure is still appropriate. Dixon is explicit: you have to break status quo bias first — if you haven't done that, you don't have an indecision problem yet.

**Backfires when:** you dial up FOMO on a buyer who is already indecisive. Dixon's data: dialing up FOMO increases the odds of losing to no-decision 87% of the time when the buyer is stuck in indecision.

## Signature questions

1. At this stage of the process, are you and your team aligned on what needs to be in versus out of the proposal?
2. Is there a believability gap on the ROI numbers — something that would make it hard to sell this internally to the CFO?
3. What's the worst-case outcome for you personally if this doesn't deliver what we're projecting?
4. Are you still doing reference calls or research? What would you need to see to feel like you've done enough due diligence?
5. Is the hesitation about whether this is the right *choice*, or whether it's going to actually *work* once you've signed?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Dixon explicitly warns against is applying JOLT tactics to a buyer who hasn't yet acknowledged that their status quo is broken. JOLT only addresses the gap between intent and action — it does nothing to overcome genuine status quo indifference. Jumping to de-risking before you've earned the buyer's intent just signals you're desperate to close and makes the buyer more nervous, not less.

A secondary misuse: treating JOLT as a linear four-step checklist. Dixon is clear that the J (diagnosis) determines the order of everything else. If the buyer's fear is ROI believability, you skip straight to T. If it's choice overload, you go to O. The J is a diagnostic tool, not a starting ritual.
