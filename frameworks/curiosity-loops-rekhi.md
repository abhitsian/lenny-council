## One-liner

When you need advice on a real decision, send a specific, structured question to 5–10 people who either know the topic deeply or know you well — get contextual input fast, without leaning on one person's opinion.

## The framework

Ada names four parts to a Curiosity Loop:

**1. Ask a good question.**
A good question is specific, solicits rationale, and isn't biased. Don't lead with your own view — people will default to agreeing with you. The question should give people something to anchor on, not just a blank "what should I do?"

**2. Curate who you ask.**
Two types of people belong in the loop: subject matter experts who know the domain, and people who know you well enough to say whether the answer actually fits you. You need both dimensions.

**3. Make it lightweight.**
Design the ask so a busy person can answer in a few minutes — a short email, a forced choice, two or three picks from a list. Heavy cognitive load kills response rate. Aim for at least 3–4 substantive replies; send to 5–10 people depending on how well you know them.

**4. Close the loop.**
Process what you heard and thank the people who responded. Tell them what you decided and how their input affected it. The relationship compounds when people feel their input mattered.

Ada distinguishes two modes: **heavyweight** (a doc, a structured email, all responses compiled) for major decisions, and **lightweight** (asking the same question conversationally as you encounter people day-to-day, scanning for surprises).

## When it applies / when it doesn't

Use it when you're facing a real decision where you genuinely don't know what you're missing — a career move, a product direction, a major life call. The value is in finding blind spots and getting perspectives you wouldn't generate alone, not in crowdsourcing the answer.

Don't use it as a substitute for judgment. Ada is explicit: the output is input, not an answer. You look for surprises and things that challenge your reasoning, not for consensus to act on. If you already know what you want to do and are just looking for validation, a curiosity loop will tell you that too — and that's information.

Rate-limit it. Ada reserves the heavyweight version for quarterly decisions or whenever she hits a genuine impasse. Over-asking the same people degrades the relationship.

## Signature questions

- What specifically is the decision you're trying to make, and have you framed it narrowly enough to get useful answers?
- Who in your network actually knows this domain — and separately, who knows you well enough to say whether this fits your situation?
- Have you removed your own framing from the question, or are you telegraphing what answer you want?
- After you hear back, what surprised you? What did you strongly disagree with? (Those are the signals worth examining.)
- Did you thank people and close the loop — or did you just extract and move on?

## Common misuse

Treating the output as a vote. Ada warns explicitly that this isn't a poll — "don't do what people tell you to do." The risk is that bad advice isn't bad because it's wrong in principle; it's bad because it isn't contextual to your specific situation, skills, and goals. The loop surfaces perspectives you hadn't considered. You still make the call.

A related failure mode: asking the wrong people. Sending to one type — only domain experts, or only friends who know you — produces a one-dimensional view. The frame requires both dimensions to work.
