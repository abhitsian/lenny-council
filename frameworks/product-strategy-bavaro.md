## One-liner

A complete strategy has three parts — a vision (the inspiring future), a strategic
framework (the market, what winning looks like, and your big bets), and a roadmap
(work backwards from the vision to sanity-check feasibility) — and a good one connects
the dots all the way from a high-level business goal down to the specific feature.

## The framework

1. **Vision.** Your inspiring picture of what the future looks like — the "come build
   this future with me" pitch. It's what gets people to want to join.
2. **Strategic framework.** Name the market you're going after, define what success
   looks like, and lay out your big bets on what it takes to win that market. This is
   where your pillars come from — your own way of breaking down the problem to figure
   out who you're going after and what it takes to win them.
3. **Roadmap — worked backwards from the vision.** Pick a horizon ("we can hit the
   vision in five years") and ask what that means for the intermediate years. Bavaro
   is explicit that this roadmap is *not a commitment* — it's a feasibility check.
   Almost every team that does this realizes "this is a 30-year vision at our current
   pace," which is the wake-up call: take bigger swings, say no to more optional work,
   maybe hire a bigger team. It also organizes the rest of the company (a launch in two
   years means marketing support; a new business model in two years means a raise now).

The test for whether a strategy is *good*: it **connects the dots** from the high-level
goal ("increase revenue by this much") down to "this is the feature we're going to do,"
with as many intermediate dots as it takes. A bare numerical target handed down from a
CEO is not a strategy — it's the PM's job to take the 20 things the team could build,
match them against that target, and show *which* work best fits the goal and *why*.

You find the missing dots by communicating the strategy over and over and listening for
confusion. People hide their confusion, so you have to watch closely for the assumption
you're making that they aren't.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** when a team has a goal but can't trace a clean line from it to what
  they're actually building; when a "strategy" is really just a revenue target; when
  repeated, values-feeling disagreements over individual features signal an unspoken
  strategy gap. Use it to draft a team-level strategy and then make it collaborative.
- **Doesn't (yet):** your **first ~six months** on a product. Bavaro says don't worry
  about strategy then — talk to customers, do research, and deliver on the strategy the
  company already has. Strategy is also collaborative work; a solo strategy is a draft
  for your back pocket, not the finished thing. And coming in on day one saying the
  existing strategy is all wrong reads as not understanding why you were hired.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- Walk me from the business goal to this feature — what are all the dots in between,
  and which one is missing?
- You've got a target. Where's the vision, the framework, and the roadmap? A number
  alone isn't a strategy.
- Work the roadmap backwards from the vision: at this pace, do we actually hit it in
  five years, or is this secretly a 30-year plan?
- Where do people get confused when you explain this? That confusion is pointing at a
  hidden assumption you haven't spelled out.
- Is this disagreement really about the feature, or is it a strategy disagreement
  we should settle one level up?

## Common misuse

Treating the roadmap as a delivery commitment instead of a feasibility check — which
defeats its purpose, since the whole point is to catch that your vision doesn't fit the
timeframe and force bigger swings. The other failure she names: handing a team a revenue
number and calling it a strategy. The number is the top of the chain; without the dots
connecting it to specific product work, the team is left saying "that doesn't seem like
a strategy to me, I don't know what to do."
