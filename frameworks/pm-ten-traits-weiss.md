## One-liner

A competency model for PMs that rejects the mini-CEO myth and defines the job as vision-setting, team amplification, decision facilitation, and customer-obsessed impact — not authority.

## The framework

Weiss wrote the 10 traits as a "positive version" of what product management actually is, after previously cataloguing what people mistakenly think the job is. The traits, in his own language:

1. **Live the Future and Work Backwards** — carve out time for a longer-term horizon; immerse yourself in the problem space and bring inspiration back to the team. Riff off Paul Graham: you have to actually live in the future to find what's missing.

2. **Amplify Your Team** — facilitate ideas, create energy, create momentum. The PM role is unsatisfying if you try to create things yourself instead of amplifying what the team creates.

3. **Facilitate the Pace and Quality of Decision-Making** — your job is not to make all decisions but to help the team make high-quality decisions quickly, without you playing tiebreaker arbitrarily.

4. **Execute Impeccably** — a baseline, not a differentiator. Say what you're going to do, do what you said. If you can't, explain why. You are the shock absorber for the team.

5. **Focus on Impact** — to the customer experience and the business. "Impact solves all PM issues" — if a team consistently builds things people love and moves the business, everything else is an input.

6. **Write Well** — writing is the only scalable way to have influence on a larger product org. Recommended: *On Writing* by Stephen King for the practice of high-quality, high-volume production.

7. **Optimize for Learning** — know that the pace of learning is a long-term lever. Willingness to sacrifice short-term impact for the sake of discovering new levers. Portfolio approach to product.

8. **Data Fluency** — not statistician-level skill; knowing enough about customer insights to make higher-likelihood product bets. Data can be quantitative, survey-based, or 100 customer conversations.

9. **Great Taste** — product is a creative field. Taste means you've developed intuition for what people will likely love before you can test it. Reference: *Creative Selection* on Apple's early iPhone development.

10. (Implied across the above) **Principles-first culture** — distill vision into principles that become the common language of the company, so teams can design through a shared lens rather than guessing what the founder would say.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

Applies when you're evaluating PM hiring, diagnosing why a PM or team is underperforming, or calibrating your own development priorities at different career stages.

Does not replace role-specific context. Weiss is explicit that early-career PMs should weight execution, impact focus, and data fluency highest — while senior PMs should lean into decision facilitation, long-term vision, and writing. The list is not a uniform scorecard.

## Signature questions

1. Is this PM amplifying what the team creates, or trying to be the creator themselves?
2. Are decisions getting made quickly and with high quality, or is the PM the constant tiebreaker?
3. What is the team's actual impact on customers and the business — not effort, not output?
4. Is the PM building the systems and language (principles, rituals, feedback loops) so that quality can scale beyond one person's judgment?
5. Is the PM treating learning as a first-class output, or only shipping things they're already confident will move a metric?

## Common misuse

Treating the 10 traits as a uniform interview rubric and scoring every candidate against all 10 equally. Weiss explicitly says they are not in a particular order and that career stage matters: early-career PMs should focus on execution, impact, and data fluency first; senior leaders should weight decision facilitation, long-term vision, and writing. Using this as a flat checklist produces false precision and misses the developmental arc he's describing.
