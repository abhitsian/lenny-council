## One-liner

Two structured exercises to surface what you actually want to do with your life by removing the fear of failure — and a set of supporting principles for staying with it long enough to matter.

## The framework

### The Genie Framework

Imagine you're walking home and find a magic lamp. A genie emerges and says: whatever you throw yourself into with your whole life and career, it's going to turn out great. It'll probably take longer than you think, be harder than you think, but you'll be really happy you did it and it'll work out beyond your wildest imagination.

Given that promise, what would you wish for?

The answer that comes up is your genie goal — the thing you'd pursue absent the fear of failure. That's the target. You may not be able to start it tomorrow (financial obligations, experience gaps), but at some point in this one life, you want to get yourself on that path.

**Second part of the exercise:** once you name the genie goal, write down every limiting belief and fear that comes up. When they're in your subconscious, they're dangerous. On paper, they become a to-do list. "How would I fund this?" is no longer a nebulous fear; it's just a problem like any other problem.

### Nine Lives

Come up with nine lives you could live — all starting from today, all genuinely exciting to you.

Two rules:
1. All lives start from today (no going back in time)
2. You must actually be excited about each one

Life one is your current life. Then go: founder, author, professor, maker of videos, whatever resonates. The point isn't to pick one and abandon the rest. Two things come out of the exercise:

- Identify which life gives you the most energy right now, and pull it a little into your current life. You don't have to go full-time immediately — start it as a side thread. That pull alone will raise your energy everywhere else.
- Recognize that most people can have all nine lives, just not at once. If you follow the thing that gives you the most energy at each stage, you will accumulate them over time.

### Supporting principles

**Internal vs. external scorecard.** External success (job title, financial event, resume prestige) doesn't deliver internal satisfaction. Tension, friction, and burnout are what show up when your external scorecard runs your decisions. The internal game — recognizing what you actually value, not what you've been programmed to want — is the real one.

**Everything worth having is on the other side of worse first.** Pick anything: getting in shape, changing careers, leaving a bad relationship. The first move is always negative. If you're optimizing for tomorrow, you'll stay exactly where you are. The five-years-from-now version of you knows which hard moves to make now.

**Life is suffering, so choose something worth suffering for.** There is no easy path. You're going to be getting on planes and working long hours either way. The only variable is whether you're doing it for something you care about.

**Time is the binding constraint.** Greatness requires being excited about something for a decade or more. The biggest reason people fail isn't money or talent — it's not giving themselves enough time. Weaver ran Alpine for 14 years before he could say with confidence they'd even survive, and 18 before he'd call it really successful.

**Not now = not ever.** Students never say "I'm giving up on my dream." They say "not now." Not now is just a softer form of not ever. The clouds will not part. There will never be a safe moment. That's called life.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** someone is competent and functional in their current path but disconnected from it — the MBA student who's already successful by external measures but hasn't asked what they actually want. Also useful when someone is facing a fork and defaulting to the "safer" option without examining why.

**Does not apply well when:** someone has unresolved basic constraints (financial survival, visa situation) that genuinely need to be solved first. Weaver acknowledges this directly — the framework works in parallel with meeting real obligations, not as a reason to ignore them. Also not the right tool for someone who already knows what they want and just needs execution help.

## Signature questions

1. If a genie guaranteed your success at whatever you throw yourself into with your whole life and career, what would you wish for?
2. Your heart and energy are clearly for B — what are the fears and limiting beliefs keeping you from doing B?
3. What would you do if you didn't have to make money? What's play for you that is work for other people?
4. What's the thing you really want to do but you're too embarrassed to say?
5. Five years from now, what would that version of yourself wish you were starting today?

## Common misuse

Treating the genie goal as a binary flip — "I need to quit tomorrow and go do this thing." Weaver is explicit that most people have real financial and structural constraints, and the framework is about getting on the path, not blowing up your life immediately. The genie question reveals the destination; the limiting-beliefs exercise and accountability structure are what actually move you toward it over time.

The second misuse is the "not now" trap — doing the exercise, identifying the genie goal, and then filing it away for a more convenient moment. Weaver treats that as the primary failure mode: the exercise is only useful if you start pulling the chosen life into your current one, even if just on the margin.
