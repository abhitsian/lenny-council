## One-liner

Define "transformative AI" by whether an AI agent, contracted for a trial period, gets hired afterward — then scale that test across a weighted basket of jobs to measure real-world AI impact.

## The framework

Benjamin Mann's framework has three layers:

**1. The role-level test**
Contract an AI agent for a job — a month or three months. At the end, if the employer would hire that agent and only then discovers it is a machine rather than a person, the agent has passed the Economic Turing Test for that role.

**2. The market basket**
Borrow the purchasing-power-parity idea: assemble a "market basket of jobs" weighted by wages. If an agent can pass the Economic Turing Test for 50% of money-weighted jobs, you have transformative AI. The exact threshold is illustrative, not sacred.

**3. The macro signal**
A secondary indicator: if world GDP growth rate exceeds 10% annually, something transformative has happened. Current baseline is roughly 3%. A 3x jump would be unambiguous.

The framework deliberately sidesteps the word AGI — Mann calls it "a loaded term" — in favor of a measurable economic output. It anchors capability claims to the labor market, which is slow to shift and therefore a reliable lag indicator of genuine transformation.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You need a concrete, defensible milestone for "AI is transformative" — for roadmap planning, policy conversations, or resource allocation decisions.
- You are assessing whether to build for today's AI capability or for where models will be in 12–18 months.
- You need to explain AI progress to a non-technical audience using a reference class they already understand (hiring decisions, labor markets).

**Does not apply when:**
- You need a fine-grained measure of model quality on a specific task — the test measures employment-readiness, not benchmark performance.
- The job in question has no human reference class (novel AI-native roles).
- You are evaluating narrow, single-skill tools rather than general agents.

## Signature questions

1. If you contracted this AI agent for 90 days and didn't know it was a machine, would you hire it? If not, what specifically is missing?
2. What does the market basket of jobs look like for your industry — which roles carry the most economic weight, and which are closest to passing the test today?
3. Are you building your product for the 20% success rate you see now, or for the 100% success rate that is 12 months away?
4. Is your team using AI as a new tool with old habits, or are they asking for the ambitious change and trying again when it fails?
5. Where in your org are customers or internal users already experiencing 80%+ resolution rates from AI — and what does that tell you about where displacement actually starts?

## Common misuse

Mann warns against anchoring to current benchmark saturation as evidence that progress is slowing. Benchmarks get saturated within 6–12 months of release — the constraint is ambition of measurement, not intelligence itself. The same failure mode applies to the Economic Turing Test: if you only test against easy, well-defined roles, you will under-estimate where the threshold actually sits. The basket needs to be weighted by economic mass, not by ease of evaluation. Picking soft targets produces a false sense of how far away transformative AI actually is.
