## One-liner

Five distinct PM archetypes — Consumer, Growth, Business/GM, Platform, and Research/AI — each wired differently, and high-performing product teams require deliberate coverage across all five rather than defaulting to a single mold.

## The framework

Peter Deng developed the five archetypes at Uber while building out hiring practices, and has applied the framework at every company since.

**1. Consumer PM**
Half designer, half product person. Wired to obsess over craft and detail — pixel precision, interaction quality, delight. The person in the room saying "this is three pixels off, I can't stand it." Often misread as overly critical; actually the archetype most attuned to product feel.

**2. Growth PM**
Half data scientist, half product person. Leads with skepticism and measurement. Default stance: "Show me the data. Let's run a test and prove it. I don't believe you." Wired to instrument everything and drive experimentation. Building a growth team is also the forcing function that surfaces how under-logged and non-rigorous the rest of the product has been.

**3. Business/GM PM**
Half MBA, half product person. Starts with the business model — margins, value creation, incentive structures. Common at marketplaces. Thinks in terms of "what is the incentive here?" rather than "what does the user feel?"

**4. Platform PM**
Deeply wired to build tools for other people rather than end users. Focused on the internal systems and abstractions that let everyone else move faster. Often overlooked, but the archetype responsible for the infrastructure that makes hyperscale possible.

**5. Research/AI PM**
Originally called "algorithms PM," renamed to reflect the current era. Half researcher, half engineer, half product person. Understands how models are trained and can bring product taste to influence model behavior and capability — not just the UX layer on top.

**Primary + secondary**
Every PM leads with one archetype and has a secondary. The framework is not a pigeonhole — it's a lens for understanding how someone naturally orients, and for diagnosing what's missing on a team.

**The RPG party model**
Deng's operating principle: build teams like RPG characters where everyone spikes differently. The leader's job is to create the environment where those different orientations surface as healthy tension, not conflict. Consumer PM and Growth PM in the same room debating is productive; a room full of the same archetype produces blind spots.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Building or auditing a product team and diagnosing coverage gaps
- Hiring and trying to identify what the team actually needs versus defaulting to "senior PM with strong product sense"
- Evaluating founder-market fit — a Consumer PM founder in a regulated B2B market is likely miserable and unlikely to see it through
- Structuring healthy tension between craft and growth metrics on a scaling product

**Does not apply when:**
- Individual contributors are early in their career and haven't yet developed a dominant orientation
- The team is very small (1-2 PMs) where one person must cover multiple modes by necessity
- The framework is used to limit someone rather than free them — Deng is explicit that it should expand people's sense of what they can be, not constrain it

## Signature questions

1. When you look at your current product team, which archetype is overrepresented? Which is missing entirely?
2. If you put your Consumer PM and your Growth PM in a room to debate a specific feature decision, what does that debate actually look like — and do you have both people?
3. What is the primary archetype of your strongest PM right now, and does the role they're in actually use that wiring?
4. When you hire, are you filling the role you have open — or filling the gap the team actually has?
5. As an investor or operator: does this founder's archetype match the problem they're solving? A platform PM building consumer social and a consumer PM building developer tools are both fighting their own wiring.

## Common misuse

Deng's explicit warning: the framework is meant to give people more space to express who they are, not to box them in. The failure mode is using archetypes as filters that narrow what someone is allowed to do, rather than as diagnostics that reveal what's missing on a team and where each person's real wiring lies.

A related misuse: hearing "Consumer PM" and concluding that's the ideal to aspire to, because that's the archetype most discussed in product discourse. Deng makes the point directly — there's no one way to be a PM, and the framework only works if all five are treated as equally legitimate.
