## One-liner

Prepare any talk by speaking it down to its bare core, then expanding back out — and before you start, decide the single sentence you want your audience to walk away remembering.

## The framework

### Accordion Method (talk preparation)

The old way is broken: dump ideas on paper, edit into a script, memorize the script. The problem is memorization works like a chain — break one link and you lose everything. Worse, you're memorizing written language and performing it as spoken language, which never sounds natural.

The Accordion Method replaces writing-then-memorizing with speaking-then-compressing:

1. **First pass (3 min):** Speak the talk out loud with a timer. Rules: stay in character the whole time, end strong. Don't worry how it sounds — you're populating your mind with your ideas in spoken form.
2. **Compress down (2 min → 1 min → 30 sec):** Each rep, shave a step. Cutting to 30 seconds forces you to find the essential pieces — the couch and the lamp, not everything in the room.
3. **Expand back up (30 sec → 1 min → 2 min → 3 min):** Each step doubles your space. You reintroduce only what belongs, and everything you add is a deliberate choice.
4. **End state:** Your talk is internalized, not memorized. You know it plastically — you can give it in 1 minute or 5 minutes, and a missed word won't derail you.

The method also works at the micro level: run a single section or a problematic slide through the accordion to gain clarity on that piece alone.

### Bow & Arrow Framework (audience focus)

The mindset mistake: preparing by thinking about what you want to say, rather than what you want your audience to remember. Research and common experience both suggest people leave a talk remembering one thing, if anything.

- **The Arrow:** One sentence — the single thing you'd be satisfied with if it were the only thing the audience remembered. Finding it takes iteration. A good arrow gives you a north star for the whole talk.
- **The Bow:** The weight that drives the arrow home — an anecdote, a data point, a story, a call to action. The bow and the arrow inform each other as you draft. A sharp arrow suggests the right bow material; testing the bow often refines the arrow.

**Combined workflow:** Run the bow and arrow thinking before the first accordion pass (rough arrow hypothesis), refine it as you compress down (what survives to 30 seconds is usually your real arrow), and lock it before you go back up.

**Applies to slides too:** Every slide needs its own one-thing arrow. If you can't name it, the slide should be split or cut. Make the slide title the arrow itself.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** You have a known talk coming up — keynote, all-hands, job interview, CEO meeting, any presentation with a deadline and a real audience.

**Applies at macro and micro level:** A 20-minute keynote and a single stubborn slide both benefit from the accordion.

**Does not replace spontaneous speaking practice:** About 80% of professional speaking is spontaneous and can't be prepared. The Conductor and Triple Step games (the other tools in Ultraspeaking) address that side. The accordion and bow-and-arrow are specifically for prepared content.

**Not needed for content you already know cold:** If you've been working the topic for months and know it deeply, you may only need one or two accordion reps plus a few bookmarks the day before.

## Signature questions

1. If your audience leaves remembering exactly one sentence from this talk, what sentence would you be satisfied with?
2. Can you say this talk in 30 seconds? What survived when you had to cut everything else?
3. Are you preparing by focusing on what you want to say, or what you want them to remember?
4. When you run this through the accordion, does your arrow change as you compress? What does that tell you about what the talk is actually about?
5. For each slide: what is the one thing you want people to remember from it — and is that the title?

## Common misuse

The most direct warning from Montebello: people treat the old write-memorize-perform loop as the only legitimate way to prepare a serious talk, and feel that "just speaking" is insufficiently rigorous. This is backwards. Writing a script creates a chain — one missed link loses everything, and you're performing written prose as if it were speech. The accordion method feels looser but produces a deeper kind of retention: the talk is in you, not in a document. The failure mode of the old way is also the failure mode people will attribute to the new way until they try it — that it won't hold up under pressure. Montebello's counter: after you've run it through the full accordion, you know it so well that you can give it at any length, and small mistakes don't cascade.
