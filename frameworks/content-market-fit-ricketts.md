## One-liner

Apply the same rigor you'd use for product-market fit to your content strategy: start with real audience pain, then build content that acts as a painkiller, not a vitamin.

## The framework

Content market fit has four components, named in the order Camille applies them:

1. **Audience definition** — Not who you wish were reading, but who is actually drawn to what you make. Identify them specifically: their role, their daily anxieties, what getting promoted looks like for them, what failure looks like.

2. **Pain mapping** — Go beyond topics to jobs. Ask: what causes this person a great deal of anxiety in their day-to-day work or life? What do they need to avoid failure? These are the painkiller jobs. Content that answers them will always outperform content that merely informs (vitamins).

3. **Content product design** — Treat the content artifact as a product. The First Round Review approach: mine tactical gems from practitioners, draw connections between ideas, pull out bigger themes. Not "here's what this person said" but "here's what it means across everything we know." Plan on eight or more hours of writing time per piece.

4. **Emotional jobs** — Beyond utilitarian jobs (you didn't know this, now you do), there are emotional jobs: you felt alone in this experience, now you don't. Camille explicitly credits this as underestimated. Sharing operator failures in First Round Review normalized hard situations and made readers feel less isolated.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when** you are building a content program from scratch or diagnosing why existing content isn't converting, when you have a defined audience with recurring professional anxieties (operators, founders, PMs), and when you can commit the time to produce at high quality (Camille's baseline: 8 hours to write after prep).

**Does not apply well** when you want to produce content at volume without depth, when your audience is too broad to map specific pains, or when you're treating content as a distribution tactic rather than a product. Vitamins at scale are still vitamins.

## Signature questions

1. Who is the audience that is actually going to be drawn to this most — not who we wish they were?
2. What causes them a great deal of anxiety in their day-to-day work or life — if you abstract content away entirely?
3. Is this content a painkiller or a vitamin? What specific pain does it relieve?
4. What emotional job does this content do — what does the reader feel before they read it, and after?
5. Are we drawing connections between ideas and pulling out bigger themes, or just reporting what someone said?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Camille warns against most directly: treating content as a distribution tactic and skipping the audience-pain step. Teams produce high-volume content in their topic area, then wonder why it doesn't convert. The missing step is the pain mapping — they've produced vitamins (useful, informative) instead of painkillers (solves a specific recurring hurt). The second failure mode: using content to talk about what the company is doing rather than to put the creations and insights of the people in your community front and center.
