## One-liner

SEO is a product problem — you only invest in it when the buyer's journey passes through search, and you build for the mid-funnel user who is self-discovering, not for top-of-funnel rankings.

## The framework

Schwartz organizes this in three sequential moves:

**1. Map the buyer journey to search behavior**
Before writing a single piece of content, answer one question: what specific thing will a user search for that leads them to need your product? If you can't answer it, don't do SEO. The journey must naturally pass through a search query — not a sales call, trade show, or social ad — for SEO to be worth the investment.

**2. Identify where in the funnel the search happens**
- **Top of funnel** (e.g., "best beach vacation under 2 hours from NYC"): AI Overviews and LLMs are eating this. Google now summarizes it. Don't build here.
- **Mid-funnel** (e.g., "online dating in Dubai", "Gmail Salesforce integration"): This is where SEO works. The user knows what they want but hasn't solved it yet. Your product is the solution.
- **Bottom of funnel** (e.g., "Riverside pricing", "Tinder premium features"): Brand-driven. You get here by winning the mid-funnel first.

**3. Build a product for that search user — not a content piece**
SEO pages should be built with design, engineering, and product resources — not just handed to a content team. The deliverable is a page that solves the specific problem the user has at that funnel stage. Zapier's integration pages, Tinder's city dating pages, and Zillow's property value pages are the canonical examples. They are products that happen to rank, not blog posts optimized for keywords.

**When to do programmatic vs editorial**
Programmatic (data-driven pages at scale) is right when there is a user use case at scale — Zapier's "connect X to Y" pages, TripAdvisor's hotel pages. Editorial is right for focused depth. Neither works if the page doesn't sit in the buyer journey.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Your product can be discovered by someone who doesn't yet know it exists (Zapier: "connect Gmail to Salesforce")
- The user can evaluate, sign up, and derive value without talking to a salesperson
- You have a freemium or self-serve model where organic traffic can convert without hand-holding

**Does not apply when:**
- Your sale requires committee buy-in, a contract, or a human sales motion (e.g., Mixpanel enterprise, Google Cloud, Vanta)
- No one searches for the problem your product solves — they don't know the problem exists yet
- Your top-of-funnel keywords are being absorbed by AI Overviews and your product doesn't solve a mid-funnel need

The clearest test: can a user who finds your page through Google convert — fully, without a sales rep — in the same session or shortly after? If not, SEO is likely the wrong channel.

## Signature questions

1. What specific search query would bring someone to your product who doesn't already know your product exists?
2. Where in the buyer journey does search happen — are they discovering, evaluating, or purchasing — and does your page sit at that stage?
3. If someone lands on your SEO page today, what action do they take, and does that action convert for your business?
4. Are you measuring SEO by rankings or traffic, or are you measuring it by the conversion metric that actually matters to the business (MQL, sign-up, trial)?
5. Is your SEO page a product — built with design, engineering, and user research — or is it just content written around a keyword?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Schwartz names explicitly: treating SEO as a default marketing channel because it seems free, then measuring success by rankings or traffic volume rather than conversions. Companies invest in content that sits nowhere near the buyer journey, optimize for keywords that will never lead to a purchase, and declare success when they hit number one for a term their customers never search with purchase intent. His framing: if you took the same budget and put it into paid, brand, or a trade show booth — and that would return more revenue faster — that's the right call. SEO is not inherently cheap, and it is not inherently right for your product.
