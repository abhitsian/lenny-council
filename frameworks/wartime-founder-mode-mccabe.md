## One-liner

When a company faces existential disruption, the founder must reclaim unilateral authority, cut ruthlessly to a single strategic lane, reshape culture through values-scored attrition, and make an all-in technology bet — with full personal accountability for the outcome.

## The framework

McCabe names five moves, executed roughly in this order:

**1. Declare wartime status**
Stop treating the situation as a normal operating problem. Acknowledge publicly (internally) that the old business is in danger and half-measures will fail. McCabe: "I said, we need to become a wartime company. If we don't fight for this, we are dead."

**2. Cut hard and pick a single lane**
Eliminate projects, offices, and cost centers that belong to the old strategy. Then force a single strategic focus — even over significant ARR — because unfocused strategy forces complex pricing and diluted execution. Intercom wrote down ~$50M ARR to simplify pricing and picked customer service as its sole lane, ignoring $80M ARR from adjacent products.

**3. Bet everything on the disruptive technology**
Allocate serious capital (Intercom committed nearly $100M of their own cash) before the outcome is certain. McCabe moved within six weeks of GPT-3.5 launching to a working Fin prototype. "You don't have a choice. AI is going to disrupt in the most aggressive violent ways. If you're not in it, you're about to get kicked out of all of it."

**4. Reshape culture through values-scored attrition**
Rewrite company values to encode the behaviors the new strategy requires — resilience, high standards, hard work, shareholder primacy. Then institutionalize a quarterly performance process that scores both goal achievement and values behavior. Hard-code a formula: below a threshold on either dimension = respectful exit. Do this for a few quarters and the org reshapes itself. McCabe describes ~40% total turnover over a couple of years, ending with 98–99% approval of management in an anonymous survey.

**5. Own outcomes as founder, not as a professional CEO**
The founder has moral authority that a hired CEO does not. A professional CEO is told "don't mess things up." A founder is bored without taking the risk of messing things up. That asymmetry is what allows the brave, unilateral decisions that produce transformation. McCabe: "The way that greatness is created is that you find a CEO who's willing to make brave hard decisions and own the results."

**Pricing as a signal, not a tactic**
Separately, outcome-based pricing is the antidote to complexity-driven pricing that erodes customer trust. Pricing from value, not cost: $0.99 per resolved ticket, aligned 100% with the outcome delivered. "I always believe that pricing should come from value and not from costs."

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- An established business faces a platform shift that will make its core product obsolete within 2–4 years
- Growth has gone flat or negative and incremental improvements have not moved it
- The founder is still present (or can return) with genuine conviction to own the risk
- There is an actual technological bet to make — not just a cultural reset

**Does not apply when:**
- The company is pre-product/market fit (wartime mode is about transformation, not discovery)
- Leadership is a professional CEO without founder-level authority and accountability
- The disruption is unclear enough that picking a lane would be premature
- The company genuinely needs democratic input to surface what the right bet is

## Signature questions

1. Are you actually in the 15th percentile of growth for your category, or are you telling yourself a story about how you're "doing fine"? What does the cold benchmark say?
2. If you had to pick one lane and kill everything else — including $80M ARR from adjacent products — could you? What's stopping you?
3. Is your values rewrite a cultural document, or does it have teeth? Are you actually exiting people who score below threshold on values behavior, or just talking about culture?
4. Are you making decisions as a founder (unilateral, accountable, brave) or as a professional CEO (consensus-seeking, risk-averse, remit-limited)?
5. Have you personally shipped something with AI in the last 90 days, or are you managing a strategy toward AI while your team does the actual learning?

## Common misuse

McCabe explicitly warns against declaring wartime and then still running consensus-driven decision processes: "I had to practice the professional CEO approach, which is, 'Hey folks, what do you all think? Let's take everyone's input. Let's put it all down on a spreadsheet.' … I said, 'Sorry, this is what we're doing.'" The failure mode is doing the cultural language of wartime (the rhetoric, the values rewrite, the all-hands speeches) while still seeking buy-in on the actual strategic call. Another misuse: treating the culture reset as a layoff disguised by values language. McCabe's version is genuinely formula-driven and quarterly — not a one-time restructuring. The mechanism is ongoing, not episodic. And finally: applying this framework when you don't have a specific technology bet to make. The framework only works because the hard decisions converged on a real product (Fin) that worked. Without that, the cultural surgery is just pain with no payoff.
