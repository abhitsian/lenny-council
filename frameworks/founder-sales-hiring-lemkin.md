## One-liner

Founders are naturally strong at the middle of sales conversations — not at opening or closing — and the first sales hires should be emotionally invested "quirky pyromaniacs" who love the product, not people with impressive logos on their LinkedIn.

## The framework

**The Middler**: Founders tend to panic about being bad at sales because they conflate sales with cold outreach and closing. Lemkin separates the sale into three parts — opening (finding prospects), the middle (deep product and problem conversations), and closing (asking for money). Founders are A+ middlers by default: they know the product, the customer problems, and can run circles around established-company sales reps in substantive conversations. They don't need to master all three parts — just get comfortable enough at prospecting to fill the funnel, let the middle work naturally, and learn to ask "What would it take to get going next week?"

**The Quirky Pyromaniac**: When hiring the first two sales reps (hire two, never one, so you can A-B test humans), ignore the resume. The one filter that matters: would you buy your own product from this person? Lemkin calls the right hire the "quirky pyromaniac" — someone who loves your specific product for reasons that might not make obvious sense on paper. They may have gotten let go, may not have the right brand names on their resume, may seem like an odd fit. But they have genuine conviction about the problem you solve. That conviction makes them trustworthy with precious early leads.

**Rules for scaling (secondary)**:
- Hire two reps before hiring a VP of Sales. The VP's job is to take you from rep 3 to 300 — they cannot find product-market fit for you.
- VP of Sales must want to actually do sales. Interview test: "What do you want to do your first two weeks?" If the answer is process, systems, or territory planning, walk away.
- Rules of Eight: 8 SDRs per manager, 8 AEs per director. The entire org can be built on eights.
- Hire someone whose last product was harder to sell than yours. They have the muscle; yours will feel easy.
- Give your VP of Sales a quarterly feature budget (story points, percentage of roadmap) rather than fighting over individual requests. Forcing prioritization transforms the product-sales dynamic.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when**: You're a founder closing your first 10–20 customers, especially in a sales-led or hybrid motion. Also applies when hiring your first two AEs in a B2B SaaS company where leads are sparse and each one is precious.

**Doesn't apply when**: You have a true self-serve product where customers never need to talk to anyone to convert (Canva pre-$500M, Slack in early days). In those cases, invest in viral loops and growth instead of founder-led sales. Also less relevant once you're hiring at scale — at 50+ reps, you need process-oriented managers for high-volume commercial sales segments.

## Signature questions

1. If I put this sales candidate in front of a live prospect right now, would I trust them with the lead — would I buy my own product from them?
2. Is more than 20% of my time going to sales and customer conversations? If not, I shouldn't be hiring yet.
3. What does the candidate want to do in their first two weeks? (If the answer is process, systems, or territory planning — disqualifying.)
4. Was the last product this sales rep sold harder or easier to sell than mine?
5. Have I interviewed at least 30 candidates? (Most founders hire too fast — the quirky pyromaniac is usually one of the last few standing.)

## Common misuse

Founders treat sales as a binary problem: either do it yourself or hand it off to a VP of Sales. Lemkin's explicit warning is that hiring a VP of Sales before two reps are hitting quota is "approaching 100% chance of failure" — the VP can't find product-market fit, learn the product, close deals, and build a team simultaneously. The second failure mode is hiring for resume pedigree ("they worked at Twilio") instead of product conviction. The third: hiring anyone in a sales leadership role who signals in the interview that they don't want to meet customers in their first two weeks. As Lemkin puts it: "If they don't want to meet customers their first 14 days, you know they're never going to want to meet customers."
