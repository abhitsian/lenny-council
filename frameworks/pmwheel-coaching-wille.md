## One-liner

Product leadership development works through a structured cycle: define what good looks like, assess where each PM is today, co-create a vision for them, build a focused development plan, and follow up consistently — anchored by the PMwheel as a shared competency compass.

## The framework

**The PMwheel — eight competency buckets:**

1. **Understand problems** — Can they surface the underlying problems of users and the business?
2. **Find solutions** — Can they generate and evaluate solutions to those problems?
3. **Plan** — Can they build roadmaps and set goals that align the team?
4. **Deliver (get it done)** — Can they work with the team to ship, write backlog items, execute?
5. **Listen and learn** — Can they gather qualitative and quantitative data, and iterate on what's shipped?
6. **Teamwork** — Do they understand how teams differ from individuals? Can they create the conditions for a team to do its best work?
7. **Personal growth** — Are they actively developing themselves? (Explicitly included so it shows up in every coaching conversation.)
8. **Agile** — Do they understand the underlying values and principles of agile ways of working, not just the ceremonies?

Each bucket carries at least 15 framing questions. Assessment runs in three directions: self-assessment, line manager assessment, and team member assessment.

**The five-ingredient coaching framework:**

1. **Define good** — Write down your own definition of what a strong PM looks like in your context. Separate personality traits (hard to develop — hire for curiosity and empathy) from skills and know-how (coachable).
2. **Assess + identify the next bigger challenge** — Know where each PM is today, and once a quarter ask: "What is the next bigger challenge I'd assign this person to if I could?" Write it down. When the opportunity appears, you'll recognize it.
3. **Align on vision** — Share your vision for the PM with them. Your perspective may open up their thinking. This step is about seeing them, not informing them.
4. **Co-create a development plan** — The PM owns the plan. You help them see the gap between your definition of good and where they are, and identify concrete steps: a book, a talk to give, a new practice to try. One or two hours a week. One theme per quarter.
5. **Follow up** — Small consistent nudges beat intensity. Ask how the development plan is going at the water cooler. Ask them how they want to be reminded. Monthly or quarterly check-ins outlast any big bang review.

**On where to start:** Wille advises skipping to step four first. PMs usually already know something they want to improve. Start with that, build the habit of the development plan and follow-up, then layer in the full assessment later.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are a first-line PM manager or director responsible for developing a team of ICs.
- Your organization lacks a formal career ladder or competency framework, and you need a working compass now rather than waiting for HR alignment.
- You want to make people development a consistent, lightweight practice rather than a twice-a-year event.

**Does not apply when:**
- You are coaching a single PM through a specific tactical problem (the PMwheel is a longitudinal development tool, not a session-by-session guide).
- You are a senior executive whose primary concern is org design or strategy rather than direct IC coaching.

## Signature questions

1. What is your definition of a strong PM in your specific context — and have you written it down, or does it live only as a feeling?
2. If you could assign each direct report one challenge to stretch them over the next quarter, what would that challenge be for each person?
3. Does your PM's vision for their own growth match what you see for them? Where does it diverge?
4. What is the one thing your PM wants to get better at right now — and what is the smallest concrete step they could take this week toward it?
5. When did you last check in on their development plan, and did you ask them how they want to be reminded?

## Common misuse

Wille warns against waiting for full organizational alignment before starting. Product leads often stall on building their competency compass because they want a version that is harmonized across all peer managers and approved by HR — a process that can drag on for months. The failure mode is paralysis. The PM in your team has only you as their line manager right now. A working compass that guides your team is better than no compass while waiting for a perfect company-wide framework.

She also flags that treating the development plan as the manager's artifact is a misuse: the PM owns their development plan. The manager's job is to help them see the gap, suggest inputs, and follow up — not to prescribe the plan or carry it for them.
