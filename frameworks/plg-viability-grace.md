## One-liner

Before building growth infrastructure, check two conditions: does the product deliver standalone value on day zero, and can any individual at any seniority level adopt it without organizational permission?

## The framework

Merci identifies two hard gates for PLG viability, plus a set of onboarding design principles that follow once those gates are cleared.

**Gate 1 — Individual Adoptability**
Anyone at any role or seniority level in the target function should be able to pick up and use the product without needing someone else's approval or access. DevTools are the clearest example: a junior or senior engineer can grab a tool, try it, and decide whether to bring it to work. If the product requires the head of HR to unlock it because it touches PII systems, the motion is sales-led from the start, regardless of how the product is designed.

**Gate 2 — Day-Zero Value**
The product must deliver real value from the first session, without asking users to invest time in exchange for future payoff. A meeting transcription app that says "you'll be glad in three months you captured all these transcripts" fails this gate. PLG works in both directions — easy to enter means easy to leave — so the value exchange needs to be immediate.

**Bottom-Up as a higher bar**
Product-led and bottom-up are related but not identical. A tool for people managers may be product-led (anyone can try it without a sales call) but it is not bottom-up because growth requires targeting a specific function. Bottom-up means literally anyone at the company could start using it and spread it organically.

**PLG + Enterprise hybrid**
Companies with enterprise sales to capture a customer can still use PLG frameworks to expand usage within that account — driving retention and seat expansion after initial adoption.

**Onboarding design principles (once PLG gates are cleared)**

- Design onboarding from day one of product design, not as a final step. "What will it be like for someone to come in here?" is a first-principles question, not a polish question.
- Skip the carousel. Most users dismiss them without reading. If someone in a usability study for a competitor's product says "I always close those," they will close yours too.
- Embed the product in teaching itself. The best onboarding uses the product to demonstrate the product — a to-do app that teaches task completion by having you complete a task is the model.
- Invites should appear early and often. Users who are social will use them; users who won't can ignore them. Waiting until they've "seen the product" to show invites means losing the connectors who would have spread it.
- Watch real humans, monthly. Conversion numbers hide tone of voice and confusion. Once a month, find someone who fits the user demographic and watch them sign up. "It is embarrassing, but very educational."

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** You're deciding whether to invest in growth infrastructure at all, or when you're evaluating whether your product is really PLG or really sales-led. Also applies when designing an enterprise expansion motion post-initial sale.

**Does not apply when:** The product intrinsically requires organizational buy-in to deliver value (systems with PII, multi-department integrations, compliance workflows). Trying to force PLG frameworks onto a fundamentally sales-led product wastes resources and confuses the go-to-market motion.

## Signature questions

1. Can a single individual at any level of seniority in the target function get value from this product today, without involving anyone else?
2. What does someone get from one session? What are the steps between the user and the full value of your app?
3. If we white-glove onboard someone and they love it — does that mean PLG is ready, or does it just mean the product works with a human guide?
4. Are we building for the user who already wants to invite, or trying to force invites from people who never would? Is the invite experience designed for the natural connectors?
5. Does your onboarding teach the product through the product itself, or is it a veneer pasted on top?

## Common misuse

Merci's explicit warning is against copying another product's onboarding framework as a shortcut. She describes seeing her own Slack onboarding replicated by companies that had no idea which parts were working and which weren't. "People try to replicate things in our product that actually weren't even working for us, but they had no insight into that." Plug-and-play onboarding templates produce experiences that feel disconnected from the product and fail to teach users through the product itself. The second misuse: assuming that because a product works with a white-glove onboarding, it's ready for PLG. The test is whether a stranger can get value alone on day zero — not whether they can get value after a 20-minute guided walkthrough.
