## One-liner

When criticism or a bad moment makes you fear someone has the wrong impression of you, don't defend yourself — identify the smallest possible action that demonstrates the opposite of what you're afraid they think, and execute it.

## The framework

Gridley calls this **counter-programming**, and it has three moves:

1. **Name the fear precisely.** When someone on your team (or you) feels wounded by a perceived slight, the first step is not to fix the situation — it's to surface the underlying fear. Ask: "What are you afraid this person thinks of you?" The fear is usually specific: *they think I'm not serious, they think I'm incompetent, they think I don't care about X.*

2. **Counter-program with action, not argument.** Do not litigate the incident. Do not correct the record. Find one small action — five to ten minutes, not a grand gesture — that demonstrates the opposite of the fear. The action does the work that explanation never can.

3. **Move forward.** The goal is to break the negative spiral and give the person agency. Managers who only validate the hurt are letting reports spiral. The challenge is: "There may not be evidence for that fear. Even if there is, what are you going to *do* to show them who you actually are?"

The framework draws directly from **behavioral activation** in cognitive behavioral therapy: act first, feel better second. Waiting until you feel better before taking action is the trap — and it applies both to the emotional spiral inside a person's head and to the external narrative they're afraid of.

Gridley pairs this with a second practice: **mental-model transparency**. She sends her team a weekly Slack note with verbatim quotes from important conversations she had, her interpretation of those quotes, and what she plans to do differently as a result. The goal is for her team to understand *how* leaders think, not just *what* they decided — so they can operate autonomously without needing approval on every output.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Someone feels their reputation took a hit from a single moment (a comment in a meeting, a misstep, a piece of critical feedback).
- A team member is spiraling on what someone else thinks of them, rather than taking action.
- You want to help someone stay in the game on hard problems instead of retreating to safe ones.

**Does not apply when:**
- The feedback reflects a genuine performance gap that needs direct conversation, not a reputation repair action.
- The person is facing a structural or political situation where small actions won't register — the problem may require a real conversation, not counter-programming.
- The manager is tempted to use counter-programming to avoid giving honest feedback to a report.

## Signature questions

1. What specifically are you afraid this person thinks of you — can you name it?
2. Is there actual evidence for that fear, or is this your projection?
3. What is one small action you could take this week that would demonstrate the opposite?
4. Are you trying to litigate what already happened, or move the narrative forward?
5. What does this person's behavior look like in a world where they're actually right — and is any of that true?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Gridley explicitly warns against is managers who **validate the spiral rather than challenging it**. When a report comes in agitated and says "this person thinks I'm bad at my job," the instinct is to sympathize and agree that yes, that sounds unfair. That's the wrong move. The manager's job is to challenge whether there's evidence for the fear and then redirect to what action the person can take — not to let them ruminate. Counter-programming only works if the manager stops the spiral early and gives the person something forward-facing to do.

She also flags the inverse problem in the "not the protagonist" framing: some people overcorrect into pure execution mode and stop bringing their own judgment and taste. The framework is not "just do what the boss says." It's "understand how the boss thinks well enough that you can find the seams where your own judgment adds genuine value."
