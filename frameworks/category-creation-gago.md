## One-liner

Decide whether to build a new product category by checking three gates — budget availability, customer language, and competition signals — and be willing to abandon the effort if those signals contradict your vision.

## The framework

Gago describes three decision gates she used across Miro, Greenhouse, and CultureAmp:

**Gate 1 — Budget validation.** Does a budget line already exist for the problem you solve? If companies have money allocated (e.g., ATS budgets at Greenhouse), you're in a real category. If no budget exists yet (e.g., visual collaboration at Miro), you're building category appetite from scratch — which is possible, but requires a longer, more vision-driven play.

**Gate 2 — Language validation.** Listen to how customers actually describe their pain and your product. If they keep reaching for existing category language despite your repositioning efforts, that's the market telling you which category they'll buy from. Greenhouse tried "recruiting optimization platform" and customers kept calling it an ATS no matter what.

**Gate 3 — Competition signals.** Competition validates a category, not the reverse. Solo market entry doesn't mean you've created a category — it may mean the category is unproven. When other companies start tagging themselves with your category label, that's confirmation the category is real.

**The dual-track approach.** Category creation and existing-category optimization run in parallel, not in sequence. Miro continued optimizing for "online whiteboard" while building the "visual collaboration" brand. Pando sells into "performance management" (existing budget, existing language) while educating the market toward "employee progression" (long-tail category build).

**When to abandon.** If your repositioning language isn't sticking after meaningful effort — press coverage, customer conversations, analyst outreach — don't force it. Park the new category frame, focus resources on elevating the existing category's value instead.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're early-stage with product-market fit but no settled category home
- The technology is genuinely disruptive and customers describe it in fragmented ways
- An inflection point (COVID, a platform shift) has created conditions for new budget formation
- Existing category language carries baggage you want to escape

**Does not apply when:**
- You're already well-established in a category — repositioning at scale is rarely worth the investment
- You're clearly in an existing category and the differentiation is degree, not kind
- Budget exists and customers use consistent language — you're already categorized; spend the resources elevating that category instead

## Signature questions

1. Do companies have a budget line for what we're selling, or are we asking them to create one?
2. When customers describe their pain to us, what words do they use — and do those words match our category name or an existing one?
3. Are other companies starting to describe themselves in our category language? If not, what does that tell us?
4. Are we ready to run two tracks — optimizing for today's category while building appetite for tomorrow's — or are we trying to replace one with the other overnight?
5. Have we actually heard our repositioning language stick in press coverage, analyst conversations, and customer calls, or are we just internally committed to it?

## Common misuse

Gago's explicit warning: using category creation as a competitive escape hatch. If you're choosing a new category because you don't want competition, you've misread the signal. Competition is what makes a category real. Avoiding it doesn't protect you — it just means you're operating in unvalidated territory with no analyst coverage, no budget line, and no customer language working for you. The right reason to pursue a new category is that the technology genuinely doesn't fit existing frames, not that the existing frames are crowded.

A secondary misuse: treating category creation as a switch to flip rather than a parallel track to build. Companies that abandon their current category positioning to go all-in on a new one early lose their near-term revenue foundation before the new category generates pull.
