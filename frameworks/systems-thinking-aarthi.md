## One-liner

Think of all players in a system, their incentives, and how they interact — not just what a single user needs — to make the tradeoffs that real product work at scale actually requires.

## The framework

Sriram calls this **systems thinking** as the practical replacement for JTBD. The steps:

1. **Map every agent in the system** — not just your target user, but every player whose incentives affect the product: the new user, the power user, the competitor, the platform, the supply chain, the advertiser.
2. **Name each player's incentive** — what does each player want, and how does serving one player affect another?
3. **Make the tradeoff explicit** — given these interactions, articulate specifically who wins and who loses in any product decision. This is the conversation JTBD doesn't let you have.
4. **Apply product intuition** — great product breakthroughs come from one person with intuition about the psychological thing the product delivers, plus systems thinking. Not JTBD offsites.

Aarthi adds a companion move: **first principles thinking**. If your product didn't exist and you had to rebuild it from scratch for these customers, would you build it the same way? This cuts through accumulated decisions and competitive noise.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're building at scale on a multi-sided or social platform where serving one user cohort necessarily affects another (social networks, marketplaces, content platforms).
- You're evaluating a product change that improves the experience for one user segment at a measurable cost to another.
- You need to make defensible tradeoffs (algorithmic ranking, notification strategy, competitive data decisions) and need a framework that names the tradeoff, not one that assumes you can optimize for a single user job.

**Doesn't replace:**
- Early-stage hypothesis generation for a V1, where mapping the job-to-be-done may still help crystallize the core value proposition before you have multiple user segments.
- User research: understanding what people want is still necessary input to systems thinking — systems thinking tells you how to weigh those wants against each other.

## Signature questions

1. Who are all the agents in this system, and what does each one want?
2. If we make this change to improve User A's experience, whose experience gets worse — and is that trade-off worth it?
3. What competitive or platform-level dynamic is forcing us to make a user's experience worse, and is that the right call?
4. Are we trotting out JTBD because we haven't had to make the hard trade-off yet?
5. If this product didn't exist and we started over, would we build it the same way — or are we just inheriting previous decisions?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Sriram explicitly names: using JTBD as the product development framework in an organization that has already reached scale, treating the milkshake example as a general model for product decisions. When you run a JTBD offsite to find the job customers hire you to do, you are assuming a single-user optimization that doesn't exist in social platforms, marketplaces, or any product where serving one cohort creates externalities for another. The tell: if your framework can't tell you whether to prioritize power users or new users in an algorithmic ranking decision, it's not a framework for real product work.
