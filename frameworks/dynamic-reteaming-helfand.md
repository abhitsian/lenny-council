## One-liner

Teams change constantly and that's fine — there are exactly five ways it happens, and each pattern has known success moves and failure modes worth learning before the change hits you.

## The framework

Helfand names five discrete patterns of team change, each with its own mechanics:

1. **One-by-One** — Someone joins or leaves the company. The change feels small but compounds. The move: make onboarding relational (first pair, not first day alone), and coach the people already there through the transition — especially if a new hire lands above them.

2. **Grow and Split** — A team grows until facilitation breaks down (meetings drag, standups lose attention, work diverges) and then splits into two or more. The tradeoff: splitting creates dependencies that didn't exist when you were one team. Shared roles — one designer, one PM — become bottlenecks.

3. **Merging** — Two or more teams combine, often during downsizing, consolidation, or acquisition. The practice Helfand recommends: "Story of Our Team" — each group makes a shared timeline of when people joined, what they built, milestones they're proud of. Merging teams share these timelines to build a common history before looking forward together.

4. **Isolation (Innovation by Isolation)** — A small team is pulled off to the side to work on something new or urgent, free from the cadence and constraints of the main org. The four conditions that make it work: (a) separate physical space or region, (b) a senior leader tells other teams not to pull them in, (c) process freedom — they set their own cadence, (d) a clear decision-making line to someone with actual authority. Works for new product bets, performance crises, and any situation where the existing delivery cadence is too slow.

5. **Switching** — People move from one team to another, at short or long intervals. Serves two purposes: individual fulfillment (working with new people, on new systems — "like a new job within the company") and organizational resilience (pair programming and rotating ownership means no single person walks out with all the knowledge).

The framework distinguishes reteaming from reorg. "Reorg" implies top-down, large-scale, no-say-for-individuals. Reteaming is any of the five patterns, at any level, with varying degrees of transparency and involvement.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A fast-growing or shrinking company is navigating structural change and wants to reduce attrition, stagnation, and knowledge silos
- Leaders are planning a team split, merge, or new product incubation and want to avoid the known failure modes
- An org needs to improve knowledge redundancy before a risky period (hiring slowdown, key people at risk of leaving)
- A team is stagnating and leadership wants to offer renewal without a full reorg

**Doesn't apply when:**
- The change is company-level (acquisition, major layoff) — those decisions don't involve team-level agency and shouldn't be treated as if they do
- You're looking for a permanent stable-state org design; Helfand's premise is that no state is permanent

## Signature questions

1. Which of the five patterns is actually happening here — and are we treating it like the right one?
2. Who's the decider on this change, who has real input, and does everyone involved know the difference? (RIDE: Requester, Input, Decider, Executor)
3. For isolation: have we given this team process freedom AND a clear line to a decision-maker with real authority, or are we just telling them to "go build something"?
4. What are people losing in this change — not just on paper but in terms of who they work with, what they own, and what gave them energy? Have we acknowledged that?
5. Are we spreading high performers across teams expecting the magic to transfer — and what does that assumption cost us?

## Common misuse

**Spreading high performers** is the most common misread. Leaders see a high-performing team and assume the individuals are the source of the output — so they distribute those people to lift other teams. Helfand documents it failing at AppFolio. The performance came from the team chemistry, the shared cadence, the specific set of relationships — not portable properties of individuals.

The second misuse is **percentage allocation**: assigning people across multiple projects at fractional capacity (10% here, 20% there). This looks like efficient resource use on a spreadsheet and reliably fails in practice because of context-switching costs and accountability diffusion.

Both errors treat reteaming as a resource-allocation problem rather than a people-and-system problem.
