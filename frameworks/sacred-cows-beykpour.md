## One-liner

Identify what your organization believes it is forbidden to change, then treat those constraints as a free, pre-built roadmap — because dismantling them is exactly how you shift culture and unlock product growth.

## The framework

Beykpour operationalized this at Twitter after joining as head of product and finding a product org that had been running on "refine the core" for years. The steps, in his practice:

1. **Surface the sacred cows explicitly.** Ask the team: what are all the things we believe we are not allowed to change? The answers are not blockers — they are the roadmap.
2. **Treat each cow as a hypothesis to test, not a principle to defend.** Twitter's examples: reverse-chronological timeline, 140-character text limits, users having no control over replies on their own tweets.
3. **Start there.** Pick the most entrenched taboo first. Its resistance reveals the real cultural hesitation — not just in the product, but in the org.
4. **Shield the work.** Leaders actively protect teams experimenting on sacred cows from internal discouragement. Beykpour describes learning that an engineer told a PM "don't work on hide replies — it's bad for your career." That is the signal you are on the right cow.
5. **Balance with portfolio.** Not every bet is speculative. Pair sacred-cow experiments with reliable core improvements (ML recommendations, onboarding fixes) that keep baseline growth stable and give you organizational runway to run the riskier bets.

Secondary component — **staff projects with believers, not whoever is available.** Once you identify the bet, put a team on it that is obsessed with the idea. A team that is skeptical or begrudging will not will the thing into existence through the inevitable hard stretches.

Third component — **acquihire the obsessed.** For the biggest and riskiest sacred-cow bets, acquire a small startup with a founder who already believes in the problem. Beykpour used this for Spaces, Communities, and Community Notes: give the founder a silo, protect them from OKR pressure, and let them run like a seed-stage startup inside the company.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** A product org has been stable or stagnant for a long time and has calcified its own assumptions into policy. When team members actively discourage experiments ("that will never ship") — that is the cow. Also when growth has returned from core optimization and there is now organizational cover to take bigger swings.

**Does not apply when:** The org is early-stage and has not yet established what works. Sacred cows require a history of defended choices. Also does not apply if the company lacks aligned top-level leadership — Beykpour is explicit that culture change requires alignment from the top; without it, you are doing politics, not product.

## Signature questions

1. What are all the things our team believes we are not allowed to change — and why do we believe that?
2. Who in the organization would actively discourage someone from working on this idea? What does that tell us?
3. Are we staffing this project with people who are obsessed with solving this problem, or with whoever is available?
4. Is our framework (OKRs, JTBD, etc.) incentivizing us to avoid the bet we actually believe in? If yes, what do we change — the framework or the bet?
5. If I used this product every day as a regular customer, would I find decisions we're making hostile to my own experience?

## Common misuse

Beykpour's explicit warning: frameworks — OKRs, Jobs-to-be-Done, any governing process — fail when followed religiously without judgment. The same logic applies to the sacred-cows method itself. The goal is not to tear down every constraint for the sake of it. The test is whether a given constraint is defended by cultural inertia or by genuine customer benefit. He names the DAU-optimization trap directly: Twitter's team was incentivized to preserve hostile customer experiences (forcing the ranked timeline back on users who wanted reverse-chron) because the metrics said it was good for aggregate engagement. Good product taste has to override the framework when they conflict — "sometimes it's just good old-fashioned judgment and product taste."
