## One-liner

Growth is the product itself doing the work across four levers — acquisition, activation,
monetization, retention — and a durable company keeps layering new growth models (product-led,
marketing-led, sales-led) on top of each other before the current one runs out of steam.

## The framework

1. **The growth levers.** Elena frames growth as product taking responsibility for some mix of
   four levers: acquisition, activation, monetization, retention. If your product owns any of
   these — not just sales or marketing — you have a product-led component, and that's where a
   growth function earns its place.
2. **Pick your motion by how you collect money.** If revenue comes through a sales team, you're
   sales-led: sales does monetization, activation, retention, and you hire sales long before
   growth. If revenue comes self-serve (freemium acquisition, trial, product-led activation and
   monetization), you're product-led, and a growth hire should come before sales.
3. **Earned channels over rented ones.** Paid and organic search make Google richer and live on
   algorithms that can give and take away. The product-led acquisition move is to build an
   *earned* channel — virality, word of mouth, user-generated content — that competitors can't buy
   into. Dropbox's file-sharing drove ~50% of acquisition and doubled as activation, because the
   recipient already saw the product work.
4. **Overlay growth models as an evolution.** A growth model that works today won't work forever.
   Andrew Chen's "Law of Shitty Clickthroughs" — over-optimize one thing and returns shrink. Most
   growth loops burn out in five-to-seven years, so you keep adding new models (product-led,
   marketing-led, sales-led) on top before the current one slows.
5. **Budget for the next horizon now.** Elena spends ~20–25% of the growth team's time annually
   on a new loop or channel, and doesn't put metric goals on it early — Miro's Mirrorverse ran
   ~18 months before she attached expectations to it.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** products with self-serve revenue or any product-led lever, where the product can do
  its own acquisition/activation/monetization/retention; companies past PMF with enough usage data
  to actually run experiments.
- **Doesn't:** before you have solid PMF and real data — Elena says don't even think about a growth
  team until then; that first wave is founder-led. And earned channels aren't universal — she walks
  back her own claim mid-sentence ("not every product can drive word of mouth loop"). They also
  aren't cheap: they cost product and engineering people to stand up, not just budget.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- How are you actually collecting the money — through a sales team or self-serve? That decides
  whether you're sales-led or product-led, and who you hire first.
- Which of the four levers does the *product* own — acquisition, activation, monetization,
  retention? If the answer is "none," you don't have a growth problem to staff yet.
- What's your earned channel — the one a competitor can't buy their way into? If everything is SEO,
  SEM, and paid social, you're renting your distribution.
- Do you have PMF and enough usage data to run real experiments? If not, this should still be
  founder-led.
- What's the next growth model you're layering on, and have you funded it before the current one
  slows down?

## Common misuse

Standing up a growth team to "fix growth" before you have PMF and usage data — Elena's repeated
warning is that the first wave has to be founder-led, and a growth team with 10 customers is just
"a G-sheet with your customers." The other failure she names: a growth team that owns acquisition
defaults to SEO/SEM/social and over-invests in rented channels, leaving its growth future "in the
hands of somebody else that you have low control over" instead of building an earned channel.
