## One-liner

Force a leadership-level trade-off conversation by sorting all product work into four named buckets — Brilliant Basics, Bread and Butter, Big Bets, and Breaking Bad — and then allocating explicit focus points across them before anyone writes a roadmap.

## The framework

Rathi's insight is that prioritization arguments inside product teams are actually unresolved strategy arguments at the leadership level. When no one agrees on the bucket allocation, each team implicitly chooses its own, and the result is a roadmap that reflects nobody's real priorities.

The four buckets:

**Brilliant Basics** — Infrastructure, tech debt, and platform work that the company runs on. Rathi's rule: never call it tech debt internally, because that frames it as optional cleanup rather than foundational investment. These are brilliant, and the company is built on them.

**Bread and Butter** — The regular product backlog: feature enhancements, bug fixes, experiments, version twos. If product managers were left alone without any big ideas or executive pressure, this is what they would work on. It is the ongoing improvement of existing lines of business.

**Big Bets** — Cross-functional initiatives that require multiple teams to commit together and cannot ship without a working-backwards press release that everyone signs. The bets may or may not work, but they need explicit buy-in and shared accountability before they start.

**Breaking Bad** — Work that redefines the company's identity: a meaningful pivot, a new market, or an expansion that turns a single-product company into a multi-product company. At Swiggy, moving from food delivery to convenience (adding groceries) was a Breaking Bad move.

The allocation step is the framework's core mechanism. The head of product and CEO distribute a fixed budget of focus points — say, 100 — across the four buckets and do it in at least three divergent scenarios. Each scenario makes a different trade-off explicit: "If we go heavy on Breaking Bad, here is what we give up in Brilliant Basics. Here is what the world looks like in 12 months." Teams then know what they are trading off, not just what they are building.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies** when a company has enough product work in flight that implicit trade-offs are happening constantly, or when leadership and product managers are in recurring conflict over priorities without a shared vocabulary. It works in both single-product and multi-product contexts.

**Does not apply** to very early-stage companies (one product, one team, one bet — everything is effectively a Big Bet). Also less useful in pure platform or back-office contexts where Bread and Butter and Brilliant Basics are almost the entire job and Breaking Bad is a board-level decision, not a product-team one.

## Signature questions

1. If I gave your product organization 100 focus points right now, how are they actually distributed across these four buckets — and did leadership ever explicitly agree to that distribution?
2. When your engineers ask for platform investment and your PMs ask for feature work, is that a capability conflict or a strategy conflict that was never resolved?
3. If you ran three divergent allocation scenarios — heavy on Brilliant Basics, heavy on Big Bets, heavy on Breaking Bad — which future sounds better to your CEO, and which one are you actually building toward?
4. For each Big Bet on your roadmap, could you produce a working-backwards press release that your VP of Engineering and head of marketing have both stress-tested?
5. Is your next Breaking Bad move a deliberate choice with a story behind it, or is it happening because a competitor forced your hand?

## Common misuse

Rathi warns that teams often treat the Four BBs as a labeling exercise — they rename their existing roadmap items and move on — without ever having the explicit allocation conversation with leadership. The framework only works if leadership commits to a distribution of focus before teams plan their work. Running the allocation in three divergent scenarios is not optional polish: it is how you surface the real disagreement so it gets resolved at the right level instead of cascading down as unspoken pressure on individual PMs.

A second failure mode is conflating Breaking Bad with Big Bets. Big Bets are large, cross-functional, but still within the company's existing identity. Breaking Bad changes what the company is. Treating a moonshot feature as a Big Bet when it is actually a Breaking Bad move means it will be under-resourced and under-committed from the start.
