## One-liner

Replace timeline-based roadmaps with three columns — Now, Next, Later — that reflect how certainty decreases the further out you plan, and treat the roadmapping process itself (not the roadmap artifact) as the source of value.

## The framework

**Three columns, not a timeline.**
- **Now** — work actively in progress. Specific enough to carry a date if one exists; team and stakeholders can see what's shipping in weeks.
- **Next** — problems you plan to take on after Now; less precise, no required dates.
- **Later** — directional bets, intentionally fuzzy. Detail here would be invented.

**The cone of uncertainty principle.** The further out you plan, the less you actually know. A timeline roadmap forces false precision on everything by putting time on the X axis — every item inherits a due date by structure, whether or not one is warranted.

**Roadmap as strategy prototype.** A roadmap is not a delivery plan. It is a prototype for your strategy — a way of surfacing your current assumptions about which problems to solve and in what order, so you can share them, pressure-test them, and update them. Like any prototype, the artifact is disposable; the process is what creates value.

**Dates when they're real, not by default.** Regulatory deadlines (e.g., GDPR), seasonal windows (school year, holiday rush), or hard external commitments can and should carry dates. Those items require upfront planning and buffers. The rest do not.

**Soft launch / hard launch separation.** Decouple development completion from marketing launch. Developers ship when the code is ready (soft launch); marketing has a real, working product to build the campaign around, not design mockups. This removes the pressure to synchronize two fundamentally different project types on a single date.

**Psychological safety as the prerequisite.** Teams naturally adopt this model when they have enough safety to question assumptions, do discovery, and run retrospectives. The adoption of Now/Next/Later is a symptom of a healthier team culture, not just a formatting change.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Teams are building iteratively with ongoing discovery — they genuinely don't know in advance which experiments will succeed.
- Stakeholders are conflating "roadmap" with "delivery contract."
- Teams keep sliding dates on a Gantt chart because the underlying uncertainty was never acknowledged.
- You want to hold the team accountable to outcomes from experiments rather than to hitting specific dates.

**Does not apply (or needs adjustment) when:**
- There is a genuine hard external deadline — regulatory, contractual, seasonal. Those items need project planning, buffers, and a soft launch built in well before the date.
- The team is shipping a one-time, defined-scope project (more project than product).

## Signature questions

1. When was the last time your team delivered a roadmap item on the date shown on the roadmap?
2. If no one can reliably hit the dates, what is the timeline actually communicating — to you, to engineering, to leadership?
3. What problems are you solving in Now, Next, and Later — not what features, what problems?
4. Is your sales or marketing team being held to exact delivery dates on their work? Why are product teams being held to a different standard?
5. What happens to your roadmap when an assumption in the Next column turns out to be wrong?

## Common misuse

The most common failure is treating Now/Next/Later as a rebrand of the Gantt chart — adding loose date ranges to every column and recreating the same false-precision problem in new packaging. Bastow is explicit: the columns represent certainty levels, not time buckets. Later is intentionally vague because committing detail to it is making things up. The second misuse is skipping the roadmapping conversation entirely and just posting the artifact — the value is in sharing assumptions with teammates and customers and updating based on what you learn, not in the document itself.
