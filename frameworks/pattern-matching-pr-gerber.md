## One-liner

Get press by stripping your jargon, naming the incumbent you beat, and sending a three-sentence pitch to the reporter who already covers that exact beat.

## The framework

Gerber's approach rests on three moves, applied in sequence:

**1. Pattern match to an incumbent**
Reporters work by recognizing categories. Name the household-name product you're taking on and say how you're better. Do not describe yourself as a category creator. "RAMP is taking on Bill.com" travels further than "RAMP is the first all-in-one finance platform." The reporter already has a frame for Bill.com; give them one sentence that slots your company into it.

**2. Strip the language**
Say it out loud. If you'd be embarrassed hearing yourself say "human in the loop collaboration" at a coffee shop, cut it. Marketing speak does not make you sound more serious — it makes reporters stop reading. Swap jargon for answers to three questions: What does this product do? Who is it for? What is new or timely right now?

**3. Match the reporter's beat, not your story**
Before you pitch, find the reporter who covers your vertical. Put their beat and your company in the subject line. Offer an exclusive. Keep the email to three sentences. Follow up with new information — not just "checking in" — after three or four days. If no reply, try someone else; repeat three or four times with the most relevant reporters.

**Second-order effects over first-order growth**
For B2B companies especially, press drives validation rather than direct signups. The logo on the website, the link in the AE's cold email, the recruiter's LinkedIn note — these are where press earns its return. Manage expectations accordingly.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have a concrete launch event (funding, product, partnership) with a clear amount or milestone to anchor the pitch
- Your product operates in a space where an incumbent already exists and readers recognize it
- You're targeting TechCrunch, VentureBeat, Axios, Fast Company, or similar beat-reporter publications

**Does not apply when:**
- You are genuinely building a category no one has heard of (rare; more likely you're pitching to the wrong outlet in the wrong format)
- You are a consumer brand reliant on affiliate marketing — that is a separate discipline with separate economics
- You are ready for NYT or WSJ — those publications require on-record valuation, hard revenue metrics, and a Series B+ profile before they consider a feature

## Signature questions

1. Can you name the Salesforce, the Zapier, the Bill.com that this product is better than? If not, what does the reporter use as their frame of reference?
2. Say your product description out loud. Does it sound like a human talking to a colleague, or does it sound like a press release?
3. Who is the specific reporter that covers this beat at TechCrunch, Axios, or Fast Company — not the publication, the person?
4. What is the second-order benefit your target customer will get from seeing a logo on your website or a link in an outbound email?
5. Is there a contrarian angle — something most people in the space believe that you'd push back on — that would give a reporter a reason to run a more interesting story?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Gerber names explicitly: founders conflate the messaging that works in sales emails with the messaging that works in press pitches. Sales messaging explains the category, the trend, the problem statement. Press pitches need pattern recognition — a familiar name, a clear comparison, a concrete number. The same copy that persuades a VP of Finance to try a demo will confuse a reporter who has 200 emails in their inbox and three minutes to decide what to write.

The second failure mode is treating warm reporter relationships as leverage for editorial decisions. Relationships help you get an open and a reply; they do not change what a reporter chooses to cover.
