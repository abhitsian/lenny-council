## One-liner

Organize teams around measurable customer outcomes—not feature volume—using four interlocking constraints: impact-first goal-setting, ambitious targets, time-boxes, and radical metrics transparency.

## The framework

Lereya names four pillars, all drawn from Monday.com's scaling journey from $4M to $1B ARR:

**1. Impact-first goal-setting**
Before any solution conversation, the PM must answer two questions: what will change for customers, and how will we know we moved the needle? Both are required. "We're going to enhance / augment / extend value" fails this test — it names a direction, not an outcome. Every team has explicit goals tied to real customer behavior metrics (adoption, retention, conversion), not ship dates.

**2. Ambitious goals as a forcing function**
When Monday's competitors shipped 30 column types while Monday had five, the team didn't ask how to work harder. They asked what goal would force entirely different thinking. "25 columns in one month" was unreachable by doing the same work faster — it required re-architecting the column infrastructure and running a one-day hackathon. Lereya's rule: the goal must be set so that sleeping in the office can't achieve it. If a goal could be met by working harder, it's not ambitious enough.

**3. Time-boxes to eliminate invented scope**
Teams planning bottom-up protect themselves by adding more features. Planning from a fixed deadline — an earnings call, a kickoff, a two-week sprint — forces the opposite: cut to the core value. Lereya calls this the "deadline trap" and uses it deliberately. An enterprise alpha released "too early" that drew feedback of "this is premature, we need more" is a success, not a failure. The feedback you want is a list of specific missing pieces, not "this is amazing."

**4. Radical metrics transparency**
Every team has a daily numbers update — a Slack message with the team's key metrics, sent daily, generating daily conversation. Office dashboards show company-level KPIs visible to all employees and interviewees. Even post-IPO, Monday structured 10b5 auto-sell plans for product managers so they could keep full access to live financial metrics. The goal is "shared brains mode": everyone's intelligence applied to the same data, not centralized analysis passed down as conclusions.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A team can identify a customer outcome metric but hasn't committed to it as the definition of success
- Execution feels busy but teams can't point to one transformative thing done in the last quarter
- Planning conversations spiral into long lists of what could go wrong (fear-driven scope creep)
- Leadership holds data that teams need in order to make good decisions

**Doesn't apply well when:**
- Work is genuinely infrastructure-strategic (Lereya explicitly carves out MondayDB — took three years, no near-term metric justification, required intuition and conviction, not a customer outcome goal)
- Early user research phases where you don't yet know what outcome to target
- Teams so small that daily metrics transparency creates more noise than signal

## Signature questions

1. What is going to change for your users, and how will you see that it actually happened?
2. If I ask you in three months what's the most meaningful thing you did, can you answer immediately with one thing?
3. Does this goal require people to work harder, or to work completely differently?
4. What would you ship if you had to ship to the next earnings call — what's the actual core of the value?
5. Are your team members living the numbers, or hearing summaries of them?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Lereya explicitly names is building without a committed outcome goal — teams use words like "enhance," "augment," or "extend value" and call that a goal. The problem isn't vague language; the problem is that without a specific metric and a specific target, teams invent scope. Every feature idea sounds plausible, nothing gets cut, and months pass with no proof anything changed for customers. The framework collapses if goal-setting stays soft: ambitious time-boxes and daily metrics create pressure and visibility, but they can't rescue work that never had a defined outcome to drive toward.
