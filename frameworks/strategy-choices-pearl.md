## One-liner

Strategy is an integrated set of choices — winning aspiration, where to play, and how to win — that give product teams the clarity to say no and build something genuinely great for a defined set of people.

## The framework

Drawn from the book *Playing to Win* (Lafley & Martin), Annie Pearl operationalizes the strategy cascade at three nested levels:

**1. Company strategy (three-year horizon)**
Answer four questions in order:
- **Winning aspiration** — what does winning look like for us? (Calendly's: "the best place to schedule, prepare for and follow up on your external meetings")
- **Where to play** — which markets, which segments within those markets, which personas within those segments?
- **How to win** — what's the differentiator for that specific target audience?
- **What to deprioritize** — the flip side of where to play. Explicitly naming what you will not build is part of the strategy, not an afterthought.

**2. Horizon resource allocation**
Once the strategy is set, map it to three horizons and assign explicit resource percentages:
- Year 1: 70% horizon 1 / 30% horizon 2 / 0% horizon 3
- Year 2: 50% / 50% / 0%
- Year 3: 30% / 60% / 10%

The percentages force the trade-off conversation into the open rather than letting it happen implicitly in backlogs.

**3. Product development lifecycle**
Execute inside four phases, with commitments made only for the phase directly ahead:
- **Discovery** — research the problem space; commit to when the effort concludes, not to what you'll build
- **Solutioning** — prototype and user-test; commit to when you'll land on a solution
- **Build** — engineering estimation and delivery; date commitments happen here, not earlier
- **Launch / Measure / Iterate**

**Reinforcement mechanisms**
Embed the strategy into recurring artifacts: OPA (Opportunity/Problem Assessment) meeting templates, PRD templates, product review templates. Every template prompts: who's the target customer, what are their needs, how does this beat every alternative?

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A product serves a broad horizontal user base and teams are struggling to prioritize because "everything serves someone"
- The company is transitioning from one growth motion (PLG) to another (SLG) and needs to align people, process, and product around a new ICP
- OKRs exist at the team level but aren't connected to company-level objectives

**Does not apply when:**
- The company is pre-product/market fit and the personas haven't been validated yet — committing to a "where to play" too early locks you into the wrong segment
- The product has a single clear persona with no competing uses; the framework adds ceremony without adding clarity

## Signature questions

1. Who exactly are we building for — which market, which segment, which persona? If I ask your most junior PM, will they give the same answer?
2. What is the thing we are explicitly not building because it falls outside that persona? Can you name one?
3. What percentage of engineering capacity sits in each horizon right now? Is that split intentional or accidental?
4. Are your team-level OKRs traceable to a company-level key result, or are they running in parallel?
5. Are you making date commitments on projects where you haven't finished discovery? What do you actually know at the point you're committing?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Annie explicitly calls out: assuming you have persona clarity when you don't. Most companies believe they've answered "where to play," but when you go ask individual PMs and designers, the answer varies. The strategy hasn't landed until the people doing the daily prioritization calls can answer the same question the same way. Documenting the strategy in a slide deck and calling it done is the trap — the reinforcement has to live in the templates and meetings people use every day.
