## One-liner

Great PMs specialize toward one edge of a three-skill triangle (creative vision, data analytics, general management) rather than aiming for the center — and the ability to think in cause-and-effect chains across a complex ecosystem is what separates them at senior levels.

## The framework

Hari's model has two connected parts:

**Part 1: The PM Skills Triangle**

Three skill domains form the corners of the triangle:
- **Creative vision** — Steven Spielberg-type product imagination; the ability to define what should exist
- **Data analytics** — pattern recognition, data science, using numbers to see the future
- **General management** — leading teams, running budgets, inspiring people, operating cross-functionally at scale

The insight: great PMs don't sit in the center. They live at one or two edges. The center is competent but undifferentiated. The edges are where the distinctive contribution happens.

**Part 2: Complexity-First Thinking**

For PMs building interconnected systems (marketplaces, ecosystems, multi-sided platforms), Hari identifies three operating skills:

1. **Cause-and-effect mapping** — trace second and third-order effects before shipping. A change to one side of the marketplace will ripple to the other; model it before you move.
2. **Structured decision-making** — use explicit ownership frameworks (LinkedIn uses RAPID: Recommender, Agree, Input, Perform, Decide) to ensure every decision has a named decision-maker. Complexity produces tie-breakers; you need a system for them.
3. **Escalation discipline** — LinkedIn's five-day alignment rule: if a conflict isn't resolved within five days, it moves to the next level. This prevents complexity from becoming paralysis.

For hiring and promoting PMs in complex systems, Hari looks for people who can see the whole system and simplify it — not just report on their own piece.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're building or managing a product with meaningful cross-side interdependencies (marketplaces, feeds, enterprise SaaS with multiple personas)
- You're evaluating or developing PM talent and want a framework beyond "did their metric go up"
- You're deciding which kind of PM role to pursue and want to match your strengths to the right type of product

**Doesn't apply when:**
- You're building a standalone product with low interdependency (Hari's own example: writing a children's book — "once it was done, it was done")
- You're early in your career and still building baseline skills across all three corners; the edge-specialization is a later-stage bet

## Signature questions

1. Which corner of the triangle are you closest to? Are you building toward that edge, or trying to compensate for your weak corners?
2. When you last made a product change, how far did you trace the second and third effects? What did you miss?
3. In the last decision that got stuck in your org, was there a named decision-maker? What happened when there wasn't?
4. When you look at the most complex thing you've ever built — can you simplify how it worked in two minutes? (This is Hari's own interview question.)
5. Is your planning process top-down on the big rocks, or bottom-up with a leader reshuffling? What does that cost you?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Hari explicitly warns against is trying to fill in your weak corners rather than leaning into your edge. PMs who think they need to be well-rounded end up pursuing growth paths that don't play to their strengths. The triangle is a positioning tool, not a gap analysis checklist.

A related misuse: applying cause-and-effect thinking to the wrong scope. Hari distinguishes clearly between interconnected ecosystems (where you must trace ripple effects) and standalone products (where you can move faster without modeling the whole). Over-engineering the decision process on a simple product is waste; under-modeling it on a complex one is risk.
