## One-liner

A PM-authored strategy document that walks from company mission all the way down to the specific team's bets, so that every stakeholder can locate exactly where they disagree — instead of just saying "I don't agree with you."

## The framework

Maggie calls this a living Google Doc, not a formal deliverable. The chain runs in this order:

1. **Mission** — what is the point of the company, what are the high-level goals?
2. **Landscape** — market dynamics, competitors, SWOT, key risks. Dump everything you know here. Screenshots of competitor marketing pages belong here too.
3. **Current state** — honest accounting of the product today, the business overall, and the specific area you own. What works, what doesn't. Customer feedback, support tickets, bottoms-up signals.
4. **Technical hurdles** — what tech debt and engineering investments are already being demanded? What's coming down the pipe?
5. **Opportunity** — from all of the above, what are the top one or two places to play? Where is there a unique competitive advantage? Where can you win?
6. **Challenges** — what has to be true about the world for that opportunity to work? What will be hardest?
7. **Solution** — three bullets maximum. What you would build and roughly how.
8. **Plan** — sequencing, resourcing, costs, how you'd organize the team. If no one else had an opinion, how would you go about it?

At the top of the finished doc, write a summary above the fold — Minto-style: conclusion first, supporting argument second. The long version is homework; the summary is what people read.

Share the whole doc with everyone. Engineers and designers in your triad will almost always engage with it deeply because they're on the line too.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies:** Annual or quarterly planning where the question is genuinely large — director-level decisions, pivots, new opportunity areas, situations where you want alignment across engineering and design before committing resources.

**Doesn't apply:** Feature-level or small-slice work. If you have user feedback and a clear narrow problem, Maggie says don't overcomplicate it — just do the stuff that makes sense. The doc is homework for decisions where being wrong is expensive.

## Signature questions

1. Can I walk from the company mission to the specific priority on my team and show the logic chain at every step?
2. If someone disagrees with the plan, can they point to exactly which section of context they dispute — rather than just "I don't agree with you"?
3. Have I done an honest current-state accounting, including the tech debt my engineers keep asking about?
4. What has to be true about the world for this opportunity to actually work?
5. When I look at my current roadmap against this strategy, what percentage of resources are committed to things that don't track against it?

## Common misuse

Maggie is explicit: the document is primarily homework for the PM, not a communication artifact. The failure mode is writing it to perform strategy for executives rather than to stress-test your own thinking. If you write it without genuinely doing the landscape and current-state research — if you skip discovery and just assert an opportunity — you end up with what she describes as the rewrite disaster: arrogance plus skipped discovery equals a two-and-a-half-year project that still wasn't done.

A second failure: treating "data-driven" as sufficient strategy. Quantitative dashboards tell you what is happening; they don't tell you why. The strategy doc should surface the qualitative signals — customer feedback, support tickets, team frustrations — not just metrics.
