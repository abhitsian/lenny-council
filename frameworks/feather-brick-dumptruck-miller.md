## One-liner

Your body signals burnout in escalating stages — feather (subtle fatigue), brick (emotional blowup), dump truck (health or career collapse) — and the way to interrupt that sequence is to change your physical state first, not your mental story.

## The framework

**Feather, Brick, Dump Truck** names three escalating stages of burnout:

- **Feather**: subtle early signals the body sends — waking up a little tired, mild exhaustion, low-grade restlessness. Easy to miss or dismiss.
- **Brick**: ignored feathers escalate. Three or four weeks later: a fight with someone, losing your cool, feeling frustrated and terrible in a way that seems disproportionate.
- **Dump Truck**: a month or a year later — full-blown health crisis, type 2 diabetes, getting fired, or a complete breakdown. The thing that finally forces a stop.

The goal is to catch the feather. Most people don't; they experience the dump truck first.

**Emotional Debt** is the mechanism underneath the escalation. When the nervous system absorbs stress without completing a full mobilization-and-recovery cycle, that load gets stored in the body as what Miller calls emotional debt — a direct parallel to technical debt. Small amounts are fine and even necessary. Unmanaged, it compounds and eventually breaks the system.

Early warning signs of accumulating emotional debt:
- Disproportionate emotional reactivity (snapping at small things)
- Sleep quality degrading
- Inability to downshift at end of day without wine, CBD, or another external substance
- Relationships — especially intimate ones — starting to fray

**State Over Story** is the companion principle. Most people try to manage anxiety or stress by reasoning their way through it ("this talk is going to go fine," "I shouldn't feel this way"). Miller's intervention: change the physical state first. The body sends four times more signals to the brain than the brain sends to the body (afferent vs. efferent neuron asymmetry), so shifting your physiology produces faster and more durable results than changing the story you're telling yourself.

**APE** is the interoception check-in that makes this practical day-to-day:
- **Awareness**: is your awareness narrow and tense, or expanded and relaxed?
- **Posture**: what is your body doing right now?
- **Emotion**: what sensations, moods, or feelings are present?

Running APE before a meeting, during a stressful moment, or as a morning practice builds what Miller calls interoceptive capacity — the ability to notice feathers before they become bricks.

**Breath as the state lever**: exhale twice as long as inhale (e.g., 4-4-8 breathing) activates the parasympathetic nervous system and creates calm. Rapid exhale pumps through the nose (espresso breath) activate energy. These are the primary tools for real-time state shifting.

**"If this, then breathe"**: Miller recommends building a personal recipe set — if overwhelmed, hum; if anxious, 4-4-8 breathing; if lethargic, espresso breath — so the right tool is accessible when you're least likely to think clearly.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies to:**
- Founders and executives operating at sustained high intensity who normalize the "always on" state
- Anyone who can buffer stress well (high-capacity nervous systems) — because high capacity delays the warning signals, making the dump truck worse when it arrives
- Leaders whose emotional state has downstream effects on their team ("the nervous system of an organization is a reflection of the nervous system of the CEO")

**Does not apply cleanly to:**
- People already past the dump truck stage, where the primary need is recovery, not prevention
- Those with very high interoception who are already overwhelmed by stimuli — for them, the priority is building capacity to hold stress, not increasing awareness of it further
- Acute crisis situations that require external intervention, not solo practice

## Signature questions

1. Are you able to downshift at the end of the day without an external substance — wine, CBD, a screen? If not, what does that tell you about where you are on the emotional debt curve?
2. What does your feather look like? Have you identified the early, subtle signals your body sends you before things escalate?
3. When you feel anxious or nervous before something important, are you trying to change the story or change the state? Which one are you actually doing?
4. In the last month, have you had a reaction to someone or something that felt disproportionate to what happened? What was your body doing in that moment?
5. How are you complicit in creating the conditions you say you don't want? (Miller credits this question to Jerry Colonna.)

## Common misuse

Miller's explicit warning: treating this as a purely cognitive or mindfulness problem. Talk therapy alone — without somatic or body-based work — addresses the story but not the stored state. Understanding in precise detail what happened years ago doesn't pay off emotional debt if you can't connect it to the correlating sensations in the body. The misuse pattern is substituting intellectual insight for actual state change, which leaves the underlying mobilization reflex incomplete and the debt accumulating.
