## One-liner

Startups win by reaching escape velocity on a new distribution platform before
the incumbent copies them — and every new platform runs the same four-step cycle
(market conditions met → a moat gets identified → the platform opens to
third-party developers → the platform closes down to control and monetize), so
you time your bet to the open window and plan for the close.

## The framework

The core thesis first: a great product is necessary but not sufficient. The real
separation is distribution, and "startups is a game of trying to get distribution
before the incumbent can copy" (Balfour citing Alex Rampell's 2015 a16z post).
The biggest distribution opportunities show up when a new platform emerges,
because startups move onto it faster than incumbents do.

The four steps Balfour names (he counts from zero):

1. **Step Zero — market conditions are met.** Consensus forms that a huge new
   category is coming (social, mobile, now AI chat), but there's no clear winner
   yet — usually five to seven major players fighting it out. These dynamics end
   in monopolies or duopolies, so the stakes are high and competition is fierce.
2. **Step One — the moat gets identified.** Someone figures out the source of
   defensibility (for Facebook it was the friend graph; for AI chat his
   hypothesis is context + memory). You can't gather the moat alone, so you need
   an ecosystem.
3. **Step Two — the platform opens.** The platform recruits third-party
   developers and content creators with a value exchange: build on my platform
   and add use cases/engagement, and I'll give you distribution. (Facebook's
   "canvas" + access to notification and feed channels.) This is the gold-rush
   window where startups grow virally and fast.
4. **Step Three — the platform closes.** To keep growing and to prevent its own
   disruption, the platform peels the value exchange back: takes a cut, builds
   first-party apps that absorb the highest-value use cases, and suppresses the
   organic distribution it once handed out to push you toward paid. By the time
   it closes, the platform owner is far enough ahead that it doesn't matter.

How to bet (his applied layer): your move depends on your stage. Late-stage
companies can spread chips across several platforms and wait to see the winner —
the risk is waiting too long. Startups have no such luxury: pick one platform and
go all in. When evaluating which platform, he weighs retention and depth of
engagement over vanity metrics like MAU or signups, and user quality / ability to
monetize (his example: bet on iOS, not Android).

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** choosing whether and when to build distribution on top of an
  emerging platform — today that's ChatGPT / AI chat, historically Facebook,
  iOS, Google, Twitter, Udemy. Useful any time your growth plan leans on a
  channel you don't own.
- **Doesn't:** Balfour is explicit that timing in the AI market has been very
  hard to predict, and "it's always shorter than you think." The cycle tells you
  the *shape* of what happens, not the exact date — so treat the timing as a
  judgment call, not an output of the framework. It also says nothing about
  whether your product is good; it assumes the product question is handled
  elsewhere.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- Which step is this platform in right now — is the window still open, or is it
  already closing the value exchange?
- What's the moat the platform is gathering, and does your presence help it
  gather that moat (which buys you goodwill) or threaten it (which gets you shut
  down)?
- When this platform closes — takes a cut, builds the first-party version of your
  use case, throttles your organic reach — what's your plan? Are you building a
  moat of your own on top of it?
- You're a startup, so you can't spread your chips. Which single platform are you
  going all-in on, and why that one?
- Are you judging this channel on retention and monetization, or on signup /
  MAU vanity numbers?

## Common misuse

Building your whole growth on a platform's open phase and assuming it stays open.
Balfour lived the Facebook social-gaming cycle "both painfully and also in good
ways" and warns the close is structural, not optional — the platform *has* to
keep growing and prevent its own disruption, so "anybody who is sitting in their
shoes... is going to follow the exact same playbook." Don't read the close as the
platform turning evil; read it as the predictable last step, and have your own
moat ready before it arrives.
