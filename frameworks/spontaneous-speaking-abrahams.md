## One-liner

Structure halves the burden of spontaneous speaking — it handles the "how to say it" so your brain only needs to solve "what to say."

## The framework

Abrahams argues that most professional communication is spontaneous (Q&A, feedback requests, toasts, small talk), and that the path to getting better is combining mindset shifts with named structures you can reach for when put on the spot.

**Mindset layer**

- **Dare to be dull.** Strive for connection over perfection. When you stop judging everything you say against a standard of perfection, you free up cognitive bandwidth to actually connect and communicate well. Your brain is like a CPU — every app you have open (self-evaluation) takes resources away from the task.
- **Prepare to be spontaneous.** Counterintuitive but true: athletes and jazz musicians are being spontaneous during performance. What makes them good is everything they practiced beforehand. Communication works the same way.
- **Reframe anxiety as excitement.** The physiological arousal of anxiety and excitement is identical — heart racing, shallow breathing, slight shakiness. The label you put on it changes how you perform. Saying "I'm excited to share this" instead of "I'm nervous" causes measurable improvements in how you communicate.

**Structure layer — four named structures**

1. **PREP** (for making a point): Point → Reason → Example → Point restated. Use when someone asks your opinion or you need to make a recommendation.

2. **What–So What–Now What** (for updates and presentations): What is it → Why does it matter to this audience → What comes next. Works for product updates, standups, feedback sessions.

3. **ADD** (for Q&A answers): Answer the question cleanly → give a Detailed example → Describe the relevance. Stops you from assuming the audience will connect the dots themselves.

4. **WHAT** (for toasts and tributes): Why are we here → How are you connected to the event → Anecdote → Thank you/gratitude. Works for wedding toasts, work tributes, all-hands shout-outs.

5. **AAA** (for apologies): Acknowledge the actual behavior → Appreciate the difficulty it caused → Amends (specific, actionable next step). Key distinction: apologize for what you did, not for how the person feels.

**Anxiety-management toolkit** (supporting the mindset layer)

- Visualization: put yourself in the room before you're there, walk through getting up, speaking, stepping off — draws on 1980s desensitization research.
- Breathing: exhale twice as long as your inhale ("rule of lung"). The relaxation response lives in the exhale.
- Mantras: "I have value to bring" before speaking. Counteracts the negative self-talk that sets you up to fail.
- Distraction at the start: ask a question, show a video — anything that gets attention off you for 60 seconds while you settle in. Most speakers are most nervous in the minute before and the first minute of speaking.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** you're fielding questions in a meeting, giving feedback on a design or roadmap, asked to make a toast, running Q&A after a presentation, doing job interviews, or navigating small talk at conferences.

**Boundary:** these structures are scaffolding for spontaneous contexts. For a prepared keynote or a major board presentation, you have time to craft something far more tailored — defaulting to PREP or What–So What–Now What there is fine but undersells what's possible with prep time.

**Also limited:** the anxiety techniques are tools in a personal plan, not a universal sequence. Abrahams notes his first book had 50 techniques — you find the three or four that work for you, not all of them.

## Signature questions

*(Questions Abrahams would ask in a council context)*

1. What structure did they use? (habit-building: analyze communication before deploying it)
2. What's the one specific thing you want the audience to remember or do? (the "now what" check)
3. Are you striving for connection or for perfection right now? (the dare-to-be-dull diagnostic)
4. What's the impact on the other person — not how did it make them feel, but what specifically did you do? (apology/feedback framing)
5. Have you put yourself in the room yet, even mentally? (visualization check before high-stakes communication)

## Common misuse

Abrahams explicitly warns against over-validating in Q&A — saying "good question" to every question and ending every answer with "does that make sense?" Both are attempts to buy time or validate, but both backfire. "Good question" signals you're buying time and plays favorites; "does that make sense" repeated enough makes people wonder if you actually make sense.

The bigger structural misuse: listing and itemizing instead of structuring. Under pressure, most speakers take their audience "on the journey of their own discovery" — thinking out loud, adding items, circling back. Brains are not wired for lists (his rule of thumb: more than four items and you need to write it down). Structure isn't decoration; it's what makes the content land.
