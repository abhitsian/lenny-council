## One-liner

Build great products by deeply understanding why people hire your product, using data to root-cause your problems, designing for clarity over cleverness — and sustaining all of it with a dissatisfaction that drives you to constantly make things better.

## The framework

Robby names three chapters he would write in a book on building great products, plus a fourth coda:

**Chapter 1: Deeply Understand People**
Use the Jobs to Be Done lens — but lowercase, not the rigorous academic version. Think of users as hiring your product to get something done. Study *causation*, not just behavior. Conduct interrogative interviews that trace back to the "big hire" moment: where were they, what triggered the use, what were they trying to accomplish? This applies equally to non-use: why did someone *not* hire your product?

**Chapter 2: Analytical Rigor**
Instrument your product and get close to root causes. When something isn't working, dissect it: Is the problem in a region? A device? A demographic? A specific use case? Treat your metrics as a guide, not a dictator. They won't tell you what to do — they tell you where the disease is. You still have to find the treatment.

**Chapter 3: Design for Clarity over Cleverness**
Lean into standards and global conventions rather than reinventing them. If something is a camera icon, use the camera icon. If a door needs to tell you to push or pull, design that in. The temptation is to differentiate through design; the failure mode is that people can't figure out what to do. When you lean into what people already understand, you get compounding leverage.

**Coda: Be Humble**
Constantly question yourself. Treat product like golf — you're always one stroke away from shanking. As soon as you think you're good, you stop learning from users.

**Underlying ethos: Embodying Relentless Improvement**
The through-line across all three chapters is a deep dissatisfaction with the status quo — not unhappiness, but a driven desire to make things better. The best product people are the harshest critics of their own work. They identify the sticker on the fruit and ask: why is this here, and how do we make it better?

**On team sizing (counter-narrative):**
Stein pushes back on the "cult of lean" directly. For products requiring technological breakthroughs, small teams kept too small for too long fail to reach escape velocity. The heuristic: keep a small team until you have internal conviction (validated by people actually returning to use it days 30, 60, 90 — not just friends being polite), then invest enough to ship the best version you can.

**On S-curves and resource allocation:**
Track growth curves at the feature and product level. When you see diminishing marginal return — where adding more people stops moving the needle — that's a signal to go first-principles on the next growth driver rather than optimizing what's already plateauing.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're inheriting or growing an existing product and need to find the next inflection
- You're debating how to extend a format (new content type, new surface) into your existing product
- Your metrics are down and you don't know the real reason
- You're naming or designing something and tempted to be clever

**Doesn't apply (or needs adjustment) when:**
- You're building something in a totally new category with no existing user mental model to snap to
- Your product is primarily creative/generative and clarity conventions don't yet exist
- Team size decisions in high-capital domains (robotics, foundational models) where the resource math is categorically different from software apps

## Signature questions

1. What is someone actually trying to get done when they hire this product — and what's the emotional job underneath the functional one?
2. If I trace back the moment someone first decided to use this, where were they and what triggered it?
3. When this metric dropped, did we actually root-cause it down to device, region, demographic, or use case — or did we stop at the surface?
4. Are we differentiating through design because it actually serves users, or because we wanted to be clever?
5. Are we keeping the team small out of discipline, or are we just starving it and calling that lean?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Stein explicitly warns against is treating the three pillars as sequential phases rather than an integrated operating system. Teams that do user research once, build, then hand off to analytics miss the point — the relentlessness means you cycle back. The Close Friends example is instructive: the product failed, got stripped down, got renamed, got its design updated (green ring moved to the outside, not buried inside the story), and had its recommended list size changed — all driven by the same framework applied iteratively over two to three years. Another explicit warning: assuming a feature that works in someone else's system will work in yours. You have to translate it to your users' expectations, not just port it over.
