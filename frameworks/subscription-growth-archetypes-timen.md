## One-liner

Match your subscription product to one of three growth engines — paid, viral/referral, or SEO — based on product characteristics, then focus with objective abandon criteria instead of spreading thin or pivoting prematurely.

## The framework

**Part 1: Three growth archetypes**

Timen categorizes subscription companies into archetypes based on the product's native properties:

- **High-LTV / prosumer paid archetype**: Single-player LTVs in the hundreds of dollars (think Grammarly, Canva at their core buyer level). When free-to-paid conversion is 5%+, paid acquisition has a viable payback window. This archetype can sustain paid loops.

- **Network-effect viral archetype**: The product utility increases with more users — collaboration tools (Airtable, Whimsical, monday.com), marketplaces (Airbnb), or platforms with inherent multiplayer utility. Product network effects are either present from inception or extremely hard to manufacture. Layer referral and viral loops here. Even products without network effects can work if the brand is beloved enough (e.g., fresh dog food subscription with a give-one-get-one referral).

- **Long-tail data / programmatic SEO archetype**: Products with rich template or data angles that map to high-volume search demand. Canva's early growth was built on this — any design project type became a landing page. Requires checking: (1) unique editorial angle, (2) unique programmatic angle, (3) unique data angle. If you can check two of three, SEO is worth pursuing.

**Part 2: Focus with guard rails**

Rather than choosing between pure focus and full diversification, Timen advocates for a middle path:

1. **Pick the primary engine** that fits your archetype. At early stage, one thing likely accounts for 80%+ of growth — that's fine and expected. Don't dilute into diversification until the primary engine is a real strategic asset.

2. **Set objective abandon criteria before you start** — not after you've lost patience. For paid channels: minimum impressions, creative angles tested, click-through rate benchmarks. If results fall outside the range after a time-boxed experiment (three months is a useful heuristic for SEO), abandon without guilt and move to the next thing.

3. **Distinguish premature abandonment from legitimate failure**. The failure mode Timen keeps seeing: companies conclude a channel doesn't work when the test was never designed to have a shot. "We tried YouTube" often means: wrong creative angle, wrong budget, no benchmarks, cut early.

4. **Stage-specific diversification logic**: Early-stage companies should resist diversifying even when one channel dominates. Series A founders often want to diversify too soon — Timen talks them out of it. Late-stage companies (50M+ ARR) often have a blind spot in the other direction — 90%+ reliance on a single channel is fragile and needs proactive remediation.

**Conversion benchmarks for prosumer SaaS** (used as a quick fitness test before committing to paid):
- Website visit → free account: 20–35% is healthy at scale; 40–50% at early stage with strong PMF in a narrow segment
- Free → paid: under 5% is a long-term problem regardless of top-of-funnel volume; 7%+ is the target

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when**: You're building a self-serve subscription product (consumer or prosumer SaaS) and trying to decide where to put your first $50K–$500K of growth investment. Works best when at least one archetype clearly fits your product's structural properties.

**Doesn't apply well when**: The product is enterprise-led (no meaningful self-serve), or growth is primarily driven by direct sales cycles. The archetype model assumes consumerized growth loops are in play.

**SEO timing caveat**: Timen notes this is shifting — previously SEO was a Series B+ luxury given its 6–12 month payback. In a capital-constrained environment, Series A companies are now looking at SEO earlier because they can't afford short-payback-window paid strategies.

## Signature questions

1. Does the product utility increase as more users join, or is it fundamentally single-player? (The answer determines whether viral/referral loops are worth chasing.)
2. What are your free-to-paid conversion rates? If it's under 5%, is paid acquisition even viable right now, or do you need to fix the funnel first?
3. When you say you "tried" a channel — what exactly did you try, and what were the objective criteria for success?
4. Are you diversifying because the primary engine is genuinely maxed out, or because it makes you nervous to rely on one thing? (These require very different responses.)
5. Do you have a unique editorial, programmatic, or data angle that no one else can replicate — or would you be competing with established players on generic SEO?

## Common misuse

The most explicit failure mode Timen calls out: concluding a channel doesn't work based on a test that was never designed to work. Teams under-resource an experiment, skip proper creative testing, run below the minimum viable impression volume, and then file it as "tried and failed" — foreclosing on a potentially valid channel. The fix is setting success criteria and minimum effort thresholds *before* starting, not deciding in retrospect whether the test was good enough.

A second misuse: treating the "focus" principle as permanent. Timen is clear that 80/20 reliance on one channel is the right early-stage posture, not a destination. Late-stage companies that never diversified have created strategic fragility — a Grammarly-style over-reliance on performance marketing is a risk, not a badge of honor.
