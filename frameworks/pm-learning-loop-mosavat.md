## One-liner

PM development is a repeating loop of Execute → Generalize → Communicate → Scale; the only way to go faster is to complete more real loops on real products, not to substitute training for them.

## The framework

Fareed describes a four-stage career acceleration loop. The stages are his exact language:

**1. Execute**
Do real work on a real product with real customers and real data. This is the unavoidable foundation. At junior levels, the problem and solution are both known — your job is delivery. As you level up, more unknowns are introduced.

**2. Generalize**
After each execution cycle, extract transferable lessons. Not "I know what the best settings page for Slack looks like" — but "for high-bar-to-activation products, good friction during onboarding can increase retention." Reading, courses, and conversations with experts are useful here as inputs, but they cannot replace the execution that gives the generalization meaning.

**3. Communicate**
No one knows what you're learning if you don't tell them. Communication is what converts personal learning into organizational trust. It also surfaces you for sponsorship — people who will hand you a bigger problem.

**4. Scale**
Move from known-problem/known-solution work to increasing unknowns: known problem/unknown solution, then unknown problem/unknown solution. Scaling also means moving from doer to editor — making the work of others better rather than owning every deliverable yourself.

**The Four Types of Product Work** (developed with Casey Winters at Reforge):

| Type | What it is |
|------|-----------|
| **Feature work** | Adding capabilities to drive engagement with the existing customer base |
| **Growth work** | Helping customers connect with existing experiences; driving top-line metrics |
| **PMF expansion** | New audience via same product (internationalization, new vertical) or new product to same audience (bundling) |
| **Scaling work** | Work created by growth itself — technical scaling, trust/safety, user-at-scale problems |

The leadership trap: specialists become leaders and apply only their one type to every problem. The growth leader who turns everything into an experiment. The solution is building a portfolio across all four types and knowing which you're weaker in.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A PM is trying to understand why their career isn't accelerating despite doing good work
- A first-time manager is falling into the pattern of keeping the most important work for themselves
- A product leader needs to diagnose whether their team's roadmap is over-indexed on one work type
- Someone is transitioning from IC to manager and wondering why the skills that got them there aren't working

**Does not apply when:**
- The bottleneck is breaking into PM entirely (Fareed acknowledges that is a separate problem, not covered by this loop)
- The organization itself has no real sponsorship culture — the loop requires someone external to hand you a bigger opportunity, which requires that person to exist
- You're evaluating a completely new product category at zero-to-one stage, where the four types map differently

## Signature questions

1. "What have you actually shipped in the last 90 days, and what did you generalize from it — not just what happened, but what rule did you take away that you'd apply to a different product?"
2. "Who in the organization knows that you know what you know? Have you communicated it, or are you just doing the work quietly?"
3. "Is your manager aware of your top three priorities right now? Do you know their top three? Do you know their boss's?"
4. "When you think about your current portfolio of work — what percentage is feature, growth, PMF expansion, and scaling? Is that the right mix for where the company is?"
5. "Are you solving the problem with the resources you have, or are you owning the outcome and making the case for what resources you actually need?"

## Common misuse

The loop gets short-circuited at the Generalize step. PMs execute well but treat each project as its own island — they don't step back to extract transferable lessons. Without generalization, communication has nothing to communicate, and the Scale step never arrives because leadership has no evidence that the PM can think beyond their current domain.

A second failure Fareed explicitly warns against: conflating sponsorship with mentorship. A mentor improves your day-to-day work. A sponsor hands you a bigger problem and trusts you publicly. The loop requires sponsors, not just mentors. Optimizing for mentorship while neglecting to communicate work visibly means you may get better at the job without ever being given a harder one.
