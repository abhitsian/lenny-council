## One-liner

The right growth advisor compounds years of experimentation into seconds of insight — structure the relationship for impact, not activity, with equity alignment and a three-month cliff to keep incentives honest on both sides.

## The framework

Luc treats the growth advisor relationship as a structured investment, not a retainer. He organizes it around three principles:

**1. Impact over activity**
The only measure of a growth advisor's value is outcome, not effort. Luc learned this directly at Facebook, where Zuckerberg's first question after Luc's strategy presentation was, "Now when are we going to start seeing results?" Impact is the north star — both for advisors and for internal growth teams. Teams should be incentivized on outcomes, not on the volume of work shipped.

**2. Equity alignment with a three-month cliff**
Luc exclusively takes equity (not cash) as an advisor because it puts both parties on the same side of the table. He structures the equity with a three-month cliff: if it isn't working for either side within three months, both parties tear up the deal and walk away. This removes the incentive to hoard knowledge and pushes advisors to share everything fast. The vesting schedule should be front-loaded — commensurate with value, which is heaviest early. He also insists on a long tail (10+ years) because exits take time.

**3. The 10X growth advisor thesis**
Just as the best engineers are 10X more productive than average, the right growth advisor is company-changing — not because they work harder, but because they've seen thousands of experiments and can recognize the needle in the haystack in minutes. The knowledge takes years to acquire; communicating it takes seconds. One day-one observation at a public company became a "large impact" change three weeks later. That asymmetry is the whole point.

**Hiring playbook (finding signs of excellence)**
When hiring growth talent or vetting advisors, Luc looks for repeated signs of excellence — not one moment, but a pattern. Signs include: recurring success across contexts, a former boss who left and came back to poach them, or demonstrable achievements outside of work. He stack-ranks sourcing: internal hire first, surrounded by advisors; agencies are a last resort.

**SEO two-bucket model**
Luc divides companies into two SEO buckets: (1) small, static-page sites that need a content strategy to generate traffic, and (2) large, auto-generating sites (UGC, marketplaces) with massive optimization surfaces. The strategic insight: a single keyword can define an entire industry. Getting rank #1 on a high-intent keyword is not a marginal gain — it can be the company.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have product-market fit and are ready to pour fuel on growth.
- You need to compress years of channel learning into months.
- You want to hire or vet growth talent and don't have the pattern-recognition to do it yourself.
- You're structuring an advisorship and want to avoid misaligned incentives.

**Does not apply when:**
- You don't have product-market fit yet. Growing a product that doesn't work exposes it to the market before it's ready — users who have a bad experience rarely return.
- You're looking for a pure executor. An advisor's job is to share knowledge fast and train the internal team; they should eventually make themselves unnecessary.

## Signature questions

1. What's the impact? (Not: what did you build, what did you try — what moved the needle?)
2. Have you seen repeated signs of excellence in this person across multiple contexts, or just one?
3. Is this advisor being paid to deliver impact and then leave, or are they structurally incentivized to stay needed?
4. In your SEO footprint, are you a small-page site or a large auto-generating site — and does your strategy reflect that distinction?
5. Are you growing before you have product-market fit? If so, what damage are you doing to your brand with users who leave and never come back?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Luc explicitly warns against is structuring the advisorship so the advisor becomes permanently necessary — kept on "as insurance." A good advisor should be incentivized to share everything fast and train the team so that the company no longer needs them. If you're dependent on the advisor indefinitely, the structure is wrong. The second misuse: hiring for public halo (large Twitter following, conference talks) instead of vetting for past performance in high-rep environments. The best growth people don't have time to tweet — they're running experiments.
