## One-liner

Before you build anything, make four explicit choices — your customers, your competition, your validation source, and your distribution channel — because each one locks in everything else about your product and your future.

## The framework

Seth lays out four choices that product people routinely gloss over, treating them as a given rather than an active decision:

**1. Choose your customers.**
Picking your smallest viable audience is not a constraint — it is the entire product brief. When you name who they are (their language, budget, problem, technical facility, temperament), you have already decided what the product needs to do and what your working life will feel like. Choosing everyone is choosing no one.

**2. Choose your competition.**
Deciding which space you operate in defines the rules you play by. If you compete against Walmart, you have implicitly agreed to a price race. The competitive frame sets the boundaries of what moves are even available to you.

**3. Choose your validation source.**
Who are you trying to please? If the answer is your boss, every meeting will be about their taste. If you can name the 400 customers you serve and get agreement that they are the arbiter, every product review becomes simpler and more honest. This choice determines whose feedback has standing.

**4. Choose your distribution.**
Distribution is not a downstream sales problem — it shapes the product itself. A game built for mass retail (Target, RadioShack) is a different product from one built for Steam or downloadable shareware. When distribution shifts, everything about the product must shift too. If you have not chosen this, you have not chosen your product.

Seth pairs this with a theory of **tension**: when you make a promise to a customer, you create productive tension — the gap between what they imagined and whether you delivered. That tension is not stress; it is the engine of brand-building. Every brand is a promise, and loyalty is whether someone would pay extra to stick with you, not whether they accumulate points.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are at the founding or early-stage decision point, before the roadmap is set
- You are re-examining a stalled product and want to find the root cause
- You are making a go-to-market or distribution decision and feel like you have to do everything at once
- Your team is arguing about what to build and no one has named who they are building for

**Does not apply when:**
- The four choices are genuinely locked in by contract or platform (e.g., you are a white-label vendor with a named enterprise customer) — in that case, the choices were made for you and the work is execution inside those constraints
- You are iterating within a well-validated market; the framework is most powerful at the strategy-setting layer, not the feature-level layer

## Signature questions

1. Who specifically is this for — and who is it explicitly not for? Can you name ten people who fit the profile?
2. Which competitor are you benchmarked against, and have you accepted the rules that come with that comparison?
3. When your team disagrees about the product, whose judgment are you using as the tiebreaker — your boss, your customer, or your own taste?
4. If your distribution channel disappeared tomorrow, would the product still make sense? What does that tell you about how deeply you've coupled the product to a specific channel?
5. What promise are you making to the customer? Is that promise difficult enough to create real tension — and honest enough to actually keep?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Seth names explicitly: treating the four choices as background assumptions rather than active decisions. Most product people behave as if their customer is "anyone," their competition is "the market," their validation is their boss's approval, and their distribution is "whatever works." That is not a strategy — it is an avoidance of strategy. The choices feel limiting but are actually liberating: once you make them, every subsequent product and roadmap decision has a clear arbiter. The other misuse he flags is confusing buzz with traction. Jaguar's rebrand, the Cybertruck, IHOP's burger stunt — all generated attention without generating customer traction. A remarkable product is one people tell their friends about because doing so improves their own life, not because it is loud.
