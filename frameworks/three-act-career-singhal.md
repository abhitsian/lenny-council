## One-liner

Plan your career in three acts across a 60-year horizon — and never let catching the rabbit leave you with nowhere to run.

## The framework

Nikhyl structures a career as three distinct acts, each with different goals and the threat of stalling out if you don't plan what comes next.

**Act One — Learn and build.** Early career. You pick up craft, accumulate diverse experiences (pre-PMF, scaling, B2B, consumer), and choose one ambiguity to master: crafter, market expert, organizational expert, domain expert, or team builder. The job is to become world-class at something and assemble a story worth telling.

**Act Two — Build the factory.** Mid-career. You manage, scale, and execute. You're no longer building the car — you're building the system that builds cars. Promotion, influence, and organizational leverage are the currency here.

**Act Three — Give back, at scale.** Late career — and it can last 30 years. Most people never design this act deliberately. They catch the rabbit (VP, company exit, wealth target), lose their North Star, and keep working out of habit while getting sadder. Designing act three in advance — around giving, not accumulating — is what prevents the greyhound trap.

**The Skip concept** runs through all three acts. Always think not about the next job but the one after it — your boss's boss's role, the career after the one you're chasing. Plan backwards from that end state, then ask what the current job is in service of. Promotion is a company system; career is your system.

**Shadows of superpowers** is the act-two unlock. Every strength that got you to a senior role casts a shadow at the next level. A brilliant collaborator becomes conflict-avoidant. A decisive entrepreneur becomes unable to incorporate peer input. A great storyteller loses grip on details. The development area that's blocking you is almost always a shadow of what made you successful — and it's the last thing you'll believe is a problem.

**Promotion blockers (four root causes):**
1. No advocate who sees your magic
2. The next role doesn't exist (especially common in post-hypergrowth layoffs)
3. Impatience — leadership promotions are slower by design
4. An unaddressed development area the individual refuses to hear

## When it applies / when it doesn't

Applies to any knowledge-work career where long-term arc matters more than the next move. Especially relevant for PMs and product leaders hitting act two plateaus or approaching a significant milestone (company exit, CPO role, big financial outcome).

Less useful as a tactical guide for someone just starting out who needs to pick a lane first before thinking about long-horizon arcs. If you're at the beginning of act one, the skip concept is the one thing to take from here; the rest is more relevant in mid-career.

Also less directly useful for someone navigating an acute short-term crisis (layoff, PIP, broken team) — the framework is a design lens, not a triage tool.

## Signature questions

1. If you were to leave this role in 18 months, what is the one paragraph you want to be able to write about what you solved and what skill you built? Does the work you're doing today earn that paragraph?
2. What job comes after the next job you're targeting — and is what you're doing now in service of that, or just the obvious next step?
3. You're telling me you aren't being promoted. Which of these four things is actually true: no advocate, no role, impatience, or a development area you haven't faced yet?
4. What feedback have you received more than once that you've dismissed? Why did you discard it — and what if you were actually wrong to do so?
5. You're 30 years into a 60-year career. What is act three for you — and have you designed it deliberately, or are you just going to let it happen?

## Common misuse

The most common misuse is treating the framework as a promotion playbook. Nikhyl is explicit: "promotion is our system at this company to see you moving forward" — it's a company mechanism. Career is your system. People who come to him for "career advice" and immediately ask what they need to do to get promoted in 18 months instead of 24 are collapsing the two. When the answer to "what's the promotion in service of?" is "so I can leave," the whole construct falls apart.

A second misuse: focusing on the skip as a job-hopping rationale. Thinking about the job after next is meant to make you more deliberate about staying and learning, not to justify lateral moves. Many short-term moves look like progress and are "by definition not forward moves."

The third: treating shadows of superpowers as a performance review exercise. The insight is structural — your strength and your blind spot are the same thing. The failure mode Nikhyl warns against is dismissing contradictory feedback as "anomalies" because your manager or your own track record seems to disprove it. The discarded pile is exactly where to look.
