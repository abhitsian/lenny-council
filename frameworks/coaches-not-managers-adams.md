## One-liner

Replace traditional management with specialty-based peer coaches who track individual growth, and build a culture where people proactively hand off ownership as their scope multiplies — so the team scales faster than any individual could alone.

## The framework

**Giving Away Your Legos** — the core mechanic:
As the company scales, each person's job grows in complexity faster than one person can handle it. The person who wrote all the emails at 1M users cannot write all the emails at 100M users across 100 languages. Growth requires identifying the moments where you must hand off work you love, bring in others, build systems, and find your joy in multiplying the impact of those around you rather than in personal execution.

**Coaches Not Managers** — the structural enabler:
Canva does not have traditional managers. Instead, every employee has a **coach** — a peer who shares their specialty (engineering, product, design, etc.) and:
- Tracks the individual's skill development in that craft
- Identifies pivot points when it's time to move to the next level of scope
- Works one-on-one through sessions, helps with strategy docs, and provides ongoing check-ins
- Is drawn from the broader employee base — at 4,500 employees, Canva has ~800–1,000 coaches

A separate **360 feedback circle** from day-to-day collaborators handles performance inputs. The coach handles growth.

A small group (~5) of professional-only coaches handle special situations, but the system is built on developing coaching skills broadly across the company.

**Opportunity as the accelerant:**
Talking about growth without giving people the chance to grow is ineffective. Coaches identify moments to push someone beyond their comfort zone — handing them ownership of a new idea, a new feature, or a new team. The push plus the safety of a coach who knows the craft is what makes the system work.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- The company is scaling fast enough that job scope is doubling every 12–24 months
- The craft of individual specialties (design, PM, engineering) is central to what makes the company good
- You have enough tenured people in each specialty to serve as coaches — requires depth before breadth
- You want organic, trust-driven growth over imported-expertise growth

**Does not apply when:**
- The company is too early-stage for specialty depth (first 10–20 people)
- Roles are highly generalist and specialty-coaching is not meaningful
- Performance management needs to be separated from coaching — in this model they are partially coupled, which requires maturity to not conflate the two

## Signature questions

1. When is the last time you handed something off that you loved doing? What did that free you up to do instead?
2. Who is the coach for this person — someone with the same specialty who can actually judge their craft? If there isn't one, what does that tell us about the team?
3. Is this person being asked to grow or just being told to grow? What's the actual opportunity we've handed them?
4. At the scale we're heading toward, what does this role look like in two years — and does the person in it today have a path to that version, or are we hoping they figure it out?
5. Are leaders here finding joy in multiplying others' impact, or are they still hoarding the best work for themselves?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Cam warns against is **telling people to grow without actually handing them the opportunity.** Saying "please grow" or writing "growth mindset" into a review cycle does nothing. The mechanism is ownership transfer — a real piece of work, a new team, a new feature — paired with a coach who can navigate that transition with them. Without the opportunity, the coaching is just performance theater.

A second failure mode is importing leaders who come in with "preconceived notions" and try to change how things work before understanding why they work. Cam's explicit advice: listen for a couple of months before you try to change anything — and the coaching model has to be internalized, not just explained.
