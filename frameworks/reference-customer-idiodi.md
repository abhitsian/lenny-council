## One-liner

Find 6–8 reference customers (B2B) or 15–25 (B2C) who will put their reputation on the line for your product — that is the clearest signal you have hit product-market fit and the fastest path to it.

## The framework

Idiodi breaks the work into two parallel tracks that run simultaneously:

1. **Discover who has the problem.** Get out of the building. Find people who genuinely have the problem — not via survey, not via user interview, but by immersing yourself in their environment until you can feel the problem yourself.

2. **Discover and deliver the solution alongside them.** Don't prototype in isolation. Work with your target customers hands-on, iterating until something real and useful emerges. The designer and engineer are in the room from day one — immersion replaces requirements docs.

3. **Gate on the reputation test.** The indicator of product-market fit is not NPS, not a usability score, not "they said they loved it." It is: will this person stake their reputation on recommending your product to someone else? If they hesitate to leave a public 5-star review or a reference call, you have not solved the problem well enough yet.

4. **Hit the number.** B2B: 6–8 validated reference customers. B2C: 15–25. These numbers come from adoption-curve research (IBM's supercomputer sales data, App Store review patterns). The numbers exist because any smaller cohort could be an anomaly; hitting them means you have found a real market segment.

5. **On day one of launch, the reviews are already there.** Idiodi will not submit an app to the App Store until 25 people have already agreed to post their 5-star review. The launch is a distribution event, not a discovery event.

The output is not just validated product. It is word-of-mouth primed to work, marketing copy that comes verbatim from real customers, and a sales motion that is trivially easy because reference customers close other reference customers.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** building something new — a new product line, a new market entry, anything where you are genuinely uncertain whether the problem is real and whether your solution addresses it. Also applies when restarting a struggling product.

**Does not replace other discovery methods, but subsumes them.** The immersion work naturally surfaces usability issues, feasibility constraints, and pricing signals without needing separate research sprints.

**Does not apply when:** iterating on an existing product where you already have a stable reference base and the question is incremental improvement, not market validation. And it only works if the PM is willing to do genuinely unscalable work — driving to job sites, recruiting people by hand, calling no-shows. Teams looking for a lightweight method will resist this.

## Signature questions

1. Can you find 6–8 people (B2B) or 15–25 people (B2C) who are willing to put their reputation on the line for this product? If you can't find them, ask whether the problem is real enough to solve.
2. Are you immersed in the environment of the problem, or are you still in the building running interviews?
3. When you ask a customer to leave a public reference or a 5-star review, what is their hesitation? That hesitation is your next product problem.
4. Is the marketing copy you're writing coming from what your reference customers actually said, or from what your team thinks sounds good?
5. Have you validated that all your reference customers want the same core thing — or are you building a different product for each of them?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Idiodi explicitly warns against: stopping at "they said they love it." People will say almost anything to be polite or to get you out of their face. The reputation test is the forcing function — it surfaces the real gap between what customers say and what they will actually do. Teams that skip this step and launch on verbal enthusiasm end up with a product that looks validated but has no word-of-mouth engine.

A second failure mode: cherry-picking a single enthusiastic customer and treating that as validation. The numbers (6–8, 15–25) exist precisely to prevent this. One or two early adopters might have an idiosyncratic problem nobody else shares. The cohort requirement forces you to confirm you have found a real segment.
