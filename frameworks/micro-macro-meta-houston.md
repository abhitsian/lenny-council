## One-liner

Founder decision-making operates at three simultaneous levels — the tactical mechanics of execution (micro), the strategic business model and market positioning (macro), and the shifting systemic cycles of markets and how business gets done (meta) — and falling behind on any one level will undermine the others.

## The framework

Drew draws the structure explicitly from competitive StarCraft, then maps it onto running a company.

**Micro** — the day-to-day mechanics of execution. Product design decisions, engineering choices, distribution tactics, how the viral loop works, how a sales motion functions. Early founders spend nearly all their time here because there's no one else. The question at this level is: can you execute the craft?

**Macro** — the strategic layer above the mechanics. Business model, market positioning, competitive dynamics, financial sustainability, how the category evolves from growth to maturity. The questions at this level are: what markets are we in, how do we win, who are we differentiating from, how does the organizational structure need to change as we grow?

**Meta** — the game itself is changing. Business cycles (boom/bust), permanent shifts in how markets communicate and operate, new distribution channels displacing old ones. Drew's example: Dropbox launched Dash with almost no coverage from conventional tech press, but direct social and podcast distribution massively outperformed. The meta had shifted. The relevant question is: what about how business is fundamentally done has permanently changed, not just cyclically shifted?

The hard part, in Drew's framing, is that you cannot pick two and ignore one. The founder who is only micro-focused builds a technically excellent product that gets strategically outmaneuvered. The founder who only thinks macro never ships. And neither survives a meta shift they didn't see coming.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** a founder or CEO is trying to diagnose why something that used to work has stopped working, or when they feel like they're working hard but not making progress. The framework surfaces which layer the problem actually lives on. Also useful when reviewing a company's strategic priorities — it separates "are we good at the craft" from "are we playing the right game."

**Applies less when:** you're pre-product-market-fit and operating with a team of two or three. At that stage, almost everything is micro, and adding meta analysis creates noise. The framework is most useful once a company has survived its first era and is navigating the transition from "how do we build this" to "how do we build the right thing in a changing market."

## Signature questions

1. Which level is actually broken right now — are we executing poorly, positioned poorly, or just not seeing how the game is changing?
2. When did we last zoom out to the meta layer? What assumptions about how this market operates are we carrying from three years ago?
3. Is our personal growth curve keeping ahead of the company's growth curve — and if not, which layer of the game are we behind on?
4. Are we confusing a cyclical shift (business cycle) with a structural one (permanent change to how the game is played)?
5. Think about what you'll need to know a year from now, two years, five years — are those three lists different, and are you working on any of them today?

## Common misuse

Drew explicitly warns against treating the three levels as a hierarchy where meta > macro > micro. The failure mode is a founder who reads widely, thinks philosophically about market cycles, and completely loses touch with whether the product actually works. The framework is a simultaneous operating system, not a progression. Drew also warns against confusing a cyclical shift (the SaaS boom/bust) with a structural meta shift (the permanent change in how products get distributed). Cyclical shifts require riding out; structural shifts require changing how you play the game entirely.

A secondary failure mode Drew describes from his own experience: spending too long on the treadmill of micro execution without ever surfacing to macro or meta, until the company is strategically out of position and it's painful to correct.
