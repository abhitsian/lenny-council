## One-liner

Re-derive every product decision from today's actual building blocks rather than inheriting the path-dependence of existing solutions, then play a 100-year positional game where you never pull the monetization levers you're patiently building.

## The framework

Tobi describes two integrated disciplines:

**1. First Principles Derivation**
- Every solution you encounter is "path-dependent, highly, highly, highly path-dependent, and often path-dependent based on having to make compromises, based on things that were true at the time a decision was made but are no longer true."
- The exercise: given every fundamental building block available *right now*, how would you solve this from scratch? This includes knowing what Goodhart's Law / overfitting has done to existing solutions — the metric-as-goal corrupts the original intent.
- Re-run the derivation periodically. At COVID's start, one Boolean flipped (are people allowed to leave a house?), which invalidated an entire branch of Shopify's location strategy. The full decision tree re-computed to a different landing zone.
- Skipping the derivation and copying what everyone else does is an "abdication of product leadership." Sometimes you re-derive and land at the same answer as the consensus — that means the consensus encoded more wisdom than you expected. That outcome is fine and worth discovering.

**2. Positional vs. Tactical Game (Infinite Game)**
- Business strategy has two largely independent layers: the positional game (territory, trust, role, what the product *means* to customers) and the tactical game (A/B tests, conversion optimization, pricing moves).
- Most companies over-index on tactics. Tactics are finite games within the infinite game. You need enough tactical competence to not die, but the sum of all future tactical value stays available only if your positional game is sound.
- On a long enough timeline, the correct play is always to coordinate, not defect. Tobi frames the Shopify/Stripe partnership as iterated prisoner's dilemma: every turn you can defect (extract short-term value) or coordinate. The CEO job is to keep adding monetization levers to the room — and then not pull them.
- 100-year time horizon: at 100 years you can't talk about a software feature, only about the mission. Long time horizons make defection obviously suboptimal and align the founder's incentives with customers' success.

**3. Maximizing Human Potential**
- The same logic applies to people: every person is far below their actual potential. The product leader's job is to remind people of that potential and hold them to it — "reminding people of their own potential constantly is actually a wonderful thing to do."
- Products that eliminate unnecessary complexity (taxes, payments, fraud) don't just make UX nicer — they literally cause more businesses to exist, because entrepreneurs encountering confusion on the wrong day close the browser and quit.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You're pitching or evaluating a new product direction and the proposal is "a good version of what already exists"
- You're making a foundational decision (remote vs. in-person, major architectural choice, strategic partnership) and some of the underlying assumptions may have shifted
- You're deciding how much to optimize for quantifiable metrics vs. qualitative signals like delight, taste, and passion

**Does not apply as stated when:**
- Speed is the only constraint and you need a shipped increment, not a derived-from-scratch solution
- The competitive environment changes faster than the re-derivation cycle (tactical response required)
- The organization hasn't built enough positional equity to afford deferring monetization levers — "Don't die" is objective number one

## Signature questions

1. What decisions does this solution inherit from constraints that no longer exist? What would we build if we started today?
2. Is this proposal doing something better than what already exists — or just a good version of the same thing? (Those are not the same ask.)
3. Are we optimizing the quantifiable 20% of the value space and leaving the unquantifiable 80% on the table?
4. Is this a positional move or a tactical move? Are we extracting the trust we've built, or expanding it?
5. If this is an iterated game we're playing with our customers over 100 years, is this the move that makes sense — or are we pulling a marshmallow?

## Common misuse

The owner explicitly warns against two failure modes:

**Skipping the derivation.** Doing the same thing as everyone else without re-deriving it is not humility or pragmatism — it's an abdication of product leadership. The exercise isn't about always arriving at a different answer; sometimes you re-derive and confirm the consensus. The point is doing the work.

**Overfitting to the quantifiable.** Goodhart's Law applies internally: the moment a metric becomes a goal, it stops being a useful metric. Companies that build OKR/metric culture end up unable to defer to unquantifiable quality signals (taste, delight, passion) even when those signals are the better proxy for long-term value. Shopify deliberately avoids OKRs specifically to preserve the organizational muscle of acting on the unquantifiable.
