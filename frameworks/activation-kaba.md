## One-liner

Durable growth comes from getting people to the product's first aha moment and into a
habit, only counting acquisition that actually retains, and constantly moving the product
toward the "adjacent user" — the next person who could be using it but for whom it doesn't
quite work yet.

## The framework

1. **Find the aha moment, then build the habit.** Most of the opportunity sits in the
   onboarding-to-habit-building stretch: what it takes for someone to understand the value
   for the first time, and then how they build a routine around it. Kaba's blunt observation
   is that a shocking number of teams can't say what that first moment even is for their
   product.
2. **Judge acquisition by whether it retains.** Top-of-funnel matters, but volume you can't
   keep is worthless — "if you're bringing a lot of people but they're not staying around,
   then you just have a leaky bucket." The first one-to-three months of experience is what
   you're actually optimizing.
3. **Know your current user, then name the next (adjacent) user.** In a fast-growing
   product, understand who uses it today and why — and then who is the *next* user who could
   use it but for whom it doesn't work yet. They're often less tech-savvy, closer to the late
   majority, in a new market, on different phones.
4. **Watch for the signal in the data: cohort curves declining.** When new signups three or
   six months out are "doing a worse job" even though nothing in the product changed, that's
   the adjacent user arriving. Conversion that quietly drops 15% after breaking into a new
   market is the same signal, not a bug.
5. **Go be the adjacent user.** Dog-food the product in its adjacent-user *state* (e.g.,
   create a brand-new account, not your power-user account). Watch them, talk to them, visit
   them, see what they do in real time — because the product a power user sees is loaded with
   history and informed knowledge a new user doesn't have.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** hypergrowth or fast-growing consumer products where the user base is turning
  over quarter to quarter (Kaba's lens is Instagram going 440M → 636M in a year). The
  adjacent-user idea is sharpest when you're "growing at some really strong pace, 30, 40, 50%
  or more per year" and breaking into new markets or segments.
- **Doesn't (cleanly):** the "next user from non-existing users" framing assumes you still
  have a big circle of people just outside your current base. For a mature or saturated
  product the adjacent user may be harder to define, and the activation work is the part that
  generalizes more than the specific cohort-curve signal. (Stated as a boundary, not
  something Kaba walks through on the show — keep this claim soft.)

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- What is the first aha moment for this product, exactly — and can you point to where in the
  flow it happens? (If the team can't name it, that's the work.)
- Of the people you're acquiring, how many are still here at month three? Are you growing the
  top of the funnel into a leaky bucket?
- Who is your adjacent user — the next person who could use this but for whom it doesn't work
  yet? How are they different from the user you built for?
- Are your cohort curves declining for new signups even though nothing in the product
  changed? What does that tell you about who's arriving now?
- When did you last use this product *as* the adjacent user — a fresh account, their device,
  their context — rather than as the power user you are?

## Common misuse

Building and testing the product as yourself — the power user with years of history and
informed knowledge — and assuming the new user sees what you see. Kaba warns the experience
for a fresh account is "completely different and significantly worse." The fix is to put
yourself in the adjacent user's state and watch them directly, rather than trusting your own
fluent experience as representative.
