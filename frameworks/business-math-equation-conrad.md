## One-liner

Every company is a mathematical equation that describes how you turn investment into returns — if the equation is fundamentally broken, no amount of product craft or iteration can fix the output.

## The framework

Conrad distinguishes two layers of any product business:

1. **The product layer** — the features, the UX, the craft, the "art form." This is where most product thinkers live. It matters.

2. **The business equation** — the higher-order model that describes LTV, CAC, content costs, licensing constraints, cost structure, and distribution assumptions. The equation has variables, and the product layer influences some of them, but not all.

The framework asks: **before you optimize leaf nodes, have you modeled the whole equation to find the high-leverage points?**

Conrad's practice at Zero: build a spreadsheet that aggregates business intelligence data, subscription transactions, historical results, and expenses, then use it to model future scenarios by manipulating variables. This surfaces which dimensions have real leverage versus which feel important but don't move the needle at the equation level.

The failure mode he names explicitly: going straight to leaf-node optimization (install-to-registered conversion, trial rate) without first establishing whether the top-level equation can close at all.

**Quibi's broken equation:** the math required a bespoke content library expensive enough to drive subscription and retention, at scale, for roughly $2B. Conrad's conclusion was that the library needed to be 3–5x bigger, meaning the real cost was $6–10B. No product iteration could make $2B of content feel like $10B of content.

**Pandora's licensing constraint:** the statutory licensing regime the company operated under was structurally incompatible with the direct-deals model the labels preferred and Spotify used. That was an equation variable that product execution could not override.

**Pets.com's arms-race dynamic:** three overfunded competitors in the same category, each forced to match irrational marketing spend. Excess capital became the albatross — it drove decisions the equation couldn't support.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are evaluating whether to join, fund, or double-down on a company
- You are a product leader who has been optimizing funnels without a model of the whole business
- A product is iterating hard but results are not compounding — the equation may be structurally broken
- Cost structure has hard constraints outside the product team's control (licensing, content, regulatory)

**Does not replace product craft:** Conrad is explicit that he still sees software as an art form and still enjoys getting into the details. The equation layer sits above the product layer; it does not substitute for it. A sound equation with a bad product still fails.

## Signature questions

1. If you manipulate the key variables in your business model, what does the output look like in 24 months — and does it close?
2. Which variables in your equation are actually under your control as a product team, and which are structural constraints?
3. Have you identified the highest-leverage point in the equation, or are you optimizing leaf nodes?
4. What distribution or acquisition assumption does your model require on day one — and what is the honest probability of hitting it?
5. Is there a capital or cost floor in your model that, if wrong by 3x, breaks the entire thesis?

## Common misuse

The failure Conrad warns against is treating the equation as a post-hoc rationalization tool — running the model only after you are already committed, then adjusting assumptions to make it close. The point is to build the model *before* you scale so you can see which variables must be true for the business to work, then honestly assess how probable each one is.

He also flags a related mistake he made at Quibi: knowing that a key assumption (landing in the top 10 app store on day one and staying there) was "highly improbable" but not "structurally impossible" — and letting that ambiguity quiet the concern rather than surfacing it loudly to leadership. The equation flags the risk; the product leader's job is to beat the drum on assumptions that are improbable even when they are not zero.
