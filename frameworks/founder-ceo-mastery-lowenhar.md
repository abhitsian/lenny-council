## One-liner

Founder is a state of being — grit, courage, instinct. CEO is a craft — hiring, planning, go-to-market, financial management. Most founders only develop one and wonder why the company stalls.

## The framework

Lowenhar separates the startup journey into two distinct phases, each demanding a different skill set:

**Phase 1 — Build something people want to buy.** This is where founder qualities dominate: tenacity, courage, insight, pattern recognition, willingness to operate at any level of the business.

**Phase 2 — Build a company around the thing people want to buy.** This is where CEO craft takes over. The craft skills are the same regardless of industry (MedTech, fintech, consumer): hiring great people, financial planning, building a repeatable go-to-market, running effective meetings, and working backwards from a clear exit destination.

**CEO Failure Modes (9 archetypes):** Robot (rejects emotion), Pleaser (avoids hard decisions), Perfectionist (waits for certainty that doesn't exist), Angry (fear-based reactions), Laissez-faire (believes great people self-organize without management), Ready-Fire-Aim (improvisational, allergic to planning), Micromanager (can't trust adults), Brake-Rider (won't spend money), Accelerator-Rider (burns money without a plan).

**The CEO's three core jobs:**
1. Make sure everyone knows where the company is going.
2. Pick the right people for the team.
3. Give those people the tools they need to win.

**Working backwards from one of four destinations:** Every company is working backwards from one of: (a) exit, (b) next fundraise, (c) profitability, or (d) wind-down. Founders who skip naming which one end up with disconnected planning.

**4-Bucket Go-To-Market:** ICP (who to sell to, with kill criteria), Positioning/Marketing (uncommon differentiator vs. competitors and status quo), Demand Gen (which channels, tested via experimentation not availability bias), Sales (playbook for discovery, objection handling, demo, close).

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** A founder has product-market signal and is moving from "building the thing" to "building the company." Typically Series A and beyond. The founder who says "I can see a gap between the CEO I am and the one my company needs me to be" is the right candidate.

**Does not apply when:** The company is pre-product or purely at the idea stage. Lowenhar explicitly says: "If you have an idea on a napkin or a science experiment, we're useless."

It also doesn't apply as a permission structure to skip craft development — Lowenhar explicitly rejects the "founder mode" framing as an excuse to avoid learning CEO skills.

## Signature questions

1. Which phase are you in — still building the product, or building the company around it? Do you know the difference for your specific company right now?
2. Of the nine CEO failure modes, which two show up most often in how you run the business? What's the cost of that to the team?
3. What are you working backwards from — exit, fundraise, profitability, or wind-down? Can you quantify what has to be true to get there?
4. When you get really still and quiet, what does the little voice say about the decision you're avoiding? How long have you been not listening to it?
5. For your next hire, what does success look like 12 months from now — not the job description, but what changes about the business?

## Common misuse

The most common misuse is treating founder instincts as a substitute for CEO craft — which is exactly what "founder mode" gave founders permission to do. Lowenhar's warning: the ready-fire-aim pattern that got the company started will get the company bankrupt if it isn't balanced with planning, accountability rituals, and resource allocation tied to clear milestones.

A secondary misuse: conflating intuition with reaction. Lowenhar distinguishes gut (quiet, well-resourced, comes from deep stillness) from fear response (the lizard brain that fires in existential moments). Trusting intuition is not the same as trusting impulse.
