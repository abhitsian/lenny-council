## One-liner

When evaluating a zero-to-one opportunity, look for at least two of three inflection points firing at the same time: a tech step-function, a consumer behavior shift, and a business model shift.

## The framework

Aparna calls these the three inflection points that make the timing right for a new product:

1. **Tech step-function** — A genuine capability leap, not an incremental improvement. Deep learning for Google Lens, mobile as a financial interface for Robinhood, LLMs and reasoning models today. The key word is step-function: the underlying tech had to cross a threshold.

2. **Consumer behavior shift** — People are already doing something differently, and the product rides that wave. For Lens: when phone storage became essentially free, people stopped taking photos only to share and started using the camera as a keyboard for the real world — orders of magnitude more photos, orders of magnitude more need to understand them.

3. **Business model shift** — A new monetization structure that wasn't viable before. Search unlocked second-price auctions and CPCs. SaaS unlocked recurring enterprise revenue. AI is still working out whether it's seats, usage-on-tap, or outcome-based fees.

**The threshold:** Two out of three is sufficient. All three firing together is exceptional. One alone is usually not enough.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** You're in the "solve" mode — building something genuinely new, zero-to-one, trying to decide if now is the right moment to start.

**Does not apply when:** You're in scale mode — post-product-market fit, optimizing a known product, or doing a feature iteration on an established surface. At that stage the timing question is settled; execution and metrics take over.

Also does not apply as a completeness check after launch. It's a launch timing diagnostic, not a product quality rubric.

## Signature questions

1. What is the specific tech step-function that makes this possible today but not two years ago? If you can't name it precisely, the inflection may not be real.
2. What consumer behavior has shifted that creates demand for this? Are people already doing the thing differently, or are you betting they will?
3. Is there a new business model available that wasn't viable before? Or are you trying to win on the same monetization logic as incumbents?
4. Are you in "solve" mode or "scale" mode right now? Are you comfortable with wide lurches in direction before the product finds its shape?
5. What is the local hill you could get stuck on if you fix on an idea too early?

## Common misuse

Aparna explicitly warns against premature metric-setting in the solve phase: calling out "grownup metrics" too early (CTR, retention) when the user base is still tiny gives false precision and nudges teams toward the wrong hill. The framework is paired with a second discipline — **solve before scale** — which means staying deliberately chaotic and direction-flexible until the core thing works. Teams that apply the two-of-three check to approve a launch but then immediately impose scale-mode metrics undermine what the framework is trying to protect.
