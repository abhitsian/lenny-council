## One-liner

Pick business ideas in underserved niches where demand exists but professionals aren't already swarming — because competition always erodes margin.

## The framework

Wilkinson draws directly from Charlie Munger's aphorism and builds it into a practical selection lens with three tests:

**1. Crowding check**
Ask how many people wake up each morning wanting to start this kind of business. Cafes, restaurants, project management tools, bars — everyone has the idea. Funeral homes, pest control, government form-filling software — almost nobody does. The less crowded the starting point, the more room you have.

**2. Unfair advantage test**
Find the profitable niche *within* your passion, not a business you love in the abstract. Wilkinson's example: a marketing student charging restaurants $1,000/month for social media versus charging wealth managers $5,000/month for the same work. The pivot is small; the margin difference is enormous. You need a reason you specifically can win here — domain knowledge, distribution, relationships, or a skill others lack.

**3. Moat requirement**
Once you find a niche, look for one of three defensible positions:
- **Brand** — pricing power through loyalty (AeroPress, Coca-Cola)
- **Network effects** — value compounds with users (Letterboxd, Instagram)
- **Switching costs** — painful to leave, even if customers hate you (Salesforce)

**Baby weights corollary**
For first-time entrepreneurs especially, the niche-first approach also means starting simple enough to get an early win. A web design agency that bills on delivery beats an ambitious SaaS competing against venture-backed companies. The early win builds the narrative that lets you keep going.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Choosing among multiple ideas and comparing competitive density
- Evaluating whether a bootstrap path is viable versus needing VC
- Pivoting a skill you already have toward a more lucrative customer segment
- Buying or investing in businesses (looking for moats, not just ideas)

**Does not apply when:**
- The market genuinely requires scale to work — satellite infrastructure, banking rails, AI foundation models. These need capital to compete and the niche-first logic doesn't hold.
- You've already built the reps (multiple successful ventures) and are ready to "deadlift 300 pounds." The gym analogy cuts both ways: experienced operators can tackle competitive markets that would destroy a first-timer.

## Signature questions

1. How many people wake up this morning wanting to start this kind of business? If the answer is "millions," what does that do to your margins in five years?
2. Where is your unfair advantage — not in the passion, but in the specific profitable wedge *within* that passion?
3. What moat will this business have? Brand, network effects, or switching costs — and which one applies here?
4. Is this a business or a job? At what scale does it stop requiring you to do the thing you hate, and is that scale achievable?
5. Are there dead bodies in this space — companies that failed before you? If so, do you actually understand *why* they died, or are you assuming you'll do it better?

## Common misuse

Wilkinson explicitly warns against applying this framework as an excuse to avoid hard things entirely. The gym analogy is directional — you start with baby weights, not forever weights. The failure mode is choosing *forever boring* instead of *strategically boring now*. He also warns against letting passion lead directly to business — he followed passion into movies (funding films, 90% loss rate) and only found the right vehicle after months of studying the industry (Letterboxd, which had network effects and a moat). The framework is market selection logic, not a passion bypass.

A second misuse: assuming that because a market is boring, it's automatically viable. The form-filling software example works because there's *real customer demand* with willingness to pay — the $20,000 grant creates obvious ROI on a $1,000 fee. Boring without demonstrable demand is just obscure.
