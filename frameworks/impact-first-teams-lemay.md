## One-liner

Three-step framework for anchoring product team goals directly to business outcomes — one step away from company goals, at every step of the work — to escape the Low Impact PM Death Spiral.

## The framework

LeMay organizes the approach into three sequential steps, applied at the team level regardless of how the broader organization runs product development:

**Step 1 — Set team goals no more than one step away from company goals.**
The goal should connect to the company's top-line success with a single mathematical operator or one "Y because" statement. If you can't add your team's goal directly back to the company goal, the cascade has gone too far. LeMay references Christina Wodtke's "center of gravity" model from *Radical Focus*: company goals at the center, every team goal orbiting one level out — not a multi-level waterfall.

**Step 2 — Keep impact first at every step.**
The impact goal is not a planning artifact filed away until the next OKR season. It drives how epics are written, how strategy decisions get made, and how team conversations run throughout the year. Don't let it get cascaded into oblivion as teams go deep into intermediate frameworks.

**Step 3 — Connect every bit of work back to impact.**
When prioritizing, measure impact in the same unit as the team goal. If the team goal is "convert X single-product users to multi-product users," impact scores in the prioritization matrix should be expressed as number of users converted — not abstract scores. This keeps the team honest about whether the work they're choosing can realistically reach the goal.

**The diagnostic question that unlocks the framework:**
"If you were the CEO of this company, would you fully fund your own team?"

LeMay uses this to surface the misalignment before the three steps. Most teams, when asked this question, don't know the answer right away. That uncertainty is the signal.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**When it applies:**
- Product teams operating at medium-to-large companies where team goals have drifted away from commercial outcomes through layers of OKR cascades
- Any team that has been running as a "feature factory" and needs to reconnect to what the business is actually measuring
- PMs preparing for or worried about layoffs — this framework directly addresses what CEOs cite when they cut teams ("work around the work")
- B2B, regulated industries, and companies with quarterly targets: LeMay explicitly argues those constraints are guides, not obstacles. Quarterly financial targets mean you already know what the business cares about.

**When it doesn't apply cleanly:**
- Very early-stage startups where the "company goal" is still a hypothesis
- Teams where the primary mandate from investors is growth at all costs, with profitability explicitly deprioritized — the revenue goal framing needs adjustment to match the actual metric investors care about
- Individual IC contributors who have no latitude to change how their team sets goals (though the diagnostic question still has personal value)

## Signature questions

1. If you were the CEO of this company, would you fully fund your own team?
2. How do your team's lowest-level goals add up to the company's highest-level goals — can you show that in one formula or one sentence?
3. What's the one sentence you'd want to be able to say at the end of this year, running into the CEO in the hallway, that would leave you feeling awesome about the team's work?
4. When you're scoring impact in your prioritization matrix, are you measuring impact in the same unit as your team goal — or in an abstract score that could mean anything?
5. What promises has your company made to the board, shareholders, or the market at large — and how does your team's work contribute to those specific promises?

## Common misuse

LeMay's explicit warning is against **sweating the middle** — spending energy making the intermediate layers of goals (objectives, initiatives, bets, strategies, OKRs) theoretically consistent with each other, while losing the thread back to the actual company goal. Teams convince themselves they're doing rigorous goal-setting because the middle layers are in harmony, but if you can't trace those layers back to what the company is actually measured on, the work has incurred compounding risk at every abstraction step.

A secondary misuse he names: treating the diagnostic question as confrontational and skipping it. The gentle version — "What's one sentence you'd want to say to the CEO at year-end?" — surfaces the same misalignment while bringing the team into the conversation collaboratively.

He also warns against conflating the PM as the sole "mini CEO" on the team. The PM's job is to bring CEO-level commercial thinking to the whole team — not to own it alone. Engineers and designers often arrive at the specific impact-level goal before the PM does.
