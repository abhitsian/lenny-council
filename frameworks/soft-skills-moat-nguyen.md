## One-liner

As AI commoditizes hard skills — coding, writing, design — competitive advantage shifts to the things models still cannot do well: creative thinking, listening, rapid iteration on user feedback, and people management.

## The framework

Nguyen draws a distinction between two categories of skill:

**Hard skills (being commoditized)**
- Coding, front-end engineering, writing, design
- These are replicable from patterns in training data
- Models are approaching the top 1% in many of these

**Soft skills (durable moat)**
- **Creative thinking** — generating a large, diverse idea space and filtering it; aesthetic judgment; connecting dots across disparate fields
- **Listening** — building something that a general-purpose model won't replace, because the moat lives in how well you understand a specific set of users and can rapidly iterate on their feedback
- **People management** — AI research progress is bottlenecked by research management: allocating scarce compute to the highest-conviction paths, running prioritization across experiments, and composing teams for maximum output
- **Collaboration and emotional intelligence** — the ability to form high-performing human groups around ambiguous goals; Canvas wouldn't have shipped without this

The underlying logic: hard skills are learnable from data at scale. Soft skills require modeling people — their preferences, trust levels, emotional states — and that's precisely where models are still weakest.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Deciding which skills to develop or hire for as AI tooling improves
- Evaluating whether a role is durable or will compress over a 3–5 year horizon
- Thinking about what makes a product team defensible when the underlying model keeps getting better for free

**Does not apply when:**
- The work requires frontier technical depth (model training, evals design) — those hard skills still compound with AI, not against it
- You are early in a career and haven't yet built baseline hard skills; the moat argument assumes you have the floor already

## Signature questions

1. If a general-purpose model gets 10x better next year, what specific part of your job is it actually replacing — and what's left?
2. Where does your real moat live: in the technical output, or in how well you listen to the people using what you build?
3. How are you generating and filtering ideas differently than what a prompted model would produce?
4. Who on this team would you keep if the model could write all the code? What do those people actually do?
5. What's the highest-ROI place to allocate your attention right now — and how confident are you that AI couldn't make that call as well as you?

## Common misuse

Nguyen explicitly does not argue that hard skills are worthless. The failure mode is using this framework as permission to stop building technical depth. Her own career move — from engineer to researcher after Claude got good at front-end — was toward deeper, harder-to-replicate technical work, not away from technical work entirely. The framework targets the specific hard skills that are direct targets of AI substitution. Frontier research, evals design, and model training compound in value alongside AI progress; commodity execution work does not.
