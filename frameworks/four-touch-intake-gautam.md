## One-liner

Embed product teams directly with users through four recurring touchpoints — Demo Days, Working Sessions, Dress Rehearsals, and Office Hours — to maintain real-time feedback loops in high-chaos, high-stakes environments.

## The framework

Upasna implemented this system at CNN to manage editorial intake for a content management platform serving journalists globally. The four touchpoints each serve a distinct purpose:

**1. Demo Days (weekly)**
Facilitated by the PM, design lead, and tech lead. Used to deep-dive existing features, preview upcoming ones, and recreate journalist workflows as a show-and-tell. Open to the full business, not just direct editorial partners. Primary function: evangelism and smart repetition.

**2. Working Sessions**
Small breakout groups of journalists and editors recreate their actual workflows inside the new platform. Originally called "user testing sessions" but renamed to "working sessions" to signal collaboration rather than evaluation. Each team gets 3–6 sessions of ~2 hours before onboarding, because workflow patterns differ radically across beats (politics, entertainment, health, homepage). Primary function: friction surfacing and deep feedback.

**3. Breaking News Dress Rehearsals**
Scripted simulations of a breaking news scenario — from the first news-gathering email to the moment the article is published. Engineers and editorial support observe in real time while PMs and editorial leads facilitate. Designed to stress-test the platform at breaking-news speed. Primary function: platform stability validation under live conditions.

**4. Office Hours (weekly, sometimes twice weekly during heavy onboarding)**
Open blocks of time for the PM, design partner, and tech lead to answer questions, troubleshoot workflow friction, and gather unstructured feedback. Primary function: ongoing support and low-barrier communication.

The model works because customers — journalists — are embedded into the product discovery process throughout, not just consulted at launch. When the team has to tell them something isn't viable, they already understand the reasoning because they've been along for the ride.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Your users have unpredictable, high-stakes schedules that compete with product team access (journalists, doctors, emergency responders, trading floor operators).
- You're migrating users off a legacy platform onto a new one, requiring both trust-building and workflow validation.
- You need to serve diverse user segments with meaningfully different workflows on the same platform.
- Relationship-building between product and your user group has historically been absent or fragmented.

**Does not apply when:**
- Your users are consumers at scale — you can't run working sessions with millions of people. The model is designed for a bounded, expert user base.
- Your product team has no authority to dedicate recurring time to structured touchpoints. The model demands consistent schedule commitment.
- User workflows are homogeneous enough that a single round of research suffices.

## Signature questions

1. How far are you from your users right now, and how consistently are you having conversations with them?
2. If breaking news broke tomorrow, could your platform handle it — have you stress-tested it at that speed?
3. When you tell your users something isn't viable, do they believe you — or does it feel like a black box decision?
4. Are your engineers embedded in user feedback sessions, or do they only hear about user needs filtered through the PM?
5. What would not exist in your product without your direct initiative? (Gautam's interview question — she uses it to screen for high agency.)

## Common misuse

Gautam explicitly warns against treating the model as a fixed, rigid schedule that runs regardless of context. Her point about buffers is central: the whole system only works because it accounts for chaos. Teams that try to enforce the touchpoints as non-negotiable ceremonies — insisting journalists show up even when breaking news is happening — will destroy the trust the model is designed to build. The model works because it treats the users' primary job (journalism) as the real priority and positions product access as something that has to be earned and scheduled flexibly around that.

A secondary misuse: running the four touchpoints without closing the loop. Gautam's team earns credibility specifically because after gathering feedback they tell users either "we built it," "we optimized what exists," or "it's not viable and here's why." Collecting feedback without a response is worse than not asking.
