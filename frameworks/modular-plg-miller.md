## One-liner

Map your customer journey segment by segment and only remove humans where the conditions on the ground — complexity, buyer type, price point, migration burden — actually support self-service; use humans as a backstop everywhere else.

## The framework

Miller's approach has four parts:

**1. Define what PLG means before you start**
Ask: why do you want to be product led? What do you assume changes for the business or the customer? And define what "product led" actually means in your context before building toward it. Many teams conflate PLG with fully self-service, which Miller treats as a category error.

**2. Diagnose the journey segment by segment**
For each stage — acquisition, activation, purchase, expansion — answer:
- Who is the customer? How do they currently buy and how would they prefer to buy?
- Is this a top-down or bottoms-up decision?
- How complex is the migration or onboarding? Is non-consumption or a competitor the baseline?
- How comfortable is the target market with the technology category?

The answers tell you where self-service is favorable and where a human is still the right call.

**3. Use humans as a backstop, not the engine**
The design goal is: your product grows revenue, and humans step in where the product genuinely can't. Hardship cancellations, complex migrations, IT security reviews — these may warrant a human escalation path without making the company any less product led. The test is whether the human involvement is a structural requirement or just organizational inertia.

**4. Radical accountability to find the opportunities**
Within the growth team, treat every neglected surface as your problem. If no one owns something important, take it. The pricing page story at HubSpot — finding an abandoned checkout page, taking it, redesigning it around discoverability / desirability / doability — came from this posture. A step-function change in revenue followed.

The macro flywheel that ties it together: **give value before you extract value**. Free software should deliver genuine value and let customers naturally hit the ceiling of what free covers. If the product has done its job, purchase becomes a no-brainer without anyone selling.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- Your business serves a range of customer segments with meaningfully different buying behaviors (some self-serve easily, others need hand-holding).
- You have the appetite to instrument the funnel and run experiments — the framework is data-dependent.
- You are willing to operate a hybrid motion rather than chase a pure self-service ideal.

**Does not apply when:**
- You are selling a single, highly complex enterprise product where every deal is bespoke — the modular analysis still produces the answer "use humans everywhere."
- Your organization is unwilling to give a growth team real engineering cycles and tooling. The biggest PLG failure mode Miller names is "hiring a head of growth, giving them no resources, and expecting them to pull a rabbit out of their hat."

## Signature questions

1. Why do you want to be product led? What do you assume changes for the business or the customer if you get there?
2. Walk me through the customer journey from zero to one — at which stages does a human actually need to be involved, and why?
3. Is self-service checkout even the right starting point given where your biggest leverage is? Or are there earlier-funnel problems to solve first?
4. What does your data instrumentation look like right now? If you can't measure activation, why run PLG experiments at all?
5. Are you solving a customer problem or a business problem? If it's a business problem, have you asked why the customer problem hasn't solved it yet?

## Common misuse

The most explicit warning in the transcript: treating PLG and full self-service as interchangeable. Miller says this directly — companies that "assume that in order to be a PLG company you can use that interchangeably with being a fully self-service business" are missing the point. Brute-forcing a self-service motion onto customers who need migration help, security conversations, or a considered purchase decision is solving for the business's desires, not the customer's. The result is churn, not efficiency.

A secondary failure mode: expecting PLG to produce short-term revenue lift like incremental sales headcount. PLG is R&D — it plants seeds. Companies that cut bait too early because they don't see immediate results misunderstand the investment horizon.
