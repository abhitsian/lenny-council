## One-liner

Founder-led sales works in two phases: a research collection phase (where the founder learns faster than any salesperson could) and a revenue phase (where that learning earns you the right to close), and most founders skip straight to revenue and miss the whole point.

## The framework

**Phase 1 — Research collection (zero to ~$500K–$1M ARR)**

The goal is not revenue. It is learning as fast as possible to reach product-market fit pulse. The founder — not a salesperson — must run this phase because only the founder can see the "budding moments" in a conversation that signal what the market actually wants.

Key moves:
- **Lead with vulnerability.** Tell prospects you are early stage. This produces honest feedback. Presenting a fully baked product shuts down critique and you get polite noise.
- **Qualify at the top of the funnel.** The bottleneck is almost never at the bottom. The biggest failure mode is talking to the wrong people. Gate-check every prospect: Is the problem growing? Is it being measured? Have they tried to solve it? If not, move on.
- **Messaging formula (outbound):** Relevancy → counterintuitive hook → problem focus → concise enough to read on mobile without scrolling. Do not talk about the solution. Lead with the problem.
- **Delay the demo upmarket.** The demo is the one card you fully control. Once shown, the dreaminess is gone. For deals worth hundreds of thousands, withhold it until the right people are in the room and the buyer has invested time.
- **Co-author the scope of work.** Turn the buyer into a guide by asking them to define what success looks like together. This reveals whether they are ready to buy technology at all — or need a service contract first.
- **Service-before-technology wedge.** ~40–50% of B2B SaaS enterprise deals require selling a service first. The buyer doesn't have a strategy or process for adopting the technology yet. Selling a 90-day service contract gets you in the door, earns the logo, and lets you set the adoption frame before a competitor does.

**Phase 2 — Revenue optimization (post $500K–$1M ARR)**

Once you have product-market fit pulse, shift to closing. Hire sales. Move from experimenting with who and what to exploiting what you've confirmed.

**Enterprise-specific levers:**
- Know the signer before procurement. CFO, CLO, or head of procurement — brief them directly or your deal loses queue position.
- Make procurement's job easy. Fill out their forms. Classify yourself clearly so they don't dump you into the high-risk kitchen-sink MSA.
- Split service and technology contracts to create an incentive to get through IT due diligence faster.
- Enterprise deals compound: once you are a preferred vendor, you get cross-BU access, competitor lockout (short-term), and deals that can grow 5x in a year.

**GTM segmentation:**
- **Enterprise (500+ employees):** User and buyer are different. High complexity, long cycles (6–12 months), high ACV ($50–200K). Worth the headache because of compounding returns.
- **SMB:** User and buyer are the same. Fast cycle. But churn is brutal — they leave fast and loudly.
- **Mid-market:** Straddles two different go-to-markets (high volume vs. high value). Hard to start there. Heavy integration work often lands with third-party consultants.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You are pre-product-market fit, zero to first $1M ARR
- You are selling top-down into enterprises or mid-market where buyer ≠ user
- You have a novel insight but limited brand or reference-ability
- You are trying to decide whether your sales problem is a product problem or a messaging problem

**Does not apply when:**
- You are a late-stage company with established brand and a trained sales team (April Dunford's buyer-education framework is more relevant there)
- You are selling a high-volume, low-ACV product to SMB — that is a marketing game, not a sales conversation game
- Your market already knows how to buy what you're selling and has a process in place — skip the service wedge and go straight to technology

## Signature questions

1. Is the problem you're solving growing and widening — or is it a fixed annoyance that nobody prioritizes fixing?
2. Are your prospects measuring and managing this problem today? If not, there's probably nothing to sell yet.
3. What are you hearing in the "budding moments" of these calls — the things your buyer says that you didn't expect and that are changing how you see the market?
4. Are you talking to the wrong people, or are you saying something true but uninteresting? (Qualification and messaging are almost always the real bottleneck, not closing.)
5. Does this buyer need to buy a service before they can buy technology — do they have a process and strategy in place to adopt what you're selling?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Abel explicitly warns against: treating founder-led sales as a revenue exercise from day one. Founders hire a salesperson too early (before $500K–$1M ARR), race through demos to hit pipeline numbers, and strip out the learning that only the founder can extract. The salesperson reports back "the market doesn't care" — and the founder blames the salesperson instead of the product or the insight. Another version: sending high-volume cold outreach before manually testing 30 carefully written notes first. Volume without parameters yields nothing and burns your domain health.
