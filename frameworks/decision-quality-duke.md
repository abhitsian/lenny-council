## One-liner

Judge a decision by what you knew when you made it, not by how it turned out, because outcomes are part luck and hidden information — and protect yourself from your own biases by deciding in advance what signals would make you stop.

## The framework

1. **Stop "resulting."** Resulting is reading the quality of a decision off its outcome. Good outcomes can come from bad decisions and vice versa, because luck and hidden information sit between the choice and the result. The parent of one well-behaved kid who thinks they're a great parent is resulting; with four kids you find out how little of the outcome you controlled.
2. **Shorten the feedback loop instead of waiting for the outcome.** Don't accept "our feedback loop is ten years." Name the things that are *necessary but not sufficient* for the eventual outcome, or correlated with it (for a VC: does it fund at Series A?), and grade yourself on those interim forecasts now. You are already making those forecasts implicitly — make them explicit so you can check your accuracy.
3. **Make the implicit explicit.** Turn "I just know a good founder when I see one" into a decision rubric or structured forecast. This both raises decision quality in the moment and closes feedback loops you couldn't close before. (Kahneman's hiring example: doing this lifted hit rate from ~50% to ~65%.)
4. **Run a pre-mortem to set kill criteria.** A pre-mortem on its own rarely changes the plan. Its real value is producing kill criteria: imagine the project is already dead six months out, list the early signals you'd have seen, then turn each signal into a pre-committed action ("if they only want to talk price and won't demo, we kill the deal").
5. **Pre-commit, because you won't decide well in the moment.** Once you've launched something you're slow to quit it — sunk cost, ownership, identity all push you to keep going until quitting "isn't a decision" anymore. The kill switch has to be set before you're attached.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** decisions made under genuine uncertainty where the outcome is delayed or noisy — investing, hiring, roadmap bets, long sales cycles, whether to keep funding a project. Also any retro where the team is grading a past call by its result.
- **Doesn't add much:** when the feedback loop is genuinely tight and clean and luck plays almost no role — then the outcome largely *is* the signal. And Duke is candid that knowing the biases doesn't automatically fix them: Kahneman told her he couldn't use this stuff in his own day-to-day. The lift comes from building it into process (rubrics, pre-commitments), not from individual willpower.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- If this bet works out, will you actually know your decision was good — or could you have just gotten lucky?
- You're saying the feedback loop is long. What's the nearest thing that's *necessary but not sufficient* for the outcome, that you could measure in weeks instead of years?
- What would you have to see to kill this? Have you written it down before you're attached to it?
- Are you judging this past call by what you knew then, or by how it turned out?
- The thing you're pounding the table about — is it actually predictive, or does it just feel important? How would you check?

## Common misuse

Two failure modes Duke names. First, judging decisions by outcomes ("resulting") — rewarding a call because it worked, when it only worked by luck, which teaches the team the wrong lesson. Second, running a pre-mortem and stopping there: listing risks feels productive but rarely changes the plan. The fix is the pre-commitment — each risk signal has to become a kill criterion with an attached action, decided before launch, because in the moment sunk cost and ownership will keep you in too long.
