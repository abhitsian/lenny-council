## One-liner

A four-step diagnostic process — Diagnose the real problem, Analyze competitors, Take a different path, Experiment — that replaces playbook copying with customer-grounded differentiation.

## The framework

Krithika coins the DATE acronym after walking Lenny through the four steps. Each step is a deliberate gate before the next.

**D — Diagnose the actual problem**
Before hiring for a channel or tactic, look at where the funnel breaks. If top-of-funnel is full but conversion is weak, the problem is positioning or product-market fit, not lead volume. The diagnostic tells you whether to invest in demand generation or in product marketing.

**A — Analyze competitors' approaches**
Not to copy them, but to map the space. Competitor analysis gives you a baseline and exposes gaps and niches. The goal is to understand the inputs behind others' strategies — context, timing, competitive dynamics — not just their outputs or tactics.

**T — Take a different path**
Intentional differentiation is the core of the framework. Look outside your direct category for inspiration. "Cheaper" is a race to the bottom and will not hold as a durable position. "Different" aligns with what customers actually value.

**E — Experiment, then scale what works**
Run small tests before doubling down. Discard work that doesn't move real metrics (pipeline, signups, revenue) even if it cost significant effort. Guard against the sunk cost fallacy.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- A founder or marketing leader is about to hire a channel specialist or launch a growth motion and hasn't first diagnosed the actual funnel problem
- A company is tempted to replicate a competitor's tactics without examining the context that made those tactics work
- A marketing team needs to pick between brand, demand gen, or product marketing investment and needs a principled way to decide

**Does not apply when:**
- You don't yet have product-market fit. The framework assumes you have a working product with some conversion signal — if you have a leaky bottom-of-funnel, no amount of top-of-funnel tactics will fix it, and the DATE process should surface that before you start
- You need speed over rigor in a very early, pre-signal stage where even diagnosis data is thin

## Signature questions

1. Where does your funnel actually break? Are people coming in but not converting, or are you not bringing enough people in at all?
2. When you look at what your competitors are doing, what are you learning about the inputs — the context, the timing, the customer dynamics — that led to their strategy? Not just the outputs?
3. If you took a completely different path than everyone else in your category, what would that look like? What would make you genuinely distinct rather than incrementally better?
4. What are the real metrics you are optimizing for — pipeline, revenue, signups — not vanity metrics like clicks, views, and impressions?
5. Have you run the diagnostic in the last three to six months? As the company grows, the top-level goal shifts; what was the right investment last quarter may be wrong now.

## Common misuse

The failure mode Krithika warns against explicitly is skipping the Diagnose step and jumping straight to a channel tactic. Founders often arrive having already decided they need a demand gen hire or a paid social campaign. Without diagnosing whether the actual problem is low top-of-funnel volume or weak bottom-of-funnel conversion, they may hire the wrong person entirely — a demand gen lead when the real need is a product marketer working on competitive positioning and sales enablement.

A secondary misuse: treating the Analyze step as permission to copy. Analyzing competitors is meant to reveal gaps and inform differentiation, not surface a safer version of what everyone else is doing. Taking the same approach but doing it slightly better is, in her framing, optimizing for a local maximum rather than a genuine market shift.
