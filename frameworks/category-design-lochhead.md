## One-liner

Instead of competing to be the best in an existing market, you design a new market — get
a chunk of the world to agree with your definition of a problem, then your solution — and
because the category leader captures most of the economics, that's where the money is.

## The framework

1. **Choose to create demand, not capture it.** Every founder makes a decision they often
   don't realize they made: compete inside an existing category (fight for the leftovers) or
   design a new one. Category design is "the distinction or delta between create demand and
   capture demand."
2. **Frame, name, and claim a problem.** Problems create categories. You either solve a new
   problem or "reframe, name and claim an existing problem in a very different way." When
   people see the problem differently, they become open to a new solution. The product is
   downstream of the problem, not the other way around.
3. **Build a point of view (POV).** A POV moves the world "from the way it is to a new and
   different way" — Lochhead calls these "frotos / from-tos." The POV is about the customer's
   problem, not about you. Brands are about us; categories are about customers.
4. **Use languaging.** The strategic use of language to change thinking. Use enough new
   language that the thing reads as new, but not so much that nobody understands you — "meet
   the category where it is and bring them forward." The company that creates the category's
   language wins (Starbucks teaching "venti/grande," Otis renaming the elevator a "vertical
   railway").
5. **Compete against the status quo, not a competitor.** Category designers don't fight
   product-to-product. The enemy is "the way it is now." You're leading people away from the
   old default, not claiming your widget beats their widget.
6. **Win the economics by getting the space to tip.** "The company that designs the space and
   gets it to tip at scale" — the one whose definition of the problem the market adopts —
   becomes the category king/queen and takes roughly two-thirds of the value.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

- **Applies:** when you're chasing exponential outcomes, not incremental ones — a genuinely
  new problem, or an old problem the market is ready to see reframed. Strongest for startups
  that can move first and set the agenda, and for an already-winning company that has outgrown
  its niche and needs to "uplevel" to the bigger category before a competitor does.
- **Doesn't:** when the right move is incremental better — Lochhead explicitly carves this
  out. A PM running a successful product with a big install base, stack-ranking the next
  features to build, "That's not category design." Some work (his example: the air-traffic
  control system, Boeing's safety engineering) *should* be incremental. Don't force category
  design where the existing problem and solution are already well understood — there's no room
  for a new solution there.
- **Tension with April Dunford (this card's deliberate counterpoint):** Dunford favors
  anchoring to an *existing* category so you don't burn the pitch explaining a new word, and
  she's skeptical of category creation as the default path — niching inside a known category
  is usually the easier road to traction. Lochhead calls that same instinct "category design
  for the cowards" and says modern positioning-against-competitors means you've already
  agreed to fight for the 24%. They're not arguing about facts; they disagree on the bet.
  Dunford optimizes for getting understood and winning a defined market now. Lochhead
  optimizes for the rare exponential outcome where one company takes most of the economics —
  and accepts that most attempts fail. Use Dunford when there are real alternatives and a
  sales motion to win today; reach for Lochhead when the problem itself is unclaimed and
  you're willing to spend years teaching the market. Don't run both at once on the same bet.

## Signature questions *(council field)*

- Are you trying to create demand or capture it? If you're positioning against competitors,
  you've already decided to fight for the 24% somebody else designed.
- What's the problem, and has anyone named it? If the problem is already well understood with
  a well-understood solution, why would the world want a new one?
- What's your from-to? Where are you moving people away from, and toward what?
- Are you saying your thing is *better*, or *different*? "Better" means your reference point
  is the incumbent — and your reference point is the past.
- Who designs the language here? If the category already has words and you're using them,
  you're playing in someone else's category.
- Is this an exponential-different bet or an incremental-better one? Both are legitimate —
  but only one of them is category design.

## Common misuse

The "better" trap: taking an existing problem with an existing solution, shipping the same
thing, telling the world it's better, and expecting the world to switch. Lochhead's repeated
example is Threads — a 10x-better-on-paper product attacking Twitter's well-understood
problem, which he predicted would fail because "you can't attack an incumbent category queen
unless you frame, name and claim a new or different problem." The deeper failure mode he
warns against is leading with the product instead of the problem — believing the best product
wins, when the problem is what makes the solution matter.
