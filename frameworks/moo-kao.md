## One-liner

Before presenting anything, take two minutes to name the 2–3 most obvious objections someone will raise — so you stop being blindsided by them and can address them upfront or reframe before you speak.

## The framework

**MOO = Most Obvious Objection.** Wes uses it as a daily mental filter on everything she's about to say or write.

The core move has two steps:

1. **Name the obvious objections.** Before a meeting, Slack message, pitch, or strategy doc, ask: "What are the obvious objections someone is likely to raise?" You won't catch all of them, but the obvious ones are almost always findable in under two minutes — often in seconds.

2. **Decide what to do with each one.** You have three options:
   - Volunteer the counter-argument upfront, defusing it before it lands.
   - Reframe how you're presenting the idea so the objection is less likely to arise.
   - Hold it in reserve so you're not caught flat-footed when it surfaces.

MOO sits alongside several other named frameworks Wes teaches:

- **Sales, then logistics** — Sell the why before describing the how. Get buy-in first; share the logistics only after.
- **Blast radius** — Every message has downstream cost multiplied by audience size. A confusing Slack to 15 people creates 15 confused people. Thirty extra seconds of editing prevents hours of back-and-forth.
- **Strategy, not self-expression** — Give feedback in service of behavior change, not emotional release. Trim 90% of what you want to say; keep only what moves the person toward the change you need.
- **CEDAF (delegation)** — Comprehension, Excitement, De-risk, Align, Feedback. A checklist to delegate well without lowering standards.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** You're about to share a recommendation, proposal, or ask — in writing or in a meeting — and you want to reduce surprise and stay grounded under pushback. Especially useful with execs who make fast decisions and will surface the obvious gap immediately.

**Less relevant when:** You're in a brainstorming mode where surfacing all directions matters more than defending one, or when the audience is already deeply aligned and you're just sharing status.

The broader principle — that communication is a means to an end, not self-expression — applies to all of Wes's frameworks. MOO is just the most concrete, daily-use trigger.

## Signature questions

1. What are the two or three most obvious objections someone will raise to this?
2. Am I starting with the "sale" — the why — or have I jumped straight into logistics?
3. What is the blast radius of this message? If it's confusing for fifteen people, how much back-and-forth does that generate?
4. Am I speaking at the right level of confidence — not overstating what I know as fact, not under-claiming a strong recommendation?
5. If I were giving this person feedback, am I saying it to be heard or to change their behavior?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Wes explicitly warns against is the **single-minded martyr**: someone who has anticipated objections but keeps pushing their original recommendation regardless, framing all evidence in favor of their view. MOO is not a tool for pre-loading counterarguments so you can dismiss them faster. It's a tool for honest calibration — if the obvious objection is actually right, the correct move is to right-size the proposal or pause it. Wes's client kept pitching growth initiatives while the company-wide goal was retention; she wasn't blindsided by objections, she just ignored what they were signaling.

A second misuse is treating concision as brevity. MOO works best when what you're presenting is already clear to you. Wes's underlying point is that most communication problems trace back to the speaker not yet knowing their own argument — and no amount of objection-anticipation fixes unclear thinking.
