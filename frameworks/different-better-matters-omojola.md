## One-liner

A product has to clear four bars to build durable advantage: it must be different from what exists, better than what exists, better in a way the end user actually values, and operating in a domain large enough to sustain the economics.

## The framework

Ayo developed this while building Cash App's instant settlement feature and the Cash Card. He breaks differentiation into four successive filters:

1. **Different** — Your product must do something that existing products don't. This is the lowest bar; you just have to look at what's out there and build something else.
2. **Better** — Being different isn't sufficient if the difference doesn't produce a better outcome. "Better" here means objectively superior on a dimension users care about, not just more expensive.
3. **Better in a way that matters to the end user** — The improvement has to map to something the user experiences as meaningful. Cash App's differentiator was instant access to money: send a dollar, spend it now. That was the cut-through answer to "why not Venmo?"
4. **In a domain that matters economically** — Even if you clear the first three bars, the domain has to carry the economics. Art can be different, better, and meaningful to users. It's a complex thing to scale. The full pipeline — how you ship, build, talk about it — still has to work.

The framework also carries a corollary for regulated or complex industries: you cannot outsource depth. Someone on the team has to become the true expert — not stop at the first expert's answer, but push until they reach the end. That same intensity applied to the Cash Card meant testing over 1,000 combinations of plastic, overlay, paper, envelope, and laser-engraving settings before shipping.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:** You are building in a competitive market where alternatives already exist, and you need a crisp answer to "why you instead of them" — for a consumer pitch, an internal investment case, or a hiring conversation. Also applies when deciding whether to double down on a domain: the economics check prevents investing deeply in a niche that can't scale.

**Doesn't fully apply when:** You are the first mover with no direct comparison product, or when the differentiation is network-effects-based (where "better" compounds through scale, not through an inherent feature advantage). Ayo explicitly notes he doesn't know which elements of Cash App's success are replicable — the framework is a filter, not a guarantee.

## Signature questions

1. What is the one thing your product does that the user literally cannot do anywhere else right now?
2. If someone asks "why not [competitor]?" what is the single-sentence answer — and can you demonstrate it in 30 seconds?
3. Is this domain large enough that getting the first three bars right actually produces a business, or is it a niche that tops out too small?
4. Who on the team is the actual expert on this domain — not the most credentialed, but the person who has pushed past the first expert's answer all the way to the end?
5. Is your differentiation on a dimension the user feels, or a dimension you find technically interesting?

## Common misuse

The failure mode Ayo warns against explicitly: treating differentiation as a positioning exercise rather than an execution discipline. Cash App's instant settlement wasn't a marketing claim — it was the result of going deep into regulatory wallpaper, sitting in rooms with product, engineering, legal, and compliance reading regulations blown up on a projector screen, and asking "what does this really mean?" The framework collapses if the team stops at the first expert's answer. Surface-level differentiation, claimed but not built into the product's mechanics, doesn't hold under competitive pressure — Venmo and others eventually closed many of Cash App's gaps. The durable part was the compound effect of doing all the things right simultaneously, not any single differentiator.
