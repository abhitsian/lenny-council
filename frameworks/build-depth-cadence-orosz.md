## One-liner

Build genuine expertise in a domain first, then commit to a regular publishing cadence with external accountability — paying subscribers and public deadlines create the pressure needed to produce consistently excellent work.

## The framework

Gergely describes a four-part sequence he followed (and prescribes):

1. **Build depth** — Spend years accumulating real, hands-on experience in a domain. This is the raw material. Without it, there's nothing credible to teach. Gergely spent 6+ years at Skype, Skyscanner, and Uber before any of this became possible.

2. **Start sharing on the side** — Write a blog, do meetup talks, publish drafts. You're not looking for immediate traction. You're building writing muscle and discovering what people actually respond to. The signal of pull — when strangers message you about something you wrote — tells you where the demand is.

3. **Create external accountability** — The key insight is that self-motivation is unreliable. Announcing a paid newsletter (where subscribers have paid in advance) or signing a book contract creates external obligation. You write because you have no choice. Recurring revenue is a structural feature, not just upside — it means thousands of people have already paid, and that pressure is what gets the work done every week.

4. **Maintain cadence, iterate on format** — Stick to a publishing rhythm even as you experiment. Try new post types. Measure what resonates. Adjust. The cadence itself is what compounds — both the audience and the writing quality improve with repetition.

## When it applies / when it doesn't

**Applies when:**
- You have genuine domain expertise that's hard to find in aggregate form
- You're willing to commit to a multi-year horizon — this compounded for Gergely over 6+ years before the newsletter existed
- You want recurring income from content, not one-off book royalties or ad revenue

**Doesn't apply when:**
- You're trying to shortcut the depth-building phase — launching a paid newsletter without real expertise won't hold subscribers
- You need near-term income — this framework assumes you can sustain yourself while building (Gergely used Uber savings)
- Your domain is entertainment rather than education — the cadence/accountability mechanism is built for educational or edutainment content

## Signature questions

1. Do you have genuine depth in this domain, or just opinions about it? What have you actually built or led that most people haven't?
2. Who is holding you accountable to publish? If the answer is only yourself, why will next month be different from last month?
3. Have you seen any signal of pull — a blog post that blew up, a tweet that got more responses than expected, a topic where strangers reached out? That's the direction.
4. What is your minimum viable cadence, and have you told people publicly what to expect from you?
5. Are you building something tied entirely to your personal output, or is there a path to eventually systematizing it? (And are you okay with the former for now?)

## Common misuse

The failure mode Gergely explicitly warns against: treating the newsletter as the first step, rather than as step three or four. People launch a paid newsletter before they've built domain depth or a habit of sharing publicly, expecting the recurring revenue model to do the work. The recurring structure creates pressure to produce — but it can't manufacture something worth reading. Without years of accumulated expertise and an existing audience signal, the subscription model just accelerates churn.

A secondary misuse: setting outcome-based goals ("I want 20,000 subscribers") rather than process goals ("I will publish every two weeks"). Subscriber counts are not in your control. Showing up with something good is.
